Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Probability: $P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B)$; $P(A|B)=P(A\cap B)/P(B)$; Bayes' theorem $P(A|B)=P(B|A)P(A)/P(B)$.
  • Discrete distributions: Binomial $P(X=x)=\binom nxp^x(1-p)^{n-x}$, $E=np$, $\text{Var}=np(1-p)$; Poisson $P(X=x)=e^{-\lambda}\lambda^x/x!$, $E=\text{Var}=\lambda$.
  • Continuous distributions: Uniform $E=(a+b)/2$, $\text{Var}=(b-a)^2/12$; Exponential $E=1/\lambda$, memoryless; Normal via $Z=(X-\mu)/\sigma$.
  • CLT & sampling: $\bar X\ \dot\sim\ N(\mu,\sigma^2/n)$ for large $n$; standard error $\sigma/\sqrt n$.
  • Inference: CI $\bar x\pm z_{\alpha/2}\sigma/\sqrt n$ (or $t$, $\sigma$ unknown); one-sample/paired $t=\dfrac{\bar x-\mu_0}{s/\sqrt n}$; two-sample pooled $t$; $F$-test for variances; correlation/regression.
  • ANOVA & beyond: $F=MSB/MSW$ for 3+ groups; Bonferroni correction for follow-up comparisons; nonparametric rank-based alternatives (Mann-Whitney, Wilcoxon, Kruskal-Wallis) when Normality fails.

0 Recap: Probability & Distributions (Weeks 8–9)

The exam-critical skill is recognising which distribution a scenario calls for: fixed number of independent trials with constant success probability → Binomial; count of rare events over an interval → Poisson; waiting time between Poisson events → Exponential.

Key idea

$$P(X=x)_{\text{Binom}}=\binom nxp^x(1-p)^{n-x}, \qquad P(X=x)_{\text{Poisson}}=\frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^x}{x!}, \qquad P(X>x)_{\text{Exp}}=e^{-\lambda x}$$

Worked example

Exam-style. A factory's defect rate is 4%. In a sample of 50 items, use the Poisson approximation to estimate $P(X=2)$, then find the mean time between defects if defects occur continuously at this same average rate per hour of production (assume 50 items/hour).

$\lambda=np=50(0.04)=2$. $P(X=2)=\dfrac{e^{-2}2^2}{2!}=\dfrac{0.1353\times4}{2}\approx0.2707$.

Mean time between defects (Exponential, same $\lambda=2$ per hour): $E(X)=1/\lambda=0.5$ hours $=30$ minutes.

Practice 1

A test has 6 true/false questions, all guessed. Find $P(X\ge5)$ correct.

Solution

$P(X=5)=\binom65(0.5)^5(0.5)^1=6(0.5)^6=\dfrac{6}{64}=0.09375$.

$P(X=6)=(0.5)^6=\dfrac{1}{64}=0.015625$.

$P(X\ge5)=0.09375+0.015625=0.109375$.

Practice 2

Website errors occur at a Poisson rate of 1.5 per hour. Find $P(\text{no errors in 2 hours})$.

Solution

$\lambda=1.5\times2=3$. $P(X=0)=e^{-3}\approx0.0498$.

Practice 3

A component's lifetime is Exponential with mean 800 hours. Find $P(X<400)$.

Solution

$\lambda=1/800$. $P(X<400)=1-e^{-400/800}=1-e^{-0.5}\approx0.3935$.

Practice 4

Two events: $P(A)=0.4,P(B)=0.5$, independent. Find $P(A\cap B)$ and $P(A\cup B)$.

Solution

Independent: $P(A\cap B)=0.4\times0.5=0.2$.

$P(A\cup B)=0.4+0.5-0.2=0.7$.

Practice 5

A disease affects 2% of a population; a test is 90% accurate both ways. Find $P(\text{disease}|\text{positive})$.

Solution

$P(+)=0.02(0.9)+0.98(0.1)=0.018+0.098=0.116$.

$P(D|+)=\dfrac{0.018}{0.116}\approx0.1552$.

1 Recap: Normal Distribution, CLT & Sampling (Week 10)

Nearly every Week 10 exam question reduces to correctly standardising ($Z=(X-\mu)/\sigma$ for an individual value, or $Z=(\bar X-\mu)/(\sigma/\sqrt n)$ for a sample mean) before reading probabilities from the standard Normal.

Key idea

$$Z=\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}, \qquad Z=\frac{\bar X-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt n}$$

Worked example

Exam-style. A population has $\mu=80,\sigma=12$ (shape unknown). For $n=36$, find $P(\bar X>83)$.

$\sigma_{\bar X}=12/6=2$. $Z=(83-80)/2=1.5$.

$P(Z>1.5)\approx1-0.9332=0.0668$.

Practice 6

$X\sim N(40,6^2)$. Find $P(X<35)$.

Solution

$Z=(35-40)/6=-0.833$. $P(Z<-0.833)\approx0.2023$.

Practice 7

A population has $\mu=500,\sigma=40$. For $n=64$, find the standard error and $P(\bar X<490)$.

Solution

$\sigma_{\bar X}=40/8=5$. $Z=(490-500)/5=-2.0$. $P(Z<-2)\approx0.0228$.

Practice 8

A Uniform RV $X\sim U(0,12)$ has $E(X)=6$. Find $\text{Var}(X)$.

Solution

$\text{Var}(X)=\dfrac{12^2}{12}=12$.

2 Recap: Confidence Intervals & Hypothesis Testing (Weeks 11–12)

The exam-critical skill is choosing the correct test/interval for the data structure: one sample vs. paired vs. two independent samples, and $z$ vs. $t$ depending on whether $\sigma$ is known.

Key idea

$$\bar x\pm t_{\alpha/2,n-1}\frac{s}{\sqrt n}, \qquad t_{\text{2-sample}}=\frac{\bar x_1-\bar x_2}{\sqrt{s_p^2(1/n_1+1/n_2)}}, \qquad F=\frac{s_1^2}{s_2^2}$$

Worked example

Exam-style. Two independent samples: A ($n=10,\bar x=45,s=4$); B ($n=12,\bar x=41,s=5$). (a) Test for equal variances first via $F$. (b) Then run the appropriate two-sample test.

(a) $F=25/16=1.5625$, $df_1=11,df_2=9$ — a modest ratio, plausibly consistent with equal variances (would need table comparison to confirm formally).

(b) Assuming equal variances is reasonable: $s_p^2=\dfrac{9(16)+11(25)}{20}=\dfrac{144+275}{20}=20.95$. $t=\dfrac{45-41}{\sqrt{20.95(\frac1{10}+\frac1{12})}}=\dfrac{4}{\sqrt{3.843}}\approx2.041$, $df=20$.

Practice 9

A sample of 20 has $\bar x=65,s=8$ (unknown $\sigma$). Find a 95% CI for $\mu$ (use $t_{0.025,19}=2.093$).

Solution

Margin $=2.093\times8/\sqrt{20}=2.093\times1.789\approx3.744$.

Answer: $(61.26,68.74)$.

Practice 10

A sample of 30 has $\bar x=102,s=10$. Test $H_0:\mu=100$ vs. $H_1:\mu\ne100$ by computing the test statistic.

Solution

$t=(102-100)/(10/\sqrt{30})=2/1.826\approx1.095$, $df=29$.

Practice 11

Data: $x=2,4,6,8$; $y=3,5,8,9$. Find $\bar x,\bar y$ and the sums needed for the regression slope.

Solution

$\bar x=5,\bar y=6.25$.

Deviations $x$: $-3,-1,1,3$; $y$: $-3.25,-1.25,1.75,2.75$.

Products: $9.75,1.25,1.75,8.25$; sum $=21$. $(x-\bar x)^2$ sum: $9+1+1+9=20$.

$b_1=21/20=1.05$.

3 Mixed Exam-Style Problems (Weeks 8–13)

Real exam questions often combine probability/distributions with formal inference in a single multi-part problem. Read each fully before starting — later parts often reuse an earlier result.

Key idea

No single formula — the skill is recognising which tool each part needs, and carrying results between parts.

Worked example

Exam-style, multi-part. A sample of 40 components has mean lifetime $\bar x=1200$ hours, $s=180$ hours. (a) Find a 95% CI for the true mean lifetime. (b) A manufacturer claims $\mu=1250$ hours; test this claim at $\alpha=0.05$. (c) If lifetimes are Exponential (matching part (a)'s mean as $1/\lambda$), find $P(X>1500)$ for a single component.

(a) Using $z$ (large $n$): margin $=1.96\times180/\sqrt{40}\approx55.79$. CI: $(1144.2,1255.8)$.

(b) $t=(1200-1250)/(180/\sqrt{40})=-50/28.46\approx-1.757$, $df=39$ — since 1250 falls just inside the CI from (a), this is consistent with a non-significant result (fail to reject at $\alpha=0.05$, matching that the CI from (a) contains 1250).

(c) $\lambda=1/1200$. $P(X>1500)=e^{-1500/1200}=e^{-1.25}\approx0.2865$.

Practice 12

A sample of 50 measurements has $\bar x=30,s=6$. (a) Find a 90% CI for $\mu$ (use $z_{0.05}=1.645$). (b) If the population is instead known to be Exponential with mean equal to $\bar x$, find $P(X>40)$.

Solution

(a) Margin $=1.645\times6/\sqrt{50}\approx1.395$. CI: $(28.61,31.40)$.

(b) $\lambda=1/30$. $P(X>40)=e^{-40/30}=e^{-4/3}\approx0.2636$.

Practice 13

Two independent groups: Group 1 ($n=15,\bar x=88,s=6$); Group 2 ($n=18,\bar x=84,s=7$). (a) Compute $F$ to check equal variances. (b) Compute the pooled two-sample $t$ statistic.

Solution

(a) $F=49/36\approx1.361$, $df_1=17,df_2=14$ (Group 2's variance is larger, placed on top).

(b) $s_p^2=\dfrac{14(36)+17(49)}{31}=\dfrac{504+833}{31}=\dfrac{1337}{31}\approx43.13$.

$t=\dfrac{88-84}{\sqrt{43.13(\frac1{15}+\frac1{18})}}=\dfrac{4}{\sqrt{5.267}}=\dfrac{4}{2.295}\approx1.743$, $df=31$.

Practice 14

A Binomial scenario has $n=200,p=0.02$. (a) Use the Poisson approximation to find $P(X=4)$. (b) If instead the exact defect times are Exponential with the same overall average rate (i.e. $\lambda=np=4$ defects over some reference period), find the expected time between defects.

Solution

(a) $\lambda=200(0.02)=4$. $P(X=4)=\dfrac{e^{-4}4^4}{4!}=\dfrac{0.0183\times256}{24}\approx0.1954$.

(b) $E(\text{time between defects})=1/\lambda=1/4=0.25$ (in whatever time unit the reference period represents).

Practice 15

Four groups (ANOVA), each $n=6$, give $SSB=210,SSW=300$. (a) Compute $F$. (b) If significant, explain (without computing) what a follow-up Bonferroni-corrected comparison of all pairs would require, given $k=4$ groups.

Solution

(a) $k=4,N=24$: $df_B=3,df_W=20$. $MSB=210/3=70$; $MSW=300/20=15$. $F=70/15\approx4.67$.

(b) With 4 groups, $\binom42=6$ pairwise comparisons; Bonferroni correction would require each individual comparison to meet a per-comparison threshold of $\alpha/6$ (e.g. $0.05/6\approx0.0083$ for an overall $\alpha=0.05$) rather than the standard $0.05$, to control the overall family-wise error rate across all 6 comparisons.
