Functions of Several Variables

The introduction week for Part A (Advanced Engineering Mathematics). Everything from MATH141/142 dealt with functions of a single variable, $y=f(x)$. Engineering quantities almost always depend on more than one thing at once (temperature depends on position AND time; profit depends on price AND quantity) — this week builds the vocabulary (domain, level curves, limits, continuity) needed before partial differentiation can even be defined next week. Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Function of two variables: $z=f(x,y)$ assigns a single output $z$ to each input pair $(x,y)$ in the domain — visualised as a surface in 3D, with $(x,y)$ ranging over a region of the plane.
  • Domain: the set of $(x,y)$ pairs for which $f(x,y)$ is defined — watch for square roots (need non-negative radicand), denominators (need nonzero), and logarithms (need positive argument).
  • Level curve (contour): the set of points where $f(x,y)=k$ for a fixed constant $k$ — a 2D "slice" through the 3D surface at height $k$, the same idea as a contour line on a topographic map.
  • Limit: $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(a,b)}f(x,y)=L$ means $f(x,y)$ can be made arbitrarily close to $L$ by taking $(x,y)$ sufficiently close to $(a,b)$ — from any direction of approach.
  • Two-path test (for showing a limit does NOT exist): if $f(x,y)$ approaches different values along two different paths toward $(a,b)$, the limit does not exist.
  • Continuity: $f$ is continuous at $(a,b)$ if $f(a,b)$ is defined, the limit exists, and the limit equals $f(a,b)$ — exactly the same three-part definition as single-variable calculus, just with a 2D approach.

0 Domain, Range & Level Curves

A function of two variables, $z=f(x,y)$, takes a pair of inputs and returns a single output — graphed, this produces a surface in three-dimensional space rather than a curve. Before analysing such a function, you need to know exactly where it's defined (its domain) and how to visualise its behaviour without necessarily sketching the full 3D surface.

Level curves. Setting $f(x,y)=k$ for a fixed constant $k$ produces an ordinary 2D curve in the $xy$-plane — every point on that curve corresponds to a location on the surface at the same height $k$. Sketching several level curves for different values of $k$ builds up a contour-map picture of the whole surface, without needing to draw it in 3D at all.

Key idea

$$z=f(x,y), \qquad \text{domain} = \{(x,y): f(x,y) \text{ is defined}\}, \qquad \text{level curve: } f(x,y)=k$$

Worked example

Example 1 — domain. Find the domain of $f(x,y)=\sqrt{9-x^2-y^2}$.

Need $9-x^2-y^2\ge0 \implies x^2+y^2\le9$ — the closed disk of radius 3 centred at the origin.

Example 2 — level curves. Sketch (describe) the level curves of $f(x,y)=x^2+y^2$ for $k=1,4,9$.

$x^2+y^2=k$ is a circle of radius $\sqrt k$ — so $k=1,4,9$ give circles of radius $1,2,3$: concentric circles, spacing out as $k$ increases (consistent with $f$ being a paraboloid, steepening as you move outward).

Practice 1

Find the domain of $f(x,y)=\dfrac{\sqrt{x+y}}{x-1}$.

Solution

Need $x+y\ge0$ (for the square root) AND $x\ne1$ (denominator nonzero).

Answer: $\{(x,y): x+y\ge0,\ x\ne1\}$.

Practice 2

Find the domain of $f(x,y)=\ln(y-x^2)$.

Solution

Need $y-x^2>0 \implies y>x^2$.

Answer: $\{(x,y): y>x^2\}$ — all points strictly above the parabola $y=x^2$.

Practice 3

Find the domain of $f(x,y)=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{4-x^2-y^2}}$, and explain how it differs from the domain of $\sqrt{4-x^2-y^2}$ alone.

Solution

Since this expression is in a denominator inside a square root, need $4-x^2-y^2>0$ (strictly, not $\ge0$, since the denominator can't be zero): $x^2+y^2<4$.

Answer: the open disk $\{(x,y): x^2+y^2<4\}$ — this excludes the boundary circle $x^2+y^2=4$ (where the plain square root would be defined, giving $0$, but here that would make the denominator zero).

Practice 4

Describe the level curves of $f(x,y)=y-x^2$ for $k=-1,0,1$, and identify the general shape of all level curves of this function.

Solution

$y-x^2=k \implies y=x^2+k$ — a parabola shifted vertically by $k$.

Answer: for $k=-1,0,1$: parabolas $y=x^2-1$, $y=x^2$, $y=x^2+1$ respectively. Every level curve of this function is a parabola of the same shape, just shifted vertically by $k$.

Practice 5

Describe the level curves of $f(x,y)=\dfrac{y}{x}$ (for $x\ne0$), and explain their geometric significance.

Solution

$\dfrac{y}{x}=k \implies y=kx$ — a straight line through the origin with slope $k$ (excluding the origin itself, where $f$ is undefined).

Answer: the level curves are straight lines through the origin; geometrically, this reflects that $f(x,y)=y/x$ only depends on the angle of the point $(x,y)$ from the origin, not its distance — every point on a given ray from the origin (excluding the origin) has the same function value.

Practice 6

Find the domain of $f(x,y)=\arcsin(x+y)$.

Solution

Need $-1\le x+y\le1$ (the argument of arcsin must lie in $[-1,1]$).

Answer: $\{(x,y): -1\le x+y\le1\}$ — the strip between the lines $x+y=-1$ and $x+y=1$.

Practice 7

Find the domain of $f(x,y)=\sqrt{x^2-y^2}$, and sketch (in words) the region.

Solution

Need $x^2-y^2\ge0 \implies x^2\ge y^2 \implies |x|\ge|y|$.

Answer: the region where $|x|\ge|y|$ — the "bowtie" region between the lines $y=x$ and $y=-x$, containing the positive and negative $x$-axis directions (not the $y$-axis directions).

Practice 8

Practice 9

Find the domain of $f(x,y)=\dfrac{\sqrt{x}}{\sqrt{y}}$, being careful about strict vs. non-strict inequalities.

Solution

Need $x\ge0$ (for $\sqrt x$ to be defined) and $y>0$ strictly (for $\sqrt y$ to be defined AND nonzero, since it's a denominator).

Answer: $\{(x,y): x\ge0,\ y>0\}$.

Practice 10

A manufacturer's profit is modelled by $P(x,y)=100x+150y-x^2-2y^2$, where $x,y$ are the quantities of two products. Describe what the level curve $P(x,y)=1000$ represents physically, and explain why finding several such level curves would be useful for production planning.

Solution

The level curve $P(x,y)=1000$ is the set of all combinations of quantities $(x,y)$ that yield exactly $1000$ in profit — a curve of "equal profit" combinations, analogous to an indifference curve in economics.

Usefulness: plotting several such level curves (for profit targets like $500, 1000, 1500$) lets a planner see at a glance which combinations of production quantities achieve a given profit target, and how trading off more of one product for less of the other affects the target — without needing to visualise the full 3D profit surface.

1 Limits & Continuity of Multivariable Functions

In single-variable calculus, there were only two directions to approach a point from (left and right) — agreement of both one-sided limits was enough to guarantee the limit exists. In two variables, there are infinitely many directions and paths to approach a point $(a,b)$ from, and the limit only exists if $f(x,y)$ approaches the same value along every possible path.

Proving a limit does NOT exist (the two-path test). It's enough to find just two different paths (e.g. along the $x$-axis vs. along the $y$-axis, or along $y=x$ vs. $y=-x$, or along $y=mx$ for varying $m$) that give different limiting values — this immediately proves the limit doesn't exist, without needing to check every possible path.

Proving a limit DOES exist is harder — checking a few paths and finding they agree is only suggestive, never conclusive (infinitely many paths remain unchecked). Techniques include converting to polar coordinates (turning a 2D limit into a 1D limit as $r\to0$) or using the Squeeze Theorem.

Key idea

$$\lim_{(x,y)\to(a,b)}f(x,y)=L \quad \text{(must hold along every path)}$$ $$f \text{ continuous at } (a,b) \iff f(a,b) \text{ defined, limit exists, and limit} = f(a,b)$$

Worked example

Example 1 — limit does not exist. Show $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}\dfrac{xy}{x^2+y^2}$ does not exist.

Along $y=0$: $\dfrac{0}{x^2}=0\to0$. Along $y=x$: $\dfrac{x^2}{2x^2}=\dfrac12\to\dfrac12$. Different limits along different paths — does not exist.

Example 2 — limit exists, via polar coordinates. Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}\dfrac{x^2y}{x^2+y^2}$.

Let $x=r\cos\theta,y=r\sin\theta$: $\dfrac{r^3\cos^2\theta\sin\theta}{r^2}=r\cos^2\theta\sin\theta$. As $r\to0$, this $\to0$ regardless of $\theta$ (bounded factor times $r\to0$): limit is $0$.

Practice 11

Show that $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}\frac{x^2-y^2}{x^2+y^2}$ does not exist, using the paths $y=0$ and $x=0$.

Solution

Along $y=0$: $\dfrac{x^2}{x^2}=1\to1$.

Along $x=0$: $\dfrac{-y^2}{y^2}=-1\to-1$.

Answer: different limits ($1$ vs $-1$) along different paths — the limit does not exist.

Practice 12

Show that $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}\frac{xy^2}{x^2+y^4}$ does not exist, using the paths $y=0$ and $x=y^2$ (a parabolic path, since the straight-line paths alone would misleadingly suggest the limit is $0$).

Solution

Along $y=0$ (or any straight line $y=mx$, check separately): the limit is $0$ (this is the misleading part â€” straight-line paths alone suggest existence).

Along the parabola $x=y^2$: $\dfrac{y^2\cdot y^2}{y^4+y^4}=\dfrac{y^4}{2y^4}=\dfrac12\to\dfrac12$.

Answer: the straight-line paths all give $0$, but the parabolic path gives $\dfrac12$ — different limits, so the limit does not exist. This illustrates why checking only straight-line paths is never sufficient to prove a limit exists.

Practice 13

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}\frac{x^3+y^3}{x^2+y^2}$ using polar coordinates, and verify convergence to the claimed limit.

Solution

$x=r\cos\theta,y=r\sin\theta$: $\dfrac{r^3(\cos^3\theta+\sin^3\theta)}{r^2}=r(\cos^3\theta+\sin^3\theta)$.

Since $|\cos^3\theta+\sin^3\theta|\le2$ (bounded, independent of $r$), and $r\to0$: the whole expression $\to0$ regardless of $\theta$.

Answer: the limit exists and equals $0$.

Practice 14

Determine whether $f(x,y)=\dfrac{x^2y}{x^4+y^2}$ has a limit at $(0,0)$, testing the path $y=x^2$ specifically (not just straight lines).

Solution

Along $y=mx$ (straight lines): $\dfrac{x^2(mx)}{x^4+m^2x^2}=\dfrac{mx^3}{x^4+m^2x^2}=\dfrac{mx}{x^2+m^2}\to0$ as $x\to0$ (for any fixed $m$) — suggests $0$.

Along $y=x^2$: $\dfrac{x^2\cdot x^2}{x^4+x^4}=\dfrac{x^4}{2x^4}=\dfrac12\to\dfrac12$.

Answer: the limit does not exist — straight-line paths all give $0$, but the parabolic path $y=x^2$ gives $\dfrac12$, a genuine counterexample.

Practice 15

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}\frac{\sin(x^2+y^2)}{x^2+y^2}$ using polar coordinates and the standard single-variable limit $\lim_{u\to0}\dfrac{\sin u}{u}=1$.

Solution

Let $u=x^2+y^2$; as $(x,y)\to(0,0)$, $u\to0^+$ regardless of direction (since $u=r^2\ge0$ always, using polar form).

The expression is exactly $\dfrac{\sin u}{u}$, which $\to1$ as $u\to0$ by the standard single-variable limit.

Answer: the limit exists and equals $1$.

Practice 16

Determine whether $f(x,y)=\begin{cases}\dfrac{x^2y^2}{x^2+y^2} & (x,y)\ne(0,0)\\ 0 & (x,y)=(0,0)\end{cases}$ is continuous at the origin.

Solution

$f(0,0)=0$ is defined. Check the limit using polar coordinates: $\dfrac{r^4\cos^2\theta\sin^2\theta}{r^2}=r^2\cos^2\theta\sin^2\theta$. Since $|\cos^2\theta\sin^2\theta|\le1$ and $r^2\to0$: the limit is $0$.

Since the limit ($0$) equals $f(0,0)$ ($0$): all three conditions for continuity are satisfied.

Answer: yes, $f$ is continuous at the origin.

Practice 17

Determine whether $f(x,y)=\begin{cases}\dfrac{xy}{x^2+y^2} & (x,y)\ne(0,0)\\ 0 & (x,y)=(0,0)\end{cases}$ is continuous at the origin, referencing the earlier worked example.

Solution

From the worked example above, $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}\dfrac{xy}{x^2+y^2}$ does not exist (different limits of $0$ and $\tfrac12$ along different paths).

Since the limit doesn't even exist, the continuity condition (limit must exist AND equal $f(0,0)$) automatically fails.

Answer: not continuous at the origin (regardless of how $f(0,0)$ is defined, since the limit itself doesn't exist).

Practice 18

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}(x^2+y^2)\ln(x^2+y^2)$ using polar coordinates and the standard single-variable limit $\lim_{u\to0^+}u\ln u=0$.

Solution

Let $u=x^2+y^2=r^2$; as $(x,y)\to(0,0)$, $u\to0^+$ regardless of direction.

The expression is exactly $u\ln u$, which $\to0$ as $u\to0^+$ (a standard single-variable limit, provable via L'Hôpital's Rule on $\ln u/(1/u)$).

Answer: the limit exists and equals $0$.

Practice 19

Show that $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}\frac{x^4-y^4}{x^2+y^2}$ exists by simplifying algebraically first (factor the numerator as a difference of squares), rather than using polar coordinates.

Solution

$x^4-y^4=(x^2-y^2)(x^2+y^2)$, so $\dfrac{x^4-y^4}{x^2+y^2}=\dfrac{(x^2-y^2)(x^2+y^2)}{x^2+y^2}=x^2-y^2$ (for $(x,y)\ne(0,0)$, where cancellation is valid).

As $(x,y)\to(0,0)$: $x^2-y^2\to0$.

Answer: the limit exists and equals $0$ — algebraic simplification, when available, is often faster and more rigorous than polar coordinates or path-testing.

Practice 20

Explain why, for a limit of the form $\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}g(r)\cdot h(\theta)$ (in polar form), the limit exists and equals $0$ whenever $g(r)\to0$ as $r\to0$ and $h(\theta)$ is bounded (even if $h(\theta)$ itself has no limit as $\theta$ varies).

Solution

Since $|h(\theta)|\le M$ for some constant $M$ (boundedness), we have $|g(r)h(\theta)|\le M|g(r)|$ for every angle $\theta$. As $r\to0$, $g(r)\to0$, so $M|g(r)|\to0$ too — by the Squeeze Theorem, $g(r)h(\theta)$ is squeezed between $-M|g(r)|$ and $M|g(r)|$, both of which $\to0$, forcing the whole expression to $\to0$ regardless of what $h(\theta)$ does as a function of angle. This is exactly the technique used in the worked examples and several practice problems above (e.g. $r\cos^2\theta\sin\theta$, $r^2\cos^2\theta\sin^2\theta$).

Practice 22

Find and classify stationary points of $f(x,y)=x^3-3xy^2+2x$.

Solution

Set $f_x=f_y=0$, solve critical points, then use Hessian test with $D=f_{xx}f_{yy}-f_{xy}^2$.

Solution

Practice 24

Given $z=x^2y+y^3$ with $x=t^2$, $y=e^t$, compute $dz/dt$ at $t=0$ by chain rule.

Solution

Use $dz/dt=z_x x'(t)+z_y y'(t)$, then substitute $t=0$.

Practice 41

For $f(x,y)=x^2y+\sin(xy)$: (a) find $\nabla f(1,\pi)$, (b) find the directional derivative at $(1,\pi)$ toward $(4,\pi+3)$, (c) write the tangent plane to $z=f(x,y)$ at $(1,\pi)$.

Solution

$f_x=2xy+y\cos(xy)$, $f_y=x^2+x\cos(xy)$. At $(1,\pi)$, $\cos\pi=-1$, so $\nabla f(1,\pi)=(\pi,-(1+\pi))$. Direction vector $(3,3)$ gives unit vector $\mathbf u=(1/\sqrt2,1/\sqrt2)$, hence $D_{\mathbf u}f=\nabla f\cdot\mathbf u=(\pi-(1+\pi))/\sqrt2=-1/\sqrt2$. The tangent plane is $z=f(1,\pi)+f_x(1,\pi)(x-1)+f_y(1,\pi)(y-\pi)=\pi+\pi(x-1)-(1+\pi)(y-\pi)$.

Practice 42

Use Lagrange multipliers to find extrema of $f(x,y)=x^2+4y^2$ on $x+2y=6$.

Solution

Let $g(x,y)=x+2y-6=0$. Solve $\nabla f=\lambda\nabla g$: $2x=\lambda$, $8y=2\lambda\Rightarrow x=4y$. With $x+2y=6$, get $y=1$, $x=4$. This gives the minimum value $f=16+4=20$. Along a line, a positive-definite quadratic has no maximum (unbounded above).

Practice 43

For $f(x,y)=\begin{cases}\dfrac{x^2y}{x^2+y^2},&(x,y)\ne(0,0)\\0,&(0,0)\end{cases}$: (a) test continuity at origin, (b) compute $f_x(0,0),f_y(0,0)$, (c) state differentiability at origin.

Solution

Along $y=mx$, $f=\dfrac{x^2(mx)}{x^2+m^2x^2}=\dfrac{mx}{1+m^2}\to0$, so $f$ is continuous at $(0,0)$. Partial derivatives by definition: $f_x(0,0)=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(h,0)-0}{h}=0$, $f_y(0,0)=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(0,h)-0}{h}=0$. Check differentiability via $\frac{|f(x,y)|}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}\le\frac{|x|^2|y|}{(x^2+y^2)^{3/2}}$, which is not forced to 0 on all paths; at this level report: continuous with zero partials, but differentiability is not guaranteed by partials alone.

Practice 44

Let $z=x^2y+y^3$, with $x=t^2+1$, $y=e^{-t}$. Compute $dz/dt$ and evaluate at $t=0$.

Solution

Chain rule: $\dfrac{dz}{dt}=z_x\dfrac{dx}{dt}+z_y\dfrac{dy}{dt}$, where $z_x=2xy$, $z_y=x^2+3y^2$. Also $dx/dt=2t$, $dy/dt=-e^{-t}$. At $t=0$: $(x,y)=(1,1)$, so $z_x=2$, $z_y=4$, $dx/dt=0$, $dy/dt=-1$. Hence $dz/dt=2(0)+4(-1)=-4$.
