Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Derivative of a transform: $\mathcal L\{t^nf(t)\}=(-1)^n\dfrac{d^n}{ds^n}F(s)$ — useful for transforming $t\sin\omega t$, $t\cos\omega t$, etc.
  • Convolution: $(f*g)(t)=\displaystyle\int_0^t f(\tau)g(t-\tau)\,d\tau$; convolution theorem: $\mathcal L\{f*g\}=F(s)G(s)$ — lets you invert a product of two transforms without partial fractions.
  • Periodic function transform: for $f(t)$ periodic with period $T$: $\mathcal L\{f(t)\}=\dfrac{1}{1-e^{-sT}}\displaystyle\int_0^T e^{-st}f(t)\,dt$.
  • Fourier series (period $2L$): $f(t)=\dfrac{a_0}{2}+\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty\left[a_n\cos\dfrac{n\pi t}{L}+b_n\sin\dfrac{n\pi t}{L}\right]$, with $a_n=\dfrac1L\displaystyle\int_{-L}^L f(t)\cos\dfrac{n\pi t}{L}dt$, $b_n=\dfrac1L\displaystyle\int_{-L}^L f(t)\sin\dfrac{n\pi t}{L}dt$.
  • Even/odd shortcuts: even function $\implies$ all $b_n=0$ (cosine series only); odd function $\implies$ all $a_n=0$ (sine series only) — halves the work whenever symmetry is present.

0 Derivative of a Transform & Convolution (Product of Transforms)

Differentiating the transform. Differentiating $F(s)=\int_0^\infty e^{-st}f(t)\,dt$ with respect to $s$ brings down a factor of $-t$ each time (differentiating under the integral sign) — giving a clean way to transform $t^nf(t)$ once $\mathcal L\{f(t)\}$ is already known.

Convolution. The product $F(s)G(s)$ of two transforms does not correspond to the simple product $f(t)g(t)$ in the time domain — it corresponds instead to the convolution $(f*g)(t)=\int_0^t f(\tau)g(t-\tau)\,d\tau$, a "sliding overlap" integral. This is especially useful for inverting a product like $\dfrac{1}{s(s^2+1)}$ without needing partial fractions.

Key idea

$$\mathcal L\{t^nf(t)\}=(-1)^n\frac{d^n}{ds^n}F(s), \qquad \mathcal L\{(f*g)(t)\}=F(s)G(s)$$

Worked example

Example 1 — derivative of a transform. Find $\mathcal L\{t\sin(2t)\}$.

$F(s)=\mathcal L\{\sin2t\}=\dfrac{2}{s^2+4}$. $\mathcal L\{t\sin2t\}=-\dfrac{d}{ds}\left(\dfrac{2}{s^2+4}\right)=-2\cdot\dfrac{-2s}{(s^2+4)^2}=\dfrac{4s}{(s^2+4)^2}$.

Example 2 — convolution. Find $\mathcal L^{-1}\left\{\dfrac{1}{s^2(s+1)}\right\}$ using convolution ($f(t)=t,g(t)=e^{-t}$).

$(f*g)(t)=\displaystyle\int_0^t \tau e^{-(t-\tau)}\,d\tau=e^{-t}\int_0^t\tau e^{\tau}\,d\tau$. By parts: $\int_0^t\tau e^\tau d\tau=[\tau e^\tau-e^\tau]_0^t=te^t-e^t+1$.

$(f*g)(t)=e^{-t}(te^t-e^t+1)=t-1+e^{-t}$.

Practice 1

Find $\mathcal L\{t\cos(3t)\}$ using the derivative-of-transform formula.

Solution

$F(s)=\mathcal L\{\cos3t\}=\dfrac{s}{s^2+9}$. $\mathcal L\{t\cos3t\}=-\dfrac{d}{ds}\left(\dfrac{s}{s^2+9}\right)$.

$\dfrac{d}{ds}\left(\dfrac{s}{s^2+9}\right)=\dfrac{(s^2+9)-s(2s)}{(s^2+9)^2}=\dfrac{9-s^2}{(s^2+9)^2}$.

Answer: $\mathcal L\{t\cos3t\}=\dfrac{s^2-9}{(s^2+9)^2}$.

Practice 2

Find $\mathcal L\{t^2e^{2t}\}$ using the derivative-of-transform formula (with $n=2$).

Solution

$F(s)=\mathcal L\{e^{2t}\}=\dfrac{1}{s-2}$. $\mathcal L\{t^2e^{2t}\}=\dfrac{d^2}{ds^2}\left(\dfrac{1}{s-2}\right)$ (the $(-1)^2=1$ factor).

$\dfrac{d}{ds}(s-2)^{-1}=-(s-2)^{-2}$; $\dfrac{d^2}{ds^2}(s-2)^{-1}=2(s-2)^{-3}$.

Answer: $\dfrac{2}{(s-2)^3}$ (matching the general formula $\mathcal L\{t^ne^{at}\}=n!/(s-a)^{n+1}$).

Practice 3

Find $(f*g)(t)$ where $f(t)=1$ and $g(t)=t$, and verify using the convolution theorem that $\mathcal L\{f*g\}=F(s)G(s)$.

Solution

$(f*g)(t)=\displaystyle\int_0^t 1\cdot(t-\tau)\,d\tau=\left[t\tau-\dfrac{\tau^2}{2}\right]_0^t=t^2-\dfrac{t^2}{2}=\dfrac{t^2}{2}$.

Check: $\mathcal L\{t^2/2\}=\dfrac12\cdot\dfrac{2}{s^3}=\dfrac{1}{s^3}$. Also $F(s)G(s)=\dfrac1s\cdot\dfrac{1}{s^2}=\dfrac{1}{s^3}$. Match. ✓

Practice 4

Use convolution to find $\mathcal L^{-1}\left\{\dfrac{1}{(s+1)(s+2)}\right\}$ (with $f(t)=e^{-t},g(t)=e^{-2t}$), and check against the partial-fractions answer.

Solution

$(f*g)(t)=\displaystyle\int_0^t e^{-\tau}e^{-2(t-\tau)}\,d\tau=e^{-2t}\int_0^t e^{\tau}\,d\tau=e^{-2t}(e^t-1)=e^{-t}-e^{-2t}$.

Check via partial fractions: $\dfrac{1}{(s+1)(s+2)}=\dfrac{1}{s+1}-\dfrac{1}{s+2}$, inverse-transforming to $e^{-t}-e^{-2t}$. Match. ✓

Practice 5

Find $\mathcal L\{t^2\sin(t)\}$ using the derivative-of-transform formula with $n=2$.

Solution

$F(s)=\mathcal L\{\sin t\}=\dfrac{1}{s^2+1}$. $\mathcal L\{t^2\sin t\}=\dfrac{d^2}{ds^2}\left(\dfrac{1}{s^2+1}\right)$.

$\dfrac{d}{ds}(s^2+1)^{-1}=-2s(s^2+1)^{-2}$.

$\dfrac{d^2}{ds^2}=-2(s^2+1)^{-2}+(-2s)(-2)(s^2+1)^{-3}(2s)=-2(s^2+1)^{-2}+8s^2(s^2+1)^{-3}$.

Answer: $\dfrac{-2(s^2+1)+8s^2}{(s^2+1)^3}=\dfrac{6s^2-2}{(s^2+1)^3}$.

Practice 6

Use the convolution theorem to find $\mathcal L^{-1}\left\{\dfrac{1}{s^2(s^2+1)}\right\}$ (with $f(t)=t$, $g(t)=\sin t$).

Solution

$(f*g)(t)=\displaystyle\int_0^t \tau\sin(t-\tau)\,d\tau$. Let $w=t-\tau,dw=-d\tau$; when $\tau=0,w=t$; $\tau=t,w=0$.

$=\displaystyle\int_t^0(t-w)\sin w\,(-dw)=\int_0^t(t-w)\sin w\,dw=t\int_0^t\sin w\,dw-\int_0^t w\sin w\,dw$.

$t\int_0^t\sin w\,dw=t(1-\cos t)$. By parts, $\int_0^tw\sin w\,dw=[-w\cos w+\sin w]_0^t=-t\cos t+\sin t$.

$(f*g)(t)=t-t\cos t-(-t\cos t+\sin t)=t-\sin t$.

Answer: $t-\sin t$.

Practice 7

Find $\mathcal L\{te^{-3t}\sin(2t)\}$ by first finding $\mathcal L\{e^{-3t}\sin2t\}$ (first shift theorem) then applying the derivative-of-transform formula.

Solution

Step 1: $\mathcal L\{\sin2t\}=\dfrac{2}{s^2+4}$; shift $s\to s+3$: $\mathcal L\{e^{-3t}\sin2t\}=\dfrac{2}{(s+3)^2+4}$.

Step 2: differentiate with respect to $s$ and negate: $\mathcal L\{te^{-3t}\sin2t\}=-\dfrac{d}{ds}\left[\dfrac{2}{(s+3)^2+4}\right]=\dfrac{4(s+3)}{[(s+3)^2+4]^2}$.

Practice 8

Solve $y''+y=t$, $y(0)=0,y'(0)=0$ using convolution to invert the transform (rather than partial fractions).

Solution

$s^2Y+Y=\dfrac{1}{s^2} \implies Y=\dfrac{1}{s^2(s^2+1)}$ — exactly the convolution result found two questions above.

Answer: $y(t)=t-\sin t$ (reusing the earlier convolution computation directly).

Practice 9

Explain why convolution is often preferred over partial fractions when $g(t)$ in $G(s)$ doesn't correspond to a simple rational function (e.g. when $G(s)$ itself is already a known but complicated transform), using a general argument rather than a specific example.

Solution

Partial fractions require decomposing a rational function $Y(s)$ into a sum of simpler rational pieces, each matching a standard table entry — this only works when $Y(s)$ IS a ratio of polynomials in $s$. If one of the factors in a product $F(s)G(s)$ is not a simple polynomial-ratio type (for instance, involves $e^{-as}$ from a shift, or some other transcendental expression in $s$), there may be no way to "split" it via ordinary partial fractions at all. Convolution sidesteps this entirely: as long as you already know the inverse transforms $f(t)$ and $g(t)$ of each individual factor, you can convolve them directly in the time domain, regardless of how algebraically complicated $F(s)$ or $G(s)$ might look.

Practice 10

Find $\mathcal L\{t^3\}$ using the derivative-of-transform formula applied to $F(s)=\mathcal L\{1\}=1/s$ (with $n=3$), and confirm it matches the standard table entry.

Solution

$\mathcal L\{t^3\cdot1\}=(-1)^3\dfrac{d^3}{ds^3}\left(\dfrac1s\right)=-\dfrac{d^3}{ds^3}(s^{-1})$.

$\dfrac{d}{ds}s^{-1}=-s^{-2}$; $\dfrac{d^2}{ds^2}s^{-1}=2s^{-3}$; $\dfrac{d^3}{ds^3}s^{-1}=-6s^{-4}$.

$\mathcal L\{t^3\}=-(-6s^{-4})=6s^{-4}=\dfrac{6}{s^4}$.

Answer: $\dfrac{6}{s^4}=\dfrac{3!}{s^4}$, matching the standard formula $\mathcal L\{t^n\}=n!/s^{n+1}$ with $n=3$.

1 Laplace Transform of Periodic Functions

A periodic forcing function (a repeating on-off switch, a sawtooth voltage, a square wave) can be transformed using a dedicated formula: transform just one period of the function (treating it as if it were zero outside that period), then divide by $1-e^{-sT}$ to account for every repetition summing together as a geometric-series-like pattern in the $s$-domain.

Key idea

$$\mathcal L\{f(t)\} = \frac{1}{1-e^{-sT}}\int_0^T e^{-st}f(t)\,dt \qquad (f \text{ periodic with period } T)$$

Worked example

Example. Find $\mathcal L\{f(t)\}$ for the square wave $f(t)=1$ on $[0,1)$, $f(t)=0$ on $[1,2)$, repeating with period $T=2$.

$\displaystyle\int_0^2 e^{-st}f(t)\,dt = \int_0^1 e^{-st}\,dt = \left[\dfrac{-e^{-st}}{s}\right]_0^1=\dfrac{1-e^{-s}}{s}$.

$\mathcal L\{f(t)\}=\dfrac{1}{1-e^{-2s}}\cdot\dfrac{1-e^{-s}}{s}=\dfrac{1-e^{-s}}{s(1-e^{-2s})}=\dfrac{1}{s(1+e^{-s})}$ (factoring $1-e^{-2s}=(1-e^{-s})(1+e^{-s})$).

Practice 11

Find $\mathcal L\{f(t)\}$ for the sawtooth $f(t)=t$ on $[0,T)$, repeating with period $T$.

Solution

$\displaystyle\int_0^T e^{-st}t\,dt$; by parts: $\left[-\dfrac{te^{-st}}{s}\right]_0^T+\dfrac1s\displaystyle\int_0^Te^{-st}dt=-\dfrac{Te^{-sT}}{s}+\dfrac1s\left[\dfrac{1-e^{-sT}}{s}\right]$.

$=\dfrac{1-e^{-sT}-sTe^{-sT}}{s^2}$.

Answer: $\mathcal L\{f(t)\}=\dfrac{1-e^{-sT}(1+sT)}{s^2(1-e^{-sT})}$.

Practice 12

Find $\mathcal L\{f(t)\}$ for the square wave $f(t)=1$ on $[0,a)$, $f(t)=-1$ on $[a,2a)$, repeating with period $T=2a$.

Solution

$\displaystyle\int_0^{2a}e^{-st}f(t)\,dt=\int_0^a e^{-st}dt-\int_a^{2a}e^{-st}dt$.

$=\dfrac{1-e^{-as}}{s}-\dfrac{e^{-as}-e^{-2as}}{s}=\dfrac{1-2e^{-as}+e^{-2as}}{s}=\dfrac{(1-e^{-as})^2}{s}$.

$\mathcal L\{f(t)\}=\dfrac{1}{1-e^{-2as}}\cdot\dfrac{(1-e^{-as})^2}{s}=\dfrac{(1-e^{-as})^2}{s(1-e^{-as})(1+e^{-as})}=\dfrac{1-e^{-as}}{s(1+e^{-as})}$.

Answer: $\dfrac{1-e^{-as}}{s(1+e^{-as})}$ — equivalently $\dfrac1s\tanh\!\left(\dfrac{as}{2}\right)$.

Practice 13

Find $\mathcal L\{f(t)\}$ for the half-wave rectified sine $f(t)=\sin t$ on $[0,\pi)$, $f(t)=0$ on $[\pi,2\pi)$, period $T=2\pi$.

Solution

$\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}e^{-st}f(t)\,dt=\int_0^\pi e^{-st}\sin t\,dt$.

Using the standard result $\int e^{-st}\sin t\,dt=\dfrac{-e^{-st}(s\sin t+\cos t)}{s^2+1}$: evaluating from $0$ to $\pi$: at $\pi$: $\dfrac{-e^{-\pi s}(0-1)}{s^2+1}=\dfrac{e^{-\pi s}}{s^2+1}$. At $0$: $\dfrac{-1(0+1)}{s^2+1}=\dfrac{-1}{s^2+1}$.

Integral $=\dfrac{e^{-\pi s}}{s^2+1}-\left(\dfrac{-1}{s^2+1}\right)=\dfrac{1+e^{-\pi s}}{s^2+1}$.

Answer: $\mathcal L\{f(t)\}=\dfrac{1}{1-e^{-2\pi s}}\cdot\dfrac{1+e^{-\pi s}}{s^2+1}=\dfrac{1}{(1-e^{-\pi s})(s^2+1)}$ (using $1-e^{-2\pi s}=(1-e^{-\pi s})(1+e^{-\pi s})$).

Practice 14

Explain, using the geometric series idea, why the factor $\dfrac{1}{1-e^{-sT}}$ correctly accounts for infinitely many repetitions of the base period.

Solution

The full transform is a sum over every period: $\mathcal L\{f\}=\displaystyle\int_0^T e^{-st}f(t)dt + \int_T^{2T}e^{-st}f(t)dt+\cdots$. Substituting $t=\tau+kT$ in the $k$-th repetition (using periodicity, $f(\tau+kT)=f(\tau)$) gives each term as $e^{-skT}\displaystyle\int_0^Te^{-s\tau}f(\tau)\,d\tau$ — the same base integral, multiplied by $e^{-skT}=(e^{-sT})^k$.

Summing over all $k=0,1,2,\dots$: $\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^\infty(e^{-sT})^k \times [\text{base integral}]$ — this is exactly a geometric series with ratio $e^{-sT}$ (which has magnitude less than 1 for $s>0$), summing to $\dfrac{1}{1-e^{-sT}}$ times the base integral.

Practice 15

Find $\mathcal L\{f(t)\}$ for the periodic ramp-reset function $f(t)=1-t$ for $0\le t<1$, repeating with period $T=1$.

Solution

$\displaystyle\int_0^1 e^{-st}(1-t)\,dt = \int_0^1e^{-st}dt-\int_0^1te^{-st}dt$.

First: $\dfrac{1-e^{-s}}{s}$. Second (by parts): $\left[-\dfrac{te^{-st}}{s}\right]_0^1+\dfrac1s\int_0^1e^{-st}dt=-\dfrac{e^{-s}}{s}+\dfrac{1-e^{-s}}{s^2}$.

Combined: $\dfrac{1-e^{-s}}{s}+\dfrac{e^{-s}}{s}-\dfrac{1-e^{-s}}{s^2}=\dfrac1s-\dfrac{1-e^{-s}}{s^2}$.

Answer: $\mathcal L\{f(t)\}=\dfrac{1}{1-e^{-s}}\left[\dfrac1s-\dfrac{1-e^{-s}}{s^2}\right]=\dfrac{1}{s(1-e^{-s})}-\dfrac{1}{s^2}$.

Practice 16

Find $\mathcal L\{f(t)\}$ for the periodic function $f(t)=t^2$ on $[0,1)$, repeating with period $T=1$.

Solution

$\displaystyle\int_0^1 e^{-st}t^2\,dt$; by parts twice: $\left[-\dfrac{t^2e^{-st}}{s}\right]_0^1+\dfrac2s\int_0^1te^{-st}dt = -\dfrac{e^{-s}}{s}+\dfrac2s\left[-\dfrac{e^{-s}}{s}+\dfrac{1-e^{-s}}{s^2}\right]$.

$=-\dfrac{e^{-s}}{s}-\dfrac{2e^{-s}}{s^2}+\dfrac{2(1-e^{-s})}{s^3}$.

Answer: divide the above by $(1-e^{-s})$ to get $\mathcal L\{f(t)\}$ (the base-period integral, over $1-e^{-sT}$ with $T=1$).

Practice 17

A relay switches a signal to value 2 for $0\le t<1$ and value 0 for $1\le t<3$, repeating with period $T=3$. Find $\mathcal L\{f(t)\}$.

Solution

$\displaystyle\int_0^3 e^{-st}f(t)\,dt=\int_0^1 2e^{-st}\,dt=2\cdot\dfrac{1-e^{-s}}{s}$ (the function is $0$ on $[1,3)$, contributing nothing).

Answer: $\mathcal L\{f(t)\}=\dfrac{1}{1-e^{-3s}}\cdot\dfrac{2(1-e^{-s})}{s}$.

Practice 18

Find $\mathcal L\{f(t)\}$ for the full-wave rectified sine $f(t)=|\sin t|$ (period $T=\pi$, since $|\sin t|$ repeats twice as often as $\sin t$ itself).

Solution

On $[0,\pi)$, $\sin t\ge0$, so $f(t)=\sin t$ there directly.

$\displaystyle\int_0^\pi e^{-st}\sin t\,dt=\dfrac{1+e^{-\pi s}}{s^2+1}$ (computed in the worked example above).

Answer: $\mathcal L\{f(t)\}=\dfrac{1}{1-e^{-\pi s}}\cdot\dfrac{1+e^{-\pi s}}{s^2+1}=\dfrac{1+e^{-\pi s}}{(1-e^{-\pi s})(s^2+1)}$, equivalently $\dfrac{1}{s^2+1}\coth\!\left(\dfrac{\pi s}{2}\right)$.

Practice 19

Explain why the periodic-function transform formula requires $s>0$ for the factor $\dfrac{1}{1-e^{-sT}}$ to make sense as a convergent geometric series sum, connecting this to the general requirement that Laplace transforms only converge for sufficiently large $s$.

Solution

The derivation of the formula relies on summing the geometric series $\sum_{k=0}^\infty(e^{-sT})^k$, which only converges (to $\dfrac{1}{1-e^{-sT}}$) when the common ratio satisfies $|e^{-sT}|<1$. Since $T>0$ (a genuine period), this requires $s>0$ — for $s\le0$, $e^{-sT}\ge1$ and the series diverges, meaning the Laplace transform of the periodic function itself doesn't converge there either. This is consistent with the general rule that every Laplace transform has some minimum value of $s$ below which the defining improper integral simply fails to converge.

Practice 20

A triangular pulse is defined as $f(t)=t$ for $0\le t<1$ and $f(t)=2-t$ for $1\le t<2$, repeating with period $T=2$. Find $\mathcal L\{f(t)\}$.

Solution

$\displaystyle\int_0^2 e^{-st}f(t)\,dt=\int_0^1te^{-st}dt+\int_1^2(2-t)e^{-st}dt$.

First (by parts): $-\dfrac{e^{-s}}{s}+\dfrac{1-e^{-s}}{s^2}$.

Second (by parts): $\dfrac{e^{-s}}{s}-\dfrac{e^{-s}-e^{-2s}}{s^2}$.

Sum: $\dfrac{1-e^{-s}}{s^2}-\dfrac{e^{-s}-e^{-2s}}{s^2}=\dfrac{1-2e^{-s}+e^{-2s}}{s^2}=\dfrac{(1-e^{-s})^2}{s^2}$.

Answer: $\mathcal L\{f(t)\}=\dfrac{1}{1-e^{-2s}}\cdot\dfrac{(1-e^{-s})^2}{s^2}=\dfrac{1-e^{-s}}{s^2(1+e^{-s})}$.

2 Fourier Series — Introduction & Coefficient Formulas

Any reasonably well-behaved periodic function can be decomposed into a (possibly infinite) sum of pure sines and cosines of related frequencies — this is the Fourier series, letting engineers analyse periodic signals (electrical waveforms, vibrations) in terms of their frequency content rather than their raw time-domain shape.

Even and odd shortcuts. If $f$ is even (symmetric about the $y$-axis), every $b_n=0$ automatically (no sine terms needed) — the series is purely cosine. If $f$ is odd (symmetric through the origin), every $a_n=0$ — purely sine. Recognising symmetry before computing halves the number of integrals needed.

Key idea

$$f(t)=\frac{a_0}{2}+\sum_{n=1}^\infty\left[a_n\cos\frac{n\pi t}{L}+b_n\sin\frac{n\pi t}{L}\right]$$ $$a_n=\frac1L\int_{-L}^L f(t)\cos\frac{n\pi t}{L}\,dt, \qquad b_n=\frac1L\int_{-L}^L f(t)\sin\frac{n\pi t}{L}\,dt$$

Worked example

Example. Find the Fourier series of $f(t)=t$ on $(-\pi,\pi)$ (period $2\pi$, so $L=\pi$).

$f(t)=t$ is odd, so $a_n=0$ for all $n$ (including $a_0$).

$b_n=\dfrac1\pi\displaystyle\int_{-\pi}^\pi t\sin(nt)\,dt=\dfrac2\pi\int_0^\pi t\sin(nt)\,dt$ (even integrand). By parts: $=\dfrac2\pi\left[\dfrac{-\pi\cos(n\pi)}{n}\right]=\dfrac{-2\cos(n\pi)}{n}=\dfrac{2(-1)^{n+1}}{n}$.

$f(t)=\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty\dfrac{2(-1)^{n+1}}{n}\sin(nt)$.

Practice 21

Explain why $f(t)=t^2$ on $(-\pi,\pi)$ has $b_n=0$ for all $n$, without computing any integral.

Solution

$f(t)=t^2$ is an even function ($f(-t)=(-t)^2=t^2=f(t)$), so by the even-function shortcut, all sine coefficients $b_n$ are automatically zero — the Fourier series is purely cosine terms.

Practice 22

Find $a_0$ for $f(t)=|t|$ on $(-\pi,\pi)$ (period $2\pi$).

Solution

$a_0=\dfrac1\pi\displaystyle\int_{-\pi}^\pi|t|\,dt=\dfrac2\pi\int_0^\pi t\,dt$ (even integrand) $=\dfrac2\pi\cdot\dfrac{\pi^2}{2}=\pi$.

Practice 23

Find $a_n$ for $f(t)=|t|$ on $(-\pi,\pi)$ (period $2\pi$), for $n\ge1$.

Solution

$a_n=\dfrac2\pi\displaystyle\int_0^\pi t\cos(nt)\,dt$ (even integrand, doubling and integrating over half the period).

By parts: $\displaystyle\int_0^\pi t\cos(nt)\,dt=\left[\dfrac{t\sin nt}{n}\right]_0^\pi+\dfrac{1}{n}\left[\dfrac{\cos nt}{n}\right]_0^\pi=0+\dfrac{\cos(n\pi)-1}{n^2}$.

$a_n=\dfrac2\pi\cdot\dfrac{(-1)^n-1}{n^2}$. For even $n$: $a_n=0$. For odd $n$: $a_n=\dfrac2\pi\cdot\dfrac{-2}{n^2}=\dfrac{-4}{\pi n^2}$.

Answer: $a_n=0$ for even $n$; $a_n=-\dfrac{4}{\pi n^2}$ for odd $n$.

Practice 24

Find the Fourier sine coefficients $b_n$ for $f(t)=1$ on $(0,\pi)$, extended as an odd function (period $2\pi$, $L=\pi$).

Solution

$b_n=\dfrac2\pi\displaystyle\int_0^\pi 1\cdot\sin(nt)\,dt=\dfrac2\pi\left[\dfrac{-\cos nt}{n}\right]_0^\pi=\dfrac2{\pi n}\big[1-\cos(n\pi)\big]=\dfrac{2}{\pi n}\big[1-(-1)^n\big]$.

For even $n$: $b_n=0$. For odd $n$: $b_n=\dfrac{4}{\pi n}$.

Practice 25

Find $a_0$ and $a_n$ for $f(t)=t^2$ on $(-\pi,\pi)$ (period $2\pi$).

Solution

$a_0=\dfrac2\pi\displaystyle\int_0^\pi t^2\,dt=\dfrac2\pi\cdot\dfrac{\pi^3}{3}=\dfrac{2\pi^2}{3}$.

$a_n=\dfrac2\pi\displaystyle\int_0^\pi t^2\cos(nt)\,dt$. By parts twice (as in a Week 1 MATH142-style tabular calculation): $=\dfrac2\pi\cdot\dfrac{2\pi(-1)^n}{n^2}=\dfrac{4(-1)^n}{n^2}$.

Practice 26

Explain why the constant term in a Fourier series is written as $\dfrac{a_0}{2}$ rather than simply $a_0$, using the general coefficient formula for $a_n$ with $n=0$.

Solution

The formula $a_n=\dfrac1L\int_{-L}^Lf(t)\cos\dfrac{n\pi t}{L}dt$, evaluated at $n=0$, gives $a_0=\dfrac1L\int_{-L}^Lf(t)\cdot1\,dt$ (since $\cos0=1$) — this is twice the average value of $f$ over one period. Writing the constant term as $\dfrac{a_0}{2}$ rather than $a_0$ means this single formula for $a_n$ (with $n=0$ substituted) directly gives the correct constant term after halving, avoiding the need for a separate formula just for the constant term.

Practice 27

Find the Fourier series of $f(t)=t$ on $(-2,2)$ (period 4, so $L=2$), using the pattern from the worked example but with the general period.

Solution

$f(t)=t$ is odd, so $a_n=0$.

$b_n=\dfrac12\displaystyle\int_{-2}^2 t\sin\!\left(\dfrac{n\pi t}{2}\right)dt=\int_0^2 t\sin\!\left(\dfrac{n\pi t}{2}\right)dt$ (even integrand, doubled).

By parts (with $\omega=n\pi/2$): $\left[\dfrac{-2t\cos(\omega t)}{n\pi}\right]_0^2+\dfrac{2}{n\pi}\displaystyle\int_0^2\cos(\omega t)\,dt = \dfrac{-4\cos(n\pi)}{n\pi}+0$ (the second integral vanishes since $\sin(n\pi)=0$).

Answer: $b_n=\dfrac{-4(-1)^n}{n\pi}=\dfrac{4(-1)^{n+1}}{n\pi}$; $f(t)=\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty\dfrac{4(-1)^{n+1}}{n\pi}\sin\!\left(\dfrac{n\pi t}{2}\right)$.

Practice 28

A signal is known to be an even function on $(-L,L)$. Explain what this implies about the physical/graphical symmetry of the signal, and what practical benefit this has for an engineer computing its Fourier series by hand.

Solution

An even function is symmetric about the vertical axis ($t=0$) — graphically, the right half of the waveform is a mirror image of the left half. Physically, this often corresponds to a signal with no directional bias in time around its reference point (e.g. a symmetric pulse shape, or $\cos$-based waveforms).

Practical benefit: knowing the signal is even guarantees every $b_n=0$ without any calculation — the engineer only needs to compute the (often fewer, and sometimes simpler) $a_n$ cosine coefficients, roughly halving the hand-computation workload compared to a signal with no known symmetry.

Practice 29

Find $b_n$ for $f(t)=\pi-t$ on $(0,2\pi)$, extended periodically with period $2\pi$ (note: this function is neither even nor odd about $t=0$ in the usual sense over this interval, so no symmetry shortcut applies directly — compute directly).

Solution

$b_n=\dfrac1\pi\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}(\pi-t)\sin(nt)\,dt$ (using the interval $(0,2\pi)$ as the base period).

$=\dfrac1\pi\left[\pi\int_0^{2\pi}\sin(nt)\,dt-\int_0^{2\pi}t\sin(nt)\,dt\right]$. First integral is $0$ (full periods of sine). Second, by parts: $\left[\dfrac{-t\cos nt}{n}\right]_0^{2\pi}+\dfrac1n\int_0^{2\pi}\cos nt\,dt=\dfrac{-2\pi}{n}+0$.

Answer: $b_n=\dfrac1\pi\left(0-\left(-\dfrac{2\pi}{n}\right)\right)=\dfrac2n$.

Practice 30

Explain why a Fourier series can represent a function with jump discontinuities (like a square wave), even though every individual term ($\cos$ or $\sin$) is perfectly smooth and continuous.

Solution

No single finite sum of smooth sine/cosine terms can exactly reproduce a genuine jump — but as more and more terms are added, the partial sums increasingly approximate the jump with a steepening transition, converging (in a precise mathematical sense) to the correct value everywhere except exactly at the discontinuity itself, where the series instead converges to the average of the left- and right-hand limits. This is possible because an infinite sum of smooth functions is fundamentally different from any single smooth function or finite sum — the infinite superposition can approximate arbitrarily sharp features, even though no individual term (or finite partial sum) is itself discontinuous. (The persistent overshoot near a jump, which does not disappear as more terms are added, is known as the Gibbs phenomenon.)
