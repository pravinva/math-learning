Moving from describing samples to drawing conclusions about the population they came from. A confidence interval gives a plausible range for an unknown parameter; hypothesis testing gives a formal decision procedure for whether the data supports or contradicts a specific claim. Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • CI for $\mu$, $\sigma$ known: $\bar x\pm z_{\alpha/2}\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}$.
  • CI for $\mu$, $\sigma$ unknown: $\bar x\pm t_{\alpha/2,n-1}\dfrac{s}{\sqrt n}$ — use the $t$-distribution with $n-1$ degrees of freedom.
  • Hypotheses: $H_0$ (null, the default/status-quo claim) vs. $H_1$ (alternative). Type I error ($\alpha$): rejecting a true $H_0$. Type II error ($\beta$): failing to reject a false $H_0$.
  • Test statistic & decision: compute a test statistic, find the $p$-value (probability of a result this extreme, if $H_0$ were true); reject $H_0$ if $p<\alpha$.
  • One-sample $t$-test ($\sigma$ unknown): $t=\dfrac{\bar x-\mu_0}{s/\sqrt n}$, $df=n-1$.
  • Paired $t$-test: compute the differences $d_i$ for each pair, then run a one-sample $t$-test on the differences against $\mu_0=0$: $t=\dfrac{\bar d}{s_d/\sqrt n}$.

0 Confidence Intervals for the Mean

A confidence interval gives a range of plausible values for an unknown population parameter, built from sample data. A "95% confidence interval" means that if you repeated the sampling process many times and built an interval each time using this same method, about 95% of those intervals would contain the true parameter — it is NOT the probability that this one specific interval contains the true value (that's either true or false, with no randomness left once the interval is computed).

Known vs. unknown $\sigma$. If the population standard deviation $\sigma$ is genuinely known (rare in practice, but common in textbook problems), use $z$. If $\sigma$ must be estimated from the sample (using $s$), use the $t$-distribution instead — it has slightly heavier tails than $z$, correctly reflecting the extra uncertainty from estimating $\sigma$.

Key idea

$$\bar x\pm z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n} \ (\sigma \text{ known}), \qquad \bar x\pm t_{\alpha/2,n-1}\frac{s}{\sqrt n} \ (\sigma \text{ unknown})$$

Worked example

Example 1 ($\sigma$ known). A sample of 36 items has $\bar x=50$, and $\sigma=8$ is known. Find a 95% CI for $\mu$.

$z_{0.025}=1.96$. Margin $=1.96\times\dfrac{8}{\sqrt{36}}=1.96\times1.333=2.613$.

CI: $50\pm2.613=(47.39,52.61)$.

Example 2 ($\sigma$ unknown). A sample of 16 items has $\bar x=25,s=4$. Find a 95% CI for $\mu$.

$t_{0.025,15}=2.131$. Margin $=2.131\times\dfrac{4}{\sqrt{16}}=2.131$.

CI: $25\pm2.131=(22.87,27.13)$.

Practice 1

A sample of $n=49$ has $\bar x=100$, known $\sigma=14$. Find a 95% CI for $\mu$.

Solution

Margin $=1.96\times\dfrac{14}{\sqrt{49}}=1.96\times2=3.92$.

Answer: $(96.08,103.92)$.

Practice 2

A sample of $n=25$ has $\bar x=60,s=10$ (unknown $\sigma$). Find a 90% CI for $\mu$ (use $t_{0.05,24}\approx1.711$).

Solution

Margin $=1.711\times\dfrac{10}{\sqrt{25}}=1.711\times2=3.422$.

Answer: $(56.58,63.42)$.

Practice 3

A sample of $n=100$ has $\bar x=75$, known $\sigma=20$. Find a 99% CI for $\mu$ (use $z_{0.005}=2.576$).

Solution

Margin $=2.576\times\dfrac{20}{\sqrt{100}}=2.576\times2=5.152$.

Answer: $(69.85,80.15)$.

Practice 4

Explain why increasing the confidence level (e.g. from 90% to 99%) makes a confidence interval WIDER, using the role of $z_{\alpha/2}$ (or $t_{\alpha/2}$) in the formula.

Solution

A higher confidence level requires capturing a larger proportion of possible sample outcomes within the interval — to be more confident that the interval contains the true parameter, you need to cast a wider net. This is reflected directly in the formula: a higher confidence level corresponds to a larger $z_{\alpha/2}$ (or $t_{\alpha/2}$) value (since $\alpha$ is smaller, pushing further into the tails of the distribution), which directly increases the margin of error and hence the width of the interval.

Practice 5

A sample of $n=9$ (from a Normal population) has $\bar x=40,s=6$ (unknown $\sigma$). Find a 95% CI for $\mu$ (use $t_{0.025,8}=2.306$).

Solution

Margin $=2.306\times\dfrac{6}{\sqrt9}=2.306\times2=4.612$.

Answer: $(35.39,44.61)$.

Practice 6

How large a sample is needed to estimate $\mu$ within a margin of error of 2 (with 95% confidence), if $\sigma=10$ is known?

Solution

$1.96\times\dfrac{10}{\sqrt n}\le2 \implies \sqrt n\ge\dfrac{1.96\times10}{2}=9.8 \implies n\ge96.04$.

Answer: round up to $n=97$ (always round sample size requirements UP, to ensure the margin of error requirement is actually met).

Practice 7

A researcher computes a 95% CI for $\mu$ as $(48, 56)$. Find the sample mean $\bar x$ and the margin of error used.

Solution

$\bar x$ is the midpoint: $\dfrac{48+56}{2}=52$.

Margin of error is half the width: $\dfrac{56-48}{2}=4$.

Practice 8

Explain why using the $t$-distribution (rather than $z$) when $\sigma$ is unknown results in a WIDER confidence interval for the same confidence level and sample size, in terms of what the $t$-distribution accounts for.

Solution

The $t$-distribution has heavier tails than the standard Normal $z$-distribution — meaning more probability is spread further from the centre. This directly reflects the extra uncertainty introduced by having to estimate $\sigma$ from the sample (using $s$) rather than knowing it exactly; that estimation itself is a source of additional variability that the $z$-distribution doesn't account for. Since $t_{\alpha/2,df}>z_{\alpha/2}$ for any finite degrees of freedom, the resulting margin of error (and hence interval width) is correspondingly larger, correctly reflecting this additional source of uncertainty.

Practice 9

As the sample size $n\to\infty$, explain what happens to the $t$-distribution and why this makes intuitive sense.

Solution

As $n\to\infty$ (and hence degrees of freedom $\to\infty$), the $t$-distribution converges to the standard Normal $z$-distribution — the $t$ critical values get closer and closer to the corresponding $z$ critical values.

Intuitive explanation: with a very large sample, the sample standard deviation $s$ becomes an extremely precise estimate of the true $\sigma$ — there's essentially no meaningful uncertainty left from the estimation step, so using $s$ in place of $\sigma$ introduces negligible extra variability, and the $t$-distribution's extra tail-heaviness (which exists specifically to account for that estimation uncertainty) is no longer needed.

Practice 10

A sample of 50 measurements has $\bar x=15.2$, known $\sigma=3.0$. Find an 80% CI for $\mu$ (use $z_{0.10}=1.2816$).

Solution

Margin $=1.2816\times\dfrac{3.0}{\sqrt{50}}=1.2816\times0.4243\approx0.5438$.

Answer: $(14.66,15.74)$.

1 Hypothesis Testing — General Concepts

Hypothesis testing is a formal decision procedure for evaluating a claim about a population, using sample evidence. The null hypothesis $H_0$ represents the default/status-quo assumption (typically what you'd believe absent any evidence to the contrary); the alternative $H_1$ represents the claim you're testing for. The test can never "prove" $H_0$ true — it can only fail to find sufficient evidence against it, or reject it in favour of $H_1$.

The two error types. A Type I error (probability $\alpha$, chosen in advance by the researcher) rejects a true $H_0$ — a "false alarm." A Type II error (probability $\beta$, generally unknown/harder to control directly) fails to reject a false $H_0$ — a "missed detection." These two error types trade off against each other: reducing one (for fixed sample size) increases the other.

Key idea

$$\alpha = P(\text{Type I error}) = P(\text{reject } H_0\,|\,H_0 \text{ true}), \qquad \beta = P(\text{Type II error}) = P(\text{fail to reject } H_0\,|\,H_0 \text{ false})$$

Worked example

Example. A factory claims its light bulbs last 1000 hours on average. State appropriate $H_0$ and $H_1$ for testing whether the true mean lifetime differs from this claim, and explain what a Type I error would mean in context.

$H_0: \mu=1000$; $H_1:\mu\ne1000$ (two-tailed, since we're testing for any difference, not specifically higher or lower).

A Type I error would mean concluding the true mean lifetime differs from 1000 hours, when in fact the factory's claim was correct all along — a false alarm that might lead to unnecessary (and costly) further investigation.

Practice 11

A company claims its packages weigh 500g on average. State $H_0$ and $H_1$ for testing whether the true mean is LESS than claimed (a one-tailed test, e.g. from a consumer-protection standpoint).

Solution

$H_0:\mu=500$ (or $\mu\ge500$); $H_1:\mu<500$.

Practice 12

For the light bulb example, explain in context what a Type II error would mean.

Solution

A Type II error would mean failing to detect that the true mean lifetime actually differs from 1000 hours (e.g. the bulbs are genuinely defective and last less time), when in fact the factory's claim really was incorrect — a missed problem that goes undetected.

Practice 13

A hypothesis test is run at significance level $\alpha=0.05$ and yields a $p$-value of 0.03. State the decision (reject or fail to reject $H_0$) and briefly justify it.

Solution

Since $p=0.03<\alpha=0.05$, reject $H_0$ — the observed result would be quite unlikely (only a 3% chance) if $H_0$ were actually true, providing sufficient evidence against it at this significance level.

Practice 14

A hypothesis test yields a $p$-value of 0.08, tested at $\alpha=0.05$. State the decision, and explain what this decision does (and does NOT) say about $H_0$.

Solution

Since $p=0.08>\alpha=0.05$, fail to reject $H_0$.

Important distinction: this does NOT mean $H_0$ has been proven true, or even that it's likely true — it only means the sample data didn't provide strong enough evidence to reject it at this significance level. $H_0$ remains a plausible explanation, but so might other explanations; "fail to reject" is a weaker conclusion than "accept."

Practice 15

Explain why decreasing $\alpha$ (making the test more conservative, e.g. from 0.05 to 0.01) generally increases $\beta$ (the probability of a Type II error), for a fixed sample size.

Solution

Decreasing $\alpha$ means requiring stronger evidence before rejecting $H_0$ — the rejection region (the range of test-statistic values that would trigger rejecting $H_0$) shrinks. This makes it harder to reject $H_0$ in general, including in the specific case where $H_0$ is actually false and should be rejected — so the probability of failing to reject a false $H_0$ (a Type II error) necessarily increases. This illustrates the fundamental trade-off between the two error types: for a fixed sample size, you can't simultaneously reduce both $\alpha$ and $\beta$; reducing one increases the other. The only way to reduce both simultaneously is to increase the sample size.

Practice 16

A researcher wants to test whether a new drug changes blood pressure (could go either direction). State whether this calls for a one-tailed or two-tailed test, and write appropriate hypotheses.

Solution

Two-tailed (testing for any change, not specifically an increase or decrease).

$H_0:\mu=\mu_0$ (no change from the baseline/known value); $H_1:\mu\ne\mu_0$.

Practice 17

Explain why the $p$-value is NOT the probability that $H_0$ is true, describing what it actually represents instead.

Solution

The $p$-value is the probability of observing a test statistic at least as extreme as the one actually observed, calculated assuming $H_0$ is true. It says nothing directly about the probability that $H_0$ itself is true or false — that would require a fundamentally different (Bayesian) framework incorporating prior probabilities. A small $p$-value simply means the observed data would be unusual/surprising if $H_0$ were true, which is evidence against $H_0$, but it is not literally "the probability $H_0$ is true," a common and important misinterpretation to avoid.

Practice 18

A quality control test for a manufacturing process has $\alpha=0.01$. Explain, in the context of this specific application, why the company might deliberately choose such a small $\alpha$ rather than the more common 0.05.

Solution

A very small $\alpha$ means the company requires very strong evidence before concluding something is wrong with the process (rejecting $H_0$, which likely represents "process is operating normally"). This makes sense if a Type I error (falsely concluding the process is faulty when it's actually fine) would be very costly — e.g. triggering an expensive, unnecessary production shutdown or investigation. By choosing a small $\alpha$, the company accepts a correspondingly higher risk of a Type II error (failing to catch a genuine problem), presumably because in this context, false alarms are considered more costly or disruptive than missing a real (but perhaps less severe or more slowly-developing) issue.

Practice 19

State the general form of the decision rule in hypothesis testing, in terms of comparing the $p$-value to $\alpha$.

Solution

Answer: reject $H_0$ if $p\text{-value}<\alpha$; otherwise, fail to reject $H_0$.

Practice 20

A test statistic falls exactly on the boundary, giving $p=0.05$ exactly, tested at $\alpha=0.05$. What is the conventional decision, and why might a researcher want to report the exact $p$-value rather than just "reject" or "fail to reject"?

Solution

Conventionally, since $p=\alpha$ (not strictly less than), the standard rule gives fail to reject $H_0$ (the rejection region is typically defined as $p<\alpha$, not $p\le\alpha$, though conventions can vary).

Why report the exact value: a borderline result like this is much more informative to report as "$p=0.05$" than simply as a binary reject/fail-to-reject decision — it tells the reader exactly how close the evidence came to the threshold, allowing them to judge for themselves (especially if they'd have chosen a different $\alpha$) rather than losing that nuance in a simple yes/no conclusion.

2 Hypothesis Tests for Single or Paired Samples

A one-sample $t$-test checks whether a population mean differs from a specific hypothesised value $\mu_0$, using a single sample. A paired test handles data collected in matched pairs (before/after measurements on the same subjects, or two different methods applied to the same items) — by working with the differences within each pair, it removes subject-to-subject variability that would otherwise mask a genuine effect.

Key idea

$$t=\frac{\bar x-\mu_0}{s/\sqrt n}\ (df=n-1) \quad\text{(one-sample)}, \qquad t=\frac{\bar d}{s_d/\sqrt n}\ (df=n-1) \quad\text{(paired, on differences } d_i\text{)}$$

Worked example

Example 1 — one-sample. A sample of 25 items has $\bar x=102,s=5$. Test $H_0:\mu=100$ vs. $H_1:\mu\ne100$ at $\alpha=0.05$.

$t=\dfrac{102-100}{5/\sqrt{25}}=\dfrac{2}{1}=2.0$, $df=24$.

Two-tailed $p$-value $\approx0.057$ (from a $t$-table/software) $>0.05$: fail to reject $H_0$ — insufficient evidence the mean differs from 100.

Example 2 — paired. Before/after weights for 8 subjects give differences $d_i$ with $\bar d=2.25,s_d=1.982$. Test $H_0:\mu_d=0$ vs. $H_1:\mu_d>0$ (weight loss) at $\alpha=0.05$.

$t=\dfrac{2.25}{1.982/\sqrt8}=\dfrac{2.25}{0.7008}\approx3.211$, $df=7$.

One-tailed $p$-value $\approx0.0074<0.05$: reject $H_0$ — significant evidence of average weight loss.

Practice 21

A sample of 16 items has $\bar x=48,s=6$. Test $H_0:\mu=50$ vs. $H_1:\mu\ne50$ by computing the test statistic (do not look up the exact $p$-value).

Solution

$t=\dfrac{48-50}{6/\sqrt{16}}=\dfrac{-2}{1.5}=-1.333$, $df=15$.

Practice 22

A sample of 36 items has $\bar x=105,s=12$. Test $H_0:\mu\le100$ vs. $H_1:\mu>100$ by computing the test statistic.

Solution

$t=\dfrac{105-100}{12/\sqrt{36}}=\dfrac{5}{2}=2.5$, $df=35$.

Practice 23

For 6 pairs of before/after measurements, the differences (after $-$ before) are $3,5,2,4,3,1$. Find $\bar d$ and $s_d$.

Solution

$\bar d=\dfrac{3+5+2+4+3+1}{6}=\dfrac{18}{6}=3$.

Deviations squared: $0,4,1,1,0,4$; sum $=10$. $s_d^2=\dfrac{10}{5}=2$; $s_d=\sqrt2\approx1.414$.

Practice 24

Using the paired data above ($\bar d=3,s_d=1.414,n=6$), compute the test statistic for $H_0:\mu_d=0$ vs. $H_1:\mu_d\ne0$.

Solution

$t=\dfrac{3}{1.414/\sqrt6}=\dfrac{3}{0.5774}\approx5.196$, $df=5$.

Practice 25

A machine is supposed to fill bottles with 500mL. A sample of 20 bottles has $\bar x=497,s=8$. Test $H_0:\mu=500$ vs. $H_1:\mu\ne500$ by computing the test statistic, and state (without a table) whether this seems likely to be significant at $\alpha=0.05$ given the magnitude of $t$.

Solution

$t=\dfrac{497-500}{8/\sqrt{20}}=\dfrac{-3}{1.789}\approx-1.677$, $df=19$.

Assessment: a $t$ value of magnitude around 1.68 is smaller than the typical critical value for a two-tailed test at $\alpha=0.05$ with these degrees of freedom (roughly 2.09) — suggesting this result likely would NOT be significant (fail to reject $H_0$), though the exact conclusion requires the precise critical value or $p$-value.

Practice 26

Explain why a paired design (measuring the same subjects before and after a treatment) is generally more powerful (better able to detect a real effect) than an unpaired design comparing two independent groups, in terms of what source of variability is removed.

Solution

In a paired design, each subject serves as their own baseline/control — the analysis focuses only on the within-subject change, completely removing any variability due to differences between subjects (e.g. some people naturally have higher blood pressure than others, regardless of treatment). This between-subject variability, if left in the analysis (as it would be in an unpaired/independent-groups design), adds noise that can mask a genuine treatment effect. By working with differences within each pair, the paired design isolates the effect of interest much more cleanly, generally requiring a smaller sample size to detect the same true effect compared to an unpaired design.

Practice 27

A one-sample $t$-test gives $t=1.8$ with $df=10$. Using the fact that the critical value for a two-tailed test at $\alpha=0.05,df=10$ is approximately $2.228$, state the decision.

Solution

Since $|t|=1.8<2.228$ (the critical value), the test statistic does not fall in the rejection region.

Answer: fail to reject $H_0$.

Practice 28

For 10 pairs of measurements comparing two instruments (Instrument A minus Instrument B), the mean difference is $\bar d=0.5$ with $s_d=1.2$. Test $H_0:\mu_d=0$ vs. $H_1:\mu_d\ne0$ by computing $t$, and comment on whether the instruments seem to agree well, using the magnitude of $\bar d$ relative to $s_d$.

Solution

$t=\dfrac{0.5}{1.2/\sqrt{10}}=\dfrac{0.5}{0.3795}\approx1.318$, $df=9$.

Comment: the mean difference (0.5) is less than half of $s_d$ (1.2), and the resulting $t$-value is fairly small — this suggests any systematic difference between the two instruments, if real at all, is small relative to the natural variability in the paired differences; the instruments likely agree reasonably well (though a formal conclusion requires comparing $t$ to the appropriate critical value or $p$-value).

Practice 29

Explain the key structural difference between setting up a one-sample $t$-test and a paired $t$-test, in terms of what data goes into the formula (i.e. what plays the role of "the sample").

Solution

In a one-sample $t$-test, the sample consists of $n$ independent, individual observations, and you're testing whether their mean differs from a specific hypothesised value $\mu_0$.

In a paired $t$-test, although you start with $n$ pairs of observations (e.g. before/after, or two methods on the same $n$ items), the actual analysis reduces to a one-sample $t$-test performed on the $n$ differences $d_i$ (not the original paired values themselves), testing whether their mean $\mu_d$ differs from $0$. Structurally, it's exactly a one-sample test, just applied to a derived quantity (the differences) rather than the raw original measurements.

Practice 30

A company claims their battery lasts 40 hours on average. A sample of 15 batteries has $\bar x=38.5,s=3$. Compute the test statistic for $H_0:\mu=40$ vs. $H_1:\mu<40$ (testing whether the true mean is less than claimed).

Solution

$t=\dfrac{38.5-40}{3/\sqrt{15}}=\dfrac{-1.5}{0.7746}\approx-1.936$, $df=14$.
