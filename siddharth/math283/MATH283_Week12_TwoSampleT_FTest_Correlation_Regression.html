Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Two-sample $t$-test (pooled, equal variances assumed): $s_p^2=\dfrac{(n_1-1)s_1^2+(n_2-1)s_2^2}{n_1+n_2-2}$; $t=\dfrac{\bar x_1-\bar x_2}{\sqrt{s_p^2\left(\frac1{n_1}+\frac1{n_2}\right)}}$, $df=n_1+n_2-2$.
  • Variance ratio (F) test: $F=\dfrac{s_1^2}{s_2^2}$ (larger sample variance conventionally placed on top), compared against $F_{\alpha/2,n_1-1,n_2-1}$; tests $H_0:\sigma_1^2=\sigma_2^2$.
  • Sample correlation coefficient: $r=\dfrac{\sum(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}{\sqrt{\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2\sum(y_i-\bar y)^2}}$; $-1\le r\le1$; $r^2$ = coefficient of determination (proportion of variability in $y$ explained by the linear relationship with $x$).
  • Least squares regression line: $\hat y=b_0+b_1x$, with slope $b_1=\dfrac{\sum(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}{\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2}$ and intercept $b_0=\bar y-b_1\bar x$.

0 Two-Sample T-Tests

Unlike the paired test, a two-sample $t$-test compares the means of two genuinely independent groups (e.g. a treatment group vs. a control group, drawn from different subjects entirely). The pooled version assumes both populations share a common (but unknown) variance, combining both samples' variance estimates into a single, more precise pooled estimate before computing the test statistic.

Key idea

$$s_p^2=\frac{(n_1-1)s_1^2+(n_2-1)s_2^2}{n_1+n_2-2}, \qquad t=\frac{\bar x_1-\bar x_2}{\sqrt{s_p^2\left(\frac1{n_1}+\frac1{n_2}\right)}}, \quad df=n_1+n_2-2$$

Worked example

Example. Two independent samples: Sample 1 ($n_1=12,\bar x_1=50,s_1=5$); Sample 2 ($n_2=15,\bar x_2=45,s_2=6$). Test $H_0:\mu_1=\mu_2$ vs. $H_1:\mu_1\ne\mu_2$.

$s_p^2=\dfrac{11(25)+14(36)}{25}=\dfrac{275+504}{25}=\dfrac{779}{25}=31.16$.

$t=\dfrac{50-45}{\sqrt{31.16(\frac{1}{12}+\frac{1}{15})}}=\dfrac{5}{2.162}\approx2.313$, $df=25$.

Practice 1

Two samples: Sample 1 ($n_1=10,\bar x_1=60,s_1=4$); Sample 2 ($n_2=10,\bar x_2=55,s_2=5$). Find the pooled variance $s_p^2$.

Solution

$s_p^2=\dfrac{9(16)+9(25)}{18}=\dfrac{144+225}{18}=\dfrac{369}{18}=20.5$.

Practice 2

For the samples above, compute the two-sample $t$ test statistic.

Solution

$t=\dfrac{60-55}{\sqrt{20.5(\frac1{10}+\frac1{10})}}=\dfrac{5}{\sqrt{4.1}}=\dfrac{5}{2.025}\approx2.470$, $df=18$.

Practice 3

Two independent samples of machine outputs: Sample A ($n=8,\bar x=100,s=6$); Sample B ($n=8,\bar x=95,s=6$). Note the equal sample sizes and equal $s$ — find the pooled variance directly.

Solution

Since $n_1=n_2$ and $s_1=s_2$: $s_p^2=\dfrac{7(36)+7(36)}{14}=\dfrac{7(36)(2)}{14}=36$ — simply equal to the common sample variance, as expected when both groups have identical variance estimates.

Practice 4

For the machine samples above, compute $t$.

Solution

$t=\dfrac{100-95}{\sqrt{36(\frac18+\frac18)}}=\dfrac{5}{\sqrt{9}}=\dfrac53\approx1.667$, $df=14$.

Practice 5

Explain why the pooled two-sample $t$-test formula gives EQUAL weight consideration to each sample's variance only when $n_1=n_2$, and MORE weight to the larger sample's variance when $n_1\ne n_2$.

Solution

The pooled variance formula, $s_p^2=\dfrac{(n_1-1)s_1^2+(n_2-1)s_2^2}{n_1+n_2-2}$, is a weighted average of the two sample variances, with weights $(n_1-1)$ and $(n_2-1)$ respectively. When $n_1=n_2$, these weights are equal, so both sample variances contribute equally. When one sample is larger (say $n_1>n_2$), its weight $(n_1-1)$ is correspondingly larger, giving its variance estimate more influence on the pooled result — which makes statistical sense, since a larger sample generally gives a more reliable (lower-variance) estimate of the true population variance, and should therefore be trusted more in the combined estimate.

Practice 6

A study compares reaction times for two groups: Group 1 ($n=20,\bar x=250\text{ms},s=30$); Group 2 ($n=25,\bar x=265\text{ms},s=35$). Find the pooled variance and the test statistic for $H_0:\mu_1=\mu_2$.

Solution

$s_p^2=\dfrac{19(900)+24(1225)}{43}=\dfrac{17100+29400}{43}=\dfrac{46500}{43}\approx1081.4$.

$t=\dfrac{250-265}{\sqrt{1081.4(\frac1{20}+\frac1{25})}}=\dfrac{-15}{\sqrt{97.33}}=\dfrac{-15}{9.866}\approx-1.520$, $df=43$.

Practice 7

Explain the key assumption required for using the POOLED two-sample $t$-test (as opposed to an unpooled/Welch's version), and briefly describe what you might check in the data to assess whether this assumption is reasonable.

Solution

The pooled test assumes the two populations have equal variances ($\sigma_1^2=\sigma_2^2$), even though the individual population means may differ. If this assumption is violated (the two populations have genuinely different spreads), the pooled test can give misleading results.

Checking the assumption: comparing the two sample variances $s_1^2$ and $s_2^2$ directly (e.g. via the variance ratio/F-test, the next topic this week) gives a formal way to test whether this equal-variance assumption is reasonable before proceeding with the pooled $t$-test.

Practice 8

Two paint formulations are compared for drying time: Formulation A ($n=15,\bar x=45\text{min},s=5$); Formulation B ($n=12,\bar x=50\text{min},s=6$). Compute the pooled variance and the test statistic for testing whether Formulation A dries faster ($H_1:\mu_A<\mu_B$).

Solution

$s_p^2=\dfrac{14(25)+11(36)}{25}=\dfrac{350+396}{25}=\dfrac{746}{25}=29.84$.

$t=\dfrac{45-50}{\sqrt{29.84(\frac1{15}+\frac1{12})}}=\dfrac{-5}{\sqrt{4.474}}=\dfrac{-5}{2.115}\approx-2.364$, $df=25$.

Practice 9

Explain why the degrees of freedom for a pooled two-sample $t$-test is $n_1+n_2-2$ (rather than, say, $n_1+n_2-1$ or $n_1+n_2$), connecting this to the number of means estimated from the data.

Solution

Computing the pooled variance requires first estimating two separate sample means, $\bar x_1$ and $\bar x_2$ — one for each group. Each of these estimated means "uses up" one degree of freedom from its respective sample (exactly as in the single-sample case, where estimating $\bar x$ costs one degree of freedom out of $n$, leaving $n-1$). With two independent samples, two means are estimated, so two degrees of freedom are lost in total: $(n_1-1)+(n_2-1)=n_1+n_2-2$.

Practice 10

Two independent groups of plant heights are measured: Group 1 ($n=18,\bar x=32\text{cm},s=4$); Group 2 ($n=18,\bar x=32\text{cm},s=4$) (identical summary statistics). Without any further calculation, state the value of the test statistic $t$ for $H_0:\mu_1=\mu_2$, and explain your reasoning.

Solution

Answer: $t=0$ exactly.

Reasoning: the numerator of the test statistic is $\bar x_1-\bar x_2=32-32=0$ — regardless of what the pooled variance or standard error works out to be, dividing zero by any nonzero number still gives zero. Whenever the two sample means are identical, the test statistic is always exactly zero, providing no evidence whatsoever against $H_0:\mu_1=\mu_2$.

1 The Variance Ratio (F) Test

Before running a pooled two-sample $t$-test, it's good practice to check whether the equal-variance assumption is actually reasonable. The $F$-test does exactly this, comparing two sample variances directly via their ratio.

Key idea

$$F=\frac{s_1^2}{s_2^2} \quad (\text{conventionally, larger variance on top}), \qquad H_0:\sigma_1^2=\sigma_2^2 \text{ vs. } H_1:\sigma_1^2\ne\sigma_2^2$$

Worked example

Example. Sample 1 ($n_1=12,s_1^2=36$); Sample 2 ($n_2=10,s_2^2=16$). Test $H_0:\sigma_1^2=\sigma_2^2$ at $\alpha=0.05$, given $F_{0.025,11,9}\approx3.912$.

$F=\dfrac{36}{16}=2.25$ ($df_1=11,df_2=9$, larger variance on top).

Since $F=2.25<3.912$ (the critical value), fail to reject $H_0$ — insufficient evidence the variances differ, supporting the use of the pooled $t$-test.

Practice 11

Sample 1 ($s_1^2=64,n_1=15$); Sample 2 ($s_2^2=25,n_2=10$). Compute the $F$ statistic (larger variance on top) and state the degrees of freedom.

Solution

$F=\dfrac{64}{25}=2.56$, $df_1=14,df_2=9$.

Practice 12

Sample 1 ($s_1^2=100,n_1=20$); Sample 2 ($s_2^2=81,n_2=16$). Compute $F$ and state degrees of freedom.

Solution

$F=\dfrac{100}{81}\approx1.235$, $df_1=19,df_2=15$.

Practice 13

Two production lines have sample variances $s_1^2=49$ ($n_1=10$) and $s_2^2=9$ ($n_2=8$). Compute $F$, and comment (without a table) on whether this large ratio suggests a meaningful difference in variability.

Solution

$F=\dfrac{49}{9}\approx5.44$, $df_1=9,df_2=7$.

Comment: an $F$ ratio this large (over 5) is often, though not always, statistically significant, since it means one sample's variance is more than 5 times the other's — a formal conclusion requires comparing against the appropriate critical value from an $F$-table, but the sheer size of the ratio strongly suggests a real difference in variability between the two production lines.

Practice 14

Explain why the $F$-test convention of placing the LARGER sample variance in the numerator (rather than always using $s_1^2/s_2^2$ in a fixed order) matters for using standard $F$-distribution tables.

Solution

Standard $F$-distribution tables are typically only provided for values of $F$ greater than 1 (the upper tail), since the $F$-distribution is not symmetric and tables for the lower tail would essentially just be reciprocals of the upper-tail values. Placing the larger sample variance in the numerator guarantees the computed $F$ statistic is always $\ge1$, ensuring it falls in the range the standard table actually covers, without needing separate lower-tail tables or extra reciprocal calculations.

Practice 15

Two chemical batches have sample standard deviations $s_1=8$ ($n_1=13$) and $s_2=5$ ($n_2=11$). Compute the $F$ statistic (remember to square the standard deviations first).

Solution

$s_1^2=64,s_2^2=25$. $F=\dfrac{64}{25}=2.56$, $df_1=12,df_2=10$.

Practice 16

If $F_{0.025,12,10}\approx3.62$ and the computed $F$ statistic (from the previous question) is $2.56$, state the conclusion of the variance ratio test at $\alpha=0.05$ (two-tailed).

Solution

Since $F=2.56<3.62$ (the critical value), fail to reject $H_0$ — insufficient evidence the two batches' variances differ.

Practice 17

Explain why testing for equal variances (via the $F$-test) is often done as a PRELIMINARY step before a two-sample $t$-test, rather than being the main research question of interest in most applications.

Solution

In most applied studies, the researcher's primary interest is usually in comparing the means of two groups (e.g. does a new drug lower blood pressure more than a placebo?) — the two-sample $t$-test directly addresses this. However, the standard pooled version of that $t$-test relies on the assumption that both populations share the same variance. The $F$-test serves as a diagnostic check on this assumption: if it suggests the variances are indeed roughly equal, the researcher can confidently proceed with the (simpler, more powerful) pooled $t$-test; if not, an alternative (like Welch's unpooled $t$-test, which doesn't assume equal variances) should be used instead. The $F$-test result itself is rarely the final conclusion the researcher is actually seeking to report.

Practice 18

A study wants to determine whether Method A produces MORE variable results than Method B specifically (a one-tailed variance test), with $s_A^2=45,n_A=16$ and $s_B^2=20,n_B=13$. Compute the $F$ statistic for this specific directional test.

Solution

Since the test specifically asks whether A is more variable than B, compute $F=\dfrac{s_A^2}{s_B^2}=\dfrac{45}{20}=2.25$, $df_1=15,df_2=12$ (here the numerator is fixed as $A$ regardless of which is larger, since the hypothesis is directional).

Practice 19

Two suppliers' delivery times have $s_1^2=15$ ($n_1=9$) and $s_2^2=60$ ($n_2=11$). Compute $F$ (larger variance on top) and state the degrees of freedom.

Solution

$F=\dfrac{60}{15}=4.0$ (Supplier 2's variance is larger, so it goes on top), $df_1=10,df_2=8$.

Practice 20

Explain why swapping which sample's variance is placed in the numerator (using $s_2^2/s_1^2$ instead of $s_1^2/s_2^2$) gives the reciprocal $F$ value, and why this matters when looking up degrees of freedom in a table.

Solution

Since $F=s_1^2/s_2^2$ and its reciprocal $1/F=s_2^2/s_1^2$ are simply inverses of each other, swapping the numerator and denominator directly inverts the computed $F$ value. This matters because the degrees of freedom associated with the numerator and denominator must swap correspondingly — the degrees of freedom for whichever sample's variance is on top always goes in the $df_1$ (numerator) position, and the other sample's degrees of freedom goes in $df_2$ (denominator) position. Getting this order backwards when looking up a critical value in an $F$-table would give an incorrect critical value, since the $F$-distribution is not symmetric between its two degrees-of-freedom parameters.

2 Correlation & Least Squares Linear Regression

Correlation measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two continuous variables. Linear regression goes a step further, finding the specific straight line that best fits the data (in a least-squares sense), letting you predict one variable from the other.

Correlation vs. causation. A strong correlation never by itself proves that one variable causes changes in the other — a lurking (confounding) variable, or simple coincidence, could explain the same observed pattern.

Key idea

$$r=\frac{\sum(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}{\sqrt{\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2\sum(y_i-\bar y)^2}}, \qquad b_1=\frac{\sum(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}{\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2}, \qquad b_0=\bar y-b_1\bar x$$

Worked example

Example. Data: $x=1,2,3,4,5$; $y=2,4,5,4,5$. Find $r$ and the regression line $\hat y=b_0+b_1x$.

$\bar x=3,\bar y=4$. Deviations $(x-\bar x)$: $-2,-1,0,1,2$; $(y-\bar y)$: $-2,0,1,0,1$.

$\sum(x-\bar x)(y-\bar y)=4+0+0+0+2=6$; $\sum(x-\bar x)^2=4+1+0+1+4=10$; $\sum(y-\bar y)^2=4+0+1+0+1=6$.

$r=\dfrac{6}{\sqrt{10\times6}}=\dfrac{6}{\sqrt{60}}\approx0.7746$. $b_1=\dfrac{6}{10}=0.6$; $b_0=4-0.6(3)=2.2$. Line: $\hat y=2.2+0.6x$.

Practice 21

Data: $x=1,2,3,4$; $y=3,5,6,8$. Find $\bar x,\bar y$.

Solution

$\bar x=\dfrac{1+2+3+4}{4}=2.5$. $\bar y=\dfrac{3+5+6+8}{4}=5.5$.

Practice 22

For the data above, find $\sum(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)$ and $\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2$.

Solution

Deviations $(x-\bar x)$: $-1.5,-0.5,0.5,1.5$; $(y-\bar y)$: $-2.5,-0.5,0.5,2.5$.

Products: $3.75,0.25,0.25,3.75$; sum $=8$.

$(x-\bar x)^2$: $2.25,0.25,0.25,2.25$; sum $=5$.

Using the sums from the previous question, find the regression slope $b_1$ and intercept $b_0$.

Solution

$b_1=\dfrac{8}{5}=1.6$. $b_0=5.5-1.6(2.5)=5.5-4=1.5$.

Answer: $\hat y=1.5+1.6x$.

Practice 24

Using the regression line $\hat y=1.5+1.6x$ from above, predict $y$ when $x=6$, and comment on the risk of extrapolation (since $x=6$ is beyond the original data range of $1$ to $4$).

Solution

$\hat y=1.5+1.6(6)=1.5+9.6=11.1$.

Extrapolation risk: since $x=6$ falls outside the range of the original data ($x=1$ to $4$), there's no guarantee the linear relationship observed within that range continues to hold at $x=6$ — this prediction should be treated with caution, as extrapolating beyond the observed data range is generally unreliable.

Practice 25

For the correlation example ($r\approx0.7746$), find $r^2$ (the coefficient of determination) and interpret its value in context.

Solution

$r^2\approx0.7746^2\approx0.6$.

Interpretation: approximately 60% of the variability in $y$ can be explained by its linear relationship with $x$; the remaining 40% is due to other factors (or random variability) not captured by this simple linear model.

Practice 26

Explain why $r$ (correlation coefficient) is always between $-1$ and $1$, connecting this to the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality (or, more intuitively, to the fact that $r$ is essentially a normalised covariance).

Solution

The correlation coefficient can be viewed as the covariance between $x$ and $y$, divided by the product of their individual standard deviations — this normalisation (dividing by both individual "spreads") is precisely what confines the result to the range $[-1,1]$, regardless of the original units or scales of $x$ and $y$. The underlying mathematical guarantee is the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, which states that the covariance between two variables can never exceed the product of their individual standard deviations in magnitude — dividing by exactly that product therefore always yields a value with magnitude at most 1.

Practice 27

Two variables have $r=-0.85$. Describe what this value indicates about the direction and strength of their linear relationship.

Solution

Direction: negative — as one variable increases, the other tends to decrease.

Strength: fairly strong (close to $-1$, the maximum possible negative correlation) — the data points would cluster fairly tightly around a downward-sloping straight line.

Practice 28

A study finds a strong positive correlation between ice cream sales and drowning incidents. Explain why this does NOT mean ice cream sales cause drownings, identifying a plausible lurking (confounding) variable.

Solution

Correlation alone never establishes causation — a third, unmeasured variable can produce an association between two variables that have no direct causal link to each other. Here, a very plausible lurking variable is outdoor temperature (season): hot weather independently increases both ice cream sales (people buy more ice cream when it's hot) and swimming/drowning incidents (more people swim, and more accidents occur, when it's hot). The apparent relationship between ice cream sales and drownings is really just both being separately driven by the same underlying seasonal factor, not a direct causal link between them.

Practice 29

Explain why the least squares method minimises the sum of SQUARED vertical distances from each data point to the line (rather than, say, the sum of absolute distances), in terms of the mathematical tractability this provides.

Solution

Squaring the vertical distances (residuals) before summing serves two key purposes: first, it ensures positive and negative residuals (points above vs. below the line) don't cancel out when summed, since squares are always non-negative — simply summing signed residuals could total zero even for a very poor-fitting line. Second, and just as importantly, using squared terms makes the resulting minimisation problem solvable using ordinary calculus (setting partial derivatives with respect to $b_0$ and $b_1$ to zero produces a clean, solvable system of linear equations) — using absolute values instead would make the objective function non-differentiable at certain points, requiring more complex optimisation techniques instead of the straightforward closed-form formulas used in ordinary least squares.

Practice 30

Given regression output $\hat y=10+2.5x$ with $r^2=0.81$, find $r$ (noting the sign should match the slope's sign), and state what fraction of the variability in $y$ remains UNEXPLAINED by this model.

Solution

Since the slope $b_1=2.5$ is positive, $r$ must also be positive: $r=\sqrt{0.81}=0.9$.

Unexplained variability: $1-r^2=1-0.81=0.19$, i.e. 19% of the variability in $y$ is not explained by the linear relationship with $x$.
