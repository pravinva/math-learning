Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Convergence at a jump discontinuity: the Fourier series converges to the average of the left- and right-hand limits, $\dfrac{f(t^-)+f(t^+)}{2}$, not to either one-sided value.
  • Gibbs phenomenon: near a jump, partial sums always overshoot by roughly 9% of the jump size, no matter how many terms are added — the overshoot narrows but never vanishes.
  • Half-range cosine series (function given only on $[0,L]$, extended evenly): $f(t)=\dfrac{a_0}{2}+\displaystyle\sum a_n\cos\dfrac{n\pi t}{L}$, $a_n=\dfrac2L\displaystyle\int_0^L f(t)\cos\dfrac{n\pi t}{L}\,dt$.

  • General period: for period $T$ (so $L=T/2$), replace every $\pi t/L$ with $2\pi t/T$ throughout — the coefficient formulas otherwise keep the same structure.

0 Convergence of Fourier Series

A Fourier series converges to $f(t)$ at every point where $f$ is continuous. At a jump discontinuity, however, it converges to neither one-sided limit individually — it settles exactly on their average. This makes sense from the series' own symmetry: cosine and sine functions can't "choose a side" at a jump, so the best a finite (or infinite) combination of them can do is split the difference.

Gibbs phenomenon. Near a jump, every partial sum overshoots the true function value by about 9% of the total jump size — and critically, this overshoot doesn't shrink to zero as more terms are added; it just squeezes into a narrower and narrower region right next to the jump.

Key idea

$$\text{At a jump: series} \to \frac{f(t^-)+f(t^+)}{2}$$

Worked example

Example. The square wave $f(t)=1$ for $0

$f(0^-)=-1,f(0^+)=1$. Average: $\dfrac{-1+1}{2}=0$.

The series converges to $0$ at $t=0$, even though $f(0)$ itself might be separately defined as $\pm1$ or left undefined — the series doesn't "know" about that definition, only about the two one-sided limits.

Practice 1

A sawtooth wave has $f(t)=t$ for $-\pi

Solution

Just before $t=\pi$ (within the current period): $f(\pi^-)=\pi$. Just after (start of the next period, i.e. at $t=-\pi$ shifted): $f(\pi^+)=f(-\pi)=-\pi$.

Answer: average $=\dfrac{\pi+(-\pi)}{2}=0$.

Practice 2

A function is continuous at $t=2$. What does its Fourier series converge to there?

Solution

Answer: simply $f(2)$ itself — the convergence theorem only gives something different (the average of one-sided limits) at points of discontinuity; at points of continuity, the series converges to the ordinary function value.

Practice 3

A square wave has value 3 for $0

Solution

$f(2^-)=3$ (approaching from the left, within $(0,2)$); $f(2^+)=-1$ (approaching from the right, within $(2,4)$).

Answer: $\dfrac{3+(-1)}{2}=1$ — the series converges to $1$, completely ignoring the arbitrarily-assigned value $f(2)=10$. The series only "sees" the one-sided limits, never the function's actual (possibly arbitrary) value exactly at the jump.

Practice 4

Explain why the Gibbs phenomenon's overshoot percentage (about 9%) stays roughly constant as more Fourier terms are added, rather than shrinking toward zero the way you might naively expect from a "better and better approximation."

Solution

Adding more terms to a Fourier partial sum does make the approximation converge to the true function more accurately at any fixed point away from the jump — but right at the jump, no finite combination of smooth sine/cosine terms can perfectly reproduce an instantaneous discontinuity. As more terms are added, the region where the overshoot occurs gets squeezed narrower and narrower (closer and closer to the jump location), but the height of the overshoot (as a percentage of the jump size) settles to a fixed value (~9%) rather than disappearing — a genuinely surprising and famous result, since it means adding more terms doesn't universally improve the approximation everywhere near a jump, only away from it.

Practice 5

A triangular wave is continuous everywhere (no jumps at all, just sharp corners). What does its Fourier series converge to at every point, including the sharp corners?

Solution

Answer: the actual function value $f(t)$ at every single point, including the corners — the convergence-to-the-average rule only applies at genuine jump discontinuities (where the one-sided limits actually differ). A sharp corner (like in a triangular wave) is still continuous (the one-sided limits agree, they're just not differentiable there), so ordinary convergence to $f(t)$ holds everywhere.

Practice 6

A function $f(t)$ has period $4$ and is defined as $f(t)=t$ for $-2

Solution

$f(2^-)=2$ (approaching from within the current period). $f(2^+)=f(-2)=-2$ (the next period starts, repeating the value near $t=-2$).

Answer: $\dfrac{2+(-2)}{2}=0$.

Practice 7

A half-wave rectified signal is defined as $f(t)=\sin t$ for $0\le t\le\pi$ and $f(t)=0$ for $\pi\le t\le2\pi$ (period $2\pi$). Is there a jump discontinuity at $t=\pi$? Justify your answer by checking the one-sided limits.

Solution

$f(\pi^-)=\sin(\pi^-)=0$ (since $\sin t\to0$ as $t\to\pi$ from below). $f(\pi^+)=0$ (the function is defined as $0$ there).

Answer: no jump — both one-sided limits equal $0$, so $f$ is continuous at $t=\pi$ (the corner is sharp, like a triangular wave's peak, but not discontinuous).

Practice 8

For a square wave with jump size (difference between the two plateau values) equal to 10, estimate the approximate size of the Gibbs overshoot using the standard ~9% rule.

Solution

Answer: approximately $0.09\times10=0.9$ — the partial sums will overshoot the higher plateau value by roughly $0.9$ near the jump, regardless of how many terms are included (beyond a reasonably large number).

Practice 9

Explain why engineers care about the Gibbs phenomenon when using a truncated (finite-term) Fourier series to approximate a signal with sharp edges, such as a digital square-wave clock signal, in a real circuit simulation.

Solution

Since any practical computation only ever uses a finite number of Fourier terms (never the true infinite series), a truncated approximation to a signal with genuine jumps will always exhibit the Gibbs overshoot near each jump, regardless of how many terms are used. In a circuit simulation or signal-processing context, this isn't just a mathematical curiosity — the simulated signal will show spurious oscillations and overshoot near sharp transitions that aren't present in the real physical circuit's genuine, instantaneous transition, potentially leading to inaccurate predictions (e.g. of voltage spikes) if the engineer isn't aware the overshoot is a truncation artifact rather than a real physical effect.

Practice 10

A signal $f(t)$ has a removable-looking discontinuity: $f(t)\to5$ as $t\to3^-$ and $f(t)\to5$ as $t\to3^+$, but $f(3)$ itself is separately (perhaps by typo or edge-case definition) set to $0$. What does the Fourier series converge to at $t=3$?

Solution

Since both one-sided limits agree ($f(3^-)=f(3^+)=5$), the average is simply $\dfrac{5+5}{2}=5$.

Answer: the series converges to $5$ at $t=3$, not to the separately (and inconsistently) defined value $f(3)=0$ — confirming that Fourier convergence only depends on the one-sided limits, never on an isolated, possibly anomalous function value at a single point.

1 Half-Range Cosine & Sine Series

Sometimes a function is only physically meaningful (or only given) on $[0,L]$ — e.g. the initial shape of a vibrating string of length $L$. To find a Fourier series for it, you choose how to extend it artificially to the interval $[-L,L]$ first: extending it as an even function gives a purely cosine series (the half-range cosine series); extending it as an odd function gives a purely sine series (the half-range sine series). Both represent the same original function correctly on $[0,L]$ — they only differ in how they (arbitrarily) continue it into negative $t$.

Key idea

$$\text{Cosine: } f(t)=\frac{a_0}{2}+\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\cos\frac{n\pi t}{L}, \quad a_n=\frac2L\int_0^L f(t)\cos\frac{n\pi t}{L}\,dt$$ $$\text{Sine: } f(t)=\sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n\sin\frac{n\pi t}{L}, \quad b_n=\frac2L\int_0^L f(t)\sin\frac{n\pi t}{L}\,dt$$

Worked example

Example. Find the half-range sine series for $f(t)=1$ on $(0,\pi)$ ($L=\pi$).

$b_n=\dfrac2\pi\displaystyle\int_0^\pi\sin(nt)\,dt=\dfrac2\pi\left[\dfrac{-\cos nt}{n}\right]_0^\pi=\dfrac{2}{\pi n}\big[1-(-1)^n\big]$.

For even $n$: $b_n=0$. For odd $n$: $b_n=\dfrac4{\pi n}$. Series: $f(t)=\dfrac4\pi\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^\infty\dfrac{\sin\big((2k+1)t\big)}{2k+1}$.

Practice 11

Find the half-range cosine series coefficients $a_0$ and $a_n$ for $f(t)=1$ on $(0,\pi)$.

Solution

$a_0=\dfrac2\pi\displaystyle\int_0^\pi1\,dt=2$.

$a_n=\dfrac2\pi\displaystyle\int_0^\pi\cos(nt)\,dt=\dfrac2\pi\left[\dfrac{\sin nt}{n}\right]_0^\pi=0$ (since $\sin(n\pi)=0$ for all integers $n$).

Answer: $a_0=2,a_n=0$ for $n\ge1$ — the cosine series is simply $f(t)=1$ (trivially correct, since a constant function's even extension is exactly the same constant, needing no additional cosine harmonics).

Practice 12

Find the half-range sine series for $f(t)=t$ on $(0,\pi)$ ($L=\pi$).

Solution

$b_n=\dfrac2\pi\displaystyle\int_0^\pi t\sin(nt)\,dt$. By parts: $\left[\dfrac{-t\cos nt}{n}\right]_0^\pi+\dfrac1n\displaystyle\int_0^\pi\cos nt\,dt=\dfrac{-\pi\cos n\pi}{n}+0=\dfrac{-\pi(-1)^n}{n}$.

$b_n=\dfrac2\pi\cdot\dfrac{-\pi(-1)^n}{n}=\dfrac{-2(-1)^n}{n}=\dfrac{2(-1)^{n+1}}{n}$.

Answer: $f(t)=\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty\dfrac{2(-1)^{n+1}}{n}\sin(nt)$ (matches the full-range odd-extension result from Week 5's worked example, since $f(t)=t$ is already odd).

Practice 13

Find the half-range cosine series for $f(t)=t$ on $(0,\pi)$.

Solution

$a_0=\dfrac2\pi\displaystyle\int_0^\pi t\,dt=\dfrac2\pi\cdot\dfrac{\pi^2}{2}=\pi$.

$a_n=\dfrac2\pi\displaystyle\int_0^\pi t\cos(nt)\,dt$. By parts: $\left[\dfrac{t\sin nt}{n}\right]_0^\pi+\dfrac1n\left[\dfrac{\cos nt}{n}\right]_0^\pi=0+\dfrac{(-1)^n-1}{n^2}$.

$a_n=\dfrac2\pi\cdot\dfrac{(-1)^n-1}{n^2}$: $0$ for even $n$; $\dfrac{-4}{\pi n^2}$ for odd $n$.

Answer: $f(t)=\dfrac\pi2-\dfrac4\pi\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^\infty\dfrac{\cos\big((2k+1)t\big)}{(2k+1)^2}$.

Practice 14

Find the half-range sine series for $f(t)=\pi-t$ on $(0,\pi)$.

Solution

$b_n=\dfrac2\pi\displaystyle\int_0^\pi(\pi-t)\sin(nt)\,dt=\dfrac2\pi\left[\pi\int_0^\pi\sin(nt)dt-\int_0^\pi t\sin(nt)dt\right]$.

First: $\pi\cdot\dfrac{1-(-1)^n}{n}$. Second (from the earlier practice question): $\dfrac{-\pi(-1)^n}{n}$ (before the outer $2/\pi$ factor).

$b_n=\dfrac2\pi\left[\dfrac{\pi(1-(-1)^n)}{n}-\dfrac{-\pi(-1)^n}{n}\right]=\dfrac2\pi\cdot\dfrac{\pi\big[1-(-1)^n+(-1)^n\big]}{n}=\dfrac2\pi\cdot\dfrac{\pi}{n}=\dfrac2n$.

Answer: $b_n=\dfrac2n$ for every $n$ (no even/odd split this time); $f(t)=\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty\dfrac2n\sin(nt)$.

Practice 15

Find the half-range cosine series for $f(t)=\sin t$ on $(0,\pi)$ (a case where the function itself is neither even nor odd on the extended interval by default, so the extension genuinely matters).

Solution

$a_0=\dfrac2\pi\displaystyle\int_0^\pi\sin t\,dt=\dfrac2\pi\big[-\cos t\big]_0^\pi=\dfrac2\pi(1-(-1))=\dfrac4\pi$.

$a_n=\dfrac2\pi\displaystyle\int_0^\pi\sin t\cos(nt)\,dt$ (for $n\ge1$, $n\ne1$): use product-to-sum, $\sin t\cos nt=\tfrac12[\sin((n+1)t)... ]$, more directly $\sin A\cos B=\tfrac12[\sin(A+B)+\sin(A-B)]$ with $A=t,B=nt$: $\tfrac12[\sin((1+n)t)+\sin((1-n)t)]$.

Integrating each sine term over $(0,\pi)$ and simplifying (standard but lengthy calculation) gives $a_n=\dfrac{2}{\pi}\cdot\dfrac{1+(-1)^n}{1-n^2}$ for $n\ne1$, and a separate direct calculation for $n=1$ gives $a_1=0$ (since $\int_0^\pi\sin t\cos t\,dt=\tfrac12\int_0^\pi\sin2t\,dt=0$).

Answer: $a_0=\dfrac4\pi$, $a_1=0$, $a_n=\dfrac{2(1+(-1)^n)}{\pi(1-n^2)}$ for $n\ge2$ (nonzero only for even $n$).

Practice 16

For a half-range series on $(0,5)$ (so $L=5$), write the general form of both the cosine and sine coefficient formulas, substituting the correct value of $L$.

Solution

Cosine: $a_n=\dfrac25\displaystyle\int_0^5 f(t)\cos\!\left(\dfrac{n\pi t}{5}\right)dt$.

Sine: $b_n=\dfrac25\displaystyle\int_0^5 f(t)\sin\!\left(\dfrac{n\pi t}{5}\right)dt$.

Practice 17

Find the half-range sine series for $f(t)=2$ on $(0,3)$ ($L=3$).

Solution

$b_n=\dfrac23\displaystyle\int_0^3 2\sin\!\left(\dfrac{n\pi t}{3}\right)dt=\dfrac43\left[\dfrac{-3\cos(n\pi t/3)}{n\pi}\right]_0^3=\dfrac{-4}{n\pi}\big[\cos(n\pi)-1\big]=\dfrac{4}{n\pi}\big[1-(-1)^n\big]$.

For even $n$: $b_n=0$. For odd $n$: $b_n=\dfrac{8}{n\pi}$.

Answer: $f(t)=\dfrac8\pi\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^\infty\dfrac{1}{2k+1}\sin\!\left(\dfrac{(2k+1)\pi t}{3}\right)$.

Practice 18

Explain, using a sketch-in-words, why the even extension of $f(t)=t$ on $(0,L)$ produces a "V-shaped" (triangular) function on $(-L,L)$, and why this extended function is continuous everywhere (unlike the odd extension, which has a jump).

Solution

The even extension reflects $f(t)=t$ across the $y$-axis: for $t<0$, the extended function equals $f(-t)=-t$ (since the original rule is $f(u)=u$ applied to $u=-t$). So the extended function is $t$ for $t\ge0$ and $-t$ for $t<0$ — exactly $|t|$, a symmetric "V" shape, continuous everywhere (no jump at $t=0$, since both sides approach $0$).

The odd extension instead sets the value for $t<0$ to $-f(-t)=-(-t)=t$... more carefully, odd extension means $f(-t)=-f(t)$, so as $t\to0^-$ the extended function approaches $-f(0^+)=0$ from the definition consistently in this specific case (both give $0$ at the origin), but in general odd extensions of a function with $f(0)\ne0$ produce a genuine jump at $t=0$ (approaching $f(0)$ from the right but $-f(0)$ from the left) — this is the general reason odd extensions often introduce discontinuities that even extensions avoid.

Practice 19

A vibrating string's initial displacement is given as $f(t)=\sin(\pi t/L)$ for $0\le t\le L$ (already matching the shape of the first sine harmonic). Find its half-range sine series, and comment on how many nonzero terms it has.

Solution

Since $f(t)=\sin(\pi t/L)$ is already exactly the first term of the sine series (with $b_1=1$ and every other $b_n=0$), the half-range sine series is simply $f(t)=\sin(\pi t/L)$ itself — a single-term series.

Comment: this happens because the given function already has the exact shape of one of the basis functions used to build the series; the "decomposition" is trivial, needing no combination of multiple harmonics at all.

Practice 20

Explain the practical (physical/engineering) reason for choosing a cosine series over a sine series (or vice versa) when modelling a physical quantity on $[0,L]$, using the example of temperature distribution along a rod with insulated ends (where the temperature's spatial derivative must be zero at both ends).

Solution

The cosine functions $\cos(n\pi t/L)$ all have zero derivative at $t=0$ and $t=L$ (since $\sin$ vanishes at those endpoints of the derivative). This matches the physical boundary condition of insulated ends, where no heat flows across the boundary, meaning the temperature gradient (spatial derivative) must be exactly zero there.

The sine functions $\sin(n\pi t/L)$, by contrast, are zero themselves at $t=0$ and $t=L$ — matching a different physical situation, such as a vibrating string with fixed (zero-displacement) endpoints. Choosing the series type that matches the actual physical boundary conditions of the problem (rather than an arbitrary choice) is what makes half-range series genuinely useful in engineering applications, not just a mathematical curiosity.

2 Fourier Series on a General Interval

The formulas seen so far assumed period $2\pi$ or a general $2L$. For a signal with period $T$ (any positive number, not tied to $\pi$ at all), the same structure applies with the substitution $L=T/2$, so the "angular frequency" of the $n$-th harmonic becomes $\dfrac{2\pi n}{T}$ rather than $\dfrac{n\pi}{L}$ (these are actually the same thing, since $L=T/2$).

Key idea

$$f(t)=\frac{a_0}{2}+\sum_{n=1}^\infty\left[a_n\cos\frac{2n\pi t}{T}+b_n\sin\frac{2n\pi t}{T}\right], \qquad a_n,b_n=\frac2T\int_{-T/2}^{T/2}f(t)\left\{\begin{matrix}\cos\\ \sin\end{matrix}\right\}\frac{2n\pi t}{T}\,dt$$

Worked example

Example. Find $a_0$ for the square wave $f(t)=5$ on $(0,4)$, $f(t)=0$ on $(4,10)$, repeating with period $T=10$.

$a_0=\dfrac2{10}\displaystyle\int_{0}^{10}f(t)\,dt=\dfrac15\displaystyle\int_0^4 5\,dt=\dfrac15(20)=4$.

(Using $(0,T)$ as the integration range rather than $(-T/2,T/2)$ is equally valid, since both cover exactly one full period.)

Practice 21

A signal has period $T=6$. Write the general form of its Fourier series (with correct frequency terms).

Solution

Answer: $f(t)=\dfrac{a_0}{2}+\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty\left[a_n\cos\!\left(\dfrac{n\pi t}{3}\right)+b_n\sin\!\left(\dfrac{n\pi t}{3}\right)\right]$ (using $L=T/2=3$).

Practice 22

Find $a_0$ for $f(t)=t^2$ on $(0,2)$, repeating with period $T=2$.

Solution

$a_0=\dfrac22\displaystyle\int_0^2 t^2\,dt=\left[\dfrac{t^3}{3}\right]_0^2=\dfrac83$.

Practice 23

A power supply produces a rectified sine wave $f(t)=|\sin(100\pi t)|$ (period $T=1/100$ s, corresponding to a rectified 50 Hz mains signal). Express the fundamental angular frequency of its Fourier series in terms of $T$.

Solution

Answer: fundamental angular frequency $=\dfrac{2\pi}{T}=\dfrac{2\pi}{1/100}=200\pi$ rad/s (twice the original 50 Hz mains frequency's angular equivalent, $100\pi$, since full-wave rectification doubles the effective repetition rate).

Practice 24

Find $b_n$ for $f(t)=1$ on $(0,1)$, $f(t)=0$ on $(1,2)$, repeating with period $T=2$ ($L=1$).

Solution

$b_n=\dfrac22\displaystyle\int_0^2 f(t)\sin(n\pi t)\,dt=\displaystyle\int_0^1\sin(n\pi t)\,dt=\left[\dfrac{-\cos(n\pi t)}{n\pi}\right]_0^1=\dfrac{1-\cos(n\pi)}{n\pi}=\dfrac{1-(-1)^n}{n\pi}$.

Answer: $b_n=0$ for even $n$; $b_n=\dfrac{2}{n\pi}$ for odd $n$.

Practice 25

A voltage signal has period $T=0.02$ s (50 Hz). Find the period-appropriate substitution needed in the coefficient formulas, i.e. state $L$ and the general cosine/sine argument for the $n$-th harmonic.

Solution

$L=T/2=0.01$ s. The $n$-th harmonic argument is $\dfrac{n\pi t}{L}=\dfrac{n\pi t}{0.01}=100n\pi t$ — so the coefficient formulas become $a_n,b_n=\dfrac{2}{0.02}\displaystyle\int_0^{0.02}f(t)\{\cos,\sin\}(100n\pi t)\,dt=100\displaystyle\int_0^{0.02}f(t)\{\cos,\sin\}(100n\pi t)\,dt$.

Practice 26

Find $a_0$ and explain (without computing $a_n,b_n$ fully) what $a_0/2$ represents physically for an AC voltage signal $f(t)$ with period $T$.

Solution

$a_0=\dfrac2T\displaystyle\int_0^Tf(t)\,dt$, so $\dfrac{a_0}{2}=\dfrac1T\displaystyle\int_0^Tf(t)\,dt$ — exactly the average (DC) value of the signal over one period.

Physical meaning: for a standard AC voltage (symmetric sine wave with no offset), this average is $0$, since the signal spends equal time positive and negative. A nonzero $a_0/2$ signals a DC offset/bias superimposed on the AC signal — a very practically important quantity for electrical engineers (e.g. determining if a circuit is exposed to unwanted DC bias).

Practice 27

For the general-period Fourier series with $T=2\pi$ (i.e. $L=\pi$), verify that the general formula reduces exactly to the familiar $2\pi$-period formula from Week 5.

Solution

With $T=2\pi$: $L=T/2=\pi$. The general argument $\dfrac{n\pi t}{L}=\dfrac{n\pi t}{\pi}=nt$ — exactly matching the Week 5 formulas $a_n\cos(nt)+b_n\sin(nt)$ used throughout that week's examples.

Answer: confirmed — the Week 5 formulas are simply the special case $T=2\pi$ of this week's general-period formulas.

Practice 28

A signal has $f(t)=e^{-t}$ on $(0,1)$, repeating with period $T=1$. Set up (do not evaluate) the integral for $a_1$ (the first cosine coefficient).

Solution

Answer: $a_1=\dfrac21\displaystyle\int_0^1 e^{-t}\cos(2\pi t)\,dt=2\int_0^1e^{-t}\cos(2\pi t)\,dt$ (using $L=T/2=0.5$, so the harmonic argument is $\dfrac{\pi t}{0.5}=2\pi t$).

Practice 29

A machine vibration signal has period $T=0.5$ s. Find $L$, and write the argument of the $n$-th cosine/sine term.

Solution

$L=T/2=0.25$. The $n$-th harmonic argument is $\dfrac{n\pi t}{L}=\dfrac{n\pi t}{0.25}=4n\pi t$.

Answer: $L=0.25$; harmonics are $\cos(4n\pi t)$ and $\sin(4n\pi t)$.

Practice 30

Find $a_0$ for the sawtooth $f(t)=3t$ on $(0,4)$, repeating with period $T=4$, and interpret its meaning as an average value.

Solution

$a_0=\dfrac24\displaystyle\int_0^4 3t\,dt=\dfrac12\left[\dfrac{3t^2}{2}\right]_0^4=\dfrac12(24)=12$.

Answer: $a_0=12$, so $a_0/2=6$ is the average value of $f(t)=3t$ over one period $(0,4)$ — matching the direct average $\dfrac1{4}\int_0^43t\,dt=6$. ✓

Practice 61

For $f(x)=|x|$ on $(-\pi,\pi)$: (a) derive Fourier series, (b) use it at $x=0$ to evaluate $\sum_{n\,odd}\frac1{n^2}$.

Solution

Even function: $b_n=0$, $a_0=\frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^\pi xdx=\pi$. Also $a_n=\frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^\pi x\cos(nx)dx=\frac{2}{\pi}\frac{(-1)^n-1}{n^2}$. So $|x|=\frac\pi2-\frac{4}{\pi}\sum_{n\,odd}\frac{\cos(nx)}{n^2}$. Set $x=0$: $0=\frac\pi2-\frac4\pi\sum_{n\,odd}\frac1{n^2}$, so $\sum_{n\,odd}\frac1{n^2}=\frac{\pi^2}{8}$.

Practice 62

Find half-range cosine series on $(0,L)$ for $f(x)=x^2$.

Solution

$a_0=\frac{2}{L}\int_0^Lx^2dx=\frac{2L^2}{3}$. For $n\ge1$, $a_n=\frac{2}{L}\int_0^Lx^2\cos\frac{n\pi x}{L}dx=\frac{4L^2(-1)^n}{n^2\pi^2}$. Therefore $x^2=\frac{L^2}{3}+\frac{4L^2}{\pi^2}\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^n}{n^2}\cos\frac{n\pi x}{L}$.

Practice 63

A piecewise periodic signal has a jump from 1 to -1 at $x=0$. What value does its Fourier series converge to at $x=0$? Briefly justify.

Solution

At a jump, Fourier series converges to midpoint of one-sided limits: $\frac{1+(-1)}2=0$. This is the Dirichlet convergence theorem.

Practice 64

For $f(x)=1$ on $(0,\pi)$, derive half-range sine series and explain why only odd harmonics appear.

Solution

$b_n=\frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^\pi\sin(nx)dx=\frac{2}{n\pi}(1-(-1)^n)$. Hence $b_n=0$ for even $n$, $b_n=\frac{4}{n\pi}$ for odd $n$. So $1=\frac4\pi\left(\sin x+\frac{1}{3}\sin3x+\frac{1}{5}\sin5x+\cdots\right)$ on $(0,\pi)$.
