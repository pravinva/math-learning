Partial Derivatives, the Chain Rule & Double Integrals (Introduction)

Differentiating a multivariable function means asking how it changes with respect to ONE variable at a time, holding the others fixed — this single idea (the partial derivative) unlocks the multivariable chain rule, implicit differentiation, linear approximation/error estimation, and (reversing the process) double integration. Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Partial derivative: $\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x}$ treats $y$ (and any other variables) as a constant, differentiating with respect to $x$ alone; $\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial y}$ does the reverse.
  • Higher-order & mixed partials: $f_{xy}=\dfrac{\partial^2f}{\partial y\partial x}$; for well-behaved (continuous second partials) functions, $f_{xy}=f_{yx}$ (Clairaut's theorem) — a useful check.
  • Multivariable chain rule (for $z=f(x,y)$ with $x=x(t),y=y(t)$): $\dfrac{dz}{dt}=\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial x}\dfrac{dx}{dt}+\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial y}\dfrac{dy}{dt}$.
  • Implicit differentiation (for $F(x,y)=0$): $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-\dfrac{F_x}{F_y}$.
  • Linear approximation / differentials: $dz=f_x\,dx+f_y\,dy \approx \Delta z$ — used for error estimation: if $x,y$ have measurement errors $dx,dy$, the resulting error in $z=f(x,y)$ is approximately $dz$.
  • Double integral (Cartesian, over a rectangle or general region): $\displaystyle\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA = \int_c^d\int_a^b f(x,y)\,dx\,dy$ (or the other integration order) — evaluated as an iterated integral, working from the inside out.

0 Partial Derivatives

For $z=f(x,y)$, the partial derivative $\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x}$ measures the rate of change of $f$ in the $x$-direction alone — found by differentiating with respect to $x$ exactly as in single-variable calculus, while treating every other variable ($y$, and any others) as a fixed constant. $\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial y}$ does the same in the $y$-direction.

Higher-order partials. Differentiating $f_x$ again with respect to $x$ gives $f_{xx}$; differentiating $f_x$ with respect to $y$ instead gives the mixed partial $f_{xy}$. For essentially all functions encountered in this course (those with continuous second partial derivatives), the order of mixed differentiation doesn't matter: $f_{xy}=f_{yx}$ — a handy way to check your work.

Key idea

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(x+h,y)-f(x,y)}{h}, \qquad f_{xy}=f_{yx} \ \text{(Clairaut's theorem, when continuous)}$$

Worked example

Example 1. Find $f_x$ and $f_y$ for $f(x,y)=x^3y^2+\sin(xy)$.

$f_x=3x^2y^2+y\cos(xy)$ (treat $y$ as constant); $f_y=2x^3y+x\cos(xy)$ (treat $x$ as constant).

Example 2. Verify $f_{xy}=f_{yx}$ for $f(x,y)=x^2y^3$.

$f_x=2xy^3 \implies f_{xy}=6xy^2$. $f_y=3x^2y^2 \implies f_{yx}=6xy^2$. Match. ✓

Practice 1

Find $f_x$ and $f_y$ for $f(x,y)=e^{xy}\ln(x+y)$.

Solution

$f_x=ye^{xy}\ln(x+y)+e^{xy}\cdot\dfrac{1}{x+y}$ (product rule, treating $y$ constant).

$f_y=xe^{xy}\ln(x+y)+e^{xy}\cdot\dfrac{1}{x+y}$ (by symmetry, treating $x$ constant).

Practice 2

Find $f_{xx}$, $f_{yy}$, and $f_{xy}$ for $f(x,y)=x^4y^3-2x^2y$.

Solution

$f_x=4x^3y^3-4xy$; $f_{xx}=12x^2y^3-4y$.

$f_y=3x^4y^2-2x^2$; $f_{yy}=6x^4y$.

$f_{xy}=\dfrac{\partial}{\partial y}(4x^3y^3-4xy)=12x^3y^2-4x$.

Practice 3

Find $\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial x}$ and $\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial y}$ for $z=\tan^{-1}\left(\dfrac{y}{x}\right)$.

Solution

Let $u=y/x$; $\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial u}=\dfrac{1}{1+u^2}$.

$\dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x}=-\dfrac{y}{x^2}$: $z_x=\dfrac{1}{1+y^2/x^2}\cdot\left(-\dfrac{y}{x^2}\right)=\dfrac{-y}{x^2+y^2}$.

$\dfrac{\partial u}{\partial y}=\dfrac1x$: $z_y=\dfrac{1}{1+y^2/x^2}\cdot\dfrac1x=\dfrac{x}{x^2+y^2}$.

Practice 4

Verify Clairaut's theorem ($f_{xy}=f_{yx}$) for $f(x,y)=\sin(x^2y)$.

Solution

$f_x=2xy\cos(x^2y)$; $f_{xy}=2x\cos(x^2y)+2xy\cdot(-x^2\sin(x^2y))=2x\cos(x^2y)-2x^3y\sin(x^2y)$.

$f_y=x^2\cos(x^2y)$; $f_{yx}=2x\cos(x^2y)+x^2\cdot(-2xy\sin(x^2y))=2x\cos(x^2y)-2x^3y\sin(x^2y)$.

Answer: both mixed partials match exactly. ✓

Practice 5

The ideal gas law gives $P=\dfrac{nRT}{V}$ ($n,R$ constant). Find $\dfrac{\partial P}{\partial T}$ and $\dfrac{\partial P}{\partial V}$, and interpret each physically.

Solution

$\dfrac{\partial P}{\partial T}=\dfrac{nR}{V}$ — at fixed volume, pressure increases linearly with temperature.

$\dfrac{\partial P}{\partial V}=-\dfrac{nRT}{V^2}$ — at fixed temperature, pressure decreases (and does so more steeply at smaller $V$) as volume increases, consistent with Boyle's Law.

Practice 6

Find all second-order partial derivatives ($f_{xx},f_{yy},f_{xy},f_{yx}$) of $f(x,y)=e^x\cos y$.

Solution

$f_x=e^x\cos y$; $f_{xx}=e^x\cos y$.

$f_y=-e^x\sin y$; $f_{yy}=-e^x\cos y$.

$f_{xy}=\dfrac{\partial}{\partial y}(e^x\cos y)=-e^x\sin y$; $f_{yx}=\dfrac{\partial}{\partial x}(-e^x\sin y)=-e^x\sin y$. Match. ✓

Practice 7

Show that $u(x,t)=\sin(x-ct)$ satisfies the wave equation $\dfrac{\partial^2u}{\partial t^2}=c^2\dfrac{\partial^2u}{\partial x^2}$.

Solution

$u_x=\cos(x-ct)$; $u_{xx}=-\sin(x-ct)$.

$u_t=-c\cos(x-ct)$; $u_{tt}=-c^2\sin(x-ct)$.

Check: $u_{tt}=-c^2\sin(x-ct)=c^2\big[-\sin(x-ct)\big]=c^2u_{xx}$. ✓ Satisfies the wave equation for any constant $c$.

Practice 8

Find $f_x(1,2)$ and $f_y(1,2)$ for $f(x,y)=x^2y+3xy^2$, and interpret the sign of each.

Solution

$f_x=2xy+3y^2 \implies f_x(1,2)=2(1)(2)+3(4)=4+12=16$.

$f_y=x^2+6xy \implies f_y(1,2)=1+12=13$.

Interpretation: both are positive, meaning $f$ is increasing at $(1,2)$ in both the $x$-direction and the $y$-direction (moving slightly in either positive direction increases $f$'s value).

Practice 9

Find $\dfrac{\partial w}{\partial x}$, $\dfrac{\partial w}{\partial y}$, and $\dfrac{\partial w}{\partial z}$ for the three-variable function $w=x^2yz^3+\ln(xz)$.

Solution

$w_x=2xyz^3+\dfrac1x$ (treating $y,z$ constant).

$w_y=x^2z^3$ (treating $x,z$ constant; the $\ln(xz)$ term has no $y$, so its partial is $0$).

$w_z=3x^2yz^2+\dfrac1z$ (treating $x,y$ constant).

Practice 10

Explain, using the definition of the partial derivative as a limit, why $\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x}$ at a point $(a,b)$ can be interpreted as the ordinary single-variable derivative of the "slice" function $g(x)=f(x,b)$ (holding $y=b$ fixed) at $x=a$.

Solution

By definition, $\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x}(a,b)=\displaystyle\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(a+h,b)-f(a,b)}{h}$ — note that $y=b$ is held fixed throughout this limit, never varying. Defining $g(x)=f(x,b)$ (the single-variable function obtained by "slicing" the surface at the fixed height $y=b$), this limit is exactly $\lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{g(a+h)-g(a)}{h}=g'(a)$ — the ordinary derivative of $g$ at $x=a$. This is precisely why computing a partial derivative just means "treat the other variable as a constant and differentiate normally": you're differentiating the single-variable slice function through that fixed value.

1 The Multivariable Chain Rule & Implicit Differentiation

If $z=f(x,y)$ and both $x$ and $y$ are themselves functions of a third variable $t$ (e.g. both position coordinates changing over time), the total rate of change of $z$ with respect to $t$ has to account for both pathways of dependence — this is the multivariable chain rule, summing the contribution through $x$ and the contribution through $y$.

Implicit differentiation, revisited. An equation like $F(x,y)=0$ implicitly defines $y$ as a function of $x$ (locally). Treating $F$ as a function of two variables and applying the chain rule along the curve $F(x,y(x))=0$ gives a clean formula for $dy/dx$ directly in terms of the partial derivatives of $F$ — no need to laboriously isolate $y$ first.

Key idea

$$\frac{dz}{dt}=\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt}+\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dt}, \qquad \frac{dy}{dx}=-\frac{F_x}{F_y} \ \text{(for } F(x,y)=0\text{)}$$

Worked example

Example 1 — chain rule. If $z=x^2y$, $x=\cos t$, $y=\sin t$, find $\dfrac{dz}{dt}$ at $t=\pi/4$.

$z_x=2xy,z_y=x^2$; $\dfrac{dx}{dt}=-\sin t,\dfrac{dy}{dt}=\cos t$. $\dfrac{dz}{dt}=2xy(-\sin t)+x^2\cos t$.

At $t=\pi/4$: $x=y=\dfrac{\sqrt2}{2}$. $\dfrac{dz}{dt}=2\cdot\tfrac12\cdot\left(-\tfrac{\sqrt2}{2}\right)+\tfrac12\cdot\tfrac{\sqrt2}{2}=-\tfrac{\sqrt2}{2}+\tfrac{\sqrt2}{4}=-\tfrac{\sqrt2}{4}$.

Example 2 — implicit differentiation. Find $dy/dx$ for $x^3+y^3=6xy$.

$F(x,y)=x^3+y^3-6xy$; $F_x=3x^2-6y,F_y=3y^2-6x$. $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-\dfrac{3x^2-6y}{3y^2-6x}=\dfrac{2y-x^2}{y^2-2x}$.

Practice 11

If $z=x^2+3y^2$, $x=t^2$, $y=2t$, find $\dfrac{dz}{dt}$ using the chain rule, then verify by direct substitution.

Solution

Chain rule: $z_x=2x,z_y=6y$; $dx/dt=2t,dy/dt=2$.

$\dfrac{dz}{dt}=2x(2t)+6y(2)=2(t^2)(2t)+6(2t)(2)=4t^3+24t$.

Direct substitution: $z=(t^2)^2+3(2t)^2=t^4+12t^2$; $\dfrac{dz}{dt}=4t^3+24t$. Match. ✓

Practice 12

Find $dy/dx$ implicitly for $\sin(xy)=x+y$.

Solution

$F(x,y)=\sin(xy)-x-y$; $F_x=y\cos(xy)-1$; $F_y=x\cos(xy)-1$.

Answer: $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-\dfrac{y\cos(xy)-1}{x\cos(xy)-1}=\dfrac{1-y\cos(xy)}{x\cos(xy)-1}$.

Practice 13

If $w=x^2y-z^3$, $x=t$, $y=t^2$, $z=t^3$, find $\dfrac{dw}{dt}$ using the (three-variable) chain rule.

Solution

$w_x=2xy,w_y=x^2,w_z=-3z^2$; $dx/dt=1,dy/dt=2t,dz/dt=3t^2$.

$\dfrac{dw}{dt}=2xy(1)+x^2(2t)+(-3z^2)(3t^2) = 2(t)(t^2)+t^2(2t)-9z^2t^2$.

$=2t^3+2t^3-9(t^3)^2t^2=4t^3-9t^8$.

Practice 14

Find $dy/dx$ implicitly for $e^{xy}=x^2-y^2$.

Solution

$F(x,y)=e^{xy}-x^2+y^2$; $F_x=ye^{xy}-2x$; $F_y=xe^{xy}+2y$.

Answer: $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-\dfrac{ye^{xy}-2x}{xe^{xy}+2y}=\dfrac{2x-ye^{xy}}{xe^{xy}+2y}$.

Practice 15

A point moves along the curve $x=\cos t, y=\sin t$ on the surface $z=xy$. Find the rate of change of the point's height $z$ at time $t=\pi/3$.

Solution

$z_x=y,z_y=x$; $dx/dt=-\sin t,dy/dt=\cos t$. $\dfrac{dz}{dt}=y(-\sin t)+x\cos t$.

At $t=\pi/3$: $x=\tfrac12,y=\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2}$. $\dfrac{dz}{dt}=\dfrac{\sqrt3}{2}\left(-\dfrac{\sqrt3}{2}\right)+\dfrac12\cdot\dfrac12=-\dfrac34+\dfrac14=-\dfrac12$.

Answer: $-\dfrac12$ (the height is momentarily decreasing at this instant).

Practice 16

Use implicit differentiation to find the slope of the tangent line to the curve $x^2y+y^3=10$ at the point $(1,2)$ (verify the point lies on the curve first).

Solution

Check: $1^2(2)+2^3=2+8=10$. ✓ Point lies on the curve.

$F(x,y)=x^2y+y^3-10$; $F_x=2xy$; $F_y=x^2+3y^2$.

$\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-\dfrac{2xy}{x^2+3y^2}$; at $(1,2)$: $-\dfrac{2(1)(2)}{1+12}=-\dfrac{4}{13}$.

Answer: slope $=-\dfrac{4}{13}$.

Practice 17

If $z=f(x,y)$ where $x=r\cos\theta,y=r\sin\theta$ (polar coordinates), use the chain rule to express $\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial r}$ in terms of $z_x,z_y,\theta$.

Solution

$\dfrac{\partial x}{\partial r}=\cos\theta$, $\dfrac{\partial y}{\partial r}=\sin\theta$ (holding $\theta$ fixed).

Answer: $\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial r}=z_x\cos\theta+z_y\sin\theta$.

Practice 18

A conical tank's volume is $V=\dfrac{1}{3}\pi r^2h$. If both $r$ and $h$ are increasing functions of time, with $dr/dt=2$ cm/min and $dh/dt=3$ cm/min at the instant $r=4$ cm, $h=10$ cm, find $dV/dt$ at that instant.

Solution

$V_r=\dfrac23\pi rh$, $V_h=\dfrac13\pi r^2$.

$\dfrac{dV}{dt}=V_r\dfrac{dr}{dt}+V_h\dfrac{dh}{dt}=\dfrac23\pi(4)(10)(2)+\dfrac13\pi(16)(3)$.

$=\dfrac{160\pi}{3}+16\pi=\dfrac{160\pi}{3}+\dfrac{48\pi}{3}=\dfrac{208\pi}{3}$.

Answer: $\dfrac{208\pi}{3}\approx217.8\ \text{cm}^3\text{/min}$.

Practice 19

Find $dy/dx$ implicitly for the circle $x^2+y^2=r^2$ (a fixed constant $r$), and verify your answer matches the standard geometric fact that a tangent to a circle is perpendicular to the radius at the point of tangency.

Solution

$F(x,y)=x^2+y^2-r^2$; $F_x=2x,F_y=2y$. $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-\dfrac{2x}{2y}=-\dfrac{x}{y}$.

Geometric check: the radius to point $(x,y)$ has slope $\dfrac{y}{x}$ (from the origin). The tangent line's slope is $-\dfrac{x}{y}$, which is the negative reciprocal of $\dfrac{y}{x}$ — confirming the tangent is perpendicular to the radius, as expected from basic geometry.

Practice 20

Explain why the implicit differentiation formula $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-\dfrac{F_x}{F_y}$ fails (becomes undefined) at any point where $F_y=0$, and give the geometric interpretation of what's happening at such a point on the curve $F(x,y)=0$.

Solution

The formula divides by $F_y$, so it's simply undefined whenever $F_y=0$ — ordinary division by zero.

Geometric interpretation: $F_y=0$ typically signals that the curve $F(x,y)=0$ has a vertical tangent line at that point (the curve momentarily isn't expressible as a function $y=g(x)$ with a finite slope there) — for example, the rightmost and leftmost points of a circle $x^2+y^2=r^2$ have $y=0$, giving $F_y=2y=0$, exactly where the circle's tangent is vertical.

2 Applications of Partial Derivatives — Linear Approximation & Error Estimation

Just as $dy=f'(x)\,dx$ approximates the change in a single-variable function, the multivariable version $dz=f_x\,dx+f_y\,dy$ approximates the change in $z=f(x,y)$ when $x$ and $y$ both change slightly — summing the individual contributions from each variable's change.

Error estimation. If $x$ and $y$ are measured quantities with possible errors $dx,dy$ (e.g. instrument precision), the resulting error in a computed quantity $z=f(x,y)$ is approximated by $dz=f_x\,dx+f_y\,dy$ — letting engineers estimate how measurement uncertainty propagates into a final calculated result, without needing to recompute $f$ at every possible error combination.

Key idea

$$dz = f_x\,dx+f_y\,dy \approx \Delta z = f(x+\Delta x,y+\Delta y)-f(x,y)$$

Worked example

Example 1 — linear approximation. Use differentials to estimate $\sqrt{(3.02)^2+(3.97)^2}$.

$f(x,y)=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$; at $(3,4)$: $f=5$. $f_x=\dfrac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}},f_y=\dfrac{y}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}$; at $(3,4)$: $f_x=0.6,f_y=0.8$.

$dx=0.02,dy=-0.03$: $dz=0.6(0.02)+0.8(-0.03)=0.012-0.024=-0.012$. Estimate: $5-0.012=4.988$.

Example 2 — error estimation. A cylinder's radius is measured as $r=5$ cm (±0.1 cm) and height $h=10$ cm (±0.2 cm). Estimate the maximum error in the computed volume $V=\pi r^2h$.

$V_r=2\pi rh,V_h=\pi r^2$; at $(5,10)$: $V_r=100\pi,V_h=25\pi$.

$dV=100\pi(0.1)+25\pi(0.2)=10\pi+5\pi=15\pi\approx47.1\ \text{cm}^3$.

Practice 21

Use differentials to estimate $(2.01)^3(1.98)^2$.

Solution

$f(x,y)=x^3y^2$; at $(2,2)$: $f=8(4)=32$. $f_x=3x^2y^2,f_y=2x^3y$; at $(2,2)$: $f_x=3(4)(4)=48,f_y=2(8)(2)=32$.

$dx=0.01,dy=-0.02$: $dz=48(0.01)+32(-0.02)=0.48-0.64=-0.16$.

Answer: $32-0.16=31.84$.

Practice 22

The dimensions of a rectangular box are measured as $l=10$ cm, $w=8$ cm, $h=5$ cm, each with a possible error of ±0.1 cm. Estimate the maximum error in the computed volume $V=lwh$.

Solution

$V_l=wh=40,V_w=lh=50,V_h=lw=80$ (all at the given measurements).

$dV=40(0.1)+50(0.1)+80(0.1)=4+5+8=17$.

Answer: maximum estimated error $\approx17\ \text{cm}^3$.

Practice 23

The resistance of two resistors in parallel is $R=\dfrac{R_1R_2}{R_1+R_2}$. If $R_1=100\ \Omega$ and $R_2=200\ \Omega$, each measured with possible error ±2 $\Omega$, estimate the maximum resulting error in $R$.

Solution

$R=\dfrac{R_1R_2}{R_1+R_2}$; at $(100,200)$: $R=\dfrac{20000}{300}=\dfrac{200}{3}$.

$R_{R_1}=\dfrac{R_2^2}{(R_1+R_2)^2}=\dfrac{40000}{90000}=\dfrac49$; $R_{R_2}=\dfrac{R_1^2}{(R_1+R_2)^2}=\dfrac{10000}{90000}=\dfrac19$.

$dR=\dfrac49(2)+\dfrac19(2)=\dfrac89+\dfrac29=\dfrac{10}{9}\approx1.11\ \Omega$.

Answer: maximum estimated error $\approx1.11\ \Omega$.

Practice 24

Use differentials to estimate the change in $z=\sqrt{x^2+y^3}$ as $(x,y)$ moves from $(3,2)$ to $(3.1,1.98)$.

Solution

At $(3,2)$: $z=\sqrt{9+8}=\sqrt{17}$. $z_x=\dfrac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+y^3}},z_y=\dfrac{3y^2}{2\sqrt{x^2+y^3}}$; at $(3,2)$: $z_x=\dfrac{3}{\sqrt{17}},z_y=\dfrac{6}{\sqrt{17}}$.

$dx=0.1,dy=-0.02$: $dz=\dfrac{3}{\sqrt{17}}(0.1)+\dfrac{6}{\sqrt{17}}(-0.02)=\dfrac{0.3-0.12}{\sqrt{17}}=\dfrac{0.18}{\sqrt{17}}\approx0.0437$.

Answer: $z$ increases by approximately $0.0437$.

Practice 25

A pendulum's period is $T=2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$. If $L=1.0$ m (±0.005 m) and $g=9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$ (±0.01 m/s²), estimate the maximum resulting error in $T$.

Solution

$T_L=2\pi\cdot\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{Lg}}=\dfrac{\pi}{\sqrt{Lg}}$; $T_g=2\pi\sqrt{L}\cdot\left(-\dfrac12\right)g^{-3/2}=-\pi\sqrt{L}\,g^{-3/2}$.

At $(1.0,9.8)$: $\sqrt{Lg}=\sqrt{9.8}\approx3.130$; $T_L=\dfrac{\pi}{3.130}\approx1.004$.

$T_g=-\pi(1)(9.8)^{-3/2}=-\dfrac{\pi}{9.8\sqrt{9.8}}\approx-\dfrac{\pi}{30.68}\approx-0.1024$.

$dT=1.004(0.005)+|-0.1024|(0.01)=0.00502+0.00102=0.00604$.

Answer: maximum estimated error $\approx0.006$ s.

Practice 26

Use differentials to approximate $\ln(1.02^3+0.97^3-1)$ (treat as $f(x,y)=\ln(x^3+y^3-1)$ near $(1,1)$).

Solution

At $(1,1)$: $f=\ln(1+1-1)=\ln1=0$.

$f_x=\dfrac{3x^2}{x^3+y^3-1},f_y=\dfrac{3y^2}{x^3+y^3-1}$; at $(1,1)$: both equal $\dfrac{3}{1}=3$.

$dx=0.02,dy=-0.03$: $dz=3(0.02)+3(-0.03)=0.06-0.09=-0.03$.

Answer: $\approx0-0.03=-0.03$.

Practice 27

A surveying instrument measures the two legs of a right triangle as $a=30$ m and $b=40$ m, each with possible error ±0.05 m. Estimate the maximum resulting error in the computed hypotenuse $c=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$.

Solution

At $(30,40)$: $c=\sqrt{900+1600}=\sqrt{2500}=50$.

$c_a=\dfrac{a}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2}}=\dfrac{30}{50}=0.6$; $c_b=\dfrac{b}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2}}=\dfrac{40}{50}=0.8$.

$dc=0.6(0.05)+0.8(0.05)=0.03+0.04=0.07$.

Answer: maximum estimated error $\approx0.07$ m.

Practice 28

Explain why the differential formula $dz=f_x\,dx+f_y\,dy$ gives the maximum estimated error when $dx$ and $dy$ are taken as the maximum possible measurement errors (rather than their actual, unknown signed values), and why engineers typically use $|f_x||dx|+|f_y||dy|$ in practice.

Solution

The true error $\Delta z$ could have either measurement error contribute positively or negatively to the final result, depending on the (unknown, in practice) actual signs of the individual measurement errors. Since we don't know those signs in advance, the worst case (maximum possible combined error) occurs when both terms happen to have the same sign, adding constructively rather than partially cancelling. Using $|f_x||dx|+|f_y||dy|$ (absolute values throughout) guarantees this worst-case bound is captured, giving a safe upper estimate on how large the error in $z$ could possibly be — which is what matters for engineering tolerances and safety margins.

Practice 29

The volume of a cone is $V=\dfrac13\pi r^2h$. If $r$ is measured with a 2% error and $h$ with a 3% error, use differentials (in terms of percentage/relative error) to estimate the resulting percentage error in $V$.

Solution

Relative error approach: $\dfrac{dV}{V}=\dfrac{V_r\,dr+V_h\,dh}{V}$. Since $V_r=\dfrac23\pi rh$ and $V=\dfrac13\pi r^2h$: $\dfrac{V_r\,dr}{V}=\dfrac{2\,dr}{r}$ (a 2% error in $r$ contributes twice, since $V\propto r^2$).

Since $V_h=\dfrac13\pi r^2$ and $V=\dfrac13\pi r^2h$: $\dfrac{V_h\,dh}{V}=\dfrac{dh}{h}$ (a 1-to-1 contribution, since $V\propto h^1$).

Total relative error: $2(2\%)+1(3\%)=4\%+3\%=7\%$.

Answer: approximately 7% error in $V$ (this "multiply by the exponent" shortcut for relative errors in power-law formulas is a fast, commonly-used technique).

Practice 30

A company's revenue is modelled as $R(p,q)=pq$ where $p$ is price and $q$ is quantity sold. If the current price is $\$50$ with quantity 200 units, and a market analysis estimates $dp=\$2$ and $dq=-15$ units (price increase causing a quantity decrease), estimate the resulting change in revenue, and explain the economic meaning of each term.

Solution

$R_p=q,R_q=p$; at $(50,200)$: $R_p=200,R_q=50$.

$dR=R_p\,dp+R_q\,dq=200(2)+50(-15)=400-750=-350$.

Answer: revenue decreases by approximately $\$350$.

Economic meaning: the first term ($200\times2=400$) is the revenue gained purely from selling the same 200 units at $\$2$ more each. The second term ($50\times(-15)=-750$) is the revenue lost from selling 15 fewer units at the (new) price. Since the quantity-loss effect outweighs the price-gain effect here, the net result is a revenue decrease — a direct illustration of price elasticity of demand.

3 Double Integrals in Cartesian Coordinates (Introduction)

Just as a single integral sums up thin slices to find an area, a double integral sums up thin rectangular pieces of a 2D region to find a volume under a surface $z=f(x,y)$. In practice, a double integral is evaluated as an iterated integral: integrate with respect to one variable first (treating the other as constant, exactly like a partial derivative in reverse), then integrate the result with respect to the second variable.

Order of integration. For a rectangular region $a\le x\le b,\ c\le y\le d$, the order doesn't matter: $\displaystyle\int_a^b\int_c^d f(x,y)\,dy\,dx = \int_c^d\int_a^b f(x,y)\,dx\,dy$. For non-rectangular regions, the bounds of the inner integral typically depend on the outer variable — correctly identifying those bounds is the main new skill this topic introduces (with reverse-order integration and polar coordinates arriving next week).

Key idea

$$\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA = \int_a^b\left[\int_c^d f(x,y)\,dy\right]dx$$

Worked example

Example 1 — rectangular region. Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^2\int_0^1 (x^2+y)\,dy\,dx$.

Inner integral (with respect to $y$, $x$ fixed): $\displaystyle\int_0^1(x^2+y)\,dy = \left[x^2y+\dfrac{y^2}{2}\right]_0^1=x^2+\dfrac12$.

Outer integral: $\displaystyle\int_0^2\left(x^2+\dfrac12\right)dx=\left[\dfrac{x^3}{3}+\dfrac{x}{2}\right]_0^2=\dfrac83+1=\dfrac{11}{3}$.

Example 2 — region with variable bounds. Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_0^x xy\,dy\,dx$ (a triangular region, $0\le y\le x\le1$).

Inner: $\displaystyle\int_0^x xy\,dy=x\left[\dfrac{y^2}{2}\right]_0^x=\dfrac{x^3}{2}$. Outer: $\displaystyle\int_0^1\dfrac{x^3}{2}\,dx=\dfrac18$.

Practice 31

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_1^3\int_0^2 (3x+2y)\,dy\,dx$.

Solution

Inner: $\displaystyle\int_0^2(3x+2y)\,dy=\left[3xy+y^2\right]_0^2=6x+4$.

Outer: $\displaystyle\int_1^3(6x+4)\,dx=\left[3x^2+4x\right]_1^3=(27+12)-(3+4)=39-7=32$.

Answer: $32$.

Practice 32

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_0^{y^2} (2x)\,dx\,dy$ (variable inner bounds, integrating $x$ first).

Solution

Inner: $\displaystyle\int_0^{y^2}2x\,dx=\left[x^2\right]_0^{y^2}=y^4$.

Outer: $\displaystyle\int_0^1 y^4\,dy=\left[\dfrac{y^5}{5}\right]_0^1=\dfrac15$.

Answer: $\dfrac15$.

Practice 33

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi}\int_0^1 x\sin(xy)\,dy\,dx$ (inner integral requires basic substitution).

Solution

Inner (with respect to $y$, treating $x$ as constant): $\displaystyle\int_0^1 x\sin(xy)\,dy = \left[-\cos(xy)\right]_0^1=1-\cos x$ (since $\int x\sin(xy)\,dy=-\cos(xy)+C$, differentiating with respect to $y$ confirms this).

Outer: $\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi}(1-\cos x)\,dx=\left[x-\sin x\right]_0^{\pi}=(\pi-0)-0=\pi$.

Answer: $\pi$.

Practice 34

Set up and evaluate the double integral for the volume under $z=x+y+1$ over the rectangle $0\le x\le2,0\le y\le3$.

Solution

$V=\displaystyle\int_0^2\int_0^3(x+y+1)\,dy\,dx$.

Inner: $\displaystyle\int_0^3(x+y+1)\,dy=\left[xy+\dfrac{y^2}{2}+y\right]_0^3=3x+4.5+3=3x+7.5$.

Outer: $\displaystyle\int_0^2(3x+7.5)\,dx=\left[\dfrac{3x^2}{2}+7.5x\right]_0^2=6+15=21$.

Answer: $V=21$.

Practice 35

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_x^1 e^{y^2}\,dy\,dx$ (this integral cannot be done in the given order — explain why, then reverse the order of integration and evaluate).

Solution

$\displaystyle\int e^{y^2}\,dy$ has no elementary antiderivative (similar to the Gaussian integral), so the inner integral (as given) cannot be evaluated in closed form directly.

Reverse the order: the region is $0\le x\le1,\ x\le y\le1$, equivalently $0\le y\le1,\ 0\le x\le y$ (sketching the triangular region confirms this swap). $$\int_0^1\int_0^y e^{y^2}\,dx\,dy$$

Inner (now trivial, since $e^{y^2}$ doesn't depend on $x$): $\displaystyle\int_0^y e^{y^2}\,dx = ye^{y^2}$.

Outer: $\displaystyle\int_0^1 ye^{y^2}\,dy$; let $u=y^2,du=2y\,dy$: $\dfrac12\int_0^1e^u\,du=\dfrac12(e-1)$.

Answer: $\dfrac{e-1}{2}\approx0.859$ (this is a classic example previewing next week's reverse-order-integration topic).

Practice 36

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^2\int_0^{4-x^2} y\,dy\,dx$ (region under a parabola).

Solution

Inner: $\displaystyle\int_0^{4-x^2}y\,dy=\left[\dfrac{y^2}{2}\right]_0^{4-x^2}=\dfrac{(4-x^2)^2}{2}$.

Outer: $\displaystyle\int_0^2\dfrac{(4-x^2)^2}{2}\,dx=\dfrac12\int_0^2(16-8x^2+x^4)\,dx$.

$=\dfrac12\left[16x-\dfrac{8x^3}{3}+\dfrac{x^5}{5}\right]_0^2=\dfrac12\left(32-\dfrac{64}{3}+\dfrac{32}{5}\right)$.

Common denominator 15: $32=\tfrac{480}{15},\tfrac{64}{3}=\tfrac{320}{15},\tfrac{32}{5}=\tfrac{96}{15}$: $\dfrac{480-320+96}{15}=\dfrac{256}{15}$.

Answer: $\dfrac12\cdot\dfrac{256}{15}=\dfrac{128}{15}$.

Practice 37

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_0^1 \frac{1}{(x+y+1)^2}\,dy\,dx$.

Solution

Inner: let $u=x+y+1$, $du=dy$: $\displaystyle\int\frac{1}{u^2}\,du=-\frac1u$. Evaluated from $y=0$ ($u=x+1$) to $y=1$ ($u=x+2$): $-\dfrac{1}{x+2}+\dfrac{1}{x+1}$.

Outer: $\displaystyle\int_0^1\left(\frac{1}{x+1}-\frac{1}{x+2}\right)dx=\Big[\ln(x+1)-\ln(x+2)\Big]_0^1$.

At $x=1$: $\ln2-\ln3$. At $x=0$: $\ln1-\ln2=-\ln2$.

Subtract: $(\ln2-\ln3)-(-\ln2)=2\ln2-\ln3=\ln\dfrac{4}{3}$.

Answer: $\ln\dfrac43\approx0.2877$.

Practice 38

Set up (do not evaluate) the double integral for the area of the region bounded by $y=x^2$ and $y=2x$, choosing the order of integration and finding the correct bounds.

Solution

Find intersections: $x^2=2x\implies x(x-2)=0\implies x=0,2$.

For $0\le x\le2$, $2x\ge x^2$ (check at $x=1$: $2\ge1$), so $y$ ranges from the lower curve $y=x^2$ to the upper curve $y=2x$.

Answer: $$A=\int_0^2\int_{x^2}^{2x} 1\,dy\,dx$$

Practice 39

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_{\sqrt y}^1 \sqrt{1+x^3}\,dx\,dy$ by reversing the order of integration (the integrand has no elementary antiderivative in $x$ directly, so reversing is necessary).

Solution

Original region: $0\le y\le1,\ \sqrt y\le x\le1$. Reversed: for $0\le x\le1$, $y$ ranges from $0$ to $x^2$ (since $x\ge\sqrt y \iff y\le x^2$).

$$\int_0^1\int_0^{x^2}\sqrt{1+x^3}\,dy\,dx$$

Inner (trivial, integrand doesn't depend on $y$): $\displaystyle\int_0^{x^2}\sqrt{1+x^3}\,dy=x^2\sqrt{1+x^3}$.

Outer: let $u=1+x^3,du=3x^2dx$: $\displaystyle\int_0^1 x^2\sqrt{1+x^3}\,dx=\dfrac13\int_1^2\sqrt u\,du=\dfrac13\cdot\dfrac23\left[u^{3/2}\right]_1^2=\dfrac29(2\sqrt2-1)$.

Answer: $\dfrac{2(2\sqrt2-1)}{9}\approx0.407$.

Practice 40

Explain, using a sketch-in-words of the region, why $\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_0^x f(x,y)\,dy\,dx$ and $\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_y^1 f(x,y)\,dx\,dy$ describe integration over the exact same triangular region, just with the two variables' roles in the bounds swapped.

Solution

The first form describes: for each $x$ from $0$ to $1$, $y$ ranges from $0$ up to the line $y=x$ — this is the triangular region bounded by $y=0$, $x=1$, and $y=x$, i.e. all points below the diagonal line and within the unit square.

The second form describes: for each $y$ from $0$ to $1$, $x$ ranges from the line $x=y$ up to $1$ — this describes exactly the same triangular region (points where $x\ge y$, within the unit square), just scanned in horizontal strips instead of vertical strips. Both integrals sweep out the identical set of $(x,y)$ points, just organised in a different order — which is exactly why reversing the order of integration is always a legitimate operation, provided the new bounds are set up to describe the same region.
