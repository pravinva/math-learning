Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Reversing the order of integration: sketch the region first; re-describe it with the opposite variable as the outer one, then re-derive the (usually different) bounds.
  • Area via double integral: $A=\displaystyle\iint_R 1\,dA$. Volume under a surface: $V=\displaystyle\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA$.
  • Polar double integral: $dA=r\,dr\,d\theta$ (the extra factor of $r$ comes from the area of a thin polar sector) — use whenever the region or integrand involves $x^2+y^2$.
  • Laplace transform: $\mathcal{L}\{f(t)\}=F(s)=\displaystyle\int_0^\infty e^{-st}f(t)\,dt$.
  • Essential transform pairs: $\mathcal L\{1\}=\dfrac1s$; $\mathcal L\{t^n\}=\dfrac{n!}{s^{n+1}}$; $\mathcal L\{e^{at}\}=\dfrac{1}{s-a}$; $\mathcal L\{\sin\omega t\}=\dfrac{\omega}{s^2+\omega^2}$; $\mathcal L\{\cos\omega t\}=\dfrac{s}{s^2+\omega^2}$.
  • Gamma function: $\Gamma(n)=\displaystyle\int_0^\infty t^{n-1}e^{-t}\,dt$; $\Gamma(n+1)=n\Gamma(n)$; for positive integers, $\Gamma(n+1)=n!$; $\Gamma(1/2)=\sqrt\pi$.

0 Reverse Order of Integration

Some double integrals simply cannot be evaluated in the order they're given — the inner integral has no elementary antiderivative. Sketching the region of integration and re-describing it with the opposite variable as the outer one almost always resolves this, since the same region can typically be swept out either "by vertical strips" or "by horizontal strips."

Key idea

$$\int_a^b\int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)} f(x,y)\,dy\,dx = \int_c^d\int_{h_1(y)}^{h_2(y)} f(x,y)\,dx\,dy \quad \text{(same region, re-described)}$$

Worked example

Example. Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_{x}^{1} e^{y^3}\,dy\,dx$ by reversing the order.

Region: $0\le x\le1,\ x\le y\le1$ — equivalently $0\le y\le1,\ 0\le x\le y$.

$\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_0^y e^{y^3}\,dx\,dy=\int_0^1ye^{y^3}\,dy$. Let $u=y^3$: $\dfrac13\int_0^1e^u\,du=\dfrac{e-1}{3}$.

Practice 1

Reverse the order of integration for $\displaystyle\int_0^4\int_{\sqrt x}^{2} f(x,y)\,dy\,dx$.

Solution

Region: $0\le x\le4,\ \sqrt x\le y\le2$, i.e. $0\le y\le2,\ 0\le x\le y^2$.

Answer: $\displaystyle\int_0^2\int_0^{y^2} f(x,y)\,dx\,dy$.

Practice 2

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_{y}^{1} \sin(x^2)\,dx\,dy$ by reversing the order.

Solution

Region: $0\le y\le1,y\le x\le1 \to 0\le x\le1,0\le y\le x$.

$\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_0^x\sin(x^2)\,dy\,dx=\int_0^1 x\sin(x^2)\,dx$. Let $u=x^2$: $\dfrac12\int_0^1\sin u\,du=\dfrac12(1-\cos1)$.

Answer: $\dfrac{1-\cos1}{2}\approx0.2298$.

Practice 3

Reverse the order for $\displaystyle\int_{-1}^1\int_{x^2}^{1} f(x,y)\,dy\,dx$.

Solution

Region: $-1\le x\le1,x^2\le y\le1 \to 0\le y\le1,\ -\sqrt y\le x\le\sqrt y$.

Answer: $\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_{-\sqrt y}^{\sqrt y} f(x,y)\,dx\,dy$.

Practice 4

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^2\int_{x/2}^{1} e^{-y^2}\,dy\,dx$ by reversing the order.

Solution

Region: $0\le x\le2,\ x/2\le y\le1 \to 0\le y\le1,\ 0\le x\le2y$.

$\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_0^{2y}e^{-y^2}\,dx\,dy=\int_0^1 2ye^{-y^2}\,dy$. Let $u=y^2$: $-e^{-u}\Big|_0^1=1-e^{-1}$.

Answer: $1-e^{-1}\approx0.632$.

Practice 5

Reverse the order for $\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_{2y}^{2} f(x,y)\,dx\,dy$.

Solution

Region: $0\le y\le1,\ 2y\le x\le2 \to 0\le x\le2,\ 0\le y\le x/2$.

Answer: $\displaystyle\int_0^2\int_0^{x/2} f(x,y)\,dy\,dx$.

Practice 6

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_{\arcsin y}^{\pi/2}\cos x\sqrt{1+\cos^2x}\,dx\,dy$ by reversing the order (needs $u=\sin x$ substitution after reversing).

Solution

Region: $0\le y\le1,\arcsin y\le x\le\pi/2 \to 0\le x\le\pi/2,0\le y\le\sin x$.

$\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^{\sin x}\cos x\sqrt{1+\cos^2x}\,dy\,dx=\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin x\cos x\sqrt{1+\cos^2x}\,dx$.

Let $u=\cos x,du=-\sin x\,dx$: $-\displaystyle\int_1^0 u\sqrt{1+u^2}\,du=\int_0^1 u\sqrt{1+u^2}\,du$. Let $w=1+u^2$: $\dfrac12\int_1^2\sqrt w\,dw=\dfrac13\left[w^{3/2}\right]_1^2=\dfrac13(2\sqrt2-1)$.

Answer: $\dfrac{2\sqrt2-1}{3}\approx0.609$.

Practice 7

Explain, with a sketch-in-words, why the region for $\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_0^{1-x} f\,dy\,dx$ is a triangle, and give the reversed-order integral.

Solution

The region is $0\le x\le1,\ 0\le y\le1-x$ — bounded below by $y=0$, on the left by $x=0$, and above-right by the line $x+y=1$: a right triangle with vertices $(0,0),(1,0),(0,1)$.

Reversed: $\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_0^{1-y} f(x,y)\,dx\,dy$ (by the symmetry of the triangle in $x$ and $y$).

Practice 8

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_{x^2}^{1} 12xe^{y^2}\,dy\,dx$ by reversing the order.

Solution

Region: $0\le x\le1,x^2\le y\le1 \to 0\le y\le1,0\le x\le\sqrt y$.

$\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_0^{\sqrt y}12xe^{y^2}\,dx\,dy=\int_0^1\left[6x^2e^{y^2}\right]_0^{\sqrt y}dy=\int_0^1 6ye^{y^2}\,dy$.

Let $u=y^2,du=2y\,dy$: $3\displaystyle\int_0^1e^u\,du=3(e-1)$.

Answer: $3(e-1)\approx5.15$.

Practice 9

Reverse the order for $\displaystyle\int_0^2\int_0^{\sqrt{4-x^2}} f(x,y)\,dy\,dx$, and describe the region in words.

Solution

Region: $0\le x\le2,\ 0\le y\le\sqrt{4-x^2}$ — the quarter disk of radius 2 in the first quadrant.

Reversed: $\displaystyle\int_0^2\int_0^{\sqrt{4-y^2}} f(x,y)\,dx\,dy$ (by the symmetry of the quarter circle).

Practice 10

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_{3y}^3 e^{x^2}\,dx\,dy$ by reversing the order.

Solution

Region: $0\le y\le1,\ 3y\le x\le3 \to 0\le x\le3,\ 0\le y\le x/3$.

$\displaystyle\int_0^3\int_0^{x/3}e^{x^2}\,dy\,dx=\int_0^3\dfrac{x}{3}e^{x^2}\,dx$. Let $u=x^2$: $\dfrac16\int_0^9e^u\,du=\dfrac{e^9-1}{6}$.

Answer: $\dfrac{e^9-1}{6}\approx1350.1$.

1 Areas & Volumes via Double Integrals

Setting $f(x,y)=1$ in a double integral over a region $R$ simply adds up the area of every tiny piece of $R$ — giving the total area, a purely geometric double integral. Keeping $f(x,y)$ as the height of a surface gives the volume between that surface and the $xy$-plane over $R$.

Key idea

$$A = \iint_R 1\,dA, \qquad V = \iint_R f(x,y)\,dA$$

Worked example

Example. Find the area between $y=x^2$ and $y=x+2$.

Intersections: $x^2=x+2\implies x^2-x-2=0\implies(x-2)(x+1)=0\implies x=-1,2$.

$A=\displaystyle\int_{-1}^2\int_{x^2}^{x+2}1\,dy\,dx=\int_{-1}^2(x+2-x^2)\,dx=\left[\dfrac{x^2}{2}+2x-\dfrac{x^3}{3}\right]_{-1}^2=\dfrac{9}{2}$.

Practice 11

Find the area of the region bounded by $y=\sqrt x$ and $y=x^3$ (for $x\ge0$).

Solution

Intersections: $\sqrt x=x^3\implies x^{1/2}=x^3\implies x^{1/2}(1-x^{5/2})=0\implies x=0,1$.

On $(0,1)$, $\sqrt x\ge x^3$: $A=\displaystyle\int_0^1(\sqrt x-x^3)\,dx=\left[\dfrac23x^{3/2}-\dfrac{x^4}{4}\right]_0^1=\dfrac23-\dfrac14=\dfrac{5}{12}$.

Practice 12

Find the volume under $z=6-x-y$ over the rectangle $0\le x\le2,0\le y\le1$.

Solution

$V=\displaystyle\int_0^2\int_0^1(6-x-y)\,dy\,dx=\int_0^2\left[6y-xy-\dfrac{y^2}{2}\right]_0^1dx=\int_0^2\left(5.5-x\right)dx$.

$=\left[5.5x-\dfrac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^2=11-2=9$.

Practice 13

Find the area enclosed between $y=x^2-4$ and $y=4-x^2$.

Solution

Intersections: $x^2-4=4-x^2\implies2x^2=8\implies x=\pm2$.

$A=\displaystyle\int_{-2}^2\left[(4-x^2)-(x^2-4)\right]dx=\int_{-2}^2(8-2x^2)\,dx=2\int_0^2(8-2x^2)\,dx$ (even function).

$=2\left[8x-\dfrac{2x^3}{3}\right]_0^2=2\left(16-\dfrac{16}{3}\right)=2\cdot\dfrac{32}{3}=\dfrac{64}{3}$.

Practice 14

Find the volume of the solid under $z=xy$ over the triangular region bounded by $x=0,y=0,x+y=1$.

Solution

$V=\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_0^{1-x}xy\,dy\,dx=\int_0^1 x\left[\dfrac{y^2}{2}\right]_0^{1-x}dx=\int_0^1\dfrac{x(1-x)^2}{2}\,dx$.

$=\dfrac12\int_0^1(x-2x^2+x^3)\,dx=\dfrac12\left(\dfrac12-\dfrac23+\dfrac14\right)=\dfrac12\cdot\dfrac{1}{12}=\dfrac{1}{24}$.

Practice 15

Find the area of the region bounded by $y=e^x, y=e^{-x}$, and $x=1$.

Solution

For $0\le x\le1$, $e^x\ge e^{-x}$: $A=\displaystyle\int_0^1(e^x-e^{-x})\,dx=\left[e^x+e^{-x}\right]_0^1=(e+e^{-1})-2$.

Answer: $e+e^{-1}-2\approx1.086$.

Practice 16

Find the volume under $z=x^2+y^2$ over the square $-1\le x\le1,-1\le y\le1$.

Solution

$V=\displaystyle\int_{-1}^1\int_{-1}^1(x^2+y^2)\,dy\,dx=\int_{-1}^1\left[x^2y+\dfrac{y^3}{3}\right]_{-1}^1dx=\int_{-1}^1\left(2x^2+\dfrac23\right)dx$.

$=\left[\dfrac{2x^3}{3}+\dfrac{2x}{3}\right]_{-1}^1=\left(\dfrac23+\dfrac23\right)-\left(-\dfrac23-\dfrac23\right)=\dfrac83$.

Practice 17

Find the area between $y=\sin x$ and $y=\cos x$ from $x=0$ to $x=\pi/2$.

Solution

Intersection at $x=\pi/4$. For $0\le x\le\pi/4$, $\cos x\ge\sin x$; for $\pi/4\le x\le\pi/2$, reversed.

$A=\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/4}(\cos x-\sin x)dx+\int_{\pi/4}^{\pi/2}(\sin x-\cos x)dx$.

First: $[\sin x+\cos x]_0^{\pi/4}=\sqrt2-1$. Second: $[-\cos x-\sin x]_{\pi/4}^{\pi/2}=(-1)-(-\sqrt2)=\sqrt2-1$.

Answer: $2(\sqrt2-1)\approx0.828$.

Practice 18

Find the volume under $z=4-x^2-y^2$ over the region where $z\ge0$ using Cartesian bounds only (setup and evaluate using symmetry, integrating $y$ first over the appropriate range).

Solution

Region: $x^2+y^2\le4$. For fixed $x\in[-2,2]$, $y$ ranges over $\pm\sqrt{4-x^2}$.

$V=\displaystyle\int_{-2}^2\int_{-\sqrt{4-x^2}}^{\sqrt{4-x^2}}(4-x^2-y^2)\,dy\,dx$. Inner (even in $y$): $2\displaystyle\int_0^{\sqrt{4-x^2}}(4-x^2-y^2)\,dy=2\left[(4-x^2)y-\dfrac{y^3}{3}\right]_0^{\sqrt{4-x^2}}$

$=2\left[(4-x^2)^{3/2}-\dfrac{(4-x^2)^{3/2}}{3}\right]=\dfrac43(4-x^2)^{3/2}$.

Outer: $\displaystyle\int_{-2}^2\dfrac43(4-x^2)^{3/2}dx$; using the standard result $\int_{-a}^a(a^2-x^2)^{3/2}dx=\dfrac{3\pi a^4}{8}$ with $a=2$: $\dfrac{3\pi(16)}{8}=6\pi$.

$V=\dfrac43(6\pi)=8\pi$ (matching the known formula for this paraboloid volume, confirmable via polar coordinates next section).

Practice 19

Find the area of the region bounded by $x=y^2$ and $x=y+2$.

Solution

Intersections (solve for $y$): $y^2=y+2\implies y^2-y-2=0\implies(y-2)(y+1)=0\implies y=-1,2$.

For $-1\le y\le2$, the line $x=y+2$ is to the right of the parabola $x=y^2$ (check $y=0$: $2>0$).

$A=\displaystyle\int_{-1}^2\left[(y+2)-y^2\right]dy=\left[\dfrac{y^2}{2}+2y-\dfrac{y^3}{3}\right]_{-1}^2$.

At $y=2$: $2+4-\tfrac83=6-\tfrac83=\tfrac{10}{3}$. At $y=-1$: $\tfrac12-2+\tfrac13=-\tfrac76$.

Answer: $\dfrac{10}{3}-\left(-\dfrac76\right)=\dfrac{20}{6}+\dfrac{7}{6}=\dfrac{27}{6}=\dfrac92$.

Practice 20

Find the volume under $z=xy^2$ over the region bounded by $y=x$ and $y=x^2$ (for $0\le x\le1$).

Solution

On $(0,1)$, $x\ge x^2$, so $y$ ranges from $x^2$ to $x$.

$V=\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_{x^2}^x xy^2\,dy\,dx=\int_0^1 x\left[\dfrac{y^3}{3}\right]_{x^2}^x dx=\int_0^1\dfrac{x(x^3-x^6)}{3}\,dx=\dfrac13\int_0^1(x^4-x^7)\,dx$.

$=\dfrac13\left(\dfrac15-\dfrac18\right)=\dfrac13\cdot\dfrac{3}{40}=\dfrac{1}{40}$.

2 Double Integrals in Polar Coordinates

Regions bounded by circles, and integrands involving $x^2+y^2$, are usually far easier in polar coordinates. The area element itself changes: a thin polar "rectangle" of width $dr$ and angular width $d\theta$ at radius $r$ has area $r\,dr\,d\theta$, not simply $dr\,d\theta$ — the extra factor of $r$ accounts for the fact that arcs further from the origin sweep out more actual area for the same angular width.

Key idea

$$\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA = \int_\alpha^\beta\int_{r_1(\theta)}^{r_2(\theta)} f(r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta)\,r\,dr\,d\theta$$

Worked example

Example. Find the volume under $z=x^2+y^2$ over the disk $x^2+y^2\le4$.

$V=\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2 r^2\cdot r\,dr\,d\theta=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2 r^3\,dr\,d\theta$.

Inner: $\left[\dfrac{r^4}{4}\right]_0^2=4$. Outer: $\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}4\,d\theta=8\pi$.

Practice 21

Find $\displaystyle\iint_R e^{-(x^2+y^2)}\,dA$ over the whole plane (i.e. $R=\mathbb{R}^2$), a classic result underlying the normal distribution.

Solution

$\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\infty e^{-r^2}r\,dr\,d\theta$. Inner: let $u=r^2$: $\tfrac12\int_0^\infty e^{-u}du=\tfrac12$.

Outer: $\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}\dfrac12\,d\theta=\pi$.

Answer: $\pi$ (this result is the key step in deriving the normal distribution's normalisation constant, met again in Week 10).

Practice 22

Find the volume of the solid bounded above by $z=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ (a cone) and below by $z=0$, over the disk $x^2+y^2\le9$.

Solution

$V=\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^3 r\cdot r\,dr\,d\theta=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^3 r^2\,dr\,d\theta$.

Inner: $\left[\dfrac{r^3}{3}\right]_0^3=9$. Outer: $9(2\pi)=18\pi$.

Practice 23

Find the area of one petal of the region enclosed by (Cartesian) $x^2+y^2\le4$ but only in the first quadrant, using polar coordinates.

Solution

$A=\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^2 r\,dr\,d\theta$. Inner: $\left[\dfrac{r^2}{2}\right]_0^2=2$. Outer: $2\cdot\dfrac{\pi}{2}=\pi$.

Answer: $\pi$ (a quarter of the full disk's area $4\pi$, as expected).

Practice 24

Convert $\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_0^{\sqrt{1-x^2}} (x^2+y^2)\,dy\,dx$ to polar coordinates and evaluate.

Solution

Region: quarter disk, radius 1, first quadrant: $0\le\theta\le\pi/2,0\le r\le1$.

$\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^1 r^2\cdot r\,dr\,d\theta=\int_0^{\pi/2}\left[\dfrac{r^4}{4}\right]_0^1d\theta=\int_0^{\pi/2}\dfrac14\,d\theta=\dfrac{\pi}{8}$.

Practice 25

Find $\displaystyle\iint_R \frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}\,dA$ where $R$ is the annulus $1\le x^2+y^2\le4$.

Solution

$\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}\int_1^2\dfrac1r\cdot r\,dr\,d\theta=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_1^2 1\,dr\,d\theta=\int_0^{2\pi}1\,d\theta=2\pi$.

Practice 26

Find the volume under $z=e^{-(x^2+y^2)}$ over the disk $x^2+y^2\le1$.

Solution

$V=\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 e^{-r^2}r\,dr\,d\theta$. Inner: let $u=r^2$: $\dfrac12\left[-e^{-u}\right]_0^1=\dfrac12(1-e^{-1})$.

Outer: $2\pi\cdot\dfrac12(1-e^{-1})=\pi(1-e^{-1})$.

Answer: $\pi(1-e^{-1})\approx1.986$.

Practice 27

Find the area of the region inside $r=1+\cos\theta$ using a double integral in polar form (setup as $\iint r\,dr\,d\theta$, confirming the direct polar-area formula from MATH142).

Solution

$A=\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{1+\cos\theta} r\,dr\,d\theta=\int_0^{2\pi}\dfrac{(1+\cos\theta)^2}{2}\,d\theta$.

Expanding and integrating (as in MATH142 Week 5): $=\dfrac{3\pi}{2}$ — confirming the direct formula $A=\tfrac12\int r^2d\theta$ is simply this double integral with the inner integration already carried out.

Practice 28

Convert $\displaystyle\int_{-2}^2\int_0^{\sqrt{4-x^2}} (x^2+y^2)^{3/2}\,dy\,dx$ to polar and evaluate.

Solution

Region: upper half disk, radius 2: $0\le\theta\le\pi,0\le r\le2$.

$\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi}\int_0^2 r^3\cdot r\,dr\,d\theta=\int_0^{\pi}\left[\dfrac{r^5}{5}\right]_0^2d\theta=\int_0^{\pi}\dfrac{32}{5}\,d\theta=\dfrac{32\pi}{5}$.

Practice 29

Find the volume common to the cylinder $x^2+y^2\le4$ and under the plane $z=x+3$ (over the region where $z\ge0$, which holds throughout since $x\ge-2$).

Solution

$V=\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2(r\cos\theta+3)r\,dr\,d\theta=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2(r^2\cos\theta+3r)\,dr\,d\theta$.

Inner: $\left[\dfrac{r^3}{3}\cos\theta+\dfrac{3r^2}{2}\right]_0^2=\dfrac83\cos\theta+6$.

Outer: $\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}\left(\dfrac83\cos\theta+6\right)d\theta=0+12\pi=12\pi$ (the cosine term integrates to zero over a full period).

Practice 30

Explain why the extra factor of $r$ in $dA=r\,dr\,d\theta$ is necessary, using a geometric argument about the area of a thin polar "rectangle."

Solution

A thin polar region spanning radius $r$ to $r+dr$ and angle $\theta$ to $\theta+d\theta$ is approximately a rectangle with one side of length $dr$ (radial) and the other of length $r\,d\theta$ (the arc length swept by angle $d\theta$ at radius $r$ — NOT simply $d\theta$, since arc length scales with radius). Multiplying these two side lengths gives area $\approx r\,dr\,d\theta$ — the factor of $r$ directly reflects that the same angular width $d\theta$ corresponds to a longer arc (and hence a bigger area) further from the origin.

3 Introduction to Laplace Transform & the Gamma Function

The Laplace transform converts a function of time, $f(t)$, into a function of a new variable $s$, via an improper integral. Its real power (explored fully next week) is turning differential equations into ordinary algebra — for now, the focus is computing transforms directly from the definition, and building a table of standard pairs.

The Gamma function generalises the factorial to non-integer (and even complex) values, and appears naturally when computing Laplace transforms of $t^n$ for non-integer $n$, among many other places in engineering mathematics.

Key idea

$$\mathcal L\{f(t)\}=F(s)=\int_0^\infty e^{-st}f(t)\,dt, \qquad \Gamma(n)=\int_0^\infty t^{n-1}e^{-t}\,dt, \quad \Gamma(n+1)=n\Gamma(n)$$

Worked example

Example 1. Find $\mathcal L\{e^{3t}\}$ from the definition.

$\displaystyle\int_0^\infty e^{-st}e^{3t}\,dt=\int_0^\infty e^{-(s-3)t}\,dt=\left[\dfrac{-e^{-(s-3)t}}{s-3}\right]_0^\infty=\dfrac{1}{s-3}$ (for $s>3$).

Example 2. Evaluate $\Gamma(5)$ using the factorial connection, and $\Gamma(7/2)$ using the recursion and $\Gamma(1/2)=\sqrt\pi$.

$\Gamma(5)=4!=24$. $\Gamma(7/2)=\dfrac52\Gamma(5/2)=\dfrac52\cdot\dfrac32\Gamma(3/2)=\dfrac52\cdot\dfrac32\cdot\dfrac12\Gamma(1/2)=\dfrac{15}{8}\sqrt\pi$.

Practice 31

Find $\mathcal L\{t\}$ directly from the definition using integration by parts.

Solution

$\displaystyle\int_0^\infty te^{-st}\,dt$; $u=t,dv=e^{-st}dt$: $v=-\dfrac{e^{-st}}{s}$.

$=\left[-\dfrac{te^{-st}}{s}\right]_0^\infty+\dfrac1s\displaystyle\int_0^\infty e^{-st}dt=0+\dfrac1s\cdot\dfrac1s=\dfrac{1}{s^2}$.

Practice 32

Find $\mathcal L\{\cos(2t)\}$ directly from the definition (using the Week 1 IBP "solve for I" trick from MATH142).

Solution

$I=\displaystyle\int_0^\infty e^{-st}\cos2t\,dt$. Applying by parts twice and solving for $I$ (as in the MATH142 $e^x\sin x$ pattern) gives $I=\dfrac{s}{s^2+4}$.

Answer: $\mathcal L\{\cos2t\}=\dfrac{s}{s^2+4}$ (matching the standard table entry with $\omega=2$).

Practice 33

Using $\Gamma(n+1)=n!$ for integers, evaluate $\Gamma(6)$ and $\Gamma(1)$.

Solution

$\Gamma(6)=5!=120$. $\Gamma(1)=0!=1$.

Practice 34

Evaluate $\Gamma(9/2)$ using the recursion and $\Gamma(1/2)=\sqrt\pi$.

Solution

$\Gamma(9/2)=\dfrac72\Gamma(7/2)=\dfrac72\cdot\dfrac{15}{8}\sqrt\pi=\dfrac{105}{16}\sqrt\pi$.

Practice 35

Use the definition of the Laplace transform to find $\mathcal L\{e^{-2t}t\}$ directly (do not use the shift theorem, which arrives next week).

Solution

$\displaystyle\int_0^\infty e^{-st}te^{-2t}\,dt=\int_0^\infty te^{-(s+2)t}\,dt$.

By parts (as in the $\mathcal L\{t\}$ example, with $s+2$ replacing $s$): $=\dfrac{1}{(s+2)^2}$.

Practice 36

Express $\displaystyle\int_0^\infty x^4e^{-x}\,dx$ in terms of the Gamma function and evaluate.

Solution

This matches $\Gamma(n)=\int_0^\infty t^{n-1}e^{-t}dt$ with $n-1=4\implies n=5$.

Answer: $\Gamma(5)=4!=24$.

Practice 37

Show, using integration by parts on the definition, that $\Gamma(n+1)=n\Gamma(n)$ (the fundamental recursion).

Solution

$\Gamma(n+1)=\displaystyle\int_0^\infty t^ne^{-t}\,dt$. Let $u=t^n,dv=e^{-t}dt$: $du=nt^{n-1}dt,v=-e^{-t}$.

$=\left[-t^ne^{-t}\right]_0^\infty+n\displaystyle\int_0^\infty t^{n-1}e^{-t}\,dt=0+n\Gamma(n)$.

Answer: $\Gamma(n+1)=n\Gamma(n)$, confirmed. (The boundary term vanishes since $t^ne^{-t}\to0$ as $t\to\infty$, and equals $0$ at $t=0$ for $n>0$.)

Practice 38

Use the definition of the Laplace transform to find $\mathcal L\{t^2\}$ (extend the by-parts pattern from $\mathcal L\{t\}$, or use the general formula $\mathcal L\{t^n\}=n!/s^{n+1}$ and verify with $n=2$).

Solution

Using the general Laplace/Gamma connection: $\mathcal L\{t^n\}=\displaystyle\int_0^\infty t^ne^{-st}dt$. Substituting $u=st$: $=\dfrac{1}{s^{n+1}}\displaystyle\int_0^\infty u^ne^{-u}\,du=\dfrac{\Gamma(n+1)}{s^{n+1}}=\dfrac{n!}{s^{n+1}}$.

For $n=2$: $\mathcal L\{t^2\}=\dfrac{2!}{s^3}=\dfrac{2}{s^3}$.

Practice 39

Using the substitution $u=st$ (as in the general $t^n$ derivation above), express $\mathcal L\{t^{3/2}\}$ in terms of the Gamma function, then evaluate using $\Gamma(5/2)=\dfrac34\sqrt\pi$.

Solution

$\mathcal L\{t^{3/2}\}=\dfrac{\Gamma(5/2)}{s^{5/2}}$ (using $n=3/2$, so $n+1=5/2$).

Answer: $\dfrac{\tfrac34\sqrt\pi}{s^{5/2}}=\dfrac{3\sqrt\pi}{4s^{5/2}}$.

Practice 40

Explain why the Laplace transform integral $\int_0^\infty e^{-st}f(t)\,dt$ requires $s$ to be sufficiently large (or restricted in some way) for convergence, using $f(t)=e^{3t}$ as a specific example.

Solution

For $f(t)=e^{3t}$, the integrand is $e^{-st}e^{3t}=e^{-(s-3)t}$. This integral converges (as $t\to\infty$) only if the exponent's coefficient is negative, i.e. $s-3>0\implies s>3$. If $s\le3$, the integrand doesn't decay (or actually grows) as $t\to\infty$, and the improper integral diverges. This is why every Laplace transform table entry comes with a restriction on $s$ (e.g. "$s>a$" for $\mathcal L\{e^{at}\}$) — the transform is only defined where the defining integral actually converges.
