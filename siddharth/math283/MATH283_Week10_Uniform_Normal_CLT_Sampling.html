The Uniform & Normal Distributions, the Central Limit Theorem & Sampling Distributions

Two more continuous distributions, then the single most important theorem in all of statistics. The Normal distribution needs its own standardisation technique to use with a single shared table; the Central Limit Theorem explains why so many real-world quantities end up approximately Normal, and why sample means behave predictably even when the underlying population doesn't. Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Uniform distribution $X\sim U(a,b)$: $f(x)=\dfrac{1}{b-a}$ for $a\le x\le b$; $E(X)=\dfrac{a+b}{2}$; $\text{Var}(X)=\dfrac{(b-a)^2}{12}$.
  • Standardising a Normal RV: $Z=\dfrac{X-\mu}{\sigma}$, giving the standard Normal $Z\sim N(0,1)$, whose probabilities are read from tables (or software).
  • Central Limit Theorem (CLT): for a sample of size $n$ from any population with mean $\mu$ and finite variance $\sigma^2$, the sample mean $\bar X$ is approximately $N\!\left(\mu,\dfrac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)$ for large enough $n$ (rule of thumb: $n\ge30$) — regardless of the shape of the original population.
  • Standard error of the mean: $\sigma_{\bar X}=\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}$ — the spread of the sampling distribution of $\bar X$ shrinks as $n$ grows, at a rate of $1/\sqrt n$.
  • Sampling distribution of $\bar X$ (standardised): $Z=\dfrac{\bar X-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt n}$.

0 The Uniform Distribution

The Uniform distribution describes a continuous random variable equally likely to take any value within a fixed interval $[a,b]$ — the "flattest possible" continuous distribution, with constant density throughout its range and zero elsewhere.

Practice 6

Explain why the Uniform distribution's density function $f(x)=1/(b-a)$ must be constant (rather than varying) across its support, connecting this to the definition of "uniformly at random."

Solution

"Uniformly at random" means every sub-interval of a given length within $[a,b]$ is equally likely to contain the random value, regardless of where that sub-interval is located. Since probability corresponds to area under $f(x)$, having equal probability for equal-length intervals anywhere in $[a,b]$ requires $f(x)$ to have the exact same height everywhere — any variation in height would make some regions more likely than others of the same width, contradicting the definition of uniformity.

Practice 7

Verify that $f(x)=1/(b-a)$ integrates to exactly 1 over $[a,b]$, confirming it's a valid density.

Solution

$\displaystyle\int_a^b\frac{1}{b-a}\,dx=\frac{1}{b-a}\big[x\big]_a^b=\frac{b-a}{b-a}=1$.

Practice 18

Explain, using the standardisation formula, why $Z=\dfrac{X-\mu}{\sigma}$ always has mean 0 and standard deviation 1, regardless of the original $\mu$ and $\sigma$ of $X$.

Solution

$E(Z)=E\!\left(\dfrac{X-\mu}{\sigma}\right)=\dfrac{E(X)-\mu}{\sigma}=\dfrac{\mu-\mu}{\sigma}=0$ (using linearity of expectation, and $E(X)=\mu$ by definition).

$\text{Var}(Z)=\text{Var}\!\left(\dfrac{X-\mu}{\sigma}\right)=\dfrac{1}{\sigma^2}\text{Var}(X-\mu)=\dfrac{1}{\sigma^2}\text{Var}(X)=\dfrac{\sigma^2}{\sigma^2}=1$ (subtracting a constant doesn't change variance; dividing by $\sigma$ divides variance by $\sigma^2$).

Answer: the subtraction of $\mu$ recentres the distribution to mean 0; the division by $\sigma$ rescales it to standard deviation 1 — this works for any Normal (or indeed any) random variable, which is exactly why one single standard Normal table suffices for every possible Normal distribution.

2 The Central Limit Theorem

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) is arguably the single most important result underlying practical statistics: no matter what shape the original population's distribution has (skewed, bimodal, anything with finite variance), the distribution of the sample mean $\bar X$ becomes approximately Normal as the sample size $n$ grows — and it becomes a very good approximation even for moderate $n$ (a common rule of thumb is $n\ge30$).

Why this matters. It means you can apply Normal-based methods (confidence intervals, hypothesis tests) to sample means drawn from almost any population, as long as the sample is reasonably large — a huge practical simplification.

Key idea

$$\bar X \ \dot\sim\ N\!\left(\mu,\frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right) \ \text{(large } n\text{)}, \qquad Z=\frac{\bar X-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt n}$$

Worked example

Example. A population has $\mu=50,\sigma=8$ (shape unspecified). A sample of $n=16$ is taken. Find $P(\bar X>52)$.

$\sigma_{\bar X}=\dfrac{8}{\sqrt{16}}=2$. $Z=\dfrac{52-50}{2}=1.0$.

$P(\bar X>52)=P(Z>1)\approx1-0.8413=0.1587$.

Practice 24

Explain why the CLT allows you to treat $\bar X$ as approximately Normal even when the underlying population (e.g. lifetimes, which are often right-skewed) is clearly NOT Normal, provided the sample size is large enough.

Solution

The CLT is a statement specifically about the sampling distribution of the mean, not about the original population itself. Averaging together many independent observations (even from a skewed or unusual population) tends to "smooth out" the individual quirks and extremes of the original distribution — a very high value in one observation tends to be balanced out by lower values elsewhere in the same sample, and this averaging effect happens more thoroughly as $n$ grows. The theorem guarantees that regardless of the original population's shape, this averaging process makes the distribution of $\bar X$ increasingly Normal-shaped as $n$ increases, as long as the population has a well-defined, finite variance.

Practice 25

Solution

$\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}\le1.5 \implies \sqrt n\ge\dfrac{12}{1.5}=8 \implies n\ge64$.

Answer: $n\ge64$.

Practice 26

Explain why the standard error of the mean, $\sigma/\sqrt n$, decreases as $n$ increases, but at a progressively slower rate (e.g. quadrupling $n$ only halves the standard error, rather than quartering it).

Solution

Since the standard error is proportional to $1/\sqrt n$ (a square-root relationship, not a direct 1-to-1 relationship), increasing $n$ by a factor of $k$ only reduces the standard error by a factor of $\sqrt k$, not by a factor of $k$ itself. For example, to halve the standard error, you'd need to quadruple $n$ ($\sqrt4=2$); to reduce it to a third, you'd need $9\times$ the sample size. This "diminishing returns" pattern is why, in practice, doubling an already-large sample size often isn't worth the extra cost — the resulting precision improvement is much smaller than the doubled sample size might naively suggest.

Practice 29

Solution

For a single student's score ($X$ itself), the Normal distribution is NOT appropriate, since the underlying population is known to be skewed — the CLT says nothing about the distribution of individual observations, only about the sampling distribution of the mean.

For the mean of 40 students ($\bar X$), the Normal distribution IS appropriate (by the CLT), since $n=40$ is reasonably large (above the common rule-of-thumb threshold of 30) — even though the original population is skewed, the sampling distribution of $\bar X$ will be approximately Normal.

3 Sampling Distributions

A sampling distribution describes how a statistic (like $\bar X$, or a sample proportion) varies from sample to sample, if you imagine repeating the sampling process over and over. Understanding the sampling distribution of a statistic is the foundation for everything in the rest of the course — confidence intervals and hypothesis tests are both built directly on knowing (or approximating) the sampling distribution of the statistic being used.

Key idea

$$\bar X \sim N\!\left(\mu,\frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right) \ \text{(exactly, if population is Normal; approx. otherwise via CLT)}$$

Worked example

Example. A Normal population has $\mu=60,\sigma=9$. For samples of size $n=9$, find $P(58<\bar X<63)$ (note: population is exactly Normal here, so the result is exact for any $n$, not just large $n$).

$\sigma_{\bar X}=\dfrac{9}{\sqrt9}=3$. $Z_1=\dfrac{58-60}{3}=-0.667$; $Z_2=\dfrac{63-60}{3}=1.0$.

A Normal population has $\mu=30,\sigma=6$. For $n=4$ (small sample, but population is exactly Normal so this is valid), find $P(\bar X>33)$.

$\sigma_{\bar X}=\dfrac{6}{\sqrt4}=3$. $Z=\dfrac{33-30}{3}=1.0$.

Practice 32

Explain the key difference between the requirement for using $\bar X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2/n)$ EXACTLY (for any sample size) versus using it as an APPROXIMATION (only for large $n$, via the CLT).

Solution

If the original population itself is already exactly Normal, then $\bar X$ is exactly Normal for any sample size $n$ (even $n=2$) — this is a special, stronger mathematical property of the Normal distribution (sums/averages of Normal random variables are themselves exactly Normal).

If the original population is NOT Normal (any other shape), $\bar X$ is only approximately Normal, and this approximation only becomes good when $n$ is sufficiently large (the CLT) — for small $n$ from a non-Normal population, treating $\bar X$ as Normal could give a poor, unreliable approximation.

$P(|\bar X-80|>3)=P(\bar X<77)+P(\bar X>83)$. $Z=\dfrac{3}{1.697}\approx1.768$.

Practice 35

Explain why the sampling distribution of $\bar X$ becomes progressively narrower (more concentrated around $\mu$) as $n$ increases, connecting this directly to the formula $\sigma_{\bar X}=\sigma/\sqrt n$ and to the intuitive idea of "averaging reduces variability."

Solution

Averaging several independent observations together tends to cancel out random fluctuations — an unusually high individual observation tends to be balanced by an unusually low one elsewhere in the same sample, so the average is typically closer to the true population mean than any single observation would be. As $n$ increases, there are more opportunities for such fluctuations to cancel out, making $\bar X$ increasingly reliable (concentrated) as an estimate of $\mu$.

This intuition matches the formula exactly: $\sigma_{\bar X}=\sigma/\sqrt n$ shows the standard deviation of $\bar X$ shrinking as $n$ grows, quantifying precisely how much this "averaging out" effect reduces variability for any given sample size.

Practice 39

Explain why, for a Normal population, the sampling distribution of $\bar X$ is exactly Normal even for a sample as small as $n=2$, while for a non-Normal (e.g. strongly skewed) population, $n=2$ would give a very poor Normal approximation for $\bar X$.

Solution

A special mathematical property of the Normal distribution is that any linear combination (including a simple average) of independent Normal random variables is itself exactly Normal, regardless of how many are combined — this holds even for just two observations. Non-Normal distributions don't have this special closure property; averaging just two values from a strongly skewed population still largely reflects that skewness, since there aren't enough independent observations yet for the "averaging out" effect (which the CLT relies on) to have taken hold. The CLT's guarantee of approximate Normality only kicks in gradually as $n$ grows, whereas the Normal population's special property gives exact Normality immediately, at any $n$.
