Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Discrete RV: $E(X)=\displaystyle\sum x\,p(x)$; $\text{Var}(X)=E(X^2)-[E(X)]^2=\displaystyle\sum x^2p(x)-[E(X)]^2$.
  • Binomial distribution $X\sim B(n,p)$: $P(X=x)=\binom{n}{x}p^x(1-p)^{n-x}$; $E(X)=np$; $\text{Var}(X)=np(1-p)$.
  • Poisson distribution $X\sim\text{Poisson}(\lambda)$: $P(X=x)=\dfrac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^x}{x!}$; $E(X)=\text{Var}(X)=\lambda$ — the Poisson approximates the Binomial when $n$ is large and $p$ is small ($\lambda=np$).
  • Continuous RV: $P(a\le X\le b)=\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$; $E(X)=\displaystyle\int xf(x)\,dx$; total area under $f(x)$ over its support equals 1.
  • Exponential distribution $X\sim\text{Exp}(\lambda)$: $f(x)=\lambda e^{-\lambda x}\ (x\ge0)$; $P(X>x)=e^{-\lambda x}$; $E(X)=1/\lambda$; $\text{Var}(X)=1/\lambda^2$; models the waiting time between Poisson events, and has the memoryless property.

0 Discrete Random Variables — Expected Value & Variance

A discrete random variable's probability distribution assigns a probability to each possible value it can take. The expected value $E(X)$ is a probability-weighted average — the long-run average value if the random experiment were repeated many times. Variance measures the spread of that distribution around its expected value.

Key idea

$$E(X)=\sum x\,p(x), \qquad \text{Var}(X)=E(X^2)-[E(X)]^2, \qquad \sum p(x)=1$$

Worked example

Example. A discrete RV has $P(X=0)=0.2,P(X=1)=0.5,P(X=2)=0.3$. Find $E(X)$ and $\text{Var}(X)$.

$E(X)=0(0.2)+1(0.5)+2(0.3)=0+0.5+0.6=1.1$.

$E(X^2)=0(0.2)+1(0.5)+4(0.3)=0.5+1.2=1.7$. $\text{Var}(X)=1.7-1.1^2=1.7-1.21=0.49$.

Practice 1

A discrete RV has $P(X=1)=0.3,P(X=2)=0.4,P(X=3)=0.3$. Find $E(X)$.

Solution

$E(X)=1(0.3)+2(0.4)+3(0.3)=0.3+0.8+0.9=2.0$.

Practice 2

For the RV above, find $\text{Var}(X)$.

Solution

$E(X^2)=1(0.3)+4(0.4)+9(0.3)=0.3+1.6+2.7=4.6$.

$\text{Var}(X)=4.6-2.0^2=4.6-4=0.6$.

Practice 3

A game costs \$5 to play. You win \$20 with probability 0.1, \$0 otherwise. Find the expected net profit (winnings minus cost).

Solution

$E(\text{winnings})=20(0.1)+0(0.9)=2$.

Expected net profit $=E(\text{winnings})-5=2-5=-3$ (a \$3 expected loss per play).

Practice 4

Find the missing probability $p$ if a discrete RV has $P(X=0)=0.15,P(X=1)=p,P(X=2)=0.35,P(X=3)=0.20$.

Solution

Total probability must sum to 1: $0.15+p+0.35+0.20=1 \implies p=1-0.70=0.30$.

Practice 5

A discrete RV representing the number of defects in a batch has $P(X=0)=0.5,P(X=1)=0.3,P(X=2)=0.15,P(X=3)=0.05$. Find $E(X)$ and interpret it in context.

Solution

$E(X)=0(0.5)+1(0.3)+2(0.15)+3(0.05)=0.3+0.3+0.15=0.75$.

Interpretation: on average, a batch is expected to contain 0.75 defects — a long-run average over many batches, even though no single batch can actually have a fractional number of defects.

Practice 6

Verify that $\text{Var}(X)\ge0$ always, using the formula $\text{Var}(X)=E[(X-\mu)^2]$ directly (rather than the computational shortcut), explaining why this alternate form makes non-negativity obvious.

Solution

$\text{Var}(X)=E[(X-\mu)^2]=\displaystyle\sum(x-\mu)^2p(x)$. Since $(x-\mu)^2\ge0$ for every possible value $x$ (a square is never negative), and $p(x)\ge0$ always, every single term in this sum is non-negative — so the sum itself must be non-negative.

This makes it immediately obvious why variance can never be negative, whereas the computational shortcut $E(X^2)-[E(X)]^2$ (used for calculation speed) doesn't make this fact as visually apparent, even though it's mathematically equivalent.

Practice 7

A random variable $X$ has $E(X)=10$ and $\text{Var}(X)=4$. Find $E(2X+3)$ and $\text{Var}(2X+3)$.

Solution

$E(2X+3)=2E(X)+3=2(10)+3=23$ (linearity of expectation).

$\text{Var}(2X+3)=2^2\text{Var}(X)=4(4)=16$ (variance scales by the square of the multiplier; adding a constant doesn't affect variance).

Practice 8

A spinner has three outcomes: 1 (probability 0.5), 5 (probability 0.3), 10 (probability 0.2). Find $E(X)$ and the standard deviation of $X$.

Solution

$E(X)=1(0.5)+5(0.3)+10(0.2)=0.5+1.5+2.0=4.0$.

$E(X^2)=1(0.5)+25(0.3)+100(0.2)=0.5+7.5+20=28$.

$\text{Var}(X)=28-16=12$. $\text{SD}(X)=\sqrt{12}\approx3.46$.

Practice 9

Explain why $E(X^2)\ne[E(X)]^2$ in general, using the variance formula to justify your answer, and state the one special case where they ARE equal.

Solution

Rearranging $\text{Var}(X)=E(X^2)-[E(X)]^2$ gives $E(X^2)=\text{Var}(X)+[E(X)]^2$. Since variance is generally a positive number (whenever $X$ actually has some spread/randomness), $E(X^2)$ must be strictly greater than $[E(X)]^2$ in general.

Special case: they are equal exactly when $\text{Var}(X)=0$ — i.e. when $X$ is not actually random at all, but a constant (every outcome is the same fixed value with probability 1).

Practice 10

An insurance policy pays out \$10,000 with probability 0.02 (a claim) and \$0 otherwise. If the company wants an expected profit of \$50 per policy, what premium should it charge?

Solution

$E(\text{payout})=10000(0.02)+0(0.98)=200$.

Let premium $=P$. Expected profit $=P-E(\text{payout})=P-200=50 \implies P=250$.

Answer: the company should charge a premium of \$250.

1 The Binomial Distribution

The Binomial distribution counts the number of "successes" in $n$ independent, identical trials, each with the same success probability $p$ (a Bernoulli trial). It's the natural model whenever you're counting occurrences out of a fixed number of attempts — defective items in a batch, correct answers on a multiple-choice test, and so on.

Key idea

$$P(X=x)=\binom{n}{x}p^x(1-p)^{n-x}, \qquad E(X)=np, \qquad \text{Var}(X)=np(1-p)$$

Worked example

Example. A machine produces defective items with probability 0.1. In a sample of 8 items, find $P(X=2)$ and $E(X)$.

$P(X=2)=\binom{8}{2}(0.1)^2(0.9)^6=28(0.01)(0.5314)\approx0.1488$.

$E(X)=np=8(0.1)=0.8$.

Practice 11

A fair coin is flipped 10 times. Find $P(X=6)$ where $X$ is the number of heads.

Solution

$P(X=6)=\binom{10}{6}(0.5)^6(0.5)^4=210(0.5)^{10}=\dfrac{210}{1024}\approx0.2051$.

Practice 12

For $X\sim B(10,0.5)$ (coin flips above), find $E(X)$ and $\text{Var}(X)$.

Solution

$E(X)=10(0.5)=5$. $\text{Var}(X)=10(0.5)(0.5)=2.5$.

Practice 13

A quality control inspector checks 10 items; the defect probability per item is 0.3. Find $P(X\le2)$ (at most 2 defective).

Solution

$P(X\le2)=P(0)+P(1)+P(2)$.

$P(0)=\binom{10}{0}(0.3)^0(0.7)^{10}=0.7^{10}\approx0.0282$.

$P(1)=\binom{10}{1}(0.3)(0.7)^9=10(0.3)(0.0404)\approx0.1211$.

$P(2)=\binom{10}{2}(0.3)^2(0.7)^8=45(0.09)(0.0576)\approx0.2335$.

Answer: $P(X\le2)\approx0.0282+0.1211+0.2335=0.3828$.

$p=1/4=0.25$, $n=5,x=3$. $P(X=3)=\binom53(0.25)^3(0.75)^2=10(0.015625)(0.5625)\approx0.0879$.

$\text{Var}(X)=5(0.25)(0.75)=0.9375$.

Practice 16

A factory ships items in boxes of 20. If the defect rate is 5%, find the probability a box has zero defects.

$P(X=0)=\binom{20}{0}(0.05)^0(0.95)^{20}=0.95^{20}\approx0.3585$.

Practice 17

For the same box of 20 items (5% defect rate), find the probability of at least 1 defect, using the complement rule.

$P(X\ge1)=1-P(X=0)=1-0.3585=0.6415$.

Practice 18

A basketball player has a free-throw success rate of 80%. In 6 attempts, find $P(X=5)$ (exactly 5 successes).

Solution

$P(X=5)=\binom65(0.8)^5(0.2)^1=6(0.32768)(0.2)\approx0.3932$.

Practice 19

Explain why the Binomial distribution requires the trials to be both "independent" and have a "constant" success probability $p$, using a concrete counter-example where sampling without replacement from a small finite population would violate this.

Solution

Independence means the outcome of one trial doesn't affect the probability of success on any other trial; a constant $p$ means that probability doesn't change from trial to trial.

Counter-example: drawing balls without replacement from a bag of 10 (say 3 red, 7 blue) to count "number of red balls drawn in 5 draws" is NOT binomial — after drawing a red ball, there are fewer red balls left in the bag, so the probability of drawing red on the next draw genuinely changes (decreases). This situation instead requires the Hypergeometric distribution, a related but distinct model that accounts for the changing probabilities from sampling without replacement in a finite population. Binomial only applies when trials are independent with a truly constant $p$ — typically valid for sampling with replacement, or sampling a small fraction from a very large (effectively infinite) population.

Practice 20

A binomial RV has $E(X)=12$ and $\text{Var}(X)=4.8$. Find $n$ and $p$.

Solution

$np=12$ and $np(1-p)=4.8$.

Divide: $1-p=\dfrac{4.8}{12}=0.4 \implies p=0.6$.

$n=\dfrac{12}{0.6}=20$.

Answer: $n=20,p=0.6$.

2 The Poisson Process & Poisson Distribution

The Poisson distribution counts the number of events occurring in a fixed interval of time or space, when events happen independently at a constant average rate. It's the natural model for rare, randomly-timed occurrences: customer arrivals, radioactive decays, typos per page.

Connection to the Binomial. The Poisson distribution arises as the limiting case of the Binomial when $n\to\infty$ and $p\to0$ while $np=\lambda$ stays fixed — making it a good approximation for Binomial situations with large $n$ and small $p$.

Key idea

$$P(X=x)=\frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^x}{x!}, \qquad E(X)=\text{Var}(X)=\lambda$$

Worked example

Example. A call centre receives an average of 3 calls per minute (Poisson). Find $P(X=5)$ in a given minute, and $P(X=0)$.

$P(X=5)=\dfrac{e^{-3}3^5}{5!}=\dfrac{(0.0498)(243)}{120}\approx0.1008$.

$P(X=0)=e^{-3}\approx0.0498$.

Practice 21

A website receives an average of 4 visits per minute (Poisson). Find $P(X=2)$.

Solution

$P(X=2)=\dfrac{e^{-4}4^2}{2!}=\dfrac{(0.0183)(16)}{2}\approx0.1465$.

Interpretation: there's only about a 1.8% chance of zero visits in a given minute.

Solution

$\lambda=np=200(0.015)=3$.

Practice 27

Explain why the Poisson distribution is a good approximation to the Binomial specifically when $n$ is large and $p$ is small, in terms of what stays fixed in the limiting process.

Solution

As $n\to\infty$ and $p\to0$ simultaneously, the individual trials become both extremely numerous and extremely unlikely to succeed — but their product, $\lambda=np$ (the expected number of successes), can be held fixed at some reasonable, finite value. This matches exactly the physical setup of a Poisson process: instead of thinking of "many rare independent trials," you think of a continuous stream of opportunities for a rare event to occur, with the total expected count over the interval being $\lambda$. The mathematical limit of the Binomial formula, taken this way, converges exactly to the Poisson formula — which is why the approximation works well precisely when $n$ is large and $p$ is small (a large number of low-probability "trials").

Practice 28

Machine breakdowns occur at an average rate of 0.5 per week (Poisson). Find the probability of no breakdowns in a given 4-week month.

Solution

Adjusted $\lambda$ for 4 weeks: $\lambda=0.5\times4=2$.

$P(X=0)=e^{-2}\approx0.1353$.

Practice 29

A Poisson RV has $\text{Var}(X)=7$. Find $P(X=7)$ (using $\lambda=\text{Var}(X)$ directly, since for Poisson, mean equals variance).

Solution

$\lambda=7$ (since $E(X)=\text{Var}(X)=\lambda$ for Poisson).

$P(X=7)=\dfrac{e^{-7}7^7}{7!}=\dfrac{(0.000912)(823543)}{5040}\approx0.149$.

Practice 30

Explain why it doesn't make sense to ask for the "standard deviation" of a Poisson RV without first specifying $\lambda$, and state the formula for it in terms of $\lambda$.

Solution

Since a Poisson distribution's entire shape (and hence both its mean and variance) is completely determined by the single parameter $\lambda$, asking about its standard deviation without specifying $\lambda$ is like asking "how tall is a rectangle" without giving its dimensions — the answer is $\sqrt\lambda$, but this number is meaningless without knowing which specific $\lambda$ (which Poisson distribution) is being discussed.

Formula: $\text{SD}(X)=\sqrt{\text{Var}(X)}=\sqrt\lambda$.

3 Continuous Random Variables & the Exponential Distribution

A continuous random variable's probability density function $f(x)$ doesn't give probabilities directly (unlike the discrete case) — instead, the area under $f(x)$ over an interval gives the probability of landing in that interval. The Exponential distribution models the waiting time until the next event in a Poisson process — if events occur at Poisson rate $\lambda$, the time between consecutive events is Exponential$(\lambda)$.

Memoryless property. $P(X>s+t\,|\,X>s)=P(X>t)$ — if you've already waited $s$ time units with no event, the remaining waiting time distribution is exactly the same as if you'd just started waiting. This unusual (and often counterintuitive) property is unique to the Exponential distribution among continuous distributions.

Key idea

$$f(x)=\lambda e^{-\lambda x}\ (x\ge0), \qquad P(X>x)=e^{-\lambda x}, \qquad E(X)=\frac1\lambda, \qquad \text{Var}(X)=\frac{1}{\lambda^2}$$

Worked example

Example. The time between customer arrivals at a shop is Exponential with mean 5 minutes. Find $P(X>8)$ and $P(X<3)$.

$\lambda=1/5=0.2$. $P(X>8)=e^{-0.2(8)}=e^{-1.6}\approx0.2019$.

$P(X<3)=1-e^{-0.2(3)}=1-e^{-0.6}\approx1-0.5488=0.4512$.

Practice 35

Verify that the Exponential density $f(x)=\lambda e^{-\lambda x}$ integrates to exactly 1 over $[0,\infty)$, confirming it's a valid probability density function.

Solution

$\displaystyle\int_0^\infty \lambda e^{-\lambda x}\,dx = \lambda\left[\frac{-e^{-\lambda x}}{\lambda}\right]_0^\infty = \lambda\left(0-\left(\frac{-1}{\lambda}\right)\right)=\lambda\cdot\frac1\lambda=1$.

Answer: confirmed, total probability is exactly 1 for any $\lambda>0$.

Practice 36

Derive $E(X)=1/\lambda$ for the Exponential distribution using integration by parts.

Solution

$E(X)=\displaystyle\int_0^\infty x\lambda e^{-\lambda x}\,dx$. Let $u=x,dv=\lambda e^{-\lambda x}dx$: $du=dx,v=-e^{-\lambda x}$.

$=\left[-xe^{-\lambda x}\right]_0^\infty+\displaystyle\int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda x}\,dx=0+\left[\dfrac{-e^{-\lambda x}}{\lambda}\right]_0^\infty=0+\dfrac1\lambda=\dfrac1\lambda$.

Answer: confirmed, $E(X)=1/\lambda$.

Practice 38

Explain the connection between the Poisson distribution (Week 9's earlier topic) and the Exponential distribution, in terms of what each one models about the same underlying random process.

Solution

Both distributions describe the same underlying Poisson process (events occurring independently at a constant average rate $\lambda$), but they answer two different questions about it. The Poisson distribution answers "how many events occur in a fixed time interval?" (a discrete count). The Exponential distribution answers "how long do I have to wait until the next event occurs?" (a continuous duration). They're two complementary views of the exact same random process — in fact, the parameter $\lambda$ means the same thing in both (average rate of occurrence), just used in structurally different formulas suited to a count versus a waiting time.

Solution

By the memoryless property, this equals $P(X>10)$ from a fresh start (ignoring the already-elapsed 10 minutes): $\lambda=1/15$.

$P(X>10)=e^{-10/15}=e^{-2/3}\approx0.5134$.

Practice 40

Show, using the CDF, that the Exponential distribution has the memoryless property: $P(X>s+t\,|\,X>s)=P(X>t)$, starting from the definition of conditional probability.

Solution

$P(X>s+t\,|\,X>s)=\dfrac{P(X>s+t\ \cap\ X>s)}{P(X>s)}$. Since $s+t>s$ (for $t>0$), the event $X>s+t$ already implies $X>s$, so the intersection is simply $X>s+t$: $=\dfrac{P(X>s+t)}{P(X>s)}=\dfrac{e^{-\lambda(s+t)}}{e^{-\lambda s}}=e^{-\lambda s-\lambda t+\lambda s}=e^{-\lambda t}=P(X>t)$.

Answer: confirmed — the $e^{-\lambda s}$ terms cancel exactly, leaving a result that depends only on $t$, not on how long you've already waited ($s$).
