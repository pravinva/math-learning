0 Solving ODEs with the Laplace Transform

The transform of a derivative brings down initial conditions automatically: $\mathcal L\{y'\}=sY(s)-y(0)$. This means the Laplace transform doesn't just solve a differential equation — it solves an initial value problem directly, with no separate step needed to find the arbitrary constants.

The method. (1) Transform every term of the ODE. (2) Substitute the given initial conditions. (3) Solve the resulting algebraic equation for $Y(s)$. (4) Find $y(t)$ by taking the inverse transform (typically via partial fractions to break $Y(s)$ into pieces matching the standard table).

Key idea

$$\mathcal L\{y'\}=sY(s)-y(0), \qquad \mathcal L\{y''\}=s^2Y(s)-sy(0)-y'(0)$$

Worked example

Example. Solve $y''-3y'+2y=0$, $y(0)=1,y'(0)=0$.

Transform: $\big[s^2Y-s(1)-0\big]-3\big[sY-1\big]+2Y=0$.

$s^2Y-s-3sY+3+2Y=0 \implies Y(s^2-3s+2)=s-3 \implies Y=\dfrac{s-3}{(s-1)(s-2)}$.

Partial fractions: $\dfrac{s-3}{(s-1)(s-2)}=\dfrac{A}{s-1}+\dfrac{B}{s-2}$. At $s=1$: $A=2$. At $s=2$: $B=-1$.

$y(t)=2e^t-e^{2t}$.

Practice 1

Solve $y'+3y=0$, $y(0)=5$.

Solution

$sY-5+3Y=0 \implies Y(s+3)=5 \implies Y=\dfrac{5}{s+3}$.

Answer: $y(t)=5e^{-3t}$.

Practice 2

Solve $y''+4y=0$, $y(0)=0,y'(0)=2$.

Solution

$s^2Y-2+4Y=0 \implies Y(s^2+4)=2 \implies Y=\dfrac{2}{s^2+4}$.

Answer: $y(t)=\sin(2t)$ (matching $\mathcal L\{\sin\omega t\}=\omega/(s^2+\omega^2)$ with $\omega=2$).

Practice 3

Solve $y'-2y=e^{3t}$, $y(0)=0$.

Solution

$sY-0-2Y=\dfrac{1}{s-3} \implies Y(s-2)=\dfrac{1}{s-3} \implies Y=\dfrac{1}{(s-2)(s-3)}$.

Partial fractions: $\dfrac{A}{s-2}+\dfrac{B}{s-3}$; $A=-1,B=1$.

Answer: $y(t)=-e^{2t}+e^{3t}=e^{3t}-e^{2t}$.

Practice 4

Solve $y''-y'-6y=0$, $y(0)=1,y'(0)=-1$.

Solution

$[s^2Y-s+1]-[sY-1]-6Y=0 \implies Y(s^2-s-6)=s-2 \implies Y=\dfrac{s-2}{(s-3)(s+2)}$.

Partial fractions: at $s=3$: $A=\tfrac15$. At $s=-2$: $B=\tfrac45$.

Answer: $y(t)=\dfrac15e^{3t}+\dfrac45e^{-2t}$.

Practice 5

Solve $y''+y=\cos t$, $y(0)=0,y'(0)=0$ (resonance case — forcing frequency matches natural frequency).

Solution

$s^2Y+Y=\dfrac{s}{s^2+1} \implies Y(s^2+1)=\dfrac{s}{s^2+1} \implies Y=\dfrac{s}{(s^2+1)^2}$.

This matches the known table entry $\mathcal L\{t\sin t\}=\dfrac{2s}{(s^2+1)^2}$, so $Y=\dfrac12\cdot\dfrac{2s}{(s^2+1)^2}$.

Answer: $y(t)=\dfrac{t\sin t}{2}$ — note the amplitude grows with $t$ (resonance), unlike the bounded oscillation from non-resonant forcing.

Practice 6

Solve $y'+2y=4t$, $y(0)=1$ (using $\mathcal L\{t\}=1/s^2$).

Solution

$sY-1+2Y=\dfrac{4}{s^2} \implies Y(s+2)=1+\dfrac{4}{s^2}=\dfrac{s^2+4}{s^2} \implies Y=\dfrac{s^2+4}{s^2(s+2)}$.

Partial fractions: $\dfrac{A}{s}+\dfrac{B}{s^2}+\dfrac{C}{s+2}$. $s^2+4=As(s+2)+B(s+2)+Cs^2$.

At $s=0$: $4=2B\implies B=2$. At $s=-2$: $8=4C\implies C=2$. Match $s^2$: $1=A+C\implies A=-1$.

Answer: $y(t)=-1+2t+2e^{-2t}$.

Practice 7

Solve $y''-4y'+4y=0$, $y(0)=3,y'(0)=1$ (repeated-root case, connecting to MATH142 Week 9's characteristic equation).

Solution

$[s^2Y-3s-1]-4[sY-3]+4Y=0 \implies Y(s^2-4s+4)=3s+1-12=3s-11$.

$Y=\dfrac{3s-11}{(s-2)^2}$. Write as $\dfrac{3(s-2)-5}{(s-2)^2}=\dfrac{3}{s-2}-\dfrac{5}{(s-2)^2}$.

Answer: $y(t)=3e^{2t}-5te^{2t}$ (matching the repeated-root form $(C_1+C_2t)e^{rt}$ from MATH142).

Practice 8

Solve $y''+2y'+5y=0$, $y(0)=0,y'(0)=1$ (complex-root case).

Solution

$[s^2Y-1]+2[sY]+5Y=0 \implies Y(s^2+2s+5)=1$.

Complete the square: $s^2+2s+5=(s+1)^2+4$. $Y=\dfrac{1}{(s+1)^2+4}$.

Matching $\mathcal L\{e^{-t}\sin2t\}=\dfrac{2}{(s+1)^2+4}$: $Y=\dfrac12\cdot\dfrac{2}{(s+1)^2+4}$.

Answer: $y(t)=\dfrac12e^{-t}\sin(2t)$.

Practice 9

Solve the system-representing single equation $y''+9y=\sin(2t)$, $y(0)=0,y'(0)=0$ (non-resonant forcing).

Solution

$s^2Y+9Y=\dfrac{2}{s^2+4} \implies Y=\dfrac{2}{(s^2+4)(s^2+9)}$.

Partial fractions (treating $s^2$ as the variable): $\dfrac{2}{(s^2+4)(s^2+9)}=\dfrac{A}{s^2+4}+\dfrac{B}{s^2+9}$. $2=A(s^2+9)+B(s^2+4)$.

Match constants ($s=0$ conceptually, or match coefficients): $A+B=0$ (coeff of $s^2$) and $9A+4B=2$ (constant). From first, $B=-A$: $9A-4A=2\implies A=\tfrac25,B=-\tfrac25$.

$Y=\dfrac25\cdot\dfrac{1}{s^2+4}-\dfrac25\cdot\dfrac{1}{s^2+9}=\dfrac15\cdot\dfrac{2}{s^2+4}-\dfrac{2}{15}\cdot\dfrac{3}{s^2+9}$.

Answer: $y(t)=\dfrac15\sin(2t)-\dfrac{2}{15}\sin(3t)$.

Practice 10

Explain why the Laplace transform method automatically produces the particular solution matching the given initial conditions, without a separate step to solve for arbitrary constants (unlike the characteristic-equation method from MATH142).

Solution

In the characteristic-equation method, you first find the general solution (with arbitrary constants $C_1,C_2$), then substitute the initial conditions afterward to solve for those constants as a separate algebraic step.

The Laplace transform method builds the initial conditions directly into the transform of each derivative ($\mathcal L\{y'\}=sY(s)-y(0)$, etc.) from the very first line of working — so by the time you solve for $Y(s)$ algebraically, the specific numerical values of $y(0)$ and $y'(0)$ are already baked in. There's no arbitrary constant left over to solve for afterward; the inverse transform directly yields the one particular solution matching those exact initial conditions.

1 The Heaviside (Unit Step) Function & Dirac Delta Function

Real engineering signals often switch on abruptly (a voltage applied at time $t=a$) or deliver an idealised instantaneous impulse (a hammer strike, a sudden shock load). The Heaviside function models the first; the Dirac delta models the second.

Heaviside function. $u(t-a)$ is $0$ before $t=a$ and $1$ from $t=a$ onward — multiplying any function by $u(t-a)$ effectively "switches it on" starting at $t=a$.

Dirac delta function. $\delta(t-a)$ represents an idealised infinite spike at $t=a$ with total "area" 1 — not an ordinary function, but a useful idealisation for a very large force/voltage delivered over a very short time. Its defining sifting property, $\int f(t)\delta(t-a)\,dt=f(a)$, "picks out" the value of $f$ exactly at $t=a$.

Key idea

$$\mathcal L\{u(t-a)\}=\frac{e^{-as}}{s}, \qquad \mathcal L\{\delta(t-a)\}=e^{-as}, \qquad \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(t)\delta(t-a)\,dt=f(a)$$

Worked example

Example 1. Find $\mathcal L\{u(t-3)\}$ and $\mathcal L\{5\delta(t-2)\}$.

$\mathcal L\{u(t-3)\}=\dfrac{e^{-3s}}{s}$; $\mathcal L\{5\delta(t-2)\}=5e^{-2s}$.

Example 2 — sifting property. Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^\infty t^3\delta(t-2)\,dt$.

By the sifting property, this equals $f(2)=2^3=8$.

Practice 11

Find $\mathcal L\{2u(t-5)\}$.

Solution

Answer: $\dfrac{2e^{-5s}}{s}$.

Practice 12

Sketch (describe) the function $f(t)=u(t-1)-u(t-3)$, and state its value for $t=0, 2, 4$.

Solution

$f(t)=0$ for $t<1$; $f(t)=1$ for $1\le t<3$ (since $u(t-1)=1$ but $u(t-3)=0$ there); $f(t)=0$ again for $t\ge3$ (both terms are $1$, cancelling).

Answer: $f(0)=0$, $f(2)=1$, $f(4)=0$ — this represents a "pulse," switched on at $t=1$ and off at $t=3$.

Practice 13

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{2t}\delta(t-1)\,dt$ using the sifting property.

Solution

By the sifting property, this equals $f(1)=e^{2(1)}=e^2$.

Practice 14

Find the Laplace transform of the pulse function $f(t)=u(t-1)-u(t-3)$ from the second practice question, using linearity.

Solution

$\mathcal L\{u(t-1)-u(t-3)\}=\dfrac{e^{-s}}{s}-\dfrac{e^{-3s}}{s}=\dfrac{e^{-s}-e^{-3s}}{s}$.

Practice 15

A force of magnitude 10 is applied as an idealised impulse at $t=4$ to a system initially at rest, modelled as $\delta$-forcing: write the Laplace transform of the forcing term $10\delta(t-4)$.

Solution

Answer: $\mathcal L\{10\delta(t-4)\}=10e^{-4s}$.

Practice 16

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^{10} (t^2+3)\delta(t-5)\,dt$, being careful to confirm the impulse location lies within the integration bounds.

Solution

The impulse is at $t=5$, which lies within $[0,10]$, so the sifting property applies: value is $f(5)=5^2+3=28$.

Answer: $28$.

Practice 17

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^{3} \cos(t)\delta(t-5)\,dt$, explaining why the answer is $0$ despite the sifting property normally giving $f(5)$.

Solution

The impulse at $t=5$ lies outside the integration bounds $[0,3]$ — the delta function contributes nothing to an integral that never actually "sees" the location of the spike.

Answer: $0$ (the sifting property only applies when the integration interval actually contains the point $a$).

Practice 18

Describe (in words) the function $g(t)=t\cdot u(t-2)$, and state $g(1)$ and $g(3)$.

Solution

$g(t)=0$ for $t<2$ (the step function is off); $g(t)=t$ for $t\ge2$ (the step function switches on, letting $t$ through unchanged).

Answer: $g(1)=0$; $g(3)=3$.

Practice 19

A switch is closed at $t=2$, applying a constant voltage of 12V from then on, modelled as $f(t)=12u(t-2)$. Find $\mathcal L\{f(t)\}$.

Solution

Answer: $\mathcal L\{12u(t-2)\}=\dfrac{12e^{-2s}}{s}$.

Practice 20

Explain the key conceptual difference between the Heaviside function and the Dirac delta function, in terms of what kind of physical situation each is used to model.

Solution

The Heaviside function $u(t-a)$ models a sustained change — something that switches on (or off) at $t=a$ and then stays in that new state indefinitely (e.g. a voltage source turned on and left on, a constant force applied from some moment onward).

The Dirac delta function $\delta(t-a)$ models a momentary, idealised impulse — an enormous effect delivered over an infinitesimally short time at the single instant $t=a$, with no lasting "on" state afterward (e.g. a hammer strike, a lightning strike, a sudden shock load) — its effect is felt all at once, not sustained.

2 The Shift Theorems

Two theorems dramatically extend how many functions can be transformed (or inverse-transformed) using just the basic table.

First shift theorem (multiplying by $e^{at}$ in the time domain shifts $s$): if $\mathcal L\{f(t)\}=F(s)$, then $\mathcal L\{e^{at}f(t)\}=F(s-a)$ — simply replace every $s$ in the known transform with $s-a$.

Second shift theorem (delaying a function in time multiplies its transform by $e^{-as}$): if $\mathcal L\{f(t)\}=F(s)$, then $\mathcal L\{f(t-a)u(t-a)\}=e^{-as}F(s)$ — this is precisely how you transform a signal that starts a delayed copy of some known function at $t=a$.

Key idea

$$\mathcal L\{e^{at}f(t)\}=F(s-a) \quad \text{(1st shift)}, \qquad \mathcal L\{f(t-a)u(t-a)\}=e^{-as}F(s) \quad \text{(2nd shift)}$$

Worked example

Example 1 — first shift. Find $\mathcal L\{e^{2t}\cos3t\}$.

$\mathcal L\{\cos3t\}=\dfrac{s}{s^2+9}$. Shift $s\to s-2$: $\mathcal L\{e^{2t}\cos3t\}=\dfrac{s-2}{(s-2)^2+9}$.

Example 2 — second shift. Find $\mathcal L\{(t-3)^2u(t-3)\}$.

$\mathcal L\{t^2\}=\dfrac{2}{s^3}$. Delay by $a=3$: $\mathcal L\{(t-3)^2u(t-3)\}=e^{-3s}\cdot\dfrac{2}{s^3}$.

Practice 21

Find $\mathcal L\{e^{-3t}t^2\}$ using the first shift theorem.

Solution

$\mathcal L\{t^2\}=\dfrac{2}{s^3}$. Shift $s\to s+3$: $\mathcal L\{e^{-3t}t^2\}=\dfrac{2}{(s+3)^3}$.

Practice 22

Find $\mathcal L\{e^{4t}\sin(2t)\}$ using the first shift theorem.

Solution

$\mathcal L\{\sin2t\}=\dfrac{2}{s^2+4}$. Shift $s\to s-4$: $\mathcal L\{e^{4t}\sin2t\}=\dfrac{2}{(s-4)^2+4}$.

Practice 23

Find $\mathcal L\{\sin(t-\pi)u(t-\pi)\}$ using the second shift theorem.

Solution

$\mathcal L\{\sin t\}=\dfrac{1}{s^2+1}$. Delay by $a=\pi$: $\mathcal L\{\sin(t-\pi)u(t-\pi)\}=e^{-\pi s}\cdot\dfrac{1}{s^2+1}$.

Practice 24

Find $\mathcal L\{(t-2)^3u(t-2)\}$ using the second shift theorem.

Solution

$\mathcal L\{t^3\}=\dfrac{6}{s^4}$. Delay by $a=2$: $\mathcal L\{(t-2)^3u(t-2)\}=e^{-2s}\cdot\dfrac{6}{s^4}$.

Practice 25

Find the inverse transform of $F(s)=\dfrac{3}{(s+5)^2}$ using the first shift theorem in reverse.

Solution

Recognise $\dfrac{1}{(s+5)^2}$ as the shifted form of $\mathcal L\{t\}=\dfrac{1}{s^2}$, with $s\to s+5$ (i.e. $a=-5$): corresponds to $e^{-5t}t$.

Answer: $\mathcal L^{-1}\left\{\dfrac{3}{(s+5)^2}\right\}=3te^{-5t}$.

Practice 26

Find the inverse transform of $F(s)=e^{-4s}\cdot\dfrac{1}{s^2+1}$ using the second shift theorem in reverse.

Solution

$\dfrac{1}{s^2+1}$ inverse-transforms to $\sin t$; the factor $e^{-4s}$ signals a time delay of $a=4$.

Answer: $\mathcal L^{-1}\{F(s)\}=\sin(t-4)u(t-4)$.

Practice 27

Solve $y'+2y=e^{-2t}$, $y(0)=0$ (a case where the forcing function matches the homogeneous solution's decay rate, requiring the first shift theorem to invert).

Solution

$sY+2Y=\dfrac{1}{s+2} \implies Y=\dfrac{1}{(s+2)^2}$.

Recognise via the first shift theorem (shifted version of $\mathcal L\{t\}=1/s^2$, with $a=-2$): $Y(s)$ inverse-transforms to $te^{-2t}$.

Answer: $y(t)=te^{-2t}$.

Practice 28

Solve $y''+4y=u(t-2)$, $y(0)=0,y'(0)=0$ (an ODE with a delayed step-function forcing, needing the second shift theorem at the inversion stage).

Solution

$s^2Y+4Y=\dfrac{e^{-2s}}{s} \implies Y=\dfrac{e^{-2s}}{s(s^2+4)}$.

Partial fractions on $\dfrac{1}{s(s^2+4)}$: $\dfrac{A}{s}+\dfrac{Bs+C}{s^2+4}$. $1=A(s^2+4)+(Bs+C)s$. At $s=0$: $A=\tfrac14$. Match $s^2$: $0=A+B\implies B=-\tfrac14$. Match $s$: $0=C$.

So $\dfrac{1}{s(s^2+4)}=\dfrac{1/4}{s}-\dfrac{s/4}{s^2+4}$, inverse-transforming to $\dfrac14-\dfrac14\cos(2t)=\dfrac14(1-\cos2t)$.

Applying the second shift theorem (delay by $a=2$, valid for $t\ge2$): $y(t)=\dfrac14\big[1-\cos(2(t-2))\big]u(t-2)$.

Answer: $y(t)=\dfrac14\big[1-\cos(2t-4)\big]u(t-2)$ — the system stays at rest until $t=2$, then begins oscillating.

Practice 29

Find $\mathcal L\{e^{-t}(t^2+3t)\}$ using the first shift theorem and linearity.

Solution

$\mathcal L\{t^2+3t\}=\dfrac{2}{s^3}+\dfrac{3}{s^2}$. Shift $s\to s+1$: $\mathcal L\{e^{-t}(t^2+3t)\}=\dfrac{2}{(s+1)^3}+\dfrac{3}{(s+1)^2}$.

Practice 30

Explain why the first shift theorem "replaces $s$ with $s-a$" while the second shift theorem "multiplies by $e^{-as}$" — i.e. why an operation in the time domain ($e^{at}$ multiplication vs. a time delay) produces such different-looking operations in the $s$-domain, working directly from the definition of the transform.

Solution

First shift: $\mathcal L\{e^{at}f(t)\}=\displaystyle\int_0^\infty e^{-st}e^{at}f(t)\,dt=\int_0^\infty e^{-(s-a)t}f(t)\,dt=F(s-a)$ — the $e^{at}$ factor combines directly with $e^{-st}$ inside the same exponential, effectively replacing $s$ with $s-a$ throughout the integral.

Second shift: $\mathcal L\{f(t-a)u(t-a)\}=\displaystyle\int_0^\infty e^{-st}f(t-a)u(t-a)\,dt=\int_a^\infty e^{-st}f(t-a)\,dt$ (since $u(t-a)=0$ before $t=a$). Substituting $\tau=t-a$: $=\displaystyle\int_0^\infty e^{-s(\tau+a)}f(\tau)\,d\tau=e^{-as}\int_0^\infty e^{-s\tau}f(\tau)\,d\tau=e^{-as}F(s)$ — here the substitution pulls a clean $e^{-as}$ factor outside the integral, rather than merging into the exponential's rate. The two operations affect fundamentally different parts of the integral's structure, which is exactly why they produce different-looking results.
