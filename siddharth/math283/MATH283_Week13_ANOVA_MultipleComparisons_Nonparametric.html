Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • One-way ANOVA: partitions total variability into between-groups (SSB) and within-groups (SSW); $F=\dfrac{MSB}{MSW}=\dfrac{SSB/(k-1)}{SSW/(N-k)}$, tests $H_0:\mu_1=\mu_2=\cdots=\mu_k$.
  • Why not just run repeated $t$-tests? Each individual test carries its own Type I error risk $\alpha$; running many pairwise tests inflates the overall (family-wise) error rate well above the nominal $\alpha$.
  • Multiple comparisons (e.g. Tukey's HSD, Bonferroni correction): control the family-wise error rate while still identifying which specific pairs of group means differ, after a significant ANOVA result.
  • Nonparametric tests (distribution-free): Mann-Whitney U (two independent samples, alternative to the two-sample $t$-test); Wilcoxon signed-rank (paired samples, alternative to the paired $t$-test); Kruskal-Wallis (three or more groups, alternative to one-way ANOVA) — all based on ranks rather than raw values, requiring no Normality assumption.

0 One-Way Analysis of Variance (ANOVA)

One-way ANOVA compares the means of three or more groups simultaneously, by comparing how much variability exists between the group means to how much variability exists within each group. If the groups really do share a common population mean, these two sources of variability should be roughly comparable; if the group means genuinely differ, the between-groups variability will be large relative to the within-groups variability.

Key idea

$$SSB=\sum_j n_j(\bar x_j-\bar{\bar x})^2, \quad SSW=\sum_j\sum_i(x_{ij}-\bar x_j)^2, \qquad F=\frac{SSB/(k-1)}{SSW/(N-k)}$$

Worked example

Example. Three groups, each $n=4$: Group 1: $23,25,21,24$; Group 2: $30,28,32,29$; Group 3: $18,20,19,17$. Test $H_0:\mu_1=\mu_2=\mu_3$.

Group means: $\bar x_1=23.25,\bar x_2=29.75,\bar x_3=18.5$. Grand mean: $\bar{\bar x}=23.833$.

$SSB=4\big[(23.25-23.833)^2+(29.75-23.833)^2+(18.5-23.833)^2\big]=4(0.34+35.01+28.44)\approx255.17$.

$SSW=$ sum of squared deviations within each group $=22.5$. $df_B=2,df_W=9$: $F=\dfrac{255.17/2}{22.5/9}=\dfrac{127.58}{2.5}\approx51.03$ — a very large $F$, strongly suggesting the group means differ.

Practice 1

Three groups have $n_j=5$ each, with group means $\bar x_1=10,\bar x_2=15,\bar x_3=12$, and grand mean $\bar{\bar x}=12.33$. Find $SSB$.

Solution

$SSB=5\big[(10-12.33)^2+(15-12.33)^2+(12-12.33)^2\big]=5\big[5.43+7.13+0.11\big]=5(12.67)\approx63.35$.

Practice 2

For an ANOVA with $k=4$ groups and $N=20$ total observations, state the degrees of freedom for the numerator (between-groups) and denominator (within-groups).

Solution

$df_B=k-1=4-1=3$. $df_W=N-k=20-4=16$.

Practice 3

An ANOVA gives $SSB=120,SSW=200$, with $k=3$ groups and $N=18$ observations. Find $F$.

Solution

$df_B=2,df_W=15$. $MSB=120/2=60$; $MSW=200/15\approx13.33$.

$F=60/13.33\approx4.50$.

Practice 4

Explain why a large $F$ statistic in ANOVA suggests the group means genuinely differ, in terms of the ratio of between-groups to within-groups variability.

Solution

$F$ is the ratio of between-groups variability (how spread out the group means are from the overall grand mean) to within-groups variability (how spread out individual observations are within each group, around their own group's mean). If the group means truly are all equal ($H_0$ true), any observed differences between group means are purely due to random sampling variation, and should be roughly comparable in magnitude to the ordinary within-group variability — giving $F$ close to 1. A large $F$ means the between-groups variability is much bigger than would be expected from random chance alone, given the amount of natural within-group noise — strong evidence that the groups' true population means genuinely differ.

Practice 5

Four groups, each with $n=6$, give $SSB=180$ and $SSW=270$. Compute $F$ and state the degrees of freedom.

Solution

$k=4,N=24$: $df_B=3,df_W=20$.

$MSB=180/3=60$; $MSW=270/20=13.5$.

$F=60/13.5\approx4.44$.

Practice 6

Explain why running three separate two-sample $t$-tests (comparing Group 1 vs 2, 1 vs 3, and 2 vs 3) instead of a single ANOVA inflates the overall Type I error rate, using approximate probability reasoning.

Solution

If each individual $t$-test is run at $\alpha=0.05$, each one carries a 5% chance of a false positive (concluding a difference exists when it doesn't), assuming all null hypotheses are actually true. Running three such tests means there are three separate opportunities for a false positive to occur. Treating the tests as roughly independent, the probability of AT LEAST ONE false positive across all three tests is approximately $1-(1-0.05)^3\approx1-0.857=0.143$, i.e. about 14.3% — nearly three times the nominal 5% rate for any single test. This inflation of the overall (family-wise) error rate is exactly why ANOVA (a single, combined test) is preferred over multiple separate pairwise tests when comparing 3 or more groups.

Practice 7

An ANOVA table shows $SSB=45,SSW=180,k=3,N=30$. Find $F$ and state whether this appears likely to be significant (given that a typical critical value at $\alpha=0.05$ for $df_B=2,df_W=27$ is around 3.35).

Solution

$MSB=45/2=22.5$; $MSW=180/27\approx6.67$.

$F=22.5/6.67\approx3.375$.

Assessment: since $F\approx3.375$ is just slightly above the stated critical value of about 3.35, this result appears to be right at the boundary of significance at $\alpha=0.05$ — likely (just) significant, though very close to the threshold.

Practice 8

Total variability in a data set is $SST=500$ (sum of squared deviations of every observation from the overall grand mean). If $SSB=150$, find $SSW$, using the fact that $SST=SSB+SSW$.

Solution

$SSW=SST-SSB=500-150=350$.

Practice 9

Explain what the null hypothesis $H_0:\mu_1=\mu_2=\cdots=\mu_k$ in ANOVA does NOT tell you if it's rejected, in terms of which specific groups differ from each other.

Solution

Rejecting the ANOVA null hypothesis only tells you that at least one group mean differs from at least one other — it does NOT identify which specific pair (or pairs) of groups are actually different. For example, with 3 groups, rejecting $H_0$ is consistent with Group 1 differing from Group 2 only, or Group 3 differing from both others, or all three being mutually different — the ANOVA $F$-test alone cannot distinguish between these scenarios. Identifying the specific differing pairs requires a follow-up procedure (multiple comparisons, next topic).

Practice 10

Five groups, each with $n=8$, have $MSB=95$ and $MSW=20$. Find $F$ and the two degrees of freedom.

Solution

$k=5,N=40$: $df_B=4,df_W=35$.

$F=95/20=4.75$.

1 Multiple Comparisons

Once ANOVA has established that the group means aren't all equal, a multiple comparisons procedure identifies specifically which pairs differ, while controlling the overall (family-wise) error rate across all the pairwise comparisons being made simultaneously.

Bonferroni correction (simple, conservative): if making $m$ comparisons and wanting an overall error rate of $\alpha$, run each individual comparison at the stricter level $\alpha/m$.

Key idea

$$\alpha_{\text{per comparison}} = \frac{\alpha}{m} \ \text{(Bonferroni, } m \text{ = number of comparisons)}$$

Worked example

Example. An ANOVA with 4 groups (6 possible pairwise comparisons) is followed up using Bonferroni correction at overall $\alpha=0.05$. Find the per-comparison significance level required.

Number of pairwise comparisons with 4 groups: $\binom42=6$.

Per-comparison $\alpha=\dfrac{0.05}{6}\approx0.0083$ — each individual pairwise test must now achieve a $p$-value below this much stricter threshold to be declared significant.

Practice 11

For an ANOVA with 5 groups, find the number of possible pairwise comparisons.

Solution

$\binom52=\dfrac{5\times4}{2}=10$.

Practice 12

Using Bonferroni correction with 5 groups (10 comparisons) at overall $\alpha=0.05$, find the required per-comparison significance level.

Solution

$\dfrac{0.05}{10}=0.005$.

Practice 13

A pairwise comparison (one of several being checked with Bonferroni correction, per-comparison threshold 0.01) yields $p=0.008$. State the decision.

Solution

Since $p=0.008<0.01$, reject the null hypothesis for this specific pairwise comparison (conclude this pair of group means genuinely differ).

Practice 14

Explain why the Bonferroni correction is described as "conservative," in terms of what happens to the power of each individual test as the number of comparisons increases.

Solution

As the number of comparisons $m$ increases, the Bonferroni-corrected per-comparison significance level $\alpha/m$ becomes smaller and smaller — making it progressively harder for any individual comparison to achieve statistical significance. This reduces the power of each individual test (the probability of correctly detecting a real difference when one truly exists), increasing the risk of Type II errors (missing genuine differences). The correction successfully controls the overall family-wise Type I error rate, but at the cost of making each individual comparison less sensitive — this trade-off is exactly why the method is called "conservative": it errs on the side of caution against false positives, at some expense of missing true positives.

Practice 15

With 6 groups being compared (15 pairwise comparisons), using Bonferroni correction at overall $\alpha=0.05$, find the per-comparison threshold, and comment on how small it has become.

Solution

$\dfrac{0.05}{15}\approx0.0033$.

Comment: this threshold is now less than 1/15th of the conventional 0.05 level — a genuinely quite strict requirement, illustrating why Bonferroni becomes increasingly conservative (and potentially overly cautious) as the number of groups/comparisons grows.

Practice 16

Following a significant ANOVA with 3 groups, all 3 pairwise comparisons are tested using Bonferroni correction (per-comparison $\alpha=0.0167$). The three $p$-values are $0.002, 0.03, 0.15$. State which pairs (if any) show a statistically significant difference.

Solution

Compare each $p$-value to the corrected threshold $0.0167$: $0.002<0.0167$ (significant); $0.03>0.0167$ (not significant); $0.15>0.0167$ (not significant).

Answer: only the first pairwise comparison ($p=0.002$) shows a statistically significant difference after correction — despite $p=0.03$ being "significant" at an uncorrected $\alpha=0.05$, it fails to meet the stricter Bonferroni-corrected threshold.

Practice 17

Explain the general logic behind WHY multiple comparison procedures are necessary at all, connecting this directly back to the earlier explanation of inflated Type I error rates from running many separate tests.

Solution

As established earlier, running multiple separate hypothesis tests (even if each is individually run at a nominal $\alpha$) accumulates an increasing overall chance of at least one false positive purely by chance, as more comparisons are made. Multiple comparison procedures (like Bonferroni) exist specifically to counteract this accumulation, adjusting the individual test thresholds so that the OVERALL (family-wise) probability of making at least one Type I error across the entire set of comparisons stays controlled at the desired level (e.g. 0.05), rather than letting it silently inflate as more and more pairs are tested.

Practice 18

For an ANOVA with 8 groups, find the number of pairwise comparisons, and the Bonferroni-corrected per-comparison significance level for an overall $\alpha=0.05$.

Solution

$\binom82=\dfrac{8\times7}{2}=28$.

Per-comparison $\alpha=\dfrac{0.05}{28}\approx0.00179$.

Practice 19

A researcher performs 4 pairwise comparisons following a significant ANOVA, using Bonferroni correction at overall $\alpha=0.10$ (a less strict overall level than usual). Find the per-comparison threshold.

Solution

$\dfrac{0.10}{4}=0.025$.

Practice 20

Explain why Tukey's HSD (Honestly Significant Difference) test, an alternative to Bonferroni correction specifically designed for comparing all possible pairs of means after ANOVA, is generally preferred over Bonferroni when ALL pairwise comparisons are of interest (rather than just a pre-planned subset).

Solution

Tukey's HSD is specifically constructed to control the family-wise error rate exactly for the particular situation of comparing all possible pairs of means simultaneously, using the actual sampling distribution of the range of sample means (rather than the more generic, conservative Bonferroni adjustment). Because it's tailored to this specific scenario, Tukey's method is generally less conservative than Bonferroni when comparing all pairs — it achieves the same overall error-rate control while sacrificing less statistical power (making it somewhat easier to detect genuine differences) compared to the more general-purpose Bonferroni approach, which doesn't take advantage of the specific structure of an all-pairs comparison.

2 Nonparametric Tests

Every hypothesis test covered so far ($t$-tests, ANOVA) assumes the underlying population(s) are approximately Normal (or relies on the CLT for large samples). When this assumption is clearly violated (strongly skewed data, small samples, ordinal-only data), nonparametric ("distribution-free") tests provide valid alternatives, typically working with the ranks of the data rather than the raw values themselves.

Key idea

Mann-Whitney U (two independent samples, alternative to two-sample $t$-test): ranks all observations together, sums the ranks in each group.

Wilcoxon signed-rank (paired samples, alternative to paired $t$-test): ranks the absolute differences, then sums ranks separately for positive and negative differences.

Kruskal-Wallis (3+ groups, alternative to one-way ANOVA): ranks all observations across all groups together, compares the sum of ranks in each group.

Worked example

Example. Explain, in a single sentence, why the Mann-Whitney test doesn't require assuming the two populations are Normally distributed, unlike the two-sample $t$-test.

The Mann-Whitney test only uses the relative ordering (ranks) of the combined data, not the actual numeric values or any assumption about their underlying distribution shape — so it remains valid regardless of whether the populations are Normal, skewed, or any other shape, as long as the two distributions have the same general shape (differing only, if at all, in location).

Practice 21

Two independent samples: Group A $=5,7,9,11$; Group B $=6,8,10,12$. Rank all 8 values together (smallest = rank 1), and find the sum of ranks for Group A.

Solution

Combined sorted: $5(A),6(B),7(A),8(B),9(A),10(B),11(A),12(B)$, ranks $1$ to $8$.

Group A ranks: $1,3,5,7$; sum $=16$.

Practice 22

For the same data, find the sum of ranks for Group B, and verify the two rank sums add to the expected total for $N=8$ observations.

Solution

Group B ranks: $2,4,6,8$; sum $=20$.

Total: $16+20=36$. Expected total for ranks $1$ to $8$: $\dfrac{8(9)}{2}=36$. Match. ✓

Practice 23

Paired data (before, after) for 5 subjects gives differences $d_i=3,-1,5,2,-2$. Rank the ABSOLUTE differences (smallest=1), noting ties if any.

Solution

Absolute differences: $3,1,5,2,2$. Two values tie at $2$ (subjects 4 and 5).

Sorted absolute values: $1,2,2,3,5$ — ranks would be $1,2.5,2.5,4,5$ (tied ranks 2 and 3 are averaged to $2.5$ each).

Practice 24

Using the ranks from the previous question (with sign attached from the original differences), find the sum of positive ranks and the sum of negative ranks for the Wilcoxon signed-rank test.

Solution

Original differences with ranks: $d=3\to\text{rank }4$; $d=-1\to\text{rank }1$; $d=5\to\text{rank }5$; $d=2\to\text{rank }2.5$; $d=-2\to\text{rank }2.5$.

Positive ranks (from $d=3,5,2$): $4+5+2.5=11.5$.

Negative ranks (from $d=-1,-2$): $1+2.5=3.5$.

Check: $11.5+3.5=15=\dfrac{5(6)}{2}$. ✓

Practice 25

Explain why the Wilcoxon signed-rank test is generally preferred over the paired $t$-test when the distribution of the paired differences is strongly skewed or has extreme outliers.

Solution

The paired $t$-test relies on the mean and standard deviation of the differences, both of which are sensitive to skewness and outliers — a single extreme difference can substantially distort the sample mean and inflate the standard deviation, potentially masking a genuine effect or creating a spurious one. The Wilcoxon signed-rank test instead works with the ranks of the absolute differences, which are far less sensitive to the exact magnitude of any single extreme value — an outlier still gets the highest rank, but its specific (possibly extreme) numeric value doesn't distort the test statistic the way it would distort a mean or standard deviation. This makes the Wilcoxon test more robust when Normality of the differences is in doubt.

Practice 26

Three groups (for a Kruskal-Wallis test) have combined rank sums $R_1=25,R_2=40,R_3=55$ with $n_1=n_2=n_3=5$ (total $N=15$). Explain conceptually (without computing the full statistic) what a large disparity between these rank sums (relative to what would be expected if all groups came from the same distribution) would suggest.

Solution

If all three groups genuinely come from the same underlying distribution (the null hypothesis), each group's average rank should be roughly similar (since ranks would be essentially randomly distributed across the three equally-sized groups) — each group of 5 would be expected to have a rank sum around $\dfrac{15(16)/2}{3}=40$ on average. Seeing the observed rank sums spread out fairly differently from this expected common value (25, 40, 55 — noticeably different from each other and from the expected value of 40) suggests the groups' underlying distributions genuinely differ in location (median), providing evidence against the null hypothesis, similar in spirit to how a large $F$ statistic in ordinary ANOVA suggests differing group means.

Practice 27

Explain the general strategy shared by all three nonparametric tests covered this week (Mann-Whitney, Wilcoxon, Kruskal-Wallis): what do they all have in common in terms of how they process the raw data before computing a test statistic?

Solution

All three tests begin by converting the raw numeric data into ranks (either within combined groups, or of absolute differences), then perform their respective calculations on those ranks rather than on the original measured values. This shared strategy is precisely what makes them "distribution-free" (nonparametric): since ranks only depend on the relative ordering of data points, not on their specific numeric values or the shape of the distribution they came from, the resulting tests remain valid under much weaker assumptions than their parametric counterparts ($t$-tests and ANOVA), which rely on the data being drawn from (approximately) Normal populations.

Practice 28

A researcher has a very small sample ($n=6$ per group) of clearly non-Normal (heavily skewed) data comparing two independent treatments. Explain why a nonparametric test (Mann-Whitney) would be preferred here over a two-sample $t$-test, referencing both the small sample size and the skewness.

Solution

The two-sample $t$-test's validity relies on either the underlying populations being approximately Normal, or (via the Central Limit Theorem) a sufficiently large sample size to make the sampling distribution of the mean approximately Normal regardless of the population's shape. With only $n=6$ per group, the sample is far too small to rely on the CLT to compensate for the underlying skewness — and the underlying population is explicitly stated to be non-Normal, so the direct Normality assumption also fails. In this situation (small sample, clearly non-Normal data), the $t$-test's validity is genuinely in question, making the Mann-Whitney test (which makes no such distributional assumption) the safer, more appropriate choice.

Practice 29

For a Mann-Whitney test comparing two groups of size $n_1=4,n_2=4$ (8 total observations), state the range of possible values for the sum of ranks in Group 1 (minimum possible sum and maximum possible sum), and explain the reasoning for each extreme.

Solution

Minimum: if Group 1 contains the 4 smallest values, its ranks would be $1,2,3,4$, summing to $10$.

Maximum: if Group 1 contains the 4 largest values, its ranks would be $5,6,7,8$, summing to $26$.

Reasoning: the rank sum for any group is minimised when that group happens to contain the smallest-valued observations (getting the lowest possible ranks) and maximised when it contains the largest-valued observations (getting the highest possible ranks) — these two extremes represent the most extreme possible degree of separation between the two groups in the combined ranking.

Practice 30

A data set of paired differences includes several ties (equal absolute values). Explain how tied ranks are conventionally handled in the Wilcoxon signed-rank test, using the specific averaging method.

Solution

When two or more absolute differences are tied (equal in value), each of the tied observations is assigned the average of the rank positions they would have occupied had they been slightly different (and thus distinguishable). For example, if two values are tied for what would otherwise be ranks 2 and 3, both are instead assigned the average rank $2.5$ — exactly the approach used in the earlier practice problem this week where two absolute differences both equalled $2$, and both received rank $2.5$ rather than arbitrarily assigning one of them rank $2$ and the other rank $3$.
