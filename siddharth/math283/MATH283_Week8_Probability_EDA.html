Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Addition rule: $P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B)$; if mutually exclusive, $P(A\cap B)=0$.
  • Complement rule: $P(A')=1-P(A)$.
  • Conditional probability: $P(A|B)=\dfrac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}$; multiplication rule $P(A\cap B)=P(A|B)P(B)$.
  • Independence: $A,B$ independent $\iff P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B) \iff P(A|B)=P(A)$.
  • Bayes' theorem: $P(A|B)=\dfrac{P(B|A)P(A)}{P(B)}$.
  • Numerical summaries: mean $\bar x=\dfrac{\sum x_i}{n}$; sample variance $s^2=\dfrac{\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2}{n-1}$; median (middle value, sorted); IQR $=Q_3-Q_1$.
  • Graphical techniques: histograms (shape/spread), boxplots (five-number summary: min, $Q_1$, median, $Q_3$, max; outliers beyond $1.5\times\text{IQR}$ from the box), stem-and-leaf plots.

0 Probability Rules & Venn Diagrams

Probability rules formalise how to combine the likelihoods of related events. A Venn diagram makes the addition rule visually obvious: adding $P(A)$ and $P(B)$ double-counts their overlap, $P(A\cap B)$, which must be subtracted back out.

Key idea

$$P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B), \qquad P(A')=1-P(A)$$

Worked example

Example. In a class, $P(\text{likes maths})=0.6$, $P(\text{likes physics})=0.5$, $P(\text{likes both})=0.3$. Find $P(\text{likes at least one})$ and $P(\text{likes neither})$.

$P(M\cup P)=0.6+0.5-0.3=0.8$.

$P(\text{neither})=1-0.8=0.2$.

Practice 1

If $P(A)=0.4$, $P(B)=0.3$, and $A,B$ are mutually exclusive, find $P(A\cup B)$.

Solution

Mutually exclusive $\implies P(A\cap B)=0$: $P(A\cup B)=0.4+0.3-0=0.7$.

Practice 2

If $P(A\cup B)=0.75$, $P(A)=0.5$, $P(B)=0.4$, find $P(A\cap B)$.

Solution

$0.75=0.5+0.4-P(A\cap B) \implies P(A\cap B)=0.9-0.75=0.15$.

Practice 3

A component fails due to defect $A$ with probability 0.05, defect $B$ with probability 0.03, and both defects with probability 0.01. Find the probability it fails due to at least one defect.

Solution

$P(A\cup B)=0.05+0.03-0.01=0.07$.

Practice 4

For events $A,B$ with $P(A)=0.6,P(B)=0.5,P(A\cap B)=0.3$, verify whether $A$ and $B$ are independent.

Solution

Independence requires $P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B)$: $P(A)P(B)=0.6\times0.5=0.3$.

Answer: since $P(A\cap B)=0.3=P(A)P(B)$, $A$ and $B$ ARE independent.

Practice 5

In a survey, 70% of respondents own a car, 40% own a bike, and 20% own both. Find the probability a randomly chosen respondent owns neither.

Solution

$P(\text{car}\cup\text{bike})=0.7+0.4-0.2=0.9$.

$P(\text{neither})=1-0.9=0.1$.

Practice 6

A die is rolled once. Let $A$ = "roll is even" and $B$ = "roll is greater than 3". Find $P(A\cup B)$ directly by listing outcomes, and verify using the addition rule.

Solution

$A=\{2,4,6\}$, $B=\{4,5,6\}$, $A\cap B=\{4,6\}$.

Direct: $A\cup B=\{2,4,5,6\}$, so $P(A\cup B)=4/6=2/3$.

Formula: $P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B)=\tfrac36+\tfrac36-\tfrac26=\tfrac46=\tfrac23$. Match. ✓

Practice 7

In a factory, 3 machines A, B, C produce items with defect probabilities 0.02, 0.03, 0.05 respectively (assume independent machines). Find the probability that at least one of the three machines produces a defect on a given check (each machine checked once).

Solution

$P(\text{no defect})=P(A')P(B')P(C')=(0.98)(0.97)(0.95)=0.9031$ (approx).

Answer: $P(\text{at least one defect})=1-0.9031=0.0969$.

Practice 8

For three mutually exclusive and exhaustive events $A_1,A_2,A_3$ with $P(A_1)=0.2,P(A_2)=0.5$, find $P(A_3)$.

Solution

Exhaustive means $P(A_1)+P(A_2)+P(A_3)=1$: $P(A_3)=1-0.2-0.5=0.3$.

Practice 9

Draw the logic (in words) for finding $P(A\cup B\cup C)$ for three general events using the inclusion-exclusion principle, stating the full formula.

Solution

Answer: $$P(A\cup B\cup C)=P(A)+P(B)+P(C)-P(A\cap B)-P(A\cap C)-P(B\cap C)+P(A\cap B\cap C)$$

Logic: adding all three individually double-counts every pairwise overlap once too many, so those are subtracted; but this subtracts the triple-overlap region three times (once in each pairwise subtraction) when it should only be removed twice net — adding it back once restores the correct count.

Practice 10

A quality control test has $P(\text{pass})=0.85$. If 4 independent items are tested, find the probability that all 4 pass, and the probability that at least one fails.

Solution

$P(\text{all 4 pass})=0.85^4\approx0.522$.

$P(\text{at least one fails})=1-0.522=0.478$.

1 Tree Diagrams & Conditional Probability

A tree diagram is the natural tool for multi-stage random experiments — each branch's probability is conditional on everything that happened at the branches before it, and multiplying along a path gives the probability of that whole sequence of outcomes. Bayes' theorem lets you "reverse" the direction of a conditional probability — going from $P(\text{effect}|\text{cause})$ (often easy to state directly) to $P(\text{cause}|\text{effect})$ (often what you actually want to know).

Key idea

$$P(A|B)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}, \qquad P(A|B)=\frac{P(B|A)P(A)}{P(B)} \ \text{(Bayes' theorem)}$$

Worked example

Example. Two factories supply a part: Factory 1 supplies 60% (defect rate 2%), Factory 2 supplies 40% (defect rate 5%). If a randomly selected part is defective, find the probability it came from Factory 2.

$P(\text{defect})=P(F_1)P(\text{def}|F_1)+P(F_2)P(\text{def}|F_2)=(0.6)(0.02)+(0.4)(0.05)=0.012+0.02=0.032$.

$P(F_2|\text{def})=\dfrac{(0.4)(0.05)}{0.032}=\dfrac{0.02}{0.032}=0.625$.

Practice 11

A bag has 5 red and 3 blue balls. Two are drawn without replacement. Find $P(\text{both red})$.

Solution

$P(\text{1st red})=5/8$. $P(\text{2nd red}|\text{1st red})=4/7$ (one red removed).

$P(\text{both red})=\dfrac58\times\dfrac47=\dfrac{20}{56}=\dfrac{5}{14}$.

Practice 12

For the same bag (5 red, 3 blue), find $P(\text{2nd ball is red})$ (unconditional — the 1st ball's colour is unknown).

Solution

Condition on the first draw: $P(\text{2nd red})=P(\text{1st red})P(\text{2nd red}|\text{1st red})+P(\text{1st blue})P(\text{2nd red}|\text{1st blue})$.

$=\dfrac58\times\dfrac47+\dfrac38\times\dfrac57=\dfrac{20}{56}+\dfrac{15}{56}=\dfrac{35}{56}=\dfrac58$.

Answer: $\dfrac58$ — interestingly, the same as $P(\text{1st red})$; by symmetry, every position in the sequence has the same marginal probability of being red.

Practice 13

A test for a disease is 95% accurate (correctly identifies both diseased and healthy people 95% of the time). The disease affects 1% of the population. Find $P(\text{disease}|\text{positive test})$ using Bayes' theorem.

Solution

$P(D)=0.01,P(D')=0.99$. $P(+|D)=0.95,P(+|D')=0.05$.

$P(+)=P(D)P(+|D)+P(D')P(+|D')=(0.01)(0.95)+(0.99)(0.05)=0.0095+0.0495=0.059$.

$P(D|+)=\dfrac{0.0095}{0.059}\approx0.161$.

Answer: only about 16.1%, despite the test being "95% accurate" — a classic and important illustration of how a low base rate (1% prevalence) dominates the result.

Practice 14

Three urns: Urn 1 has 2 red/3 blue, Urn 2 has 4 red/1 blue, Urn 3 has 1 red/4 blue. An urn is chosen at random (equally likely), then a ball is drawn. Find $P(\text{red})$.

Solution

$P(\text{red})=\dfrac13\left(\dfrac25\right)+\dfrac13\left(\dfrac45\right)+\dfrac13\left(\dfrac15\right)=\dfrac13\left(\dfrac25+\dfrac45+\dfrac15\right)=\dfrac13\times\dfrac75=\dfrac{7}{15}$.

Practice 15

For the three-urn setup above, if a red ball is drawn, find $P(\text{Urn 2}|\text{red})$.

Solution

$P(\text{Urn 2}\cap\text{red})=\dfrac13\times\dfrac45=\dfrac{4}{15}$.

$P(\text{Urn 2}|\text{red})=\dfrac{4/15}{7/15}=\dfrac47$.

Practice 16

A component passes an initial test with probability 0.9. Given it passes, it passes a second (independent, harder) test with probability 0.7. Given it fails the first test, it passes the second test with probability 0.2. Find the overall probability it passes the second test.

Solution

$P(\text{pass 2nd})=P(\text{pass 1st})P(\text{pass 2nd}|\text{pass 1st})+P(\text{fail 1st})P(\text{pass 2nd}|\text{fail 1st})$.

$=(0.9)(0.7)+(0.1)(0.2)=0.63+0.02=0.65$.

Practice 17

For the component-testing scenario above, if it passed the second test, find the probability it also passed the first test.

Solution

$P(\text{pass 1st}\cap\text{pass 2nd})=0.63$ (from above). $P(\text{pass 2nd})=0.65$.

$P(\text{pass 1st}|\text{pass 2nd})=\dfrac{0.63}{0.65}\approx0.969$.

Practice 18

Explain, using the disease-testing example above, why a highly accurate test can still give a low $P(\text{disease}|\text{positive})$ when the disease is rare, in terms of the relative sizes of true positives and false positives.

Solution

Even though the test correctly flags 95% of the (relatively few) actually-diseased people, it also incorrectly flags 5% of the (vastly more numerous) healthy people as false positives. With a 1% disease prevalence, the healthy population is about 99 times larger than the diseased population — so even a small 5% false-positive rate applied to that huge healthy group produces more false positives in absolute terms than the true positives from the small diseased group. This is exactly why the calculated probability came out so much lower than the test's stated "95% accuracy" might naively suggest.

Practice 19

Two coins are flipped: one fair, one biased with $P(\text{heads})=0.8$. A coin is chosen at random and flipped twice, showing heads both times. Find the probability the biased coin was chosen.

Solution

$P(\text{HH}|\text{fair})=0.5^2=0.25$. $P(\text{HH}|\text{biased})=0.8^2=0.64$.

$P(\text{HH})=0.5(0.25)+0.5(0.64)=0.125+0.32=0.445$.

$P(\text{biased}|\text{HH})=\dfrac{0.5\times0.64}{0.445}=\dfrac{0.32}{0.445}\approx0.719$.

Practice 20

Explain the general structure of Bayes' theorem in words: what does the numerator represent, and what does the denominator represent, in the context of the disease-testing example?

Solution

The numerator, $P(B|A)P(A)$, represents the probability of the specific joint scenario actually occurring — in the disease example, the probability of being diseased AND testing positive, i.e. a true positive.

The denominator, $P(B)$, represents the total probability of the observed evidence occurring at all, summed over every possible way it could have arisen — in the disease example, the total probability of testing positive, whether from a true positive (diseased, correctly flagged) or a false positive (healthy, incorrectly flagged). Bayes' theorem is really just "what fraction of all positive results are true positives," which is exactly the conditional probability being asked for.

2 Exploratory Data Analysis — Graphical Techniques

Before any formal statistical test, always look at the data. A histogram reveals the overall shape (symmetric, skewed, bimodal); a boxplot summarises the five-number summary and flags potential outliers at a glance; a stem-and-leaf plot preserves every individual data value while still showing the distribution's shape.

Boxplot outlier rule. A point is flagged as a potential outlier if it lies more than $1.5\times\text{IQR}$ beyond $Q_1$ or $Q_3$.

Key idea

$$\text{IQR}=Q_3-Q_1, \qquad \text{outlier if } xQ_3+1.5\,\text{IQR}$$

Worked example

Example. A data set has $Q_1=20,Q_3=35$. Find the IQR and the outlier fences.

IQR $=35-20=15$. Lower fence: $20-1.5(15)=20-22.5=-2.5$. Upper fence: $35+1.5(15)=35+22.5=57.5$.

Any value below $-2.5$ or above $57.5$ would be flagged as a potential outlier.

Practice 21

A data set has $Q_1=10,Q_3=18$. Find the IQR and both outlier fences.

Solution

IQR $=18-10=8$. Lower fence: $10-1.5(8)=10-12=-2$. Upper fence: $18+1.5(8)=18+12=30$.

Practice 22

A data set has median 50, $Q_1=42,Q_3=60$, min 30, max 95. Is the value 95 a potential outlier?

Solution

IQR $=60-42=18$. Upper fence: $60+1.5(18)=60+27=87$.

Answer: since $95>87$, yes, 95 is flagged as a potential outlier.

Practice 23

Describe (in words) the difference in shape a histogram would show for right-skewed data compared to left-skewed data.

Solution

Right-skewed (positively skewed): the bulk of the data is concentrated on the left, with a long tail stretching out to the right (toward higher values) — e.g. income data, where most people earn moderate amounts but a few earn very high amounts.

Left-skewed (negatively skewed): the bulk of the data is concentrated on the right, with a long tail stretching out to the left (toward lower values) — e.g. exam scores where most students do well but a few score very low.

Practice 24

A boxplot shows the median line positioned much closer to $Q_1$ than to $Q_3$ within the box. What does this suggest about the skewness of the middle 50% of the data?

Solution

If the median is closer to $Q_1$, the lower half of the middle 50% (between $Q_1$ and the median) is compressed into a smaller range than the upper half (between the median and $Q_3$) — suggesting the data within this central range is right-skewed (more spread out toward higher values).

Practice 25

A stem-and-leaf plot has stems $1,2,3$ (representing tens) with leaves: Stem 1: 2,5,8; Stem 2: 0,1,3,3,7; Stem 3: 4. List all the actual data values and find the median.

Solution

Data values: $12,15,18,20,21,23,23,27,34$ (9 values total).

Median (5th value when sorted, since $n=9$): $21$.

Practice 26

For the data set $4,7,9,12,15,18,22,29,35$ (already sorted, $n=9$), find $Q_1$, the median, and $Q_3$ using the standard method (median splits data in half; $Q_1,Q_3$ are medians of each half).

Solution

Median (5th value): $15$.

Lower half (excluding median): $4,7,9,12$ — $Q_1$ = average of 2nd and 3rd = $(7+9)/2=8$.

Upper half: $18,22,29,35$ — $Q_3$ = average of 2nd and 3rd = $(22+29)/2=25.5$.

Practice 27

Using the data set from the previous question ($Q_1=8,Q_3=25.5$), find the IQR and check whether the minimum value (4) is a potential outlier.

Solution

IQR $=25.5-8=17.5$. Lower fence: $8-1.5(17.5)=8-26.25=-18.25$.

Answer: since $4>-18.25$, the minimum value 4 is NOT flagged as an outlier.

Practice 28

Explain why a boxplot can hide the fact that a data set is bimodal (has two separate clusters/peaks), while a histogram would reveal it clearly.

Solution

A boxplot only displays five summary numbers (min, $Q_1$, median, $Q_3$, max) — it has no way to show the internal density or clustering pattern of the data between these markers. Two very differently-shaped underlying distributions (e.g. one genuinely bimodal, one uniformly spread) could easily produce the exact same five-number summary, and hence the exact same-looking boxplot. A histogram, by contrast, directly shows the frequency of data within many small bins across the range, so two separate peaks (bimodality) would appear as two distinct humps — a pattern the boxplot has no mechanism to reveal.

Practice 29

A company's daily sales data has mean \$5000 and median \$4200. What does the relationship between the mean and median suggest about the skewness of the sales distribution?

Solution

Since the mean (\$5000) is noticeably higher than the median (\$4200), the distribution is likely right-skewed — a few days with unusually high sales pull the mean upward, while the median (less sensitive to extreme values) stays closer to the "typical" day's sales.

Practice 30

Two data sets have the same median and IQR, but Data Set A's histogram is symmetric and unimodal, while Data Set B's histogram is bimodal. Explain why relying only on the five-number summary (as shown in a boxplot) could mislead someone comparing these two data sets.

Solution

If both data sets share identical five-number summaries, their boxplots would look completely identical — visually suggesting the two data sets have the same underlying distribution shape. In reality, Data Set B has two separate clusters of values (perhaps representing two distinct subgroups within the data), a structurally very different situation from Data Set A's single, smooth, symmetric cluster. Someone relying only on the boxplot summary would completely miss this crucial difference in the data's underlying structure, potentially leading to incorrect conclusions (e.g. treating Set B as a single homogeneous group when it may actually represent two distinct populations that should be analysed separately).

3 Exploratory Data Analysis — Numerical Summaries

Numerical summaries condense a whole data set into a handful of representative numbers: measures of centre (mean, median) and measures of spread (variance, standard deviation, IQR). Each measure has strengths and weaknesses — the mean uses every data point but is sensitive to outliers; the median ignores exact values but is robust to them.

Key idea

$$\bar x=\frac{\sum x_i}{n}, \qquad s^2=\frac{\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2}{n-1}=\frac{\sum x_i^2-n\bar x^2}{n-1}, \qquad s=\sqrt{s^2}$$

Worked example

Example. Find the mean and sample variance of $2,4,6,8,10$.

$\bar x=\dfrac{2+4+6+8+10}{5}=6$.

$\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2=16+4+0+4+16=40$. $s^2=\dfrac{40}{4}=10$; $s=\sqrt{10}\approx3.162$.

Practice 31

Find the mean and sample variance of $3,5,7,9,11,13$.

Solution

$\bar x=\dfrac{3+5+7+9+11+13}{6}=\dfrac{48}{6}=8$.

Deviations squared: $25,9,1,1,9,25$; sum $=70$. $s^2=\dfrac{70}{5}=14$.

Practice 32

Use the computational formula $s^2=\dfrac{\sum x_i^2-n\bar x^2}{n-1}$ to verify the variance of $2,4,6,8,10$ from the worked example.

Solution

$\sum x_i^2=4+16+36+64+100=220$. $n\bar x^2=5(36)=180$.

$s^2=\dfrac{220-180}{4}=\dfrac{40}{4}=10$. Matches the worked example. ✓

Practice 33

A sample of 10 measurements has $\sum x_i=150$ and $\sum x_i^2=2400$. Find the sample mean and variance.

Solution

$\bar x=\dfrac{150}{10}=15$.

$s^2=\dfrac{2400-10(15^2)}{9}=\dfrac{2400-2250}{9}=\dfrac{150}{9}\approx16.67$.

Practice 34

A data set of 8 values has mean 20 and variance 25. If every value is multiplied by 3, find the new mean and variance.

Solution

Multiplying every value by a constant $c$: new mean $=c\times\text{old mean}=3(20)=60$.

New variance $=c^2\times\text{old variance}=9(25)=225$ (variance scales by the square of the constant, since it involves squared deviations).

Practice 35

A data set has mean 50 and variance 16. If a constant 10 is added to every value, find the new mean and variance.

Solution

New mean $=50+10=60$ (shifts by the constant).

New variance $=16$ (unchanged — adding a constant shifts every value equally, so the spread/deviations from the mean don't change at all).

Practice 36

Explain why the sample variance formula divides by $n-1$ rather than $n$, in terms of "degrees of freedom."

Solution

Computing $s^2$ requires first estimating the population mean using the sample mean $\bar x$ — but $\bar x$ is itself calculated from the same $n$ data points, using up one "degree of freedom." Once $\bar x$ is fixed, only $n-1$ of the $n$ deviations $(x_i-\bar x)$ are actually free to vary independently (the last one is automatically determined, since all deviations must sum to exactly zero). Dividing by $n-1$ (rather than $n$) corrects for this loss of one degree of freedom, and results in an unbiased estimator of the true population variance — dividing by $n$ instead would systematically underestimate the population variance on average.

Practice 37

Two data sets have the same mean but Data Set A has standard deviation 2 while Data Set B has standard deviation 8. Explain what this difference tells you about the two data sets, using a real-world interpretation (e.g. exam scores or measurements).

Solution

Both data sets are centred at the same average value, but Data Set A's values are tightly clustered close to that mean (low spread/variability), while Data Set B's values are much more spread out, with individual observations potentially far from the mean in either direction.

Real-world example: if these represent exam scores, Data Set A might describe a class where most students performed very similarly (consistently around the average), while Data Set B describes a class with a much wider range of performance — some students far below average, some far above, even though both classes have the identical overall average score.

Practice 38

A sample has $n=5$ values: $10, 12, x, 16, 18$, with a known sample mean of $14$. Find the missing value $x$.

Solution

$\bar x=14 \implies \sum x_i=5(14)=70$.

$10+12+x+16+18=70 \implies 56+x=70 \implies x=14$.

Practice 39

A researcher reports the mean of a data set but not the standard deviation. Explain why this is insufficient information to fully describe the data, giving a concrete example of two very different data sets that could share the same mean.

Solution

The mean alone describes only the "centre" of the data, saying nothing about how spread out or clustered the values are around that centre — two data sets can have identical means while looking completely different in every other respect.

Example: the data set $\{9,10,11\}$ has mean $10$ with very little spread, while the data set $\{0,10,20\}$ also has mean $10$ but is far more spread out. A decision-maker relying only on the reported mean (say, average product lifespan) would have no way to distinguish a product line with highly consistent lifespans from one with wildly variable (some failing very early, some lasting very long) lifespans — a potentially critical difference for quality control or warranty planning.

Practice 40

A sample of 6 resistor values (in ohms) is: $98, 102, 99, 101, 100, 100$. Find the sample mean, sample variance, and sample standard deviation.

Solution

$\bar x=\dfrac{98+102+99+101+100+100}{6}=\dfrac{600}{6}=100$.

Deviations squared: $4,4,1,1,0,0$; sum $=10$. $s^2=\dfrac{10}{5}=2$.

$s=\sqrt2\approx1.414$ ohms.
