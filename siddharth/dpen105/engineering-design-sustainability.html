Topic 1 · Week 1
Sustainability Fundamentals
SDGs · triple bottom line · core definitions

Sustainability Principles

Sustainability is development that meets current needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet theirs. The triple bottom line (3BL) framework balances three dimensions: economic prosperity, social equity, and environmental integrity. The 17 UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) provide a global roadmap for sustainable development across all sectors.

Triple Bottom Line (TBL)
$$\text{Sustainability} = \text{Environment} + \text{Social} + \text{Economic}$$

UN SDGs in Engineering

  • SDG 6: Clean water and sanitation
  • SDG 7: Affordable clean energy
  • SDG 9: Industry, innovation, infrastructure
  • SDG 12: Responsible consumption & production
  • SDG 13: Climate action

Worked Examples

Example 1: Identifying TBL Impacts

Scenario: A renewable energy company installs solar panels on farmland.

Environmental: Reduces CO₂ emissions, displaces habitat → net positive if mitigation planned

Social: Jobs created, but local farmers lose land → requires compensation

Economic: Lower energy costs long-term, but initial capital investment high

Conclusion: Sustainable if all three pillars addressed holistically.

Example 2: SDG Alignment

Design Challenge: Improve water access in rural communities.

Primary SDG: SDG 6 (Clean Water)

Co-benefits: SDG 1 (No Poverty—income from water services), SDG 5 (Gender Equality—women's time freed)

Key insight: Single interventions can advance multiple SDGs simultaneously.

Example 3: TBL Trade-off Analysis

Scenario: Factory automation replaces 100 jobs but reduces emissions by 40%.

Economic win (efficiency) but social loss (jobs). Solution: reskilling programs, phased transition, profit-sharing → balance all three pillars.

Example 4: Measuring Sustainability

Company Metrics: Carbon emissions (kg CO₂e), water usage (m³), waste recycled (%), employee satisfaction (%), local hiring (%).

Balanced scorecard across Environment, Social, Economic ensures comprehensive sustainability assessment.

Practice Problems

Problems
  1. Explain why "environmental only" sustainability is insufficient. What happens if you optimize only the green pillar?
    Answer
    Short-term success but social inequality (unemployment, poor labor practices) + economic collapse (can't sustain business). TBL is necessary for true durability.
  2. A product uses 100% recycled materials but requires 10x the energy to produce. Is it sustainable? Justify.
    Answer
    Not inherently sustainable. Must compare full lifecycle energy (LCA) vs virgin material. If renewable energy used, yes; if coal-fired, environmental gain offset. Context matters.
  3. Which SDG does wastewater treatment support? Name 2 secondary SDGs it advances.
    Answer
    Primary: SDG 6 (Clean Water). Secondary: SDG 3 (Good Health—prevents disease), SDG 14 (Life Below Water—protects ecosystems).
  4. A sustainable development project has high upfront cost but low long-term cost. How do you justify it economically?
    Answer
    Use Net Present Value (NPV) or payback period analysis. Discount future savings to present value. If NPV positive, investment is economically sound despite initial cost.
Topic 2 · Week 2
Life Cycle Assessment
ISO 14040 · functional units · impact categories · LCA calculations

LCA Framework

Life Cycle Assessment (ISO 14040:2006) quantifies environmental impacts across a product's entire lifecycle: raw material extraction, manufacturing, transport, use, end-of-life. The functional unit defines what is being assessed (e.g., "1 kg of packaging" or "1 MJ of energy delivered"). Four phases: goal & scope, inventory analysis, impact assessment, interpretation.

LCA Impact Formula
$$\text{Total Impact} = \sum (\text{Activity Data}_i \times \text{Emission Factor}_i)$$

Key Impact Categories

  • Global Warming Potential (GWP): kg CO₂-eq
  • Acidification Potential (AP): kg SO₂-eq
  • Eutrophication Potential (EP): kg PO₄-eq
  • Ozone Depletion Potential (ODP): kg CFC-11-eq
  • Photochemical Ozone Creation: kg C₂H₄-eq

Worked Examples

Example 1: LCA Boundary Definition

Product: Single-use plastic bottle.

Cradle-to-grave: Extraction → manufacturing → transport → use → landfill.

Cradle-to-gate: Stops at factory exit (manufacturing + transport to retailer).

Cradle-to-grave best for policy; cradle-to-gate for producer comparisons.

Example 2: GWP Calculation

Scenario: Producing 1 kg of aluminum requires 12 kWh electricity. Grid: 0.5 kg CO₂/kWh.

Calculation: GWP = 12 kWh × 0.5 kg CO₂/kWh = 6 kg CO₂-eq per kg Al

Note: Primary aluminum is energy-intensive; recycled aluminum ~5% of impact.

Example 3: Functional Unit Comparison

Question: Compare glass vs plastic bottles for 1 L of beverage.

Glass: Heavier (500g), impacts from mining. Reusable → lower per-use impact.

Plastic: Lighter (30g), fossil fuels. Single-use → higher per-use impact.

If glass reused 10x: ~0.2 kg CO₂-eq/use vs plastic ~0.1 kg CO₂-eq. Context (reuse rate) critical.

Example 4: Hotspot Identification

LCA Result for Smartphone: 40% manufacturing, 55% use phase (electricity), 5% transport.

Action: Optimize electricity grid (renewable energy) → biggest impact reduction. Secondary: manufacturing efficiency.

Practice Problems

Problems
  1. Define a functional unit for a t-shirt. Should it be "1 shirt" or "1 kg clothing" or "clothing service for 5 years"?
    Answer
    Best: "Clothing service for X wears" (functional). Normalizes durability differences. "1 shirt" acceptable for quick comparison but biased toward heavier/durable designs.
  2. An LCA shows 80% of impact from electricity during manufacturing. The factory switches to 100% renewable. What's the maximum % reduction in total LCA impact?
    Answer
    Maximum ~80% reduction (if electricity was 80% and now 0). Total impact: 20% (other phases) remains. Real-world: ~70% reduction (losses in renewable conversion, transmission).
  3. Why is recycling not always the environmental winner? Give an example.
    Answer
    Recycling energy can be high. Example: recycling mixed plastic waste uses more energy than virgin plastic for some types. Also: contamination, transport distances increase impact. Case-by-case LCA needed.
  4. A product's use phase has zero emissions (no electricity). How does this affect LCA interpretation?
    Answer
    Focus shifts to manufacturing, end-of-life. Durability becomes critical—longer life amortizes manufacturing impact. Design for longevity prioritized over efficiency during use.
Topic 3 · Week 3
Carbon Footprint
Scope 1/2/3 · CO₂e · GWP · carbon calculations

Scope Framework (GHG Protocol)

Carbon footprints categorize greenhouse gas emissions by source using the GHG Protocol. Scope 1: Direct emissions from owned/controlled sources (e.g., fuel combustion, production). Scope 2: Indirect emissions from purchased electricity/steam. Scope 3: All other indirect emissions (supply chain, product use, waste). CO₂ equivalent (CO₂e) converts all GHGs to a common metric using Global Warming Potential (GWP).

CO₂ Equivalent (GWP)
$$\text{CO}_2\text{e} = \sum (\text{GHG mass}_i \times \text{GWP}_i)$$

where GWP₁₀₀: CH₄=28, N₂O=265, CO₂=1 (100-year horizon)

Carbon Footprint Formula
$$\text{CF} = \text{Scope 1} + \text{Scope 2} + \text{Scope 3}$$

Typical Scope Breakdown (Manufacturer)

  • Scope 1: ~10–20% (direct combustion, on-site)
  • Scope 2: ~5–15% (purchased electricity)
  • Scope 3: ~65–85% (supply chain, logistics, customer use)

Worked Examples

Example 1: Scope Classification

Factory emissions sources:

Scope 1: Natural gas furnace (50 kg CO₂/day), company vehicle (10 kg CO₂/day)

Scope 2: Grid electricity (200 kWh/day × 0.4 kg CO₂/kWh = 80 kg CO₂/day)

Scope 3: Supplier emissions (500 kg CO₂/day), customer transport (300 kg CO₂/day)

Total daily CF = 60 + 80 + 800 = 940 kg CO₂e

Example 2: GWP Conversion

Biogas plant emissions: 100 kg CH₄, 5 kg N₂O, 500 kg CO₂.

Calculation: (100 × 28) + (5 × 265) + (500 × 1) = 2,800 + 1,325 + 500 = 4,625 kg CO₂e

Methane dominates—CH₄ abatement is priority mitigation strategy.

Example 3: Scope Reduction Strategy

Company Goal: Reduce carbon footprint by 50%.

Baseline: Scope 1 (20%), Scope 2 (30%), Scope 3 (50%).

Strategy: Switch to renewable energy (Scope 2 → 0), improve logistics (Scope 3 → 25%), efficiency (Scope 1 → 10%).

New total: 35% (reduction of 30% from baseline, short of 50% target)

Example 4: Personal Carbon Footprint

Annual emissions: Car (3 kg CO₂/day × 250 days = 750 kg), flights (2,000 km × 0.2 kg CO₂/km = 400 kg), home energy (5 kg CO₂/day × 365 = 1,825 kg).

Total: 2,975 kg CO₂e/year (~3 tonnes). Global average ~4 tonnes; target for 1.5°C: ~2.3 tonnes → personal changes needed.

Practice Problems

Problems
  1. A company's Scope 3 emissions are 10x Scope 1. Should they focus carbon reduction efforts on Scope 1 or Scope 3?
    Answer
    Scope 3, because it's the dominant source. Maximum impact per effort. However, Scope 1 (owned sources) is controllable—hybrid approach: quick wins in Scope 1, systemic changes in Scope 3.
  2. A factory produces 1,000 tonnes/year, emitting 50 kg CO₂e per tonne. If they switch to renewable electricity, Scope 2 drops from 15,000 to 1,500 kg CO₂e. What % reduction in total if Scope 3 is 80,000 kg CO₂e?
    Answer
    Total before: 50,000 + 15,000 + 80,000 = 145,000 kg CO₂e. After: 50,000 + 1,500 + 80,000 = 131,500 kg CO₂e. Reduction: 13,500/145,000 = 9.3%.
  3. Why is Scope 3 often the largest carbon footprint for consumer goods companies?
    Answer
    Product use phase dominates (e.g., fuel consumption in cars, electricity in appliances). Supply chain and distribution also significant. Company operations (Scope 1&2) small by comparison.
  4. Explain the difference between carbon footprint (CF) and life cycle assessment (LCA).
    Answer
    CF: measures only greenhouse gas emissions (kg CO₂e). LCA: broader, includes multiple impact categories (acidification, eutrophication, etc.). CF is subset of LCA focused on climate.
Topic 4 · Week 4
Renewable Energy
Solar PV · wind · energy calculations

Renewable Energy Basics

Renewable energy sources—solar, wind, hydro, geothermal, biomass—are replenished naturally and produce zero direct emissions. Solar photovoltaic (PV) converts sunlight directly to electricity via semiconductors. Wind energy uses kinetic energy of air movement. Key metrics: nameplate capacity (kW), capacity factor (%), and energy production (kWh/year). Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) compares economics across technologies.

Solar PV Energy Output
$$E = G \times A \times \eta_{\text{PV}} \times \eta_{\text{inv}}$$

where $G$ = solar irradiance (W/m²), $A$ = panel area (m²), $\eta_{\text{PV}}$ = panel efficiency, $\eta_{\text{inv}}$ = inverter efficiency

Wind Power Output
$$P = \frac{1}{2} \rho A v^3 C_p$$

where $\rho$ = air density (1.225 kg/m³), $A$ = rotor sweep area (m²), $v$ = wind speed (m/s), $C_p$ = power coefficient (~0.59 theoretical max)

Worked Examples

Example 1: Solar PV System Design

Scenario: Rooftop system: 20 m² panels, efficiency 20%, average irradiance 5 kWh/m²/day, inverter 95% efficient.

Daily output: 5 kWh/m²/day × 20 m² × 0.20 × 0.95 = 19 kWh/day

Annual: 19 × 365 = 6,935 kWh/year

Typical household uses ~10–15 MWh/year → covers ~45–70% of demand.

Example 2: Wind Turbine Power

Turbine specs: Rotor diameter 100 m (area ≈ 7,854 m²), wind speed 12 m/s, $C_p = 0.45$.

Calculation: $P = 0.5 × 1.225 × 7,854 × 12³ × 0.45 = 3.8$ MW

With capacity factor 35% (typical onshore): ~1.33 MW average → 11.7 GWh/year.

Example 3: LCOE Comparison

System costs: Solar $800/kW, wind $1,600/kW. Lifetimes: 25 years (solar), 20 years (wind).

Solar LCOE: $0.04–0.06/kWh (high capacity factor regions)

Wind LCOE: $0.05–0.08/kWh (depends on wind resource)

Hybrid systems often optimal—complement seasonal variations.

Example 4: Energy Storage Requirement

Grid scenario: 40% solar, 30% wind, 30% dispatchable (gas/hydro). Daily solar peak: noon; wind variable.

Storage needed: ~4–6 hours of battery capacity to smooth supply-demand mismatch.

Cost: ~$200/kWh → significant investment, but battery prices falling ~15%/year.

Practice Problems

Problems
  1. A solar panel has efficiency 18% and receives 1,000 W/m² (peak conditions). How much energy does a 5 m² panel produce in 6 peak-sun-hours?
    Answer
    Output = 1,000 W/m² × 5 m² × 0.18 × 6 hours = 5,400 Wh = 5.4 kWh. Daily = 5.4 kWh (assuming 6 peak hours equivalent).
  2. Wind speed doubles. How does power output change (assume $C_p$ constant)?
    Answer
    Power ∝ $v³$. If $v$ doubles: $P_{\text{new}} = (2)³ × P_{\text{old}} = 8 × P_{\text{old}}$. Doubling wind speed increases power 8-fold—wind is exponentially sensitive to speed.
  3. A 10 MW solar farm has capacity factor 25%. How many households (12 MWh/year each) can it supply?
    Answer
    Annual output: 10 MW × 0.25 × 8,760 hours = 21,900 MWh. Households: 21,900/12 ≈ 1,825 homes. Capacity factor varies by location (15–35% typical).
  4. Why is battery storage crucial for 100% renewable grids, and what's the limiting factor?
    Answer
    Renewable output intermittent. Storage smooths supply-demand, prevents curtailment. Limiting factor: cost (still ~$200/kWh) and duration (6–24 hour storage needed). Grid-scale solutions: thermal, pumped hydro, green hydrogen.
Topic 5 · Week 5
Water & Resources
Water footprint · resource efficiency

Water Footprint & Resource Efficiency

Water footprint measures freshwater consumption across supply chains. Three components: blue water (surface/groundwater), green water (rain), grey water (pollution dilution). Resource efficiency minimizes material and energy use per unit output. Circular design principles—reduce, reuse, recycle—extend resource availability and reduce environmental burden.

Water Footprint
$$\text{WF} = \text{Blue WF} + \text{Green WF} + \text{Grey WF} \quad (\text{m}^3/\text{year})$$
Material Efficiency
$$\eta_{\text{mat}} = \frac{\text{Useful material output}}{\text{Total material input}} \times 100\%$$

Water Stress by Industry

  • Cotton: 10,000 L per kg (highest)
  • Beef: 15,000 L per kg (grazing + feed)
  • Semiconductor: 300 L per kg (processing)
  • Steel: 25 L per kg (cooling)

Worked Examples

Example 1: Water Footprint Calculation

Product: 1 kg cotton t-shirt → water footprint 10,000 L (blue water) + 5,000 L (green water) + 1,000 L (grey) = 16,000 L.

Lifetime: 100 wears → 160 L per wear

Mitigation: Organic cotton (30% lower), recycled content (90% lower), durability (extend life).

Example 2: Resource Efficiency in Manufacturing

Scenario: Aluminum casting: 100 kg raw ingot input, 80 kg finished product output. Scrap recycled: 90% reused internally.

First-pass efficiency: 80% (20 kg waste)

Effective efficiency: 80% + 18% (recycled scrap) ≈ 98% total material use.

Example 3: Water Reuse System

Factory cooling system: 1,000 m³/day fresh water used once, discharged.

Improvement: Closed-loop cooling with 95% recycling → fresh water demand drops to 50 m³/day.

Annual savings: 346,500 m³ (95% reduction) + 10 million USD (water cost + treatment).

Example 4: Critical Materials Risk

Supply chain analysis: Smartphone contains 40+ elements. Rare earths (Y, Nd) concentrated in single region (70% China).

Risk: Supply disruption, price volatility, mining impacts.

Strategy: Design for disassembly → recover 90% of rare earths from e-waste → circular supply.

Practice Problems

Problems
  1. A textile factory uses 1,000,000 L/day of water. If they implement a 40% reduction strategy, how many days of water savings in a year?
    Answer
    Reduction: 1,000,000 × 0.4 = 400,000 L/day saved. Annual: 400,000 × 365 = 146 million L. Equivalent to ~58 days of original consumption. Translates to ~50 million USD savings (at $0.34/kL).
  2. Why is blue water (freshwater) more critical to manage than green water (rain)?
    Answer
    Blue water depletes local aquifers/rivers—directly impacts communities. Green water is naturally replenished by rainfall. Grey water pollution reflects local waste treatment. Blue water scarcity is acute in arid regions (Middle East, North Africa, India).
  3. A product has 95% material efficiency but 50% recycling rate at end-of-life. What's the total effective circular material efficiency?
    Answer
    Complex: depends on recycling quality. If 50% of end-of-life material recovered at 100% purity → 95% × 50% = 47.5% circular rate. But if recycled material degraded, effective rate lower. Design matters: DfD (design for disassembly) improves recovery rates to 80%+.
  4. List 3 strategies to reduce product water footprint without redesigning the core product.
    Answer
    1. Sourcing shift (e.g., organic cotton → 30% less water). 2. Supply chain optimization (local sourcing → less water in transport, irrigation). 3. Extend product life (same footprint amortized over more years/uses). 4. Certification (only high-water-efficiency suppliers).
Topic 6 · Week 6
Green Building
LEED/Green Star · embodied energy · passive design

Green Building Principles

LEED (Leadership in Energy & Environmental Design) and Green Star are certification systems evaluating building sustainability across site, energy, water, materials, and indoor environment. Embodied energy is the total energy required to produce building materials (cement, steel, glass). Operational energy is energy consumed during building use (heating, cooling, lighting). Passive design minimizes operational needs through orientation, insulation, and natural ventilation.

Building Carbon Footprint
$$\text{Total CF} = \text{Embodied Energy} + \text{Operational Energy (60 years)}$$
Annual Operational EUI (Energy Use Intensity)
$$\text{EUI} = \frac{\text{Annual Energy (kWh)}}{\text{Floor Area (m}^2)} \quad (\text{kWh/m}^2/\text{year})$$

Typical Embodied Energy (Materials)

  • Concrete: 1–2 MJ/kg
  • Steel: 25–35 MJ/kg (primary); 6–10 MJ/kg (recycled)
  • Glass: 12–15 MJ/kg
  • Wood: 2–5 MJ/kg (renewable advantage)

Worked Examples

Example 1: Embodied vs Operational Energy

Building 1: High-performance (low operational EUI: 100 kWh/m²/year), high embodied (structural steel → 3,000 MWh total).

Building 2: Standard (operational EUI: 300 kWh/m²/year), low embodied (2,000 MWh).

Building 1: 10,000 m² × 100 × 60 years = 60,000 MWh operational + 3,000 = 63,000 MWh total

Building 2: 10,000 m² × 300 × 60 years = 180,000 MWh + 2,000 = 182,000 MWh total

Building 1 wins by 3x despite embodied penalty—operational dominates over 60-year lifecycle.

Example 2: LEED Scorecard

Project points: Site (8/26), Water (18/24), Energy (15/33), Materials (12/26), Indoor (8/15).

Total: 61/100 → LEED Silver (50–60 points)

Improvement path: Renewable energy (+8 pts) + better insulation (+5 pts) → Gold (80+ possible).

Example 3: Passive Design Savings

Scenario: Office building, conventional cooling 150 kW peak load.

Passive strategies: North-facing glazing (40% reduction), natural ventilation (30% reduction), thermal mass (20% reduction).

New peak load: 150 × 0.6 × 0.7 × 0.8 = 50 kW. Annual savings: ~200 MWh.

Example 4: Material Substitution Impact

Choice: 100 m³ of concrete (embodied: 150 MJ/kg) vs timber (3 MJ/kg).

Concrete: 100 × 2,400 kg/m³ × 1.5 MJ/kg = 360,000 MJ

Timber: 100 × 600 kg/m³ × 3 MJ/kg = 180,000 MJ

Timber: 50% less embodied energy + carbon sequestration → strategic choice.

Practice Problems

Problems
  1. A building has embodied energy of 5,000 MWh and operational EUI of 120 kWh/m²/year (10,000 m²). How long until operational energy equals embodied?
    Answer
    Annual operational: 120 × 10,000 = 1,200 MWh/year. Time: 5,000/1,200 ≈ 4.2 years. After 4–5 years, operational dominates lifecycle carbon—emphasizes importance of efficient design.
  2. Why is wood preferred over concrete in passive/low-carbon buildings, despite higher initial cost?
    Answer
    1. Embodied energy: 60% lower (3 vs 1.5 MJ/kg). 2. Carbon sequestration: living trees capture CO₂. 3. Thermal mass: wood regulates temperature naturally. 4. Renewable if from certified forests. 5. LCA shows payback in 5–10 years.
  3. A building reduces operational energy by 50% via renewable energy. Does this guarantee LEED Platinum?
    Answer
    No. LEED requires minimum scores across 5 categories (site, water, energy, materials, indoor). Renewable energy alone gets ~15 points (max 33). Must score well in other categories: water efficiency, materials sourcing, IAQ. Holistic approach needed.
  4. Explain why embodied energy matters more for low-energy buildings.
    Answer
    In conventional buildings (~300 EUI), operational dominates (90%+ of lifecycle). In Passive House (~15 EUI), embodied can be 30–50% of lifecycle. % of embodied carbon increases—material choices now critical. Amortization period longer (50+ years).
Topic 7 · Week 7
Circular Economy
Waste hierarchy · recycling · circular vs linear

Circular Economy Framework

Circular economy contrasts with linear ("take-make-dispose") by designing products for multiple lifecycles: reuse, repair, remanufacture, recycle. Waste hierarchy prioritizes: reduce → reuse → recycle → recover → dispose. Circular design considers end-of-life from inception, enabling material recovery and secondary markets. Key metrics: recycling rate (%), average lifespan, material recovery percentage, and cost savings from secondary materials.

Waste Hierarchy Efficiency
$$\text{Resource Efficiency} = \frac{\text{Material recovered (useful)}}{\text{Total material input}} \times 100\%$$
Recycling Rate
$$\text{RR} = \frac{\text{Material recycled}}{\text{Total waste generated}} \times 100\%$$

Circular vs Linear Comparison

AspectLinearCircular
LifespanSingle useMultiple cycles
Material lossHigh (~80%)Low (~10%)
Cost/unitLower upfrontHigher upfront, lower total
Environmental impactHigh (extraction/waste)Low (reuse, no mining)

Worked Examples

Example 1: Waste Hierarchy Application

Scenario: 10,000 kg plastic waste from manufacturing.

Reduce: 3,000 kg eliminated via process efficiency

Reuse: 2,000 kg (containers shipped to customers, returned, reused)

Recycle: 3,500 kg (processed into pellets for new products)

Recover: 1,000 kg (energy from incineration)

Dispose: 500 kg (contaminated → landfill)

Diversion rate: 95% from landfill (5% disposed).

Example 2: Circular vs Linear Comparison

Plastic bottle: Virgin production 2 kg CO₂/bottle (extraction, refining). Linear: single use → landfill → cost $0.20.

Circular design: 0.5 kg CO₂ (recycled material), 0.2 kg CO₂ (collection/sorting) = 0.7 kg CO₂ total. Recycled bottle → cost $0.30 (higher), but environmental payback 3x lower.

Incentive: Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) makes producer pay for end-of-life → circular becomes economically optimal.

Example 3: Product Design for Circularity

Electronics device: Linear design (glued, soldered) → 10% recycling rate. Circular design (modular, snap-fit) → 85% recycling rate.

Annual production: 100,000 units, 2 kg/unit → 200,000 kg material/year

Linear waste to landfill: 180,000 kg/year

Circular waste to landfill: 30,000 kg/year (75% reduction)

Example 4: Secondary Material Economics

Cost comparison: Virgin aluminum $2.50/kg, recycled aluminum $1.50/kg.

Annual savings: 1,000 tonnes recycled material × $1.00/kg = $1,000,000

Collection infrastructure cost: $500,000/year (sorting, transport)

Net savings: $500,000/year + avoided virgin extraction environmental cost (~$2–5/kg equivalent).

Practice Problems

Problems
  1. A landfill receives 10,000 tonnes/year from a city. Current recycling rate: 20%. What's the max waste reduction if the city achieves 70% diversion via reduce+reuse+recycle?
    Answer
    Current landfill: 80% × 10,000 = 8,000 tonnes. With 70% diversion: 30% × 10,000 = 3,000 tonnes to landfill. Reduction: 5,000 tonnes/year (63% decrease). Environmental benefits: avoided methane (~2,500 tonnes CO₂e/year).
  2. Why does recycling plastics sometimes have higher environmental cost than virgin plastic?
    Answer
    Collection, sorting, washing, reprocessing energy-intensive. Contamination reduces quality. If recycled plastic mixed grades (downgrade), value lost. Solution: design for mono-material, source-separation, and high recycling quality. Mechanical recycling profitable only for clean, sorted streams.
  3. Compare lifecycle costs: linear (single-use) vs circular (reusable) products with 5 uses. Linear: $1/unit, Circular: $10 upfront + $1/refurb.
    Answer
    Linear total: 5 × $1 = $5. Circular total: $10 + 4 × $1 = $14. Circular loses financially, but if environmental cost (carbon, waste) included: avoids ~10 kg CO₂ and 5 kg landfill waste. True cost accounting favors circular if externalities priced.
  4. A company achieves 95% recycling rate but only 10% of recycled material closes the loop (becomes new products). What's the actual circular rate?
    Answer
    Actual circular rate: 10% (only 10% truly returns to production). 85% recycled but downcycled or exported. Key insight: high recycling ≠ circular economy. Must track closed loops. Solution: Design for closed loops, establish take-back programs, invest in secondary material demand.
Topic 8 · Week 8
Design for Environment
DfE strategies · eco-design · green chemistry

Design for Environment (DfE) Principles

DfE integrates environmental considerations into product design from conception. Key strategies: material selection (low-impact), manufacturing efficiency, transportability (lightweight), durability, and end-of-life recovery. Green chemistry principles minimize hazardous substances and waste during production. Eco-design balances performance, cost, and environmental footprint through systematic evaluation and trade-off analysis.

DfE Impact Score
$$I_{\text{DfE}} = w_1 \times E_{\text{mat}} + w_2 \times E_{\text{mfg}} + w_3 \times E_{\text{use}} + w_4 \times E_{\text{eol}}$$

Weighted environmental impact across lifecycle phases

Green Chemistry Principles (12 Principles)

  • Prevent waste rather than treat it
  • Design safer chemicals
  • Use renewable feedstocks
  • Reduce hazardous substances
  • Minimize waste and by-products

Worked Examples

Example 1: Material Eco-Selection

Product: Smartphone case. Options: (1) Virgin plastic 0.1 kg, 8 MJ/kg. (2) Recycled plastic 0.1 kg, 2 MJ/kg. (3) Bio-based 0.1 kg, 1.5 MJ/kg.

Option 1: 0.8 MJ embodied energy

Option 2: 0.2 MJ (75% reduction)

Option 3: 0.15 MJ (82% reduction) + carbon-neutral if compostable at end

Choice: Bio-based + design for compostability → lowest impact.

Example 2: Manufacturing Process Optimization

Current process: Injection molding, 20% scrap waste, energy 2 kWh per part.

Improvements: Optimize mold design (scrap reduction to 5%), closed-loop cooling (energy to 1.5 kWh).

Annual impact (1M units): Scrap reduction: 150,000 kg plastic saved. Energy: 0.5 million kWh saved (~250 tonnes CO₂).

Example 3: Design for Longevity

Scenario: Consumer electronics, original lifespan 3 years, redesigned for 6 years (modular, upgradable).

Amortized footprint: Original 2 kg CO₂/year of use. Redesigned 3 kg CO₂/year (higher embodied due to modularity), but spread over 6 years = 0.5 kg CO₂/year.

Per-year impact 75% lower through extended life.

Example 4: Green Chemistry Example—Coating

Traditional polyurethane coating: VOC emissions 0.5 kg/L, hazardous solvents, long cure time (energy input).

Green alternative: Water-based polyurethane: VOC 0.05 kg/L (90% reduction), non-hazardous, low energy cure.

Cost premium: +15% material, but worker health benefits, reduced waste treatment cost, regulatory compliance premium.

Practice Problems

Problems
  1. A designer must reduce product weight by 20% while maintaining strength. Which DfE strategies apply? List 3.
    Answer
    1. Material substitution (aluminum → carbon fiber, density 1.6 vs 2.7, -40% weight). 2. Topology optimization (remove non-critical material, FEA-guided). 3. Hollow/lattice structures (cellular design, same strength with 60% weight reduction). 4. Manufacturing precision (tighter tolerances reduce safety factor → lighter design).
  2. Explain the trade-off: lightweight design (less material) vs durability (more material for strength).
    Answer
    If lightweight shortens lifespan: amortized impact worse. Example: thin plastic (light, short life) vs thick plastic (heavy, long life). Solution: use superior material (higher strength-to-weight ratio: composites, advanced alloys) to achieve both light + durable. Lifespan dominates long-term impact.
  3. A chemical process switches from hazardous carcinogenic solvent (cost $2/L) to benign alternative (cost $5/L). ROI calculation for 10,000 L/year usage.
    Answer
    Direct cost: +$30,000/year. Benefits: reduced worker health risk (avoided medical costs ~$50k/year), waste disposal savings (~$20k/year, less hazardous treatment), regulatory compliance (avoided fines ~$100k potential). Net: -$30k cost, +$170k benefits = $140k/year benefit. ROI positive despite price premium.
  4. Design a smartphone case using 3 DfE principles. Describe material, manufacturing, and end-of-life choices.
    Answer
    Material: Bio-based TPU (compostable). Manufacturing: Injection molding, 95% scrap recycled closed-loop. End-of-life: Take-back program, composting facility, nutrients return to soil. Durability: 2-year lifespan via design for repair (replaceable corners). Cost premium: ~10%, offset by avoided landfill and positive brand perception.

Economic Sustainability
NPV · payback period · cost-benefit analysis

Financial Analysis for Sustainable Projects

Sustainable projects often require upfront capital investment with long-term returns. Net Present Value (NPV) discounts future cash flows to present-day equivalents, enabling fair comparison across time horizons. Payback period is the time to recover initial investment from annual savings. Cost-benefit analysis includes non-monetary environmental and social benefits. Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) normalizes energy cost across different technologies with different capital, operating, and lifespan profiles.

Net Present Value (NPV)
$$\text{NPV} = -C_0 + \sum_{t=1}^{n} \frac{CF_t}{(1+r)^t}$$

where $C_0$ = initial investment, $CF_t$ = annual cash flow, $r$ = discount rate, $t$ = year

Payback Period
$$\text{PBP} = \frac{\text{Initial Investment}}{\text{Annual Savings}} \quad (\text{years})$$

Worked Examples

Example 1: NPV for Solar Investment

Solar system: Capital $50,000, annual savings $8,000, lifespan 25 years, discount rate 5%.

Year 1: PV = 8,000 / 1.05¹ = $7,619

Year 2: PV = 8,000 / 1.05² = $7,257

Sum (Year 1–25): $8,000 × [(1 - (1.05)⁻²⁵) / 0.05] ≈ $139,000

NPV: -$50,000 + $139,000 = $89,000 → Investment is economically sound.

Example 2: Payback Period Comparison

Energy efficiency retrofit: Insulation upgrade costs $20,000, saves $5,000/year.

Payback period: $20,000 / $5,000 = 4 years

LED lighting retrofit: Cost $5,000, saves $1,200/year.

Payback period: $5,000 / $1,200 ≈ 4.2 years

Recommendation: Insulation has longer savings tail (30+ years lifespan) → higher NPV despite similar payback. Combined package: 8-year amortization → strong financial case.

Example 3: Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA)

Project: Wastewater treatment plant, capital $10M, 20-year life.

Financial costs: $10M capital + $1M/year operations = $30M total

Financial benefits: Water sales $500k/year, avoided fines $200k/year → $14M NPV benefit (over 20 years)

Non-financial benefits: Ecosystem health ($5M), public health ($8M), job creation ($2M) → $15M equivalent value

Total B/C ratio: (14 + 15) / 30 = 0.97 → Nearly breakeven without environmental valuation; essential with environmental benefits included.

Example 4: LCOE Comparison

Energy sources: Coal plant ($1B capital, 40-year life, 0.05/kWh fuel), Solar ($200M capital, 25-year life, 0.01/kWh operational).

Coal LCOE: (~$0.08–0.12/kWh typical)

Solar LCOE: (~$0.04–0.06/kWh typical)

Solar cost-competitive despite lower capacity factor (25% vs 85%). Coal's hidden costs (carbon, health) not reflected in LCOE → true cost analysis favors renewable.

Practice Problems

Problems
  1. A sustainable product costs $100 (upfront), saves $20/year in water/energy. What's the payback period? Is it worth the investment for 10-year lifespan?
    Answer
    Payback: $100 / $20 = 5 years. Over 10-year lifespan: $100 cost + (10 - 5) × $20 = $100 + $100 = $200 total saved vs $100 invested. ROI: 100%. Yes, worthwhile. Longer lifespan (20+ years) makes it even more attractive.
  2. If discount rate increases from 3% to 7%, how does NPV of long-term investments change?
    Answer
    Higher discount rate reduces present value of future cash flows. Example: $1,000 in Year 10: at 3% discount ≈ $744; at 7% ≈ $508 (32% lower). Long-term projects (20+ years) are penalized. Implication: if policy wants to incentivize renewables, lower discount rates (through subsidies, tax breaks) help NPV breakeven.
  3. A company invests $500k in waste reduction (closed-loop process). Payback is 10 years. But competition prices it at 15-year payback. Why choose the 10-year option?
    Answer
    Shorter payback = lower risk, faster capital recovery, higher NPV if lifespan is fixed. 10-year payback leaves 10+ years of pure savings; 15-year payback leaves only 5 years. Also: tech risk lower (proven ROI sooner), regulatory risk lower (compliance guaranteed before tech obsolescence). Financial prudence favors faster payback in uncertain environments.
  4. Calculate NPV for a green building retrofit: $200k investment, $15k annual savings, 20-year lifespan, 4% discount rate.
    Answer
    NPV = -200k + 15k × [(1 - (1.04)⁻²⁰) / 0.04] = -200k + 15k × 13.59 = -200k + 203.85k ≈ +$3.85k. Marginally positive (near-breakeven). If discount rate drops to 3%: NPV = -200k + 15k × 14.88 ≈ +$23k (better). Add non-financial benefits (health, carbon): NPV strongly positive.

Case Studies

Real-World Sustainability Challenges

Case studies apply theory to practice. This topic examines real projects, analyzing how multi-disciplinary teams balance environmental, social, economic constraints. Design thinking (empathy, ideation, prototyping, testing) drives innovation. Decision-making frameworks integrate quantitative LCA, cost-benefit, and qualitative stakeholder input. Key learnings: tradeoffs are unavoidable, context-dependency critical, and iterative improvement necessary.

Sustainability Decision Framework
$$\text{Decision Score} = w_E \times S_{\text{env}} + w_S \times S_{\text{social}} + w_E \times S_{\text{econ}}$$

Weighted multi-criteria decision analysis; weights vary by stakeholder

Case Study Examples

Case Study 1: Patagonia Regenerative Organic Cotton

Challenge: Conventional cotton uses 16% of global pesticides, high water (9,000 L/kg), poor labor conditions.

Solution: Switch to organic cotton (GOTS certified), then regenerative agriculture (soil health focus), fair-trade labor practices.

Trade-off: Cost +40%, yield -10% initially. Scale reduced short-term, brand premium enabled price increase.

Outcome: Carbon-negative supply chain (soil sequestration), improved farmer income (+30%), improved brand value. NPV positive after 5 years due to price premium and supply stability.

Lesson: Premium market acceptance for authentic sustainability → business case emerges at scale.

Case Study 2: China's Circular Economy Parks

Challenge: Rapid industrialization, severe pollution, resource depletion. Linear economy model unsustainable.

Strategy: Design integrated industrial parks where waste from one factory = input for another (symbiosis).

Example: Shuanghuan Industrial Park (Guitang): Sugar refinery → molasses → ethanol plant → distillery waste → biogas → power generation. Waste: near zero.

Results: 30% energy reduction, 50% water reduction, $50M savings annually across park.

Lesson: Systems-level thinking (ecosystem approach) unlocks efficiency impossible at company level.

Case Study 3: Singapore's Zero-Energy District

Challenge: Urban heating, energy demand 300 MW for district, tropical climate (cooling-dominated).

Design: Rooftop solar (50 MW), district cooling (waste heat recycling), smart grid (real-time demand response), passive architecture.

Result: Net-zero annual energy (export surplus 20 GWh some months).

Cost: Capital +30% vs conventional, payback 8 years. Job creation: 500 FTE (operations, maintenance, smart systems).

Lesson: Integrated design (energy + water + buildings) essential for urban sustainability; economics improve with scale and policy support.

Case Study 4: IKEA Circular Supply Chain

Challenge: Growth-driven business model requires massive material throughput; sustainability requires circular loops.

Initiatives: Design for disassembly (all products), take-back programs, resale/upcycling, material recovery.

Targets: 100% recycled/renewable materials by 2030, zero waste to landfill by 2030.

Barriers: Consumer behavior (convenience vs returns), reverse logistics cost, material degradation in multi-cycle loops.

Outcome: Secondary product line (IKEA Uppskattad) launched, take-back rate now 5% (target 25% by 2030). ROI: collection network provides secondary sales channel, loyalty improvement.

Lesson: Circular business models require systemic change (product design, supply chain, consumer engagement, revenue model). Profitability emerges long-term.

Practice Problems

Problems
  1. Analyze the Patagonia case: If organic cotton supply can only meet 50% of demand, how should the company prioritize allocation among product lines?
    Answer
    Criteria: (1) Highest-impact product (water/pesticide intensive) → prioritize. (2) Brand positioning (premium line) → maximize visibility. (3) Scalability (which supply chains can grow fastest?) → invest. Recommended: Start with high-visibility premium line (e.g., core T-shirts), transition mainstream as supply grows, certify farmers for scale-up. NPV modeling shows payback if price premium sustained.
  2. The Shuanghuan example shows 30% energy reduction through symbiosis. Identify 2 barriers preventing such parks globally.
    Answer
    1. Information barriers: Factories don't know waste streams of neighbors → requires park manager as broker. 2. Economic/contractual: Long-term waste agreements risky (supply/price volatility). Solution: Industrial ecology network (shared data platform), government incentives (tax breaks for synergy agreements), risk-sharing contracts. Also cultural: competitors typically avoid cooperation.
  3. Singapore's district uses "smart grid demand response." Explain how this reduces peak load and why it's critical for renewable integration.
    Answer
    Demand response: shift non-urgent loads (e.g., HVAC, EV charging) to off-peak hours → flatten peak. Peak reduction 20–30% typical. Critical for renewables: solar peaks midday, demand peaks evening (mismatch). Storage or load shifting bridges gap. Without demand response, battery cost prohibitive (4–6 hour storage needed at peak hours). Smart grids (IoT, AI) enable automated response, making renewables economically viable.
  4. IKEA targets 25% take-back rate by 2030. What pricing/incentive models could accelerate this? Model 2 scenarios and compare NPV.
    Answer
    Scenario A: $20 credit for returns (cost $15 IKEA, customer value $20). Estimated 15% take-back rate, cost $30M/year. Secondary resale value: $50M/year → NPV +$20M/year. Scenario B: Subscription model (pay-per-year for furniture, auto take-back/refresh). Estimated 40% participation, but cannibalize retail sales. Net: break-even to slightly positive. Hybrid: Bundle credit + subscription for high-value items. Recommend phased: credit first (proven), then subscription pilot in key markets.
