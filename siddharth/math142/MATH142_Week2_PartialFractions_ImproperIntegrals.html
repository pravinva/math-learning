Extending last week's partial fractions to harder denominators, then patching the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus for infinite limits and discontinuous integrands. Exam-level worked examples and practice throughout. Solutions stay hidden until you click.

Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Repeated linear factor $(x-a)^n$: include a term for every power from 1 to $n$: $\dfrac{A_1}{x-a}+\dfrac{A_2}{(x-a)^2}+\cdots+\dfrac{A_n}{(x-a)^n}$.
  • Irreducible quadratic factor (negative discriminant): use a linear numerator, not a constant: $\dfrac{Bx+C}{x^2+bx+c}$.
  • Infinite limits: $\displaystyle\int_a^\infty f(x)\,dx = \lim_{t\to\infty}\int_a^t f(x)\,dx$; converges iff the limit is finite.
  • Discontinuous integrand at $c\in[a,b]$: split at $c$, wrap each piece in a one-sided limit; both pieces must converge for the whole integral to converge.
  • $p$-integral rule: $\displaystyle\int_1^\infty \dfrac{1}{x^p}\,dx$ converges iff $p>1$ — a result you'll reuse directly for series convergence in Week 11.

0 Partial Fractions — Repeated & Irreducible Quadratic Factors

Distinct linear factors were the simplest case. When a factor repeats, or the denominator contains a quadratic that can't be factored over the reals, the decomposition needs extra terms — built by the same coefficient-matching idea, just with a richer set of unknowns.

Repeated linear factors. For $(x-a)^n$, a single term $\dfrac{A}{x-a}$ can only reproduce a degree-0 numerator once you clear $(x-a)^n$ — matching a general proper numerator needs the extra degrees of freedom that $\dfrac{A_2}{(x-a)^2},\dots,\dfrac{A_n}{(x-a)^n}$ provide. The cover-up trick only pins down the last coefficient $A_n$ directly (substituting $x=a$ after clearing denominators); the rest require expanding and matching coefficients.

Irreducible quadratic factors. A quadratic $x^2+bx+c$ with negative discriminant doesn't factor over the reals, so it gets a linear numerator $\dfrac{Bx+C}{x^2+bx+c}$ — a constant numerator alone can't supply the $x$-term needed when you multiply through and match coefficients.

Integrating the quadratic term. $\dfrac{Bx+C}{x^2+bx+c}$ typically splits into two pieces once integrated: one matching $\int\dfrac{2x+b}{x^2+bx+c}dx$ (a logarithm, via substitution), the other needing the arctan form (often after completing the square if $b\ne0$).

Key idea

$$(x-a)^n \to \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{A_k}{(x-a)^k}, \qquad \text{irreducible } x^2+bx+c \to \frac{Bx+C}{x^2+bx+c}$$

Worked example

Example 1 — repeated linear factor. Decompose $\dfrac{3x-1}{(x-1)^2}$.

$3x-1=A(x-1)+B$. At $x=1$: $B=2$. Match $x$-coefficients: $A=3$. Result: $\dfrac{3}{x-1}+\dfrac{2}{(x-1)^2}$.

Example 2 — irreducible quadratic factor. Decompose $\dfrac{x+1}{x(x^2+1)}$.

$x+1=A(x^2+1)+(Bx+C)x$. At $x=0$: $A=1$. Match $x^2$: $B=-1$. Match $x$: $C=1$. Result: $\dfrac{1}{x}+\dfrac{-x+1}{x^2+1}$.

Practice 1

Decompose $\dfrac{x^2}{(x-1)^3}$ (three repeated-factor terms needed), then find $\displaystyle\int \frac{x^2}{(x-1)^3}\,dx$.

Solution

Set $x^2=A(x-1)^2+B(x-1)+C$. At $x=1$: $C=1$. Expanding and matching the $x^2$ coefficient: $A=1$. Matching the $x$ coefficient (or comparing constants) gives $B=2$.

Decomposition: $\dfrac{1}{x-1}+\dfrac{2}{(x-1)^2}+\dfrac{1}{(x-1)^3}$.

Integrate: $\ln|x-1|-\dfrac{2}{x-1}-\dfrac{1}{2(x-1)^2}+C$.

Answer: $\ln|x-1|-\dfrac{2}{x-1}-\dfrac{1}{2(x-1)^2}+C$.

Practice 2

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int \frac{3x^2+4}{x(x^2+4)}\,dx$ (mixing a distinct linear factor with an irreducible quadratic), including the arctan term fully evaluated.

Solution

$3x^2+4=A(x^2+4)+(Bx+C)x$. At $x=0$: $4=4A\implies A=1$. Match $x^2$: $3=A+B\implies B=2$. Match $x$: $0=C$.

Decomposition: $\dfrac{1}{x}+\dfrac{2x}{x^2+4}$.

Integrate: $\int\dfrac{1}{x}dx+\int\dfrac{2x}{x^2+4}dx = \ln|x|+\ln(x^2+4)+C$ (the second term is a clean logarithm since the numerator $2x$ is exactly the derivative of $x^2+4$ — no arctan needed here since $C=0$).

Answer: $\ln|x|+\ln(x^2+4)+C = \ln\big|x(x^2+4)\big|+C$.

Practice 3

Decompose $\dfrac{2x^2-x+4}{(x-2)(x^2+4)}$, then find $\displaystyle\int \frac{2x^2-x+4}{(x-2)(x^2+4)}\,dx$ (this one genuinely needs the arctan form).

Solution

$2x^2-x+4=A(x^2+4)+(Bx+C)(x-2)$. At $x=2$: $8-2+4=10=8A\implies A=\tfrac54$.

Match $x^2$: $2=A+B\implies B=\tfrac34$. Match constant: $4=4A-2C\implies 4=5-2C\implies C=\tfrac12$.

Decomposition: $\dfrac{5/4}{x-2}+\dfrac{\tfrac34x+\tfrac12}{x^2+4}$.

Split the quadratic term: $\dfrac{\tfrac34x}{x^2+4}$ integrates to $\tfrac38\ln(x^2+4)$ (numerator matches $\tfrac34\cdot\tfrac12(2x)$); $\dfrac{\tfrac12}{x^2+4}$ integrates to $\tfrac12\cdot\tfrac12\arctan(x/2)=\tfrac14\arctan(x/2)$.

Answer: $\dfrac54\ln|x-2|+\dfrac38\ln(x^2+4)+\dfrac14\arctan\!\left(\dfrac{x}{2}\right)+C$.

Practice 4

Explain why the repeated-factor term $(x-a)^n$ requires $n$ separate partial-fraction terms rather than just one, using a degree-counting argument, and confirm with $\dfrac{1}{(x-1)^2}$ that a single term IS enough there.

Solution

A single term $\dfrac{A}{(x-a)^k}$ (for one fixed $k$) can only reproduce, after clearing the common denominator $(x-a)^n$, a numerator that is a specific power of $(x-a)$ — it can't match an arbitrary proper numerator of the combined fraction unless every power from 1 to $n$ is represented.

For $\dfrac{1}{(x-1)^2}$: the numerator is already just the constant $1$ over $(x-1)^2$ exactly — it needs no decomposition at all, since it's already in the simplest single-term form for a repeated factor of order 2 with a matching (degree-0) numerator. This is the trivial case where $n=1$ term already suffices, precisely because the given numerator has no lower-order structure to capture.

1 Improper Integrals

Ordinary definite integrals assume a finite interval and a well-behaved (bounded, continuous) integrand. When either assumption fails — an infinite limit of integration, or a vertical asymptote inside the interval — the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus can't be applied directly. Improper integrals patch this by wrapping the troublesome point in a limit.

Infinite limits of integration. Replace the infinite bound with a finite variable, integrate normally, then take the limit as that variable heads to infinity. If the limit exists (finite), the integral converges; otherwise it diverges.

Discontinuous integrands. If $f$ has an infinite discontinuity at some point $c$ within $[a,b]$, split the integral at $c$ and wrap each piece in a one-sided limit. Both pieces must converge individually for the whole integral to converge.

Key idea

$$\int_a^\infty f(x)\,dx = \lim_{t\to\infty}\int_a^t f(x)\,dx, \qquad \int_a^b f(x)\,dx = \lim_{t\to c^-}\int_a^t f\,dx + \lim_{t\to c^+}\int_t^b f\,dx$$

Worked example

Example 1 — infinite limit, converges. Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_1^\infty \dfrac{1}{x^2}\,dx$.

$\lim_{t\to\infty}\left[-\dfrac1x\right]_1^t = \lim_{t\to\infty}\left(-\dfrac1t+1\right)=1$. Converges to $1$.

Example 2 — discontinuous integrand. Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^1 \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{x}}\,dx$.

$\lim_{t\to0^+}\left[2\sqrt{x}\right]_t^1 = \lim_{t\to0^+}(2-2\sqrt t)=2$. Converges to $2$.

Practice 5

Determine whether $\displaystyle\int_1^\infty \frac{1}{x^p}\,dx$ converges for general $p$, proving both the $p>1$ and $p\le1$ cases explicitly.

Solution

Case $p\ne1$: $\displaystyle\int_1^t x^{-p}dx = \left[\frac{x^{1-p}}{1-p}\right]_1^t = \frac{t^{1-p}-1}{1-p}$.

If $p>1$: exponent $1-p<0$, so $t^{1-p}\to0$ as $t\to\infty$, giving $\dfrac{-1}{1-p}=\dfrac{1}{p-1}$ — finite, converges.

If $p<1$: exponent $1-p>0$, so $t^{1-p}\to\infty$ — diverges.

Case $p=1$: $\displaystyle\int_1^t x^{-1}dx=\ln t\to\infty$ — diverges.

Answer: converges iff $p>1$, with value $\dfrac{1}{p-1}$; diverges for $p\le1$.

Practice 6

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{x}{1+x^2}\,dx$ carefully — show that treating it as a single symmetric limit gives a misleading answer, and find the correct (properly split) result.

Solution

The trap: if you (incorrectly) took a single symmetric limit $\lim_{R\to\infty}\int_{-R}^R \dfrac{x}{1+x^2}dx$, the odd-function symmetry makes every finite piece exactly $0$, tempting you to conclude the integral "is" $0$.

The correct approach: the definition requires the two infinite limits to be evaluated independently: $\int_{-\infty}^0+\int_0^\infty$. Consider just $\int_0^\infty\dfrac{x}{1+x^2}dx=\lim_{t\to\infty}\left[\tfrac12\ln(1+x^2)\right]_0^t=\lim_{t\to\infty}\tfrac12\ln(1+t^2)=\infty$.

Since this one-sided piece already diverges, the whole improper integral diverges — the symmetric-limit shortcut (giving $0$) is not valid here and would be marked incorrect in an exam.

Answer: diverges (the naive symmetric answer of $0$ is a common but incorrect shortcut).

Practice 7

Determine whether $\displaystyle\int_0^2 \frac{1}{(x-1)^2}\,dx$ converges, being careful about the discontinuity's location inside the interval.

Solution

The integrand is discontinuous at $x=1$, which lies strictly inside $[0,2]$ — split there: $\int_0^1+\int_1^2$.

$\displaystyle\int_0^1\frac{dx}{(x-1)^2} = \lim_{t\to1^-}\left[-\frac{1}{x-1}\right]_0^t = \lim_{t\to1^-}\left(-\frac{1}{t-1}-1\right)$. As $t\to1^-$, $t-1\to0^-$, so $-\dfrac{1}{t-1}\to+\infty$ — this piece diverges.

Since one piece already diverges, the whole integral diverges — regardless of what the second piece does.

Answer: diverges.

Practice 8

Determine whether $\displaystyle\int_2^\infty \frac{1}{x(\ln x)^2}\,dx$ converges, using the substitution $u=\ln x$.

Solution

Let $u=\ln x$, $du=dx/x$. When $x=2,u=\ln2$; as $x\to\infty,u\to\infty$.

$\displaystyle\int_{\ln2}^\infty \frac{1}{u^2}\,du = \lim_{t\to\infty}\left[-\frac1u\right]_{\ln2}^t = \lim_{t\to\infty}\left(-\frac1t+\frac{1}{\ln2}\right)=\frac{1}{\ln2}$.

Answer: converges, to $\dfrac{1}{\ln2}\approx1.4427$.

Practice 9

Decompose $\dfrac{5x+7}{(x+1)^2(x-2)}$ and evaluate $\displaystyle\int \frac{5x+7}{(x+1)^2(x-2)}\,dx$.

Solution

Use $\dfrac{A}{x-2}+\dfrac{B}{x+1}+\dfrac{C}{(x+1)^2}$. Solving gives $A=\frac{17}{9},\,B=-\frac{17}{9},\,C=-\frac{4}{3}$.

So $\int = \frac{17}{9}\ln|x-2|-\frac{17}{9}\ln|x+1|+\frac{4}{3(x+1)}+C$.

Practice 10

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int \frac{x^2+3x+5}{(x+1)(x^2+4x+13)}\,dx$ (quadratic completion required).

Solution

Set $\dfrac{A}{x+1}+\dfrac{Bx+C}{x^2+4x+13}$ and solve: $A=\frac{3}{10},\,B=\frac{7}{10},\,C=\frac{9}{5}$.

Write $x^2+4x+13=(x+2)^2+9$ and split $\dfrac{7x+18}{10(x^2+4x+13)}=\dfrac{7}{20}\dfrac{2x+4}{x^2+4x+13}+\dfrac{2}{5}\dfrac{1}{(x+2)^2+9}$.

Answer: $$\frac{3}{10}\ln|x+1|+\frac{7}{20}\ln(x^2+4x+13)+\frac{2}{15}\arctan\!\left(\frac{x+2}{3}\right)+C.$$

Practice 11

Find all real $k$ such that $\displaystyle\int_1^\infty \frac{dx}{x^k}$ converges to a value less than $2$.

Solution

Convergence requires $k>1$, and then value is $\dfrac{1}{k-1}$.

Need $\dfrac{1}{k-1}<2 \Rightarrow 1<2(k-1)\Rightarrow k>\frac{3}{2}$.

Answer: $k>\frac{3}{2}$.

Practice 12

Determine convergence of $\displaystyle\int_0^1 \frac{\ln x}{\sqrt{x}}\,dx$ and evaluate it if convergent.

Solution

Let $x=u^2$ so $dx=2u\,du$, $\sqrt{x}=u$, $\ln x=2\ln u$.

Integral becomes $4\int_0^1 \ln u\,du = 4[u\ln u-u]_0^1 =4(0-1)=-4$.

Answer: converges, value $-4$.

Practice 13

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^\infty \frac{1}{(x+1)^2}\,dx$ and classify convergence.

Solution

$\int_0^t (x+1)^{-2}dx = \left[-\frac{1}{x+1}\right]_0^t = 1-\frac{1}{t+1}$.

$t\to\infty$ gives $1$.

Answer: convergent, value $1$.

Practice 14

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^\infty \frac{x}{x^2+9}\,dx$ and explain briefly why comparison with $\int_1^\infty \frac1x dx$ is relevant.

Solution

$\int_0^t \frac{x}{x^2+9}dx=\frac12\ln(x^2+9)\big|_0^t=\frac12\ln\!\frac{t^2+9}{9}\to\infty$.

Asymptotically $\frac{x}{x^2+9}\sim\frac1x$, so divergence matches the comparison intuition.

Answer: diverges.

Practice 15

Compute $\displaystyle\int_1^\infty \frac{dx}{x^2+4x+5}$.

Solution

$x^2+4x+5=(x+2)^2+1$, so $\int_1^t \frac{dx}{(x+2)^2+1}=\arctan(x+2)\big|_1^t$.

Limit gives $\frac{\pi}{2}-\arctan 3$.

Answer: converges to $\dfrac{\pi}{2}-\arctan 3$.

Practice 16

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^1 \frac{dx}{x^{3/2}}$ and explain the singularity test near $0$.

Solution

$\int x^{-3/2}dx=-2x^{-1/2}$. From $t$ to $1$: $-2+2/\sqrt{t}\to\infty$ as $t\to0^+$.

Power test near $0$: $\int_0^1 x^{-p}dx$ converges iff $p<1$; here $p=\frac32>1$.

Answer: diverges.

Practice 17

Decompose and integrate $\displaystyle\int \frac{2x^2+7x+6}{(x+2)^2(x+1)}\,dx$.

Solution

Use $\dfrac{A}{x+1}+\dfrac{B}{x+2}+\dfrac{C}{(x+2)^2}$; solve to get $A=1,\;B=1,\;C=0$.

Integral is $\int\left(\frac1{x+1}+\frac1{x+2}\right)dx=\ln|x+1|+\ln|x+2|+C$.

Practice 18

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^\infty xe^{-2x}\,dx$ (improper at infinity, but absolutely convergent).

Solution

By parts: $\int xe^{-2x}dx=-\frac{x}{2}e^{-2x}-\frac14e^{-2x}+C$.

Evaluate $0$ to $t$ then $t\to\infty$: upper terms vanish, lower gives $-\left(-\frac14\right)=\frac14$.

Answer: converges to $\frac14$.

Practice 19

Show that $\displaystyle\int_0^\infty \frac{dx}{\sqrt{x}(1+x)}$ converges and evaluate it using $x=u^2$.

Solution

Let $x=u^2$, $dx=2u\,du$, $\sqrt{x}=u$. Integrand becomes $\dfrac{2}{1+u^2}$.

Limits: $x:0\to\infty$ gives $u:0\to\infty$. So integral is $2\int_0^\infty\frac{du}{1+u^2}=2[\arctan u]_0^\infty=\pi$.

Answer: converges to $\pi$.

Practice 20

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_3^\infty \frac{dx}{x^2-4}$ via partial fractions and improper limits.

Solution

$\frac{1}{x^2-4}=\frac14\left(\frac1{x-2}-\frac1{x+2}\right)$.

$\int_3^t = \frac14\ln\left|\frac{x-2}{x+2}\right|\Big|_3^t$. As $t\to\infty$, log term $\to\ln1=0$.

Value $= -\frac14\ln\frac{1}{5}=\frac14\ln5$.

Practice 21

Determine convergence of $\displaystyle\int_{-1}^{1}\frac{dx}{x^2}$ with full split-limit setup.

Solution

Split at $0$: $\int_{-1}^0 x^{-2}dx+\int_0^1 x^{-2}dx$.

Each piece has antiderivative $-1/x$ and blows to $+\infty$ near $0$.

Answer: diverges (both sides diverge).

Practice 22

Find constants $A,B,C$ so that $$\frac{4x+1}{(x-1)(x^2+1)}=\frac{A}{x-1}+\frac{Bx+C}{x^2+1},$$ then integrate.

Solution

Matching coefficients gives $A=\frac52,\;B=-\frac52,\;C=\frac32$.

Integral: $$\frac52\ln|x-1|-\frac54\ln(x^2+1)+\frac32\arctan x+C.$$

Practice 23

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^\infty \frac{dx}{(x+1)^p}$ in terms of $p$, and state convergence conditions.

Solution

For $p\neq1$: $\int_0^t (x+1)^{-p}dx=\frac{(t+1)^{1-p}-1}{1-p}$.

Converges iff $p>1$, then limit is $\frac{1}{p-1}$. For $p=1$, gives $\ln(t+1)\to\infty$ (diverges).

Practice 24

Challenge: determine convergence and value of $$\int_0^\infty \frac{dx}{(x+1)(x+2)}.$$ Use partial fractions and one improper limit.

Solution

$\frac{1}{(x+1)(x+2)}=\frac{1}{x+1}-\frac{1}{x+2}$.

$\int_0^t = \ln\frac{x+1}{x+2}\Big|_0^t=\ln\frac{t+1}{t+2}-\ln\frac12$.

As $t\to\infty$, first log $\to\ln1=0$. So value is $\ln2$.

Answer: converges to $\ln2$.
