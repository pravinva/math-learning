Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Conversion: $x=r\cos\theta,\ y=r\sin\theta$; and going the other way, $r^2=x^2+y^2$, $\tan\theta=y/x$ (with quadrant care).
  • Non-uniqueness: a point has infinitely many polar representations — adding $2\pi$ to $\theta$ returns to the same direction, and $r<0$ points opposite to $\theta$ (same point as $(-r,\theta+\pi)$... equivalently $(r,\theta)=(-r,\theta+\pi)$).
  • Standard curve families: $r=a$ (circle at origin); $r=a\cos\theta$ or $a\sin\theta$ (circle through the origin); $r=a\pm a\cos\theta$ (cardioid); $r=a\pm b\cos\theta,\ a\ne b$ (limaçon, with or without inner loop); $r=a\cos(n\theta)$ or $a\sin(n\theta)$ (rose: $n$ odd → $n$ petals, $n$ even → $2n$ petals).

0 Polar Coordinates: Plotting & Conversion

Cartesian coordinates locate a point by how far across and up. Polar coordinates locate the same point a completely different way — by how far from the origin ($r$), and in what direction ($\theta$, measured counterclockwise from the positive $x$-axis).

Deriving the conversion. Drop a perpendicular from the point to the $x$-axis, forming a right triangle with hypotenuse $r$ and angle $\theta$ — ordinary right-triangle trig gives $x=r\cos\theta$, $y=r\sin\theta$. Going the other way, Pythagoras gives $r$, and $\tan\theta=y/x$ gives the angle (with quadrant care, exactly as with the argument of a complex number).

Non-uniqueness. Unlike Cartesian coordinates, a single point has infinitely many valid polar representations: adding $2\pi$ to $\theta$ returns to the same direction, and allowing $r<0$ points in the opposite direction to $\theta$, landing on the same point as a positive $r$ at angle $\theta+\pi$.

Key idea

$$x=r\cos\theta, \quad y=r\sin\theta, \qquad r^2=x^2+y^2, \quad \tan\theta=\frac{y}{x}$$

Worked example

Example 1 — polar to Cartesian. Convert $\left(4,\dfrac{\pi}{3}\right)$ to Cartesian.

$x=4\cos(\pi/3)=2$, $y=4\sin(\pi/3)=2\sqrt3$. Answer: $(2,2\sqrt3)$.

Example 2 — Cartesian to polar. Convert $(-1,1)$ to polar ($0\le\theta<2\pi$).

$r=\sqrt2$; second quadrant, reference angle $\pi/4$: $\theta=\pi-\pi/4=3\pi/4$. Answer: $\left(\sqrt2,\dfrac{3\pi}{4}\right)$.

Practice 1

Convert $(-2,-2\sqrt3)$ to polar coordinates ($0\le\theta<2\pi$), showing the quadrant reasoning explicitly.

Solution

$r=\sqrt{4+12}=\sqrt{16}=4$.

$\tan\theta=\dfrac{-2\sqrt3}{-2}=\sqrt3$, reference angle $\pi/3$. Since $x<0,y<0$, the point is in the third quadrant: $\theta=\pi+\pi/3=\dfrac{4\pi}{3}$.

Answer: $\left(4,\dfrac{4\pi}{3}\right)$.

Practice 2

A point has polar coordinates $\left(-3,\dfrac{\pi}{6}\right)$ (negative $r$). Find (a) its Cartesian coordinates, and (b) an equivalent polar representation with positive $r$ and $0\le\theta<2\pi$.

Solution

(a) $x=r\cos\theta=-3\cos(\pi/6)=-3\cdot\dfrac{\sqrt3}{2}=-\dfrac{3\sqrt3}{2}$; $y=-3\sin(\pi/6)=-3\cdot\dfrac12=-\dfrac32$.

(b) Negative $r$ at angle $\theta$ is equivalent to positive $r$ at angle $\theta+\pi$: $\left(3,\dfrac{\pi}{6}+\pi\right)=\left(3,\dfrac{7\pi}{6}\right)$.

Answer: (a) $\left(-\dfrac{3\sqrt3}{2},-\dfrac32\right)$; (b) $\left(3,\dfrac{7\pi}{6}\right)$.

Practice 3

Two points have polar coordinates $(4,\pi/6)$ and $(4,2\pi/3)$. Find the straight-line (Cartesian) distance between them, using the conversion formulas.

Solution

Point A: $x_A=4\cos(\pi/6)=2\sqrt3$, $y_A=4\sin(\pi/6)=2$.

Point B: $x_B=4\cos(2\pi/3)=-2$, $y_B=4\sin(2\pi/3)=2\sqrt3$.

Distance: $\sqrt{(2\sqrt3-(-2))^2+(2-2\sqrt3)^2}$.

$2\sqrt3+2\approx5.464$; $2-2\sqrt3\approx-1.464$. $\sqrt{5.464^2+1.464^2}=\sqrt{29.86+2.14}=\sqrt{32}=4\sqrt2$.

Answer: $4\sqrt2\approx5.657$.

Practice 4

Convert the equation $x^2+y^2=6x$ (a Cartesian circle) to polar form, and simplify.

Solution

Substitute $x=r\cos\theta,y=r\sin\theta$: $r^2\cos^2\theta+r^2\sin^2\theta=6r\cos\theta \implies r^2=6r\cos\theta$.

Divide both sides by $r$ (valid for $r\ne0$, and $r=0$ satisfies both forms trivially anyway): $r=6\cos\theta$.

Answer: $r=6\cos\theta$ — a circle of diameter 6 through the origin, centred on the positive $x$-axis.

Practice 5

Convert the polar equation $r=\dfrac{2}{1-\cos\theta}$ to Cartesian form (this is a conic section in polar form).

Solution

Multiply both sides by $(1-\cos\theta)$: $r-r\cos\theta=2 \implies r=2+r\cos\theta=2+x$.

Square both sides: $r^2=(2+x)^2 \implies x^2+y^2=4+4x+x^2 \implies y^2=4+4x \implies y^2=4(x+1)$.

Answer: $y^2=4(x+1)$ — a parabola opening rightward, vertex at $(-1,0)$.

Practice 6

Find all polar coordinate pairs $(r,\theta)$ with $-2\pi<\theta\le2\pi$ that represent the same point as $(5,\pi/4)$.

Solution

Same direction, add/subtract $2\pi$: $(5,\pi/4)$ and $(5,\pi/4-2\pi)=(5,-7\pi/4)$.

Opposite direction with negative $r$: $(-5,\pi/4+\pi)=(-5,5\pi/4)$, and $(-5,5\pi/4-2\pi)=(-5,-3\pi/4)$.

Answer: $(5,\pi/4)$, $(5,-7\pi/4)$, $(-5,5\pi/4)$, $(-5,-3\pi/4)$ — four representations within the given range.

Practice 7

A surveying instrument records a landmark at polar coordinates $(120\text{ m}, 35^\circ)$ from an origin point. Find its position in Cartesian (east, north) coordinates, to the nearest 0.1 m.

Solution

$x=120\cos(35^\circ)=120(0.8192)=98.3$ m (east).

$y=120\sin(35^\circ)=120(0.5736)=68.8$ m (north).

Answer: approximately $(98.3\text{ m E}, 68.8\text{ m N})$.

Practice 8

Convert $(3,-\pi/3)$ (negative angle) to Cartesian coordinates, and then re-express the same point using a positive angle in $[0,2\pi)$.

Solution

$x=3\cos(-\pi/3)=3\cdot\tfrac12=\tfrac32$; $y=3\sin(-\pi/3)=3\cdot\left(-\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2}\right)=-\dfrac{3\sqrt3}{2}$.

Positive-angle equivalent: $-\pi/3+2\pi=\dfrac{5\pi}{3}$.

Answer: Cartesian $\left(\dfrac32,-\dfrac{3\sqrt3}{2}\right)$; equivalent polar form $\left(3,\dfrac{5\pi}{3}\right)$.

Practice 9

Three points have polar coordinates $(2,0)$, $(2,2\pi/3)$, and $(2,4\pi/3)$ (equally spaced on a circle of radius 2). Show that they form an equilateral triangle by computing all three side lengths.

Solution

Convert each to Cartesian: $P_1=(2,0)$; $P_2=\left(2\cos\tfrac{2\pi}{3},2\sin\tfrac{2\pi}{3}\right)=(-1,\sqrt3)$; $P_3=\left(2\cos\tfrac{4\pi}{3},2\sin\tfrac{4\pi}{3}\right)=(-1,-\sqrt3)$.

$|P_1P_2|=\sqrt{(2-(-1))^2+(0-\sqrt3)^2}=\sqrt{9+3}=\sqrt{12}=2\sqrt3$.

$|P_1P_3|=\sqrt{(2-(-1))^2+(0+\sqrt3)^2}=\sqrt{9+3}=2\sqrt3$ (by symmetry).

$|P_2P_3|=\sqrt{(-1-(-1))^2+(\sqrt3-(-\sqrt3))^2}=\sqrt{0+12}=2\sqrt3$.

Answer: all three sides equal $2\sqrt3$, confirming an equilateral triangle — as expected, since equally-spaced points ($120^\circ$ apart) on a circle always form one.

Practice 10

Convert the Cartesian line $y=2x+3$ to polar form, solving explicitly for $r$ in terms of $\theta$.

Solution

Substitute $x=r\cos\theta,y=r\sin\theta$: $r\sin\theta=2r\cos\theta+3$.

Collect $r$ terms: $r\sin\theta-2r\cos\theta=3 \implies r(\sin\theta-2\cos\theta)=3$.

Answer: $r=\dfrac{3}{\sin\theta-2\cos\theta}$.

1 Polar Coordinates: Graphing Curves & Symmetry

A polar equation $r=f(\theta)$ describes a curve by giving distance from the origin as a function of angle — revealing shapes (hearts, flowers, spirals) that would be far messier to describe with a single Cartesian equation.

Symmetry shortcuts. Test algebraically before plotting: symmetric about the $x$-axis if replacing $\theta$ with $-\theta$ leaves the equation unchanged; about the $y$-axis if replacing $\theta$ with $\pi-\theta$ leaves it unchanged; about the origin if replacing $r$ with $-r$ (or $\theta$ with $\theta+\pi$) leaves it unchanged. Confirming symmetry first means only sketching half (or a quarter) of the curve by hand, then reflecting.

Key idea
EquationShape
$r=a$Circle, radius $a$, centred at the origin
$r=a\cos\theta$ or $a\sin\theta$Circle of diameter $a$, passing through the origin
$r=a+b\cos\theta$ (or $\sin$), $a=b$Cardioid (heart shape)
$r=a+b\cos\theta$, $a\ne b$Limaçon (with or without an inner loop, depending on $a/b$)
$r=a\cos(n\theta)$ or $a\sin(n\theta)$Rose: $n$ odd → $n$ petals; $n$ even → $2n$ petals
Worked example

Example 1. Identify $r=2+2\cos\theta$, and find where it crosses the origin.

Coefficients match ($a=b=2$): a cardioid. Origin crossing: $2+2\cos\theta=0\implies\cos\theta=-1\implies\theta=\pi$.

Example 2 — symmetry test. Test $r=3\sin(2\theta)$ for symmetry about the origin.

Replace $\theta\to\theta+\pi$: $r=3\sin(2\theta+2\pi)=3\sin(2\theta)$ — unchanged, so symmetric about the origin (consistent with an 8-petal rose, $n=4$ even → wait, here $n=2$, so $2n=4$ petals).

Practice 11

A limaçon with an inner loop is given by $r=1+2\cos\theta$. Find (a) all values of $\theta\in[0,2\pi)$ where the curve passes through the origin, and (b) the range of $\theta$ that traces the inner loop only.

Solution

(a) $1+2\cos\theta=0 \implies \cos\theta=-\tfrac12 \implies \theta=\dfrac{2\pi}{3},\dfrac{4\pi}{3}$.

(b) The inner loop occurs where $r<0$ — i.e. where $1+2\cos\theta<0$, which happens for $\theta$ strictly between the two origin-crossings found in (a): $\theta\in\left(\dfrac{2\pi}{3},\dfrac{4\pi}{3}\right)$. As $\theta$ sweeps this range, $r$ is negative, plotting points on the opposite side of the origin from $\theta$ — tracing the small inner loop.

Answer: (a) $\theta=\dfrac{2\pi}{3},\dfrac{4\pi}{3}$; (b) $\theta\in\left(\dfrac{2\pi}{3},\dfrac{4\pi}{3}\right)$.

Practice 12

Test $r^2=4\cos(2\theta)$ (a lemniscate) for all three standard symmetries, and determine the range(s) of $\theta$ for which the curve exists at all (real $r$).

Solution

$x$-axis: replace $\theta\to-\theta$: $r^2=4\cos(-2\theta)=4\cos(2\theta)$ — unchanged. Symmetric about the $x$-axis.

$y$-axis: replace $\theta\to\pi-\theta$: $\cos(2(\pi-\theta))=\cos(2\pi-2\theta)=\cos(2\theta)$ — unchanged. Symmetric about the $y$-axis.

Origin: replace $r\to-r$: $(-r)^2=r^2$, unchanged trivially. Symmetric about the origin too.

Real $r$ requires $\cos(2\theta)\ge0$, i.e. $2\theta\in[-\pi/2,\pi/2]\cup[3\pi/2,5\pi/2]\,(\text{mod }2\pi)$, giving $\theta\in[-\pi/4,\pi/4]\cup[3\pi/4,5\pi/4]$ within one full turn — the curve exists only in these ranges, producing the lemniscate's two lobes.

Practice 13

Find the area of the region that lies inside both $r=3\cos\theta$ and $r=1+\cos\theta$ by first finding their intersection angle(s) (setup and full reasoning; the integral itself can be left indicated).

Solution

Step 1 — find intersections: $3\cos\theta=1+\cos\theta \implies 2\cos\theta=1 \implies \cos\theta=\tfrac12 \implies \theta=\pm\dfrac{\pi}{3}$.

Step 2 — identify which curve is "inner" where: for $|\theta|<\pi/3$, check e.g. $\theta=0$: $r_{3\cos\theta}=3$, $r_{1+\cos\theta}=2$ — the cardioid $1+\cos\theta$ is smaller (inner) here. For $\pi/3<\theta<\pi$, check $\theta=\pi/2$: $r_{3\cos\theta}=0$, $r_{1+\cos\theta}=1$ — now the circle $3\cos\theta$ is smaller (inner), since it shrinks toward $0$ while the cardioid stays larger.

Step 3 — set up the "inside both" area as the sum of two pieces, using whichever curve is the boundary of the smaller region in each range: $$A = \frac12\int_{-\pi/3}^{\pi/3}(1+\cos\theta)^2\,d\theta \ + \ 2\cdot\frac12\int_{\pi/3}^{\pi/2}(3\cos\theta)^2\,d\theta$$ (the second term doubled and taken over a quarter-range by symmetry, since the circle $3\cos\theta$ only exists for $-\pi/2\le\theta\le\pi/2$).

Key takeaway: the setup — correctly identifying which curve bounds the region over each sub-interval — is the actual exam skill here; once set up correctly, each integral is a routine half-angle calculation from earlier this phase.

Practice 14

Identify the curve $r=4-4\sin\theta$, find where it crosses the origin, and test it for symmetry about the $y$-axis.

Solution

Coefficients match ($a=b=4$): a cardioid.

Origin crossing: $4-4\sin\theta=0\implies\sin\theta=1\implies\theta=\pi/2$.

$y$-axis symmetry test: replace $\theta\to\pi-\theta$: $4-4\sin(\pi-\theta)=4-4\sin\theta$ (since $\sin(\pi-\theta)=\sin\theta$) — unchanged, so symmetric about the $y$-axis.

Answer: a cardioid, touching the origin at $\theta=\pi/2$, symmetric about the $y$-axis (makes sense — this cardioid points downward, along the negative $y$-axis).

Practice 15

Identify the number of petals for $r=5\cos(5\theta)$ and for $r=5\cos(6\theta)$, and sketch (in words) how the petal count rule differs between the two.

Solution

For $n=5$ (odd): the rose has $n=5$ petals.

For $n=6$ (even): the rose has $2n=12$ petals.

Answer: $r=5\cos(5\theta)$ has 5 petals; $r=5\cos(6\theta)$ has 12 petals — even $n$ doubles the petal count because the curve traces each "lobe" location twice (once with positive $r$, once with negative $r$ landing on a genuinely different petal), while odd $n$ retraces the same petals on the second half of the sweep.

Practice 16

A rose is described by $r=2\sin(3\theta)$. Find the angles $\theta\in[0,\pi]$ at which $r=0$ (i.e. the curve passes through the origin), and use these to state the angular width of a single petal.

Solution

$2\sin(3\theta)=0 \implies \sin(3\theta)=0 \implies 3\theta=0,\pi,2\pi,3\pi,\dots \implies \theta=0,\dfrac{\pi}{3},\dfrac{2\pi}{3},\pi,\dots$

Answer: zeros at $\theta=0,\pi/3,2\pi/3,\pi$ within $[0,\pi]$; each petal spans an angular width of $\pi/3$ (from one consecutive zero to the next), consistent with 3 petals fitting into a $\pi$-radian half-sweep for this odd-$n$ rose.

Practice 17

Test $r=2+4\sin\theta$ (a limaçon with an inner loop) for symmetry about the $y$-axis, and find the values of $\theta$ where it crosses the origin.

Solution

$y$-axis symmetry: replace $\theta\to\pi-\theta$: $2+4\sin(\pi-\theta)=2+4\sin\theta$ (since $\sin(\pi-\theta)=\sin\theta$) — unchanged, symmetric about the $y$-axis.

Origin crossings: $2+4\sin\theta=0\implies\sin\theta=-\tfrac12\implies\theta=\dfrac{7\pi}{6},\dfrac{11\pi}{6}$ (in $[0,2\pi)$).

Answer: symmetric about the $y$-axis; origin crossings at $\theta=7\pi/6$ and $11\pi/6$.

Practice 18

Determine whether $r=3+2\sin\theta$ is a cardioid or a limaçon (with or without a loop), by comparing $|a|$ and $|b|$, and confirm whether it ever reaches $r=0$.

Solution

Here $a=3,b=2$: since $a\ne b$, this is a limaçon, not a cardioid.

Since $|a|=3>|b|=2$, the limaçon does not have an inner loop (it never reaches $r=0$, since the minimum value of $3+2\sin\theta$ is $3-2=1>0$).

Answer: a dimpled limaçon (no inner loop, since $|a|>|b|$); minimum $r=1$, so the curve never touches the origin.

Practice 19

For the limaçon $r=1+3\cos\theta$, determine whether it has an inner loop by comparing $|a|$ and $|b|$, and if so, find the loop's angular range.

Solution

$a=1,b=3$: since $|a|<|b|$, this limaçon does have an inner loop.

Find where $r=0$: $1+3\cos\theta=0\implies\cos\theta=-\tfrac13\implies\theta=\arccos(-\tfrac13)\approx1.9106$ rad ($\approx109.47^\circ$) and its negative-angle counterpart $\theta\approx2\pi-1.9106\approx4.3726$ rad.

Answer: has an inner loop, traced for $\theta$ between approximately $1.911$ and $4.373$ radians (where $r<0$).

Practice 20

Explain, using the definition of $r$ as a function of $\theta$, why every rose curve $r=a\cos(n\theta)$ passes through the origin at least once (for any integer $n\ge1$), and find one such angle for $n=7$.

Solution

Since $\cos(n\theta)$ is a continuous function oscillating between $-1$ and $1$, it must equal $0$ at infinitely many values of $\theta$ (specifically whenever $n\theta=\pi/2+k\pi$ for integer $k$) — at each such $\theta$, $r=a\cos(n\theta)=0$, placing the curve at the origin.

For $n=7$: $7\theta=\pi/2 \implies \theta=\dfrac{\pi}{14}$.

Answer: every rose passes through the origin infinitely often (at regularly spaced angles); for $n=7$, one such angle is $\theta=\pi/14$.

Convert $(r,\theta)=\left(6,\frac{11\pi}{6}\right)$ to Cartesian.

$x=6\cos\frac{11\pi}{6}=3\sqrt3,\;y=6\sin\frac{11\pi}{6}=-3$.

Practice 24

Convert $x^2+y^2=8x$ to polar and identify the curve.

Practice 25

Convert $r=4\sin\theta$ to Cartesian and identify center/radius.

Given $r=1-\sin\theta$, determine axis of symmetry and orientation (up/down/left/right cusp).

Practice 31

Find intersection angles of $r=2$ and $r=4\cos\theta$ and state common points.

Solution

Practice 32

Convert $r=\dfrac{2}{1-\cos\theta}$ to Cartesian and classify the curve.

Solution

$r(1-\cos\theta)=2\Rightarrow r-x=2\Rightarrow \sqrt{x^2+y^2}=x+2$. Square: $y^2=4x+4$. Parabola opening right.

Practice 33

Show $(r,\theta)$ and $(-r,\theta+\pi)$ map to same Cartesian coordinates.

Solution

$x'=(-r)\cos(\theta+\pi)=(-r)(-\cos\theta)=r\cos\theta=x$ and similarly $y'=(-r)\sin(\theta+\pi)=(-r)(-\sin\theta)=y$.

Practice 34

Determine if $r^2=\sin(2\theta)$ has x-axis symmetry.

Solution

$\theta\to-\theta$ gives $r^2=-\sin(2\theta)$, not unchanged; so not x-axis symmetric.

Practice 35

Find slope $\frac{dy}{dx}$ for polar curve at $\theta=\pi/4$ when $r=2\theta$.

Solution

$\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{r'\sin\theta+r\cos\theta}{r'\cos\theta-r\sin\theta}$ with $r'=2,r=\pi/2$.

At $\theta=\pi/4$, $\sin=\cos$, giving $\frac{2+\pi/2}{2-\pi/2}=\frac{4+\pi}{4-\pi}$.

Practice 36

For $r=1+2\cos\theta$, find maximum and minimum $r$ values and corresponding angles.

Solution

Practice 37

Find polar equation of vertical line $x=-2$.

Solution

Practice 38

Find Cartesian equation for $r\cos\theta=2\sin\theta$.

Solution

$r\cos\theta=x$, $\sin\theta=y/r$. So $x=2y/r\Rightarrow xr=2y\Rightarrow x\sqrt{x^2+y^2}=2y$ (implicit Cartesian form).

Practice 39

Practice 42

For $r=2\sin\theta$, find Cartesian center/radius and identify traced interval for full circle once.

Full trace once on $\theta\in[0,\pi]$.

Practice 47

Practice 48

Find tangent angle condition for horizontal tangent when $r=\cos\theta$ using polar derivative formula.

Here $r'=-\sin\theta$, so $-\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta=\cos2\theta=0\Rightarrow\theta=\pi/4,3\pi/4,\dots$ with denominator checked nonzero.

Practice 51

Method question: when graphing $r=1+2\sin\theta$, which set of test angles gives reliable shape fastest?

Solution

Use key angles where $\sin\theta\in\{-1,0,1\}$ and quarter-turns: $0,\pi/2,\pi,3\pi/2,2\pi$, then add intersections/origin points if any.

Practice 52

Find all $\theta\in[0,2\pi)$ where $r=2+\cos\theta$ has $r=2$.

Solution

$2+\cos\theta=2\Rightarrow\cos\theta=0\Rightarrow\theta=\pi/2,3\pi/2$.

Practice 53

Convert Cartesian parabola $y^2=8x$ to polar.

Solution

$(r\sin\theta)^2=8r\cos\theta\Rightarrow r=\frac{8\cos\theta}{\sin^2\theta}$ (including $r=0$ at vertex).

Practice 54

For $r=3\cos\theta$, find maximum $y$ value on the curve.

Solution

Cartesian form is circle center $(1.5,0)$ radius $1.5$; max $y=1.5$.

Practice 55

Find points where $r=\cos\theta$ intersects the $y$-axis.

Solution

$y$-axis means $x=r\cos\theta=0$. Either $r=0$ (origin) or $\cos\theta=0\Rightarrow r=0$ anyway. Only origin.

Practice 56

Explain why rose $r=\sin(4\theta)$ has 8 petals but $r=\sin(5\theta)$ has 5, using periodicity and sign of $r$.

Solution

For even $n$, opposite-direction plotting with negative $r$ creates distinct extra petals, doubling count to $2n$. For odd $n$, second half retraces existing petals, leaving $n$ total.

Practice 57

Determine whether $r=\sin\theta\cos\theta$ is symmetric about origin.

Solution

$\theta\to\theta+\pi$ gives $\sin(\theta+\pi)\cos(\theta+\pi)=\sin\theta\cos\theta$, unchanged, so origin symmetry holds.

Practice 58

Convert $x^2+y^2+4x=0$ to polar and identify traced interval for full curve once.

Solution

$r^2+4r\cos\theta=0\Rightarrow r=-4\cos\theta$.

Circle traced once for $\theta\in[\pi/2,3\pi/2]$ where $r\ge0$ (or any equivalent full interval).

Practice 59

For $r=2(1-\cos\theta)$, find leftmost Cartesian point.

Solution

$x=r\cos\theta=2(1-\cos\theta)\cos\theta=2(\cos\theta-\cos^2\theta)$. Minimum occurs at $\theta=\pi$ giving $x=-4$ and $y=0$.

Practice 60

Capstone sketch logic: outline a 5-step exam workflow to sketch $r=1+2\cos\theta$ accurately without plotting software.

Solution

1) Identify family (limaçon, inner loop since $|a|<|b|$). 2) Symmetry test (x-axis). 3) Compute key $r$ at $\theta=0,\pi/2,\pi,3\pi/2$. 4) Solve $r=0$ for loop crossings. 5) Trace in increasing $\theta$, noting negative-$r$ segments flip direction.
