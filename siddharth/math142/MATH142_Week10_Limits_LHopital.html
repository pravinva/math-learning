Limits Revisited: Indeterminate Forms & L'Hôpital's Rule

Phase 3 begins. Direct substitution answers most limits instantly — the interesting cases are the seven indeterminate forms, where substitution gives a meaningless expression. This week: recognising them, and L'Hôpital's Rule, a single technique that resolves almost all of them by differentiating top and bottom separately. Exam-level worked examples and practice throughout. Solutions stay hidden until you click.

Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • The seven indeterminate forms: $\dfrac00,\ \dfrac{\infty}{\infty},\ 0\cdot\infty,\ \infty-\infty,\ 0^0,\ 1^\infty,\ \infty^0$.
  • Not indeterminate: forms like $\dfrac{5}{0}$ signal an infinite limit/asymptote directly — never apply L'Hôpital's Rule to these.
  • L'Hôpital's Rule: if $\lim\dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)}$ is $\dfrac00$ or $\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$, then $\lim\dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)}=\lim\dfrac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}$ (differentiate numerator and denominator separately — not the quotient rule). Reapply if still indeterminate.
  • Converting other forms: $0\cdot\infty$ and $\infty-\infty$ rewrite algebraically into $\dfrac00$ or $\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$; the exponential forms ($0^0,1^\infty,\infty^0$) take a logarithm first, since $\lim y=\lim e^{\ln y}$.
  • Always check first: factoring, rationalising, or a known standard limit (from MATH141) often resolves a limit faster than reaching for L'Hôpital's Rule.

0 Limits Review & Indeterminate Forms

Forms like $\dfrac00$ or $\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$ don't have a single, fixed value — depending on the specific functions involved, a limit that substitutes to $\dfrac00$ could equal literally any number, or not exist at all. Contrast this with $\dfrac{5}{0}$, which unambiguously signals a vertical asymptote or divergence — that form isn't indeterminate.

Before reaching for L'Hôpital's Rule, always check whether an old MATH141 technique — factoring, rationalising, or a known standard limit — resolves the problem faster.

Key idea

$$\text{Indeterminate: } \frac00,\ \frac{\infty}{\infty},\ 0\cdot\infty,\ \infty-\infty,\ 0^0,\ 1^\infty,\ \infty^0$$

Worked example

Example 1 — resolve via factoring. Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2}\dfrac{x^2-4}{x-2}$.

$\dfrac00$ form; factor: $\lim_{x\to2}(x+2)=4$.

Example 2 — recognising $\infty-\infty$. Identify the form in $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\left(\sqrt{x^2+x}-x\right)$.

Both terms $\to\infty$: $\infty-\infty$, indeterminate (would resolve via rationalising).

Practice 1

Identify the indeterminate form in $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{1-\cos x}{x\sin x}$, then resolve it using standard limits from MATH141 (no L'Hôpital needed).

Solution

Form: $\dfrac00$.

Rewrite: $\dfrac{1-\cos x}{x\sin x}=\dfrac{1-\cos x}{x^2}\cdot\dfrac{x}{\sin x}$. Using the standard limits $\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{1-\cos x}{x^2}=\dfrac12$ and $\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{x}{\sin x}=1$:

Answer: $\dfrac12\times1=\dfrac12$.

Practice 2

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to9}\frac{\sqrt{x}-3}{x-9}$ using rationalisation.

Solution

Multiply by $\dfrac{\sqrt x+3}{\sqrt x+3}$: $\dfrac{x-9}{(x-9)(\sqrt x+3)}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt x+3}$.

Answer: $\lim_{x\to9}\dfrac{1}{\sqrt x+3}=\dfrac16$.

Practice 3

Identify the indeterminate form in $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}x^{1/x}$, and explain why it's not simply $\infty^0=1$ by convention.

Solution

Form: $\infty^0$.

Unlike a fixed base raised to the power $0$ (which is unambiguously $1$), here both the base ($x\to\infty$) and exponent ($1/x\to0$) are changing simultaneously — the actual limiting value depends on the relative "speed" of these two changes, which is exactly what makes the form indeterminate rather than a simple convention. (It happens to evaluate to $1$ here, but that requires the logarithm technique to establish rigorously, not an assumption.)

Practice 4

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{e^{3x}-1-3x}{x^2}$ by recognising the indeterminate form (do not use L'Hôpital — identify the form only, and note which technique from Phase 3 would resolve it).

Solution

Direct substitution: $\dfrac{1-1-0}{0}=\dfrac00$ — indeterminate.

This is a case where L'Hôpital's Rule (next topic) or the Taylor series expansion of $e^{3x}$ (Week 12) would resolve it cleanly — recognising the form correctly is the first graded step before choosing either tool.

Practice 5

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to1}\frac{x^3-1}{x^2-1}$ using factoring, and explain why this is faster than L'Hôpital's Rule here.

Solution

$\dfrac{(x-1)(x^2+x+1)}{(x-1)(x+1)}=\dfrac{x^2+x+1}{x+1}$ (the shared factor $(x-1)$ cancels directly).

$\lim_{x\to1}\dfrac{x^2+x+1}{x+1}=\dfrac{3}{2}$.

Why faster: factoring resolves this in one algebraic step, while L'Hôpital's Rule would require differentiating both a cubic and quadratic and then substituting — more work for the same answer, since the cancellation was already visible.

Practice 6

Identify the indeterminate form in $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\left(\sqrt{x^2+3x}-\sqrt{x^2-3x}\right)$, then resolve it via rationalising.

Solution

Form: $\infty-\infty$.

Multiply by the conjugate: $\dfrac{(x^2+3x)-(x^2-3x)}{\sqrt{x^2+3x}+\sqrt{x^2-3x}}=\dfrac{6x}{\sqrt{x^2+3x}+\sqrt{x^2-3x}}$.

Divide by $x$ (for $x>0$): $\dfrac{6}{\sqrt{1+3/x}+\sqrt{1-3/x}}\to\dfrac{6}{1+1}=3$.

Answer: $3$.

Practice 7

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to4}\frac{x^2-16}{\sqrt{x}-2}$ using rationalising (of the denominator this time).

Solution

Multiply by $\dfrac{\sqrt x+2}{\sqrt x+2}$: $\dfrac{(x^2-16)(\sqrt x+2)}{x-4}=\dfrac{(x-4)(x+4)(\sqrt x+2)}{x-4}=(x+4)(\sqrt x+2)$.

At $x=4$: $(8)(2+2)=8(4)=32$.

Answer: $32$.

Practice 8

Identify the indeterminate form in $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\left(\frac{x+1}{x-1}\right)^x$, and explain (without fully evaluating) which technique from this week would resolve it.

Solution

As $x\to\infty$, $\dfrac{x+1}{x-1}\to1$ and the exponent $\to\infty$: form $1^\infty$.

This would be resolved by taking a logarithm first (converting to $x\ln\left(\dfrac{x+1}{x-1}\right)$, an $\infty\cdot0$ form, then rewriting as a quotient for L'Hôpital's Rule) — exactly the technique demonstrated in the next topic's Example 2 and further practice.

Practice 9

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2}\frac{x^3-8}{x^2-4}$ using factoring (recognise both as differences of powers).

Solution

$\dfrac{(x-2)(x^2+2x+4)}{(x-2)(x+2)}=\dfrac{x^2+2x+4}{x+2}$.

At $x=2$: $\dfrac{4+4+4}{4}=\dfrac{12}{4}=3$.

Answer: $3$.

Practice 10

Identify the indeterminate form in $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}(\sin x)^x$, and explain why this is genuinely different from the $0^0$ example in the Pattern section (which used $x^{\sin x}$), even though both involve a base and exponent approaching $0$.

Solution

As $x\to0^+$: $\sin x\to0$ and the exponent $x\to0$: form $0^0$, the same category as before.

Key difference: here the base is $\sin x$ (not $x$ itself) and the exponent is $x$ (not $\sin x$) — the roles are essentially swapped compared to $x^{\sin x}$. Despite the same indeterminate-form label, the specific logarithm computation ($\ln y=x\ln(\sin x)$ here, versus $\sin x\ln x$ before) is different and must be worked through separately; recognising the category doesn't skip the actual algebra needed to evaluate it.

1 L'Hôpital's Rule

Factoring and rationalising work for simple algebraic indeterminate forms, but fail for limits mixing transcendental functions, like $\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{\sin x}{x}$. L'Hôpital's Rule handles these — and everything else in $\dfrac00$ or $\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$ form — by differentiating the numerator and denominator separately.

Repeated application. If, after one application, the new limit is still indeterminate, apply the rule again — as many times as needed.

The other five forms. $0\cdot\infty$ and $\infty-\infty$ can usually be algebraically rewritten into $\dfrac00$ or $\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$ form. The exponential forms ($0^0,1^\infty,\infty^0$) are handled by taking a logarithm first.

Key idea

$$\lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x\to a}\frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)} \qquad \text{(for } \tfrac00 \text{ or } \tfrac{\infty}{\infty} \text{ forms only)}$$

Worked example

Example 1. Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\sin x}{x}$.

$\dfrac00$: $\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{\cos x}{1}=1$.

Example 2 — $0\cdot\infty$ converted first. Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}x\ln x$.

Rewrite as $\dfrac{\ln x}{1/x}$ ($-\infty/\infty$): $\lim_{x\to0^+}\dfrac{1/x}{-1/x^2}=\lim_{x\to0^+}(-x)=0$.

Practice 11

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\tan(3x)}{\sin(5x)}$.

Solution

$\dfrac00$; L'Hôpital: $\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{3\sec^2(3x)}{5\cos(5x)}=\dfrac{3(1)}{5(1)}=\dfrac35$.

Answer: $\dfrac35$.

Practice 12

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{\ln(x^2+1)}{\ln(x^4+3)}$.

Solution

$\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$; L'Hôpital: $\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{2x/(x^2+1)}{4x^3/(x^4+3)}=\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{2x(x^4+3)}{4x^3(x^2+1)}$.

For large $x$, dominant terms: $\dfrac{2x\cdot x^4}{4x^3\cdot x^2}=\dfrac{2x^5}{4x^5}=\dfrac12$.

Answer: $\dfrac12$.

Practice 13

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\left(x-\sqrt{x^2-3x}\right)$ (convert the $\infty-\infty$ form via rationalising, then apply L'Hôpital if still needed).

Solution

Multiply by the conjugate: $\dfrac{x^2-(x^2-3x)}{x+\sqrt{x^2-3x}}=\dfrac{3x}{x+\sqrt{x^2-3x}}$.

Divide top and bottom by $x$: $\dfrac{3}{1+\sqrt{1-3/x}}$. As $x\to\infty$, $3/x\to0$: $\dfrac{3}{1+1}=\dfrac32$.

Answer: $\dfrac32$.

Practice 14

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}x^{\sin x}$ (the $0^0$ case) using the logarithm trick.

Solution

Let $y=x^{\sin x}$; $\ln y=\sin x\ln x$, a $0\cdot(-\infty)$ form.

Rewrite: $\dfrac{\ln x}{1/\sin x}$, form $\dfrac{-\infty}{\infty}$. L'Hôpital: $\lim_{x\to0^+}\dfrac{1/x}{-\cos x/\sin^2x}=\lim_{x\to0^+}\dfrac{-\sin^2x}{x\cos x}$.

Since $\sin^2x\approx x^2$ near $0$: $\dfrac{-x^2}{x\cdot1}=-x\to0$. So $\ln y\to0\implies y\to e^0=1$.

Answer: $1$.

Practice 15

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\left(1+\frac{3}{x}\right)^x$ (the $1^\infty$ case) using the logarithm trick.

Solution

Let $y=(1+3/x)^x$; $\ln y=x\ln(1+3/x)$, form $\infty\cdot0$.

Rewrite: $\dfrac{\ln(1+3/x)}{1/x}$, form $\dfrac00$. L'Hôpital: $\lim\dfrac{\frac{-3/x^2}{1+3/x}}{-1/x^2}=\lim\dfrac{3}{1+3/x}=3$.

So $\ln y\to3 \implies y\to e^3$.

Answer: $e^3$.

Practice 16

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{x-\sin x}{x^3}$ (requires repeated application of L'Hôpital's Rule).

Solution

$\dfrac00$; L'Hôpital: $\lim\dfrac{1-\cos x}{3x^2}$ (still $\dfrac00$); again: $\lim\dfrac{\sin x}{6x}$ (still $\dfrac00$); again: $\lim\dfrac{\cos x}{6}=\dfrac16$.

Answer: $\dfrac16$.

Practice 17

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{x^3}{\ln x}$, and explain why L'Hôpital's Rule is not the ideal first choice for a polynomial-over-logarithm comparison like this, even though it works.

Solution

$\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$; L'Hôpital: $\lim\dfrac{3x^2}{1/x}=\lim3x^3=\infty$.

Answer: $\infty$ (diverges).

Although L'Hôpital's Rule works here, a faster route is simply knowing the standard hierarchy of growth rates (polynomials always dominate logarithms as $x\to\infty$) — recognising this immediately gives $\infty$ without any differentiation at all.

Practice 18

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\pi/2}(\sec x-\tan x)$ (an $\infty-\infty$ form requiring algebraic combination before L'Hôpital applies).

Solution

Combine into a single fraction: $\sec x-\tan x=\dfrac{1-\sin x}{\cos x}$, now form $\dfrac00$ as $x\to\pi/2$.

L'Hôpital: $\lim_{x\to\pi/2}\dfrac{-\cos x}{-\sin x}=\dfrac{0}{1}=0$.

Answer: $0$.

Practice 19

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\ln(1+x)-x}{x^2}$.

Solution

$\dfrac00$; L'Hôpital: $\lim\dfrac{\frac{1}{1+x}-1}{2x}=\lim\dfrac{\frac{1-(1+x)}{1+x}}{2x}=\lim\dfrac{-x/(1+x)}{2x}=\lim\dfrac{-1}{2(1+x)}$.

At $x=0$: $\dfrac{-1}{2}$.

Answer: $-\dfrac12$.

Practice 20

Explain why applying L'Hôpital's Rule to $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{x+\sin x}{x}$ would be unnecessarily complicated, and evaluate it using a simpler method instead.

Solution

L'Hôpital would give $\lim\dfrac{1+\cos x}{1}$, but $\cos x$ oscillates forever between $-1$ and $1$ and never settles to a single value as $x\to\infty$ — so L'Hôpital's Rule technically fails to produce a valid answer here (the new limit doesn't exist), even though the original limit does exist.

Simpler method: rewrite as $1+\dfrac{\sin x}{x}$; since $-\dfrac1x\le\dfrac{\sin x}{x}\le\dfrac1x$ and both bounds $\to0$ (Squeeze Theorem), $\dfrac{\sin x}{x}\to0$, so the whole limit is $1+0=1$.

Answer: $1$ (this is also a cautionary example: L'Hôpital's Rule can fail to resolve a limit that genuinely exists, if the new limit after differentiating doesn't converge).
