Second-Order DEs: the Characteristic Equation, Root Cases & SHM

A first-order linear DE with constant coefficients had solution $y=Ce^{kt}$. Trying the same exponential guess in a second-order DE turns the entire differential equation into an ordinary algebraic (quadratic) equation. This closes Phase 2, applying the result directly to simple harmonic motion. Exam-level worked examples and practice throughout. Solutions stay hidden until you click.

Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Characteristic equation: for $ay''+by'+cy=0$, try $y=e^{rx}$, giving $ar^2+br+c=0$.
  • Real, distinct roots ($b^2-4ac>0$): $y=C_1e^{r_1x}+C_2e^{r_2x}$.
  • Repeated root ($b^2-4ac=0$): $y=(C_1+C_2x)e^{rx}$.
  • Complex roots $\alpha\pm\beta i$ ($b^2-4ac<0$): $y=e^{\alpha x}(C_1\cos\beta x+C_2\sin\beta x)$.
  • Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM): $m\dfrac{d^2x}{dt^2}=-kx \implies \dfrac{d^2x}{dt^2}+\omega^2x=0$, $\omega=\sqrt{k/m}$; solution $x(t)=C_1\cos\omega t+C_2\sin\omega t$.
  • Initial value problems: a second-order DE's general solution has two arbitrary constants, so it takes two conditions (typically $y(0)$ and $y'(0)$) to pin down a particular solution — always differentiate the general solution before applying the second condition.

0 Second-Order DEs: the Characteristic Equation & Three Root Cases

For $ay''+by'+cy=0$ (homogeneous, constant coefficients), try $y=e^{rx}$. Then $y'=re^{rx}$, $y''=r^2e^{rx}$, and substituting gives $e^{rx}(ar^2+br+c)=0$. Since $e^{rx}$ is never zero, the guess works exactly when $r$ satisfies the characteristic equation $ar^2+br+c=0$ — an ordinary quadratic.

The repeated-root case needs an extra factor of $x$ because a single $e^{rx}$ isn't "enough" solutions for a second-order equation (which needs two independent building blocks); $xe^{rx}$ can be verified directly to also solve the equation whenever $r$ is repeated. The complex case converts $e^{(\alpha+\beta i)x}$ into real functions using Euler's formula.

Key idea
DiscriminantRootsGeneral solution
$b^2-4ac>0$Real, distinct: $r_1,r_2$$y=C_1e^{r_1x}+C_2e^{r_2x}$
$b^2-4ac=0$Repeated: $r$$y=(C_1+C_2x)e^{rx}$
$b^2-4ac<0$Complex: $\alpha\pm\beta i$$y=e^{\alpha x}(C_1\cos\beta x+C_2\sin\beta x)$
Worked example

Example 1 — real, distinct roots. Solve $y''-3y'+2y=0$.

$r^2-3r+2=(r-1)(r-2)=0 \implies r=1,2$. $y=C_1e^x+C_2e^{2x}$.

Example 2 — complex roots. Solve $y''+4y=0$.

$r^2+4=0 \implies r=\pm2i$ ($\alpha=0,\beta=2$). $y=C_1\cos2x+C_2\sin2x$.

Practice 1

Solve $y''+5y'+6y=0$.

Solution

$r^2+5r+6=(r+2)(r+3)=0 \implies r=-2,-3$.

Answer: $y=C_1e^{-2x}+C_2e^{-3x}$.

Solve $4y''-4y'+y=0$.

Solution

Practice 3

Solve $y''-6y'+13y=0$.

Solution

$r=\dfrac{6\pm\sqrt{36-52}}{2}=\dfrac{6\pm\sqrt{-16}}{2}=3\pm2i$.

Answer: $y=e^{3x}(C_1\cos2x+C_2\sin2x)$.

Practice 4

Solve $2y''+7y'-4y=0$.

Solution

$2r^2+7r-4=0$. Using the quadratic formula: $r=\dfrac{-7\pm\sqrt{49+32}}{4}=\dfrac{-7\pm9}{4}$.

$r=\dfrac{2}{4}=\tfrac12$ or $r=\dfrac{-16}{4}=-4$.

Answer: $y=C_1e^{x/2}+C_2e^{-4x}$.

Practice 5

Solve $y''+2y'+10y=0$.

Solution

$r=\dfrac{-2\pm\sqrt{4-40}}{2}=\dfrac{-2\pm\sqrt{-36}}{2}=-1\pm3i$.

Answer: $y=e^{-x}(C_1\cos3x+C_2\sin3x)$.

Practice 6

Solve $9y''-6y'+y=0$.

Solution

$9r^2-6r+1=(3r-1)^2=0 \implies r=\tfrac13$ (repeated).

Answer: $y=(C_1+C_2x)e^{x/3}$.

Practice 7

Verify by direct substitution that $y=xe^{-3x}$ solves $y''+6y'+9y=0$ (a repeated-root equation with $r=-3$).

Solution

$y=xe^{-3x}$; $y'=e^{-3x}-3xe^{-3x}=e^{-3x}(1-3x)$; $y''=-3e^{-3x}(1-3x)+e^{-3x}(-3)=e^{-3x}(-3+9x-3)=e^{-3x}(9x-6)$.

Substitute: $y''+6y'+9y = e^{-3x}(9x-6)+6e^{-3x}(1-3x)+9xe^{-3x}$

$=e^{-3x}\big[(9x-6)+(6-18x)+9x\big]=e^{-3x}\big[9x-6+6-18x+9x\big]=e^{-3x}(0)=0$. ✓ Confirmed.

Practice 9

Solve $y''-4y'+4y=0$, and separately verify that $y=e^{2x}$ alone would NOT be a general solution (i.e. it can't satisfy an arbitrary pair of initial conditions).

Solution

$r^2-4r+4=(r-2)^2=0 \implies r=2$ (repeated). General solution: $y=(C_1+C_2x)e^{2x}$.

Why $y=Ce^{2x}$ alone is insufficient: this form has only one arbitrary constant $C$, but a second-order DE's general solution needs two independent constants to match arbitrary initial conditions on both $y(0)$ and $y'(0)$. With only $C$, once $y(0)$ is fixed, $y'(0)=2C\cdot y(0)$-related value is automatically determined too — there's no freedom left to independently choose $y'(0)$, which a genuine second-order IVP requires.

Practice 10

Solve $y''+y'+y=0$ (leave the answer in terms of exact irrational coefficients).

Solution

$r=\dfrac{-1\pm\sqrt{1-4}}{2}=\dfrac{-1\pm i\sqrt3}{2}$, so $\alpha=-\tfrac12,\beta=\dfrac{\sqrt3}{2}$.

Answer: $y=e^{-x/2}\left(C_1\cos\dfrac{\sqrt3}{2}x+C_2\sin\dfrac{\sqrt3}{2}x\right)$.

1 Second-Order DE Applications — SHM & Initial Value Problems

A mass on a spring obeying Hooke's Law has acceleration proportional to (negative) displacement. Newton's second law turns that physical statement directly into the complex-root second-order DE: $m\dfrac{d^2x}{dt^2}=-kx \implies \dfrac{d^2x}{dt^2}+\omega^2x=0$, with $\omega=\sqrt{k/m}$ the natural angular frequency.

Two initial conditions, two constants. A second-order DE's general solution has two arbitrary constants — it takes two pieces of information (typically $x(0)$ and $x'(0)$) to pin down a particular solution.

Key idea

$$\frac{d^2x}{dt^2}+\omega^2x=0, \qquad x(t)=C_1\cos(\omega t)+C_2\sin(\omega t), \qquad \omega=\sqrt{k/m}$$

Worked example

Example 1. A mass on a spring satisfies $x''+4x=0$, $x(0)=3$, $x'(0)=0$. Find $x(t)$.

$\omega=2$: $x=C_1\cos2t+C_2\sin2t$; $x(0)=C_1=3$; $x'(0)=2C_2=0\implies C_2=0$. $x(t)=3\cos2t$.

Example 2. Solve $y''-y'-2y=0$, $y(0)=1$, $y'(0)=5$.

$r=2,-1$: $y=C_1e^{2x}+C_2e^{-x}$. Solving $C_1+C_2=1$, $2C_1-C_2=5$ gives $C_1=2,C_2=-1$: $y=2e^{2x}-e^{-x}$.

Practice 11

A mass of 4 kg on a spring with $k=36$ N/m is displaced 0.2 m and released from rest. Find $x(t)$ and the period of oscillation.

Solution

$\omega=\sqrt{k/m}=\sqrt{36/4}=3$. $x(t)=C_1\cos3t+C_2\sin3t$; $x(0)=0.2=C_1$; $x'(0)=3C_2=0\implies C_2=0$.

$x(t)=0.2\cos3t$. Period $T=\dfrac{2\pi}{\omega}=\dfrac{2\pi}{3}$.

Answer: $x(t)=0.2\cos(3t)$; period $\dfrac{2\pi}{3}\approx2.09$ s.

Practice 12

Solve $y''+3y'-4y=0$, $y(0)=2$, $y'(0)=-1$.

Solution

$r^2+3r-4=(r+4)(r-1)=0 \implies r=-4,1$. $y=C_1e^{-4x}+C_2e^{x}$.

$y'=-4C_1e^{-4x}+C_2e^x$. $y(0)=C_1+C_2=2$; $y'(0)=-4C_1+C_2=-1$.

Subtract: $(C_1+C_2)-(-4C_1+C_2)=2-(-1) \implies 5C_1=3 \implies C_1=\tfrac35$, so $C_2=2-\tfrac35=\tfrac75$.

Answer: $y=\dfrac35e^{-4x}+\dfrac75e^{x}$.

Practice 13

A mass on a spring satisfies $x''+16x=0$ with $x(0)=-1$ and $x'(0)=8$. Find $x(t)$, and rewrite it in the form $R\cos(\omega t-\phi)$ (amplitude-phase form).

Solution

$\omega=4$: $x(0)=C_1=-1$; $x'(0)=4C_2=8\implies C_2=2$. $x(t)=-\cos4t+2\sin4t$.

Amplitude: $R=\sqrt{C_1^2+C_2^2}=\sqrt{1+4}=\sqrt5$.

Phase: $\tan\phi=\dfrac{-C_1}{C_2}$... using the identity $C_1\cos\omega t+C_2\sin\omega t=R\cos(\omega t-\phi)$ with $R\cos\phi=C_1,R\sin\phi=C_2$: $\cos\phi=\dfrac{-1}{\sqrt5},\sin\phi=\dfrac{2}{\sqrt5}$, so $\phi$ is in the second quadrant: $\phi=\pi-\arctan(2)\approx2.034$ rad.

Answer: $x(t)=\sqrt5\cos(4t-\phi)$ with $\phi\approx2.034$ rad (amplitude $\sqrt5\approx2.236$).

Practice 14

Solve $y''-2y'+y=0$, $y(0)=3$, $y'(0)=1$ (repeated root case).

Solution

$r^2-2r+1=(r-1)^2=0 \implies r=1$ (repeated). $y=(C_1+C_2x)e^x$.

$y'=C_2e^x+(C_1+C_2x)e^x=(C_1+C_2+C_2x)e^x$.

$y(0)=C_1=3$; $y'(0)=C_1+C_2=1 \implies C_2=1-3=-2$.

Answer: $y=(3-2x)e^x$.

Practice 15

A mass of 0.5 kg is attached to a spring with $k=8$ N/m. Find $\omega$, and if the mass is pulled down 0.1 m and given an initial upward velocity of 0.4 m/s (taking down as positive), find $x(t)$.

Solution

$\omega=\sqrt{k/m}=\sqrt{8/0.5}=\sqrt{16}=4$.

$x(0)=0.1=C_1$; upward velocity means $x'(0)=-0.4$ (negative, since down is positive): $4C_2=-0.4 \implies C_2=-0.1$.

Answer: $x(t)=0.1\cos4t-0.1\sin4t$.

Practice 16

Solve $y''+6y'+13y=0$, $y(0)=0$, $y'(0)=6$ (complex roots with nonzero real part, combined with an IVP).

Solution

$r=\dfrac{-6\pm\sqrt{36-52}}{2}=-3\pm2i$. $y=e^{-3x}(C_1\cos2x+C_2\sin2x)$.

$y(0)=C_1=0$, so $y=C_2e^{-3x}\sin2x$.

$y'=C_2\big[-3e^{-3x}\sin2x+2e^{-3x}\cos2x\big]$; at $x=0$: $y'(0)=C_2(0+2)=2C_2=6\implies C_2=3$.

Answer: $y=3e^{-3x}\sin2x$.

Practice 17

A spring-mass system has $m=1$ kg, $k=25$ N/m, and starts at the equilibrium position with an initial velocity of 10 m/s. Find the maximum displacement reached.

Solution

$\omega=\sqrt{25/1}=5$. $x(0)=0=C_1$; $x'(0)=5C_2=10\implies C_2=2$. $x(t)=2\sin5t$.

Maximum displacement is the amplitude: since $x(t)=2\sin5t$ has no cosine term, amplitude $=|C_2|=2$.

Answer: maximum displacement is 2 m.

Practice 18

Solve $y''-5y'+6y=0$, $y(0)=2$, $y'(0)=7$, and verify your answer satisfies both initial conditions by direct substitution.

Solution

$r^2-5r+6=(r-2)(r-3)=0 \implies r=2,3$. $y=C_1e^{2x}+C_2e^{3x}$.

$y'=2C_1e^{2x}+3C_2e^{3x}$. $y(0)=C_1+C_2=2$; $y'(0)=2C_1+3C_2=7$.

Subtract twice the first from the second: $(2C_1+3C_2)-2(C_1+C_2)=7-4 \implies C_2=3$, so $C_1=2-3=-1$.

Answer: $y=-e^{2x}+3e^{3x}$. Check: $y(0)=-1+3=2$. ✓ $y'(0)=-2+9=7$. ✓

Practice 19

Explain physically why the SHM solution $x(t)=C_1\cos\omega t+C_2\sin\omega t$ never grows or decays in amplitude over time (unlike the exponential-decay solutions seen in Week 7), connecting this to the fact that the characteristic roots are purely imaginary ($\alpha=0$).

Solution

The general complex-root solution is $y=e^{\alpha x}(C_1\cos\beta x+C_2\sin\beta x)$, where the $e^{\alpha x}$ factor controls growth ($\alpha>0$) or decay ($\alpha<0$) of the oscillation's envelope. For ideal SHM, the characteristic equation $r^2+\omega^2=0$ gives purely imaginary roots $r=\pm\omega i$, meaning $\alpha=0$ exactly — so $e^{\alpha x}=e^0=1$, a constant multiplier that neither grows nor shrinks. Physically, this reflects the idealised assumption of no energy loss (no friction or damping) in the spring-mass system: the oscillation repeats with exactly the same amplitude forever.

Practice 20

A second-order DE has general solution $y=C_1e^{-2x}+C_2e^{-5x}$. If $y(0)=4$ and the solution approaches $0$ as $x\to\infty$ at the slower of the two possible exponential rates (i.e. dominated by the $e^{-2x}$ term for large $x$), find both constants given additionally that $y'(0)=-11$.

Solution

(The "slower rate at infinity" condition is automatically true for any nonzero $C_1$, since $e^{-2x}$ decays slower than $e^{-5x}$ — this phrasing is simply confirming the solution has a nonzero $C_1$, not adding new information beyond the two IVP conditions.)

$y'=-2C_1e^{-2x}-5C_2e^{-5x}$. $y(0)=C_1+C_2=4$; $y'(0)=-2C_1-5C_2=-11$.

From the first equation, $C_1=4-C_2$. Substitute: $-2(4-C_2)-5C_2=-11 \implies -8+2C_2-5C_2=-11 \implies -3C_2=-3 \implies C_2=1$, so $C_1=3$.

Answer: $C_1=3,C_2=1$; $y=3e^{-2x}+e^{-5x}$.

Practice 39

(a) Decide true/false: if roots are complex, solution is always bounded. (b) Give counterexample or proof idea.

Solution

False in general: with roots $\alpha\pm i\beta$, factor $e^{\alpha x}$ controls growth.

If $\alpha>0$ unbounded growth; if $\alpha=0$ bounded oscillation; if $\alpha<0$ decaying.

Practice 40

(a) Build characteristic equation for $y''+py'+qy=0$ with roots $-2$ and $-2+3i$ impossible? explain. (b) Give valid real-coefficient complex pair correction.

Solution

For real coefficients, nonreal roots occur in conjugate pairs. So $-2+3i$ requires $-2-3i$ too.

Valid quadratic: $(r+2-3i)(r+2+3i)=(r+2)^2+9=r^2+4r+13$.
