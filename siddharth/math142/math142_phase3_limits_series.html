W10
Limits Review & Indeterminate Forms
Recognising when direct substitution genuinely fails
Direct substitution answers most limits instantly. The interesting cases are the ones where substitution produces a meaningless expression like $\dfrac{0}{0}$ — not because the limit doesn't exist, but because the algebra needs more work before an answer appears.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

What "indeterminate" actually means. Forms like $\dfrac00$ or $\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$ don't have a single, fixed value — depending on the specific functions involved, a limit that substitutes to $\dfrac00$ could equal literally any number, or not exist at all. (Contrast this with $\dfrac{5}{0}$, which unambiguously signals a vertical asymptote or divergence — that form isn't indeterminate.)

The seven indeterminate forms. $\dfrac00$, $\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$, $0\cdot\infty$, $\infty-\infty$, $0^0$, $1^\infty$, $\infty^0$ — all of these require extra work (algebraic manipulation or, as in the next topic, L'Hôpital's Rule) before a limit's value can be determined.

Techniques already available. Factor-and-cancel and rationalising (both from MATH141) resolve many $\dfrac00$ limits without needing anything new — always try these first, since they're often faster than L'Hôpital's Rule.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \text{Indeterminate: } \frac00,\ \frac{\infty}{\infty},\ 0\cdot\infty,\ \infty-\infty,\ 0^0,\ 1^\infty,\ \infty^0 \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — resolve via factoring (no new technique needed)

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2}\dfrac{x^2-4}{x-2}$.

Direct substitution gives $\dfrac{0}{0}$ — indeterminate, but factoring resolves it immediately.

\[ \lim_{x\to2}\frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2}=\lim_{x\to2}(x+2)=4 \]
ANSWER $4$
Example 2 — recognising $\infty-\infty$

Identify the indeterminate form in $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\left(\sqrt{x^2+x}-x\right)$, without evaluating yet.

As $x\to\infty$, both $\sqrt{x^2+x}\to\infty$ and $x\to\infty$, so direct substitution gives $\infty-\infty$ — indeterminate, since neither term "wins" without more information about their relative growth rates.

ANSWER $\infty-\infty$ (would resolve via rationalising: multiply by the conjugate)
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
Before reaching for L'Hôpital's Rule (next topic), always check whether an old MATH141 technique — factoring, rationalising, or a known standard limit — resolves the problem faster. L'Hôpital's Rule is powerful but not always the fastest route.
W10
L'Hopital's Rule
A calculus shortcut for resolving 0/0 and ∞/∞ limits directly
Factoring and rationalising work for simple algebraic indeterminate forms, but they fail for limits mixing transcendental functions, like $\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{\sin x}{x}$ or $\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{\ln x}{x}$. L'Hôpital's Rule handles these — and everything else in $\frac00$ or $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$ form — with a single, remarkably simple trick: differentiate the top and bottom separately.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

The rule. If $\lim_{x\to a}\dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)}$ produces the indeterminate form $\dfrac00$ or $\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$, and $f,g$ are differentiable near $a$ with $g'(x)\ne0$, then:

\[ \lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x\to a}\frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)} \]

provided this new limit exists (or is $\pm\infty$). Crucially, this is not the quotient rule — you differentiate the numerator and denominator completely separately, not as a single fraction.

Repeated application. If, after one application, the new limit is still indeterminate, apply the rule again (differentiate top and bottom once more) — repeat as many times as needed until the indeterminate form clears.

Handling the other five indeterminate forms. $0\cdot\infty$ and $\infty-\infty$ can usually be algebraically rewritten into $\dfrac00$ or $\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$ form first (e.g. rewrite $fg$ as $\dfrac{f}{1/g}$). The exponential forms ($0^0$, $1^\infty$, $\infty^0$) are handled by taking a logarithm first — since $\lim y = \lim e^{\ln y}$, and $\ln y$ often converts the exponent into a product, itself reducible to $\dfrac00$ or $\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x\to a}\frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)} \qquad \text{(for } \tfrac00 \text{ or } \tfrac{\infty}{\infty} \text{ forms only)} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — basic application

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{\sin x}{x}$ using L'Hôpital's Rule.

Direct substitution gives $\dfrac00$.

\[ \lim_{x\to0}\frac{\sin x}{x} = \lim_{x\to0}\frac{\cos x}{1} = \cos0 = 1 \]
ANSWER $1$ (confirms the standard trig limit from MATH141)
Example 2 — the $0\cdot\infty$ form, converted first

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}x\ln x$.

This is $0\cdot(-\infty)$. Rewrite as a quotient: $x\ln x = \dfrac{\ln x}{1/x}$, now $\dfrac{-\infty}{\infty}$ form.

\[ \lim_{x\to0^+}\frac{\ln x}{1/x} = \lim_{x\to0^+}\frac{1/x}{-1/x^2} = \lim_{x\to0^+}(-x) = 0 \]
ANSWER $0$
Note Study notes ▶
Watch out
L'Hôpital's Rule only applies to genuinely indeterminate forms — applying it to a limit that already has a determinate value (like $\dfrac{5}{0}$, or a limit that doesn't produce $\dfrac00$/$\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$ at all) gives a wrong answer. Always verify the indeterminate form exists before differentiating, and re-check after every application in case it's resolved.

Sequences — Convergence & Limit Laws
A function with only whole-number inputs, and what "approaching a value" means for one
A sequence is really just a function whose domain is restricted to the positive integers — $a_n$ instead of $f(x)$. Every idea about limits already built (algebra of limits, indeterminate forms, L'Hôpital's Rule) transfers almost unchanged, just with $n\to\infty$ replacing $x\to\infty$.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Definition. A sequence $\{a_n\}$ is an ordered list $a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots$ It converges to $L$ if $\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n=L$ — the terms get arbitrarily close to $L$ as $n$ grows. If no such $L$ exists, the sequence diverges.

Reusing limit laws. Since a sequence is a special case of a function, the algebra of limits from Week 2 (MATH141) transfers directly: $\lim(a_n+b_n)=\lim a_n+\lim b_n$, and so on. If a sequence's formula matches a continuous function's formula evaluated at integers, its limit can even be found by first solving the corresponding function limit (using L'Hôpital's Rule if needed) and then restricting to integer inputs.

Monotonic and bounded sequences. A sequence is monotonic if it's always increasing or always decreasing; it's bounded if it never exceeds some fixed value (above) or falls below one (below). A key theorem: every bounded, monotonic sequence converges — this gives a way to prove convergence even without finding the exact limit.

Squeeze Theorem for sequences. If $b_n\le a_n\le c_n$ for all $n$, and $\lim b_n=\lim c_n=L$, then $\lim a_n=L$ too — identical in spirit to the Squeeze Theorem for ordinary function limits.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \lim_{n\to\infty}a_n=L \quad\text{(convergence)}, \qquad \text{bounded + monotonic} \implies \text{convergent} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — find a sequence limit

Determine whether $a_n=\dfrac{2n+1}{n+3}$ converges, and if so, to what.

\[ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2n+1}{n+3} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2+1/n}{1+3/n} = 2 \]
ANSWER Converges to $2$
Example 2 — Squeeze Theorem

Show that $a_n=\dfrac{\sin n}{n}$ converges to $0$.

Since $-1\le\sin n\le1$ for every $n$, dividing by $n>0$ gives $-\dfrac1n\le\dfrac{\sin n}{n}\le\dfrac1n$.

\[ \lim_{n\to\infty}\left(-\frac1n\right)=0=\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac1n \]
ANSWER By the Squeeze Theorem, $\lim_{n\to\infty}\dfrac{\sin n}{n}=0$
Note Study notes ▶
Uni Bridge
Sequences are the foundation the next two topics (series and Taylor polynomials) are built entirely on top of — a series is literally defined as the limit of a sequence of partial sums, so every convergence idea here reappears immediately.
W11
Series — Definition, Geometric Series & the Divergence/Integral Tests
Summing infinitely many terms, and the first two tools for deciding if that sum makes sense
Adding infinitely many numbers sounds like it should always give infinity — but $\dfrac12+\dfrac14+\dfrac18+\cdots$ visibly approaches $1$, not infinity. A series is defined precisely to capture this: not as "infinite addition" directly, but as the limit of a sequence of ever-longer finite sums.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Defining a series via partial sums. Given a sequence $\{a_n\}$, the $n$-th partial sum is $S_n=a_1+a_2+\cdots+a_n$ — an ordinary finite sum. The infinite series $\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n$ is defined to equal $\lim_{n\to\infty}S_n$, reusing the sequence-convergence machinery from the previous topic. If that limit exists (is finite), the series converges; otherwise it diverges.

Geometric series — solvable exactly. $\sum_{n=0}^\infty ar^n$ has a known closed-form partial sum $S_n=a\dfrac{1-r^n}{1-r}$ (provable by the standard "multiply by $r$ and subtract" trick). Taking $n\to\infty$: if $|r|<1$, $r^n\to0$, giving:

\[ \sum_{n=0}^\infty ar^n = \frac{a}{1-r} \qquad (|r|<1) \]

If $|r|\ge1$, the series diverges.

The Divergence Test. If $\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n\ne0$, the series $\sum a_n$ must diverge — this follows because convergent partial sums would require the terms being added to shrink toward zero eventually, or the sum could never settle. Warning: the converse is false — $\lim a_n=0$ does NOT guarantee convergence (the harmonic series below is the classic counterexample).

The Integral Test. If $f(x)$ is positive, decreasing, and continuous for $x\ge1$ with $f(n)=a_n$, then $\sum a_n$ converges exactly when $\int_1^\infty f(x)\,dx$ converges — directly reusing the improper integral machinery from Phase 1.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \sum_{n=0}^\infty ar^n=\frac{a}{1-r}\ (|r|<1), \qquad \lim a_n\ne0 \implies \text{diverges}, \qquad \sum a_n \text{ converges} \iff \int_1^\infty f(x)dx \text{ converges} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — geometric series

Evaluate $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty \left(\frac13\right)^n$.

$a=1$, $r=\tfrac13$, $|r|<1$.

\[ \sum_{n=0}^\infty \left(\frac13\right)^n = \frac{1}{1-1/3}=\frac{3}{2} \]
ANSWER $\dfrac32$
Example 2 — the harmonic series diverges (Integral Test)

Show $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac1n$ diverges, even though $\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac1n=0$.

$f(x)=\dfrac1x$ is positive, decreasing, continuous for $x\ge1$.

\[ \int_1^\infty \frac1x\,dx = \lim_{t\to\infty}[\ln x]_1^t = \infty \]
ANSWER The integral diverges, so by the Integral Test, the harmonic series diverges too — despite its terms shrinking to zero, confirming the Divergence Test's converse is false
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
Always check the Divergence Test first — it's the fastest possible check (just take a limit), and if it shows divergence, you're done immediately without needing any heavier test.

Series — Comparison & Limit Comparison Tests
Deciding convergence by comparing an unfamiliar series to a known one
Not every series matches a geometric or $p$-series pattern exactly, and not every series has an easy antiderivative for the Integral Test. When a series closely resembles a known convergent or divergent series, comparing term-by-term (or via a ratio) often settles the question without any new integration at all.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Direct Comparison Test. If $0\le a_n\le b_n$ for all $n$ (past some point), then: if $\sum b_n$ converges, so does $\sum a_n$ (it's "squeezed" below a convergent series); if $\sum a_n$ diverges, so does $\sum b_n$ (it "drags up" an already-divergent series). Intuitively: a series bounded above by something finite can't itself blow up, and a series bounded below by something infinite can't stay finite.

Limit Comparison Test. Sometimes finding a clean term-by-term inequality is awkward. Instead, compute:

\[ L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{a_n}{b_n} \]

If $L$ is a finite positive number, $\sum a_n$ and $\sum b_n$ either both converge or both diverge — they behave identically for convergence purposes, since their terms are eventually proportional. This is often easier than the direct test because it avoids needing an exact inequality, just an asymptotic (large-$n$) comparison.

Choosing a comparison series. For a series with rational-function-like terms, compare to the $p$-series matching the dominant powers of $n$ (top and bottom) — exactly the same "look at the highest power" instinct from limits at infinity.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ 0\le a_n\le b_n:\ \sum b_n \text{ conv.} \implies \sum a_n \text{ conv.} \qquad L=\lim\frac{a_n}{b_n}\in(0,\infty) \implies \text{same behaviour} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — Direct Comparison Test

Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \dfrac{1}{n^2+1}$ converges.

For all $n\ge1$: $\dfrac{1}{n^2+1}\le\dfrac{1}{n^2}$. We know $\sum\dfrac{1}{n^2}$ converges (a $p$-series with $p=2>1$).

ANSWER By the Direct Comparison Test, $\sum\dfrac{1}{n^2+1}$ converges too
Example 2 — Limit Comparison Test

Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \dfrac{n}{n^3+2}$ converges, comparing to $\sum\dfrac{1}{n^2}$.

\[ L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n/(n^3+2)}{1/n^2} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^3}{n^3+2} = 1 \]

$L=1$ is finite and positive.

ANSWER Since $\sum\dfrac{1}{n^2}$ converges ($p=2>1$) and $L$ is finite and positive, $\sum\dfrac{n}{n^3+2}$ converges too
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
The Limit Comparison Test is usually easier than the Direct Comparison Test in practice, because finding a clean term-by-term inequality (needed for direct comparison) can be fiddly, while identifying the dominant behaviour for large $n$ (needed for limit comparison) is often immediate from inspection.
W11
Series — Ratio & Root Tests
Tools built specifically for series with factorials, powers, or exponentials
Comparison tests need you to already suspect what a series behaves like. For series built from factorials or $n$-th powers — where no obvious comparison series jumps out — the Ratio and Root Tests instead look directly at how fast consecutive terms shrink, which is exactly the information needed to judge convergence for these shapes.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

The Ratio Test. Compute $L=\lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\dfrac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|$. If $L<1$, the series converges absolutely (terms shrink geometrically fast in the long run); if $L>1$ (or $L=\infty$), it diverges (terms grow, or don't shrink fast enough); if $L=1$, the test is inconclusive — try a different test.

Why factorials and exponentials suit this test. The ratio $\dfrac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}$ causes enormous cancellation for factorial terms — e.g. $\dfrac{(n+1)!}{n!}=n+1$ collapses instantly — which is exactly why the Ratio Test is the natural first choice whenever factorials appear.

The Root Test. Compute $L=\lim_{n\to\infty}\sqrt[n]{|a_n|}$. Same conclusion structure as the Ratio Test ($L<1$ converges, $L>1$ diverges, $L=1$ inconclusive) — this test is the natural choice when the entire term is already raised to the $n$-th power, since the $n$-th root then cancels that power directly.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \text{Ratio: } L=\lim\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|, \qquad \text{Root: } L=\lim\sqrt[n]{|a_n|}, \qquad L<1:\text{conv.},\ L>1:\text{div.},\ L=1:\text{inconclusive} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — Ratio Test on a factorial series

Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \dfrac{n!}{2^n}$ converges.

\[ L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{(n+1)!/2^{n+1}}{n!/2^n}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{(n+1)}{2} = \infty \]
ANSWER $L=\infty>1$ — diverges
Example 2 — Root Test on a power series

Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \left(\dfrac{n}{2n+1}\right)^n$ converges.

\[ L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\sqrt[n]{\left(\frac{n}{2n+1}\right)^n} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n}{2n+1} = \frac12 \]
ANSWER $L=\tfrac12<1$ — converges
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
If $L=1$ from either test, that's not "converges by a hair" — it genuinely tells you nothing, and you must switch to a completely different test (comparison, integral, or divergence) to settle the question.

Power Series & Taylor Polynomials
A series with x in it — and the polynomial that best approximates a function near a point
Every series so far has had fixed numerical terms. A power series lets the terms depend on $x$ — turning the series itself into a function, one built entirely from powers of $x$, which is exactly the raw material needed to approximate other, more complicated functions.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Power series and radius of convergence. A power series centred at $a$ has the form $\sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n(x-a)^n$. For each fixed value of $x$, this becomes an ordinary numerical series — so the Ratio Test (previous topic) can be applied directly, treating $x$ as a parameter, to find the values of $x$ for which the series converges. This produces an interval (typically) centred at $a$, with a radius of convergence $R$ found from $L=\lim\left|\dfrac{c_{n+1}(x-a)^{n+1}}{c_n(x-a)^n}\right|<1$.

Building a Taylor polynomial. The motivating question: what polynomial best matches a function $f$ near $x=a$, not just in value but in every derivative too? Demanding $P(a)=f(a)$, $P'(a)=f'(a)$, $P''(a)=f''(a)$, and so on up to degree $n$, and solving for each coefficient (using the fact that the $k$-th derivative of $(x-a)^k$ at $x=a$ is $k!$, and every lower-degree term vanishes) gives:

\[ P_n(x) = \sum_{k=0}^n \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k \]

This is the $n$-th degree Taylor polynomial of $f$ centred at $a$ — matching $f$'s value and its first $n$ derivatives exactly at $x=a$, and (usually) approximating $f$ increasingly well nearby as $n$ grows.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ P_n(x) = \sum_{k=0}^n \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — find a radius of convergence

Find the radius of convergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty \dfrac{x^n}{n!}$.

\[ L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{x^{n+1}/(n+1)!}{x^n/n!}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{|x|}{n+1} = 0 \]
ANSWER $L=0<1$ for every $x$ — the series converges for all real $x$, i.e. $R=\infty$
Example 2 — build a 3rd-degree Taylor polynomial

Find $P_3(x)$ for $f(x)=\cos x$ centred at $a=0$.

$f(0)=1$, $f'(x)=-\sin x\implies f'(0)=0$, $f''(x)=-\cos x\implies f''(0)=-1$, $f^{(3)}(x)=\sin x\implies f^{(3)}(0)=0$.

\[ P_3(x) = 1+0\cdot x+\frac{-1}{2!}x^2+\frac{0}{3!}x^3 = 1-\frac{x^2}{2} \]
ANSWER $P_3(x)=1-\dfrac{x^2}{2}$
Note Study notes ▶
Uni Bridge
Every derivative rule and Taylor polynomial idea here generalises directly in MATH142's successor subjects to multivariable Taylor expansions — the exact same "match value and derivatives at a point" logic, just with partial derivatives instead of ordinary ones.

Taylor & Maclaurin Series — Common Expansions & Applications
Letting the Taylor polynomial run to infinity, and using it for approximation
A Taylor polynomial of higher and higher degree approximates a function better and better near the centre point. Taking the degree to infinity — summing the entire pattern as a genuine infinite series — often reproduces the function exactly, giving a completely different (but equivalent) way to define and compute with functions like $e^x$, $\sin x$, and $\cos x$.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Taylor series. Letting $n\to\infty$ in the Taylor polynomial formula gives the (infinite) Taylor series of $f$ centred at $a$:

\[ f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k \]

Maclaurin series — the special case $a=0$. When centred at the origin, the Taylor series is specifically called a Maclaurin series — same formula, simpler because every $(x-a)$ becomes just $x$.

The essential expansions (all derivable exactly as in the previous topic, just continued to infinitely many terms):

\[ e^x = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{x^n}{n!}, \qquad \sin x = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^nx^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}, \qquad \cos x = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^nx^{2n}}{(2n)!} \]
\[ \ln(1+x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n+1}x^n}{n} \ (-1

Error/approximation. Truncating a Taylor series at degree $n$ (i.e. using $P_n(x)$ instead of the full infinite sum) leaves a remainder — for alternating series (like $\sin x$'s expansion), the error is bounded by the size of the first omitted term, giving a practical way to control approximation accuracy without summing infinitely many terms.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k \quad \text{(Taylor, general }a\text{)}, \qquad a=0 \to \text{Maclaurin} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — use a known series to approximate

Estimate $\sin(0.1)$ using the first two nonzero terms of its Maclaurin series.

\[ \sin x \approx x-\frac{x^3}{6} \implies \sin(0.1) \approx 0.1-\frac{(0.1)^3}{6} = 0.1-0.0001\overline{6} \approx 0.099833 \]
ANSWER $\approx0.099833$ (the true value is $\approx0.0998334$ — extremely close)
Example 2 — build a new series from a known one

Find the Maclaurin series for $e^{-x^2}$ using the known series for $e^x$.

Substitute $-x^2$ for $x$ in $e^x=\sum_{n=0}^\infty \dfrac{x^n}{n!}$ — this substitution trick avoids computing any new derivatives at all.

\[ e^{-x^2} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-x^2)^n}{n!} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^nx^{2n}}{n!} \]
ANSWER $\sum_{n=0}^\infty \dfrac{(-1)^nx^{2n}}{n!}$
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
Substituting into a known series (as in Example 2) is almost always faster and less error-prone than computing derivatives from scratch — memorise the handful of essential expansions well, and build new ones from them via substitution, multiplication, or term-by-term differentiation/integration wherever possible.
