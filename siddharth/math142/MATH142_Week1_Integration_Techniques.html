W1
Integration by Parts
The product rule, run in reverse
Substitution only works when the integrand already contains the derivative of something. $\int x\cos x\,dx$ has no such structure — but the product rule for derivatives, reversed, gives a completely different way in.
Pattern Deriving the formula, and choosing $u$ and $dv$ ▶

Deriving the formula. Start from the product rule: $(uv)'=u'v+uv'$. Integrate both sides with respect to $x$:

$$uv = \int u'v\,dx + \int uv'\,dx$$

Rearranging, and writing $du=u'\,dx$, $dv=v'\,dx$:

$$\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du$$

Choosing $u$ and $dv$. The goal is for $\int v\,du$ to be easier than the original integral. The mnemonic LIATE — Logarithmic, Inverse trig, Algebraic, Trigonometric, Exponential — ranks what to pick as $u$ (differentiate), roughly in order of preference, since each category tends to simplify (or at least not worsen) under differentiation.

Repeated application — the tabular method. When $u$ needs several rounds of differentiation before vanishing (e.g. $u=x^2$ or $x^3$), doing the formula three times by hand is repetitive. The tabular method organises this into two columns — derivatives of $u$ down one side, integrals of $dv$ down the other — with alternating signs, collapsing several rounds into one table.

The "solve for the integral" trick. For integrands like $e^x\sin x$, applying integration by parts twice returns the original integral on the right-hand side, multiplied by a constant. Rather than looping forever, treat the original integral as an unknown $I$, and solve the resulting equation algebraically.

Definition The formal statement ▶
$$\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du$$
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — basic application

Find $\int x\cos x\,dx$.

Let $u=x$, $dv=\cos x\,dx$. Then $du=dx$, $v=\sin x$.

$$\int x\cos x\,dx = x\sin x - \int \sin x\,dx = x\sin x+\cos x+C$$
ANSWER $x\sin x+\cos x+C$
Example 2 — repeated application via the tabular method

Find $\int x^2e^x\,dx$.

Differentiate $x^2\to2x\to2\to0$; integrate $e^x\to e^x\to e^x\to e^x$; alternate signs $+,-,+$.

$$\int x^2e^x\,dx = x^2e^x - 2xe^x + 2e^x + C$$
ANSWER $e^x(x^2-2x+2)+C$
Example 3 — the "solve for $I$" trick

Find $\int e^x\sin x\,dx$.

Let $u=\sin x,dv=e^x dx$: $\int e^x\sin x\,dx = e^x\sin x - \int e^x\cos x\,dx$. Apply by parts again to the remaining integral ($u=\cos x,dv=e^xdx$): $\int e^x\cos x\,dx=e^x\cos x+\int e^x\sin x\,dx$.

Substituting back, calling the original integral $I$: $I = e^x\sin x - e^x\cos x - I \implies 2I = e^x(\sin x-\cos x)$.

ANSWER $\tfrac12e^x(\sin x-\cos x)+C$
Note Study tips ▶
If, after applying the formula once, $\int v\,du$ looks exactly as hard as the original integral, you likely picked $u$ and $dv$ the wrong way around — swap them and try again.
For definite integrals, apply the formula to the antiderivative first, then evaluate at both bounds — don't try to substitute bounds mid-derivation, since $uv$ itself needs evaluating at both limits too: $\int_a^b u\,dv = [uv]_a^b-\int_a^b v\,du$.

Trigonometric Integrals
Products and powers of sine and cosine
Integrals like $\int\sin^3x\cos^2x\,dx$ look intimidating, but a small set of Pythagorean-identity tricks converts almost any power or product of sine and cosine into a form ordinary substitution can finish.
Pattern Three cases: odd power, both even, and products of angles ▶

Odd power present. If either $\sin x$ or $\cos x$ appears to an odd power, peel off one factor to pair with $dx$, then convert the remaining even power using $\sin^2x+\cos^2x=1$ — this sets up a clean substitution.

Both powers even. Use the half-angle identities $\sin^2x=\dfrac{1-\cos2x}{2}$, $\cos^2x=\dfrac{1+\cos2x}{2}$ to reduce the powers, at the cost of a doubled angle — repeat as needed.

Products of sines/cosines of different angles. Product-to-sum identities (e.g. $\sin A\cos B=\tfrac12[\sin(A+B)+\sin(A-B)]$) convert a product into a sum, which integrates term by term.

Definition The key identities ▶
$$\sin^2x+\cos^2x=1, \qquad \sin^2x=\frac{1-\cos2x}{2}, \qquad \cos^2x=\frac{1+\cos2x}{2}$$
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — odd power

Find $\int \sin^3x\,dx$.

$$\int \sin^3x\,dx = \int \sin x(1-\cos^2x)\,dx$$

Let $u=\cos x$, $du=-\sin x\,dx$:

$$= -\int(1-u^2)\,du = -u+\frac{u^3}{3}+C = -\cos x+\frac{\cos^3x}{3}+C$$
ANSWER $-\cos x+\dfrac{\cos^3x}{3}+C$
Example 2 — both powers even

Find $\int \sin^4x\,dx$ (apply the half-angle identity twice).

$$\sin^4x=(\sin^2x)^2=\left(\frac{1-\cos2x}{2}\right)^2=\frac{1-2\cos2x+\cos^22x}{4}$$

Apply half-angle again to $\cos^22x=\dfrac{1+\cos4x}{2}$, then integrate term by term:

ANSWER $\dfrac{3x}{8}-\dfrac{\sin2x}{4}+\dfrac{\sin4x}{32}+C$
Note Study tip ▶
Always check for an odd power first — that route is almost always shorter than the half-angle route, since it avoids introducing a doubled angle at all.

Trigonometric Substitution
Using trig identities to simplify square roots
Expressions like $\sqrt{a^2-x^2}$ resist ordinary substitution because there's no clean "derivative of the inside" sitting in the integral. Trig substitution replaces $x$ itself with a trig function, chosen so the square root collapses via a Pythagorean identity.
Pattern The three standard forms ▶
Expression containsSubstituteIdentity used
$\sqrt{a^2-x^2}$$x=a\sin\theta$$1-\sin^2\theta=\cos^2\theta$
$\sqrt{a^2+x^2}$$x=a\tan\theta$$1+\tan^2\theta=\sec^2\theta$
$\sqrt{x^2-a^2}$$x=a\sec\theta$$\sec^2\theta-1=\tan^2\theta$

After integrating. The result is in terms of $\theta$ — convert back to $x$ using the original substitution, often via a right-triangle diagram labelled from $x=a\sin\theta$ (etc.), reading off the other trig ratios in terms of $x$.

Definition The three substitutions ▶
$$\sqrt{a^2-x^2}: x=a\sin\theta \qquad \sqrt{a^2+x^2}: x=a\tan\theta \qquad \sqrt{x^2-a^2}: x=a\sec\theta$$
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — the $\sqrt{a^2-x^2}$ case

Find $\int \sqrt{4-x^2}\,dx$.

Let $x=2\sin\theta$, $dx=2\cos\theta\,d\theta$; $\sqrt{4-x^2}=2\cos\theta$.

$$\int 4\cos^2\theta\,d\theta = 2\theta+\sin2\theta+C$$

Convert back: $\theta=\arcsin(x/2)$, and using the right triangle, $\sin2\theta=2\sin\theta\cos\theta=2\cdot\dfrac{x}{2}\cdot\dfrac{\sqrt{4-x^2}}{2}$.

ANSWER $2\arcsin\!\left(\dfrac{x}{2}\right)+\dfrac{x\sqrt{4-x^2}}{2}+C$
Example 2 — the $\sqrt{a^2+x^2}$ case

Find $\int \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{9+x^2}}\,dx$.

Let $x=3\tan\theta$, $dx=3\sec^2\theta\,d\theta$; $\sqrt{9+x^2}=3\sec\theta$.

$$\int \frac{3\sec^2\theta}{3\sec\theta}\,d\theta = \int \sec\theta\,d\theta = \ln|\sec\theta+\tan\theta|+C$$

From $x=3\tan\theta$: $\tan\theta=x/3$, $\sec\theta=\sqrt{9+x^2}/3$.

ANSWER $\ln\left|\dfrac{\sqrt{9+x^2}+x}{3}\right|+C$
Note Study tip ▶
Memorising which identity matches which square root pattern is faster than re-deriving the choice each time — each substitution is chosen specifically so a Pythagorean identity collapses the square root into one clean trig function.
W1-2
Partial Fractions — Linear Factors
Splitting rational functions with distinct linear denominators
A rational function like $\dfrac{1}{x^2-1}$ can't be integrated directly, but it can be rewritten as a sum of simpler fractions, each with a single linear factor in the denominator — and each of those integrates immediately to a logarithm.
Pattern Setting up and solving the decomposition ▶

The setup. For $\dfrac{P(x)}{(x-a)(x-b)}$, propose:

$$\frac{P(x)}{(x-a)(x-b)} = \frac{A}{x-a} + \frac{B}{x-b}$$

Finding $A$ and $B$ (cover-up method). Multiply both sides by the denominator, then substitute $x=a$ (making the $B$-term vanish, isolating $A$) and $x=b$ (isolating $B$).

Improper fractions first. If the numerator's degree is $\ge$ the denominator's, do polynomial long division first, decomposing only the proper remainder.

Why it's always possible. Any proper rational function can be written this way, uniquely, once the denominator is fully factored — you're solving for specific coefficients, not guessing that a decomposition exists.

Definition The formal statement ▶
$$\frac{P(x)}{(x-a)(x-b)} = \frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}, \qquad \int \frac{A}{x-a}\,dx = A\ln|x-a|+C$$
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — cover-up method

Find $\int \dfrac{1}{x^2-1}\,dx$.

$$\frac{1}{(x-1)(x+1)} = \frac{A}{x-1}+\frac{B}{x+1}$$

Multiply by $(x-1)(x+1)$: $1=A(x+1)+B(x-1)$. At $x=1$: $A=\tfrac12$. At $x=-1$: $B=-\tfrac12$.

ANSWER $\dfrac12\ln\left|\dfrac{x-1}{x+1}\right|+C$
Example 2 — three distinct linear factors

Decompose $\dfrac{2x+1}{x(x-1)(x+2)}$.

$2x+1=A(x-1)(x+2)+Bx(x+2)+Cx(x-1)$. At $x=0$: $A=-\tfrac12$. At $x=1$: $B=1$. At $x=-2$: $C=-\tfrac12$.

ANSWER $-\dfrac{1/2}{x}+\dfrac{1}{x-1}-\dfrac{1/2}{x+2}$
Note Study tip ▶
The "cover-up" trick only works cleanly for distinct linear factors — repeated or irreducible-quadratic factors (next week) need the full coefficient-matching approach instead.

Integration by Parts
Multi-step problems, including reduction formulas and mixed techniques
1
Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/2} x^2\cos x\,dx$ using the tabular method, then confirm the antiderivative is correct by differentiating it.
Integration by Parts
▶
Full Worked Solution
1
Tabular method: differentiate $x^2\to2x\to2\to0$; integrate $\cos x\to\sin x\to-\cos x\to-\sin x$; alternate signs $+,-,+$: $$\int x^2\cos x\,dx = x^2\sin x+2x\cos x-2\sin x+C$$
2
Verify by differentiating: $\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^2\sin x+2x\cos x-2\sin x] = 2x\sin x+x^2\cos x+2\cos x-2x\sin x-2\cos x = x^2\cos x$. ✓ matches the integrand.
3
Evaluate at the bounds: at $x=\pi/2$: $(\pi/2)^2(1)+2(\pi/2)(0)-2(1)=\dfrac{\pi^2}{4}-2$. At $x=0$: $0+0-0=0$.
ANSWER $\dfrac{\pi^2}{4}-2$
2
Evaluate $\displaystyle\int e^{2x}\cos(3x)\,dx$ using the "solve for $I$" trick (apply integration by parts twice).
Integration by Parts
▶
Full Worked Solution
1
Let $I=\int e^{2x}\cos3x\,dx$. Take $u=\cos3x,dv=e^{2x}dx$: $du=-3\sin3x\,dx$, $v=\tfrac12e^{2x}$. $$I = \tfrac12e^{2x}\cos3x+\tfrac32\int e^{2x}\sin3x\,dx$$
2
Apply by parts again to $\int e^{2x}\sin3x\,dx$: $u=\sin3x,dv=e^{2x}dx$: $du=3\cos3x\,dx$, $v=\tfrac12e^{2x}$. $$\int e^{2x}\sin3x\,dx = \tfrac12e^{2x}\sin3x-\tfrac32\int e^{2x}\cos3x\,dx = \tfrac12e^{2x}\sin3x-\tfrac32I$$
3
Substitute back: $I=\tfrac12e^{2x}\cos3x+\tfrac32\left(\tfrac12e^{2x}\sin3x-\tfrac32I\right)=\tfrac12e^{2x}\cos3x+\tfrac34e^{2x}\sin3x-\tfrac94I$.
4
Solve for $I$: $I+\tfrac94I=\tfrac12e^{2x}\cos3x+\tfrac34e^{2x}\sin3x \implies \tfrac{13}{4}I = \tfrac14e^{2x}(2\cos3x+3\sin3x)$.
ANSWER $\dfrac{e^{2x}(2\cos3x+3\sin3x)}{13}+C$
3
Derive a reduction formula for $I_n=\displaystyle\int x^ne^x\,dx$ in terms of $I_{n-1}$, then use it to evaluate $\displaystyle\int x^3e^x\,dx$.
Integration by Parts
▶
Full Worked Solution
1
Let $u=x^n,dv=e^xdx$: $du=nx^{n-1}dx,v=e^x$. $$I_n = x^ne^x - n\int x^{n-1}e^x\,dx = x^ne^x-nI_{n-1}$$
2
Base case: $I_0=\int e^x\,dx=e^x+C$.
3
$I_1=xe^x-1\cdot I_0=xe^x-e^x$.
4
$I_2=x^2e^x-2I_1=x^2e^x-2xe^x+2e^x$.
5
$I_3=x^3e^x-3I_2=x^3e^x-3(x^2e^x-2xe^x+2e^x)=x^3e^x-3x^2e^x+6xe^x-6e^x$.
ANSWER $I_n=x^ne^x-nI_{n-1}$; $\int x^3e^x\,dx = e^x(x^3-3x^2+6x-6)+C$
4
Evaluate $\int \arcsin x\,dx$ (this needs integration by parts followed by an ordinary substitution to finish the leftover integral).
Integration by Parts
▶
Full Worked Solution
1
Let $u=\arcsin x,dv=dx$: $du=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}dx$, $v=x$. $$\int\arcsin x\,dx = x\arcsin x-\int\frac{x}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\,dx$$
2
For the remaining integral, let $w=1-x^2$, $dw=-2x\,dx$: $\int\dfrac{x}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}dx=-\tfrac12\int w^{-1/2}dw=-\sqrt{w}=-\sqrt{1-x^2}$.
3
Combine: $x\arcsin x-\left(-\sqrt{1-x^2}\right)=x\arcsin x+\sqrt{1-x^2}$.
ANSWER $x\arcsin x+\sqrt{1-x^2}+C$
5
Evaluate $\int \ln(x^2+1)\,dx$ (needs integration by parts, then a partial-technique finish for the remaining rational integral).
Integration by Parts
▶
Full Worked Solution
1
Let $u=\ln(x^2+1),dv=dx$: $du=\dfrac{2x}{x^2+1}dx$, $v=x$. $$\int\ln(x^2+1)\,dx = x\ln(x^2+1)-\int\frac{2x^2}{x^2+1}\,dx$$
2
Rewrite the remaining integrand as an improper fraction: $\dfrac{2x^2}{x^2+1}=2-\dfrac{2}{x^2+1}$ (polynomial division), so $$\int\frac{2x^2}{x^2+1}dx=\int\left(2-\frac{2}{x^2+1}\right)dx=2x-2\arctan x$$
3
Combine: $x\ln(x^2+1)-(2x-2\arctan x)=x\ln(x^2+1)-2x+2\arctan x$.
ANSWER $x\ln(x^2+1)-2x+2\arctan x+C$

11
Evaluate $\displaystyle\int \frac{dx}{x^2\sqrt{x^2-4}}$ using the $\sqrt{x^2-a^2}$ substitution, converting fully back to $x$.
Trigonometric Substitution
▶
Full Worked Solution
1
Let $x=2\sec\theta,dx=2\sec\theta\tan\theta\,d\theta$; $\sqrt{x^2-4}=2\tan\theta$. $$\int\frac{2\sec\theta\tan\theta}{4\sec^2\theta\cdot2\tan\theta}\,d\theta = \frac14\int\frac{1}{\sec\theta}\,d\theta = \frac14\int\cos\theta\,d\theta$$

$\frac14\int\cos\theta\,d\theta = \frac14\sin\theta+C$.

From $x=2\sec\theta$: hypotenuse $x$, adjacent $2$, opposite $\sqrt{x^2-4}$, so $\sin\theta=\dfrac{\sqrt{x^2-4}}{x}$.

12
Set up and fully evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^{2} \sqrt{4+x^2}\,dx$ (a definite integral requiring the $\sqrt{a^2+x^2}$ substitution, converting the bounds too).
Trigonometric Substitution
▶
Full Worked Solution
1
Let $x=2\tan\theta,dx=2\sec^2\theta\,d\theta$; $\sqrt{4+x^2}=2\sec\theta$. When $x=0,\theta=0$; when $x=2,\tan\theta=1\implies\theta=\pi/4$. $$\int_0^{\pi/4} 2\sec\theta\cdot2\sec^2\theta\,d\theta = 4\int_0^{\pi/4}\sec^3\theta\,d\theta$$
2
Using the standard reduction result $\int\sec^3\theta\,d\theta=\tfrac12[\sec\theta\tan\theta+\ln|\sec\theta+\tan\theta|]+C$: $$4\int_0^{\pi/4}\sec^3\theta\,d\theta = 2\big[\sec\theta\tan\theta+\ln|\sec\theta+\tan\theta|\big]_0^{\pi/4}$$
3
At $\theta=\pi/4$: $\sec=\sqrt2,\tan=1$: $\sqrt2+\ln(\sqrt2+1)$. At $\theta=0$: $0+\ln1=0$. $$2\left[\sqrt2+\ln(\sqrt2+1)\right] = 2\sqrt2+2\ln(\sqrt2+1)$$
ANSWER $2\sqrt2+2\ln(\sqrt2+1)$

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int \frac{x^3}{x^2-1}\,dx$ (improper fraction — requires polynomial division before decomposing).
Partial Fractions (Linear Factors)
▶
Full Worked Solution
1
Numerator degree (3) $\ge$ denominator degree (2): divide first. $\dfrac{x^3}{x^2-1}=x+\dfrac{x}{x^2-1}$ (since $x(x^2-1)=x^3-x$, leaving remainder $x$).

Decompose the proper remainder: $\dfrac{x}{(x-1)(x+1)}=\dfrac{A}{x-1}+\dfrac{B}{x+1}$. At $x=1$: $1=2A\implies A=\tfrac12$. At $x=-1$: $-1=-2B\implies B=\tfrac12$.
3
Integrate all three pieces: $\int\left(x+\dfrac{1/2}{x-1}+\dfrac{1/2}{x+1}\right)dx = \dfrac{x^2}{2}+\tfrac12\ln|x-1|+\tfrac12\ln|x+1|+C$.
ANSWER $\dfrac{x^2}{2}+\dfrac12\ln|x^2-1|+C$
14
Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_2^3 \frac{5x-1}{x(x-1)(x+1)}\,dx$ (definite integral, three distinct linear factors, cover-up method).
Partial Fractions (Linear Factors)
▶
Full Worked Solution
1
$5x-1=A(x-1)(x+1)+Bx(x+1)+Cx(x-1)$. At $x=0$: $-1=-A\implies A=1$. At $x=1$: $4=2B\implies B=2$. At $x=-1$: $-6=2C\implies C=-3$.
2
Antiderivative: $\ln|x|+2\ln|x-1|-3\ln|x+1|$.
3
At $x=3$: $\ln3+2\ln2-3\ln4=\ln3+2\ln2-6\ln2=\ln3-4\ln2$. At $x=2$: $\ln2+2\ln1-3\ln3=\ln2-3\ln3$.
4
Subtract: $(\ln3-4\ln2)-(\ln2-3\ln3)=4\ln3-5\ln2$.
ANSWER $4\ln3-5\ln2 \approx 0.9161$
15
A tank-mixing scenario produces the integral $\displaystyle\int \frac{100}{(x+10)(x+20)}\,dx$. Decompose and evaluate it, then find the particular antiderivative satisfying $F(0)=0$.
Partial Fractions (Linear Factors)
▶
Full Worked Solution
1
$\dfrac{100}{(x+10)(x+20)}=\dfrac{A}{x+10}+\dfrac{B}{x+20}$. At $x=-10$: $100=10A\implies A=10$. At $x=-20$: $100=-10B\implies B=-10$.
2
$\int\left(\dfrac{10}{x+10}-\dfrac{10}{x+20}\right)dx = 10\ln|x+10|-10\ln|x+20|+C = 10\ln\left|\dfrac{x+10}{x+20}\right|+C$.
3
Apply $F(0)=0$: $10\ln\left(\dfrac{10}{20}\right)+C=0 \implies 10\ln(0.5)+C=0 \implies C=-10\ln(0.5)=10\ln2$.
ANSWER $F(x)=10\ln\left|\dfrac{x+10}{x+20}\right|+10\ln2$

16
Evaluate $\int x\ln x\,dx$ and then use it to compute $\int_1^e x\ln x\,dx$.
Integration by Parts
▶
Full Worked Solution

$u=\ln x,\;dv=xdx\Rightarrow du=\frac1x dx,\;v=\frac{x^2}{2}$.

ANSWER $\int x\ln x\,dx=\frac{x^2}{2}\ln x-\frac{x^2}{4}+C,\;\int_1^e x\ln x\,dx=\frac{e^2+1}{4}$

▶
