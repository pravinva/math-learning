Integration by Parts, Trig Integrals & Substitution, Partial Fractions

Four techniques that extend substitution to integrals it can't handle alone: reversing the product rule, taming powers/products of sine and cosine, replacing square roots with trig functions, and splitting rational functions into pieces that integrate directly. This is exam-level material — the worked examples and practice below go well beyond quick drills. Solutions stay hidden until you click.

Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Integration by parts: $\int u\,dv = uv-\int v\,du$. Choose $u$ via LIATE (Log, Inverse trig, Algebraic, Trig, Exponential); use the tabular method for repeated differentiation, and the "solve for $I$" trick when the integral reappears on the right (e.g. $e^x\sin x$).
  • Trig integrals: odd power present → peel one off, convert the rest via $\sin^2x+\cos^2x=1$, substitute. Both powers even → half-angle: $\sin^2x=\dfrac{1-\cos2x}{2}$, $\cos^2x=\dfrac{1+\cos2x}{2}$. Mixed angles → product-to-sum identities.
  • Trig substitution: $\sqrt{a^2-x^2}\to x=a\sin\theta$; $\sqrt{a^2+x^2}\to x=a\tan\theta$; $\sqrt{x^2-a^2}\to x=a\sec\theta$. Convert back to $x$ via a right-triangle diagram.
  • Partial fractions (linear factors): $\dfrac{P(x)}{(x-a)(x-b)}=\dfrac{A}{x-a}+\dfrac{B}{x-b}$, solved via the cover-up method (substitute $x=a$, then $x=b$). Do polynomial long division first if the fraction is improper (numerator degree $\ge$ denominator degree).

0 Integration by Parts

Substitution only works when the integrand already contains the derivative of something. $\int x\cos x\,dx$ has no such structure — but the product rule for derivatives, reversed, gives a completely different way in.

Deriving the formula. Start from the product rule $(uv)'=u'v+uv'$ and integrate both sides: $uv=\int u'v\,dx+\int uv'\,dx$. Rearranging, with $du=u'dx$, $dv=v'dx$, gives the formula below.

Choosing $u$ and $dv$. The goal is for $\int v\,du$ to be easier than the original integral. LIATE — Logarithmic, Inverse trig, Algebraic, Trigonometric, Exponential — ranks what to pick as $u$ (differentiate), roughly in that order of preference.

Repeated application — the tabular method. When $u$ needs several rounds of differentiation before vanishing (e.g. $u=x^2$ or $x^3$), the tabular method organises this into two columns — derivatives of $u$ down one side, integrals of $dv$ down the other — with alternating signs, collapsing several rounds into one table.

The "solve for $I$" trick. For integrands like $e^x\sin x$, applying the formula twice returns the original integral, multiplied by a constant. Treat the original integral as an unknown $I$ and solve the resulting equation algebraically.

Key idea

$$\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du$$

Worked example

Example 1 — tabular method. Find $\int x^2e^x\,dx$.

Differentiate $x^2\to2x\to2\to0$; integrate $e^x\to e^x\to e^x\to e^x$; alternate signs $+,-,+$: $x^2e^x-2xe^x+2e^x+C$.

Example 2 — the "solve for $I$" trick. Find $\int e^x\sin x\,dx$.

Apply by parts twice: $I=e^x\sin x-e^x\cos x-I \implies 2I=e^x(\sin x-\cos x) \implies I=\tfrac12e^x(\sin x-\cos x)+C$.

Practice 1

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/2} x^2\cos x\,dx$ using the tabular method, then confirm the antiderivative is correct by differentiating it.

Solution

Step 1 — tabular method: differentiate $x^2\to2x\to2\to0$; integrate $\cos x\to\sin x\to-\cos x\to-\sin x$; alternate signs: $$\int x^2\cos x\,dx = x^2\sin x+2x\cos x-2\sin x+C$$

Step 2 — verify: $\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^2\sin x+2x\cos x-2\sin x] = 2x\sin x+x^2\cos x+2\cos x-2x\sin x-2\cos x = x^2\cos x$. ✓ matches.

Step 3 — evaluate bounds: at $x=\pi/2$: $\dfrac{\pi^2}{4}(1)+0-2(1)=\dfrac{\pi^2}{4}-2$. At $x=0$: $0$.

Answer: $\dfrac{\pi^2}{4}-2$.

Practice 2

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int e^{2x}\cos(3x)\,dx$ using the "solve for $I$" trick (apply integration by parts twice).

Solution

Let $I=\int e^{2x}\cos3x\,dx$. Take $u=\cos3x,dv=e^{2x}dx$: $I=\tfrac12e^{2x}\cos3x+\tfrac32\int e^{2x}\sin3x\,dx$.

Apply by parts again to $\int e^{2x}\sin3x\,dx$ ($u=\sin3x$): $=\tfrac12e^{2x}\sin3x-\tfrac32I$.

Substituting back: $I=\tfrac12e^{2x}\cos3x+\tfrac34e^{2x}\sin3x-\tfrac94I$.

Solve: $\tfrac{13}{4}I=\tfrac14e^{2x}(2\cos3x+3\sin3x)$.

Answer: $\dfrac{e^{2x}(2\cos3x+3\sin3x)}{13}+C$.

Practice 3

Derive a reduction formula for $I_n=\displaystyle\int x^ne^x\,dx$ in terms of $I_{n-1}$, then use it to evaluate $\displaystyle\int x^3e^x\,dx$.

Solution

Let $u=x^n,dv=e^xdx$: $I_n = x^ne^x - n\int x^{n-1}e^x\,dx = x^ne^x-nI_{n-1}$.

Base case: $I_0=e^x+C$. Then $I_1=xe^x-e^x$; $I_2=x^2e^x-2xe^x+2e^x$; $I_3=x^3e^x-3I_2=x^3e^x-3x^2e^x+6xe^x-6e^x$.

Answer: $I_n=x^ne^x-nI_{n-1}$; $\int x^3e^x\,dx = e^x(x^3-3x^2+6x-6)+C$.

Practice 4

Evaluate $\int \arcsin x\,dx$ (needs integration by parts followed by an ordinary substitution to finish the leftover integral).

Solution

Let $u=\arcsin x,dv=dx$: $du=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}dx$, $v=x$. $$\int\arcsin x\,dx = x\arcsin x-\int\frac{x}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\,dx$$

For the remaining integral, let $w=1-x^2$: $\int\dfrac{x}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}dx=-\sqrt{1-x^2}$.

Answer: $x\arcsin x+\sqrt{1-x^2}+C$.

Practice 5

Evaluate $\int \ln(x^2+1)\,dx$ (integration by parts, then a division trick finishes the remaining rational integral).

Solution

Let $u=\ln(x^2+1),dv=dx$: $du=\dfrac{2x}{x^2+1}dx,v=x$. $$\int\ln(x^2+1)\,dx = x\ln(x^2+1)-\int\frac{2x^2}{x^2+1}\,dx$$

Rewrite $\dfrac{2x^2}{x^2+1}=2-\dfrac{2}{x^2+1}$: $\int\dfrac{2x^2}{x^2+1}dx=2x-2\arctan x$.

Answer: $x\ln(x^2+1)-2x+2\arctan x+C$.

1 Trigonometric Integrals

Integrals like $\int\sin^3x\cos^2x\,dx$ look intimidating, but a small set of Pythagorean-identity tricks converts almost any power or product of sine and cosine into a form ordinary substitution can finish.

Odd power present. Peel off one factor to pair with $dx$, then convert the remaining even power using $\sin^2x+\cos^2x=1$ — this sets up a clean substitution.

Both powers even. Use the half-angle identities to reduce the powers, at the cost of a doubled angle — repeat as needed.

Products of sines/cosines of different angles. Product-to-sum identities (e.g. $\sin A\cos B=\tfrac12[\sin(A+B)+\sin(A-B)]$) convert a product into a sum, integrated term by term.

Key idea

$$\sin^2x+\cos^2x=1, \qquad \sin^2x=\frac{1-\cos2x}{2}, \qquad \cos^2x=\frac{1+\cos2x}{2}$$

Worked example

Example 1 — odd power. Find $\int\sin^3x\,dx$.

$\int\sin x(1-\cos^2x)\,dx$, $u=\cos x$: $-\cos x+\dfrac{\cos^3x}{3}+C$.

Example 2 — both powers even. Find $\int\sin^4x\,dx$ (half-angle applied twice).

$\sin^4x=\left(\dfrac{1-\cos2x}{2}\right)^2$, then half-angle again on $\cos^22x$: $\dfrac{3x}{8}-\dfrac{\sin2x}{4}+\dfrac{\sin4x}{32}+C$.

Practice 6

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int \sin^4x\cos^3x\,dx$ (odd power on cosine — peel one off and substitute).

Solution

Peel off one $\cos x$: $\int \sin^4x(1-\sin^2x)\cos x\,dx$. Let $u=\sin x$: $\int u^4(1-u^2)\,du=\dfrac{u^5}{5}-\dfrac{u^7}{7}+C$.

Answer: $\dfrac{\sin^5x}{5}-\dfrac{\sin^7x}{7}+C$.

Practice 7

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi} \cos^4x\,dx$ (both powers even, definite integral).

Solution

$\cos^4x=\left(\dfrac{1+\cos2x}{2}\right)^2=\dfrac38+\dfrac{\cos2x}{2}+\dfrac{\cos4x}{8}$ (after a second half-angle pass).

Antiderivative: $\dfrac{3x}{8}+\dfrac{\sin2x}{4}+\dfrac{\sin4x}{32}$. At $x=\pi$: sine terms vanish, giving $\dfrac{3\pi}{8}$. At $x=0$: $0$.

Answer: $\dfrac{3\pi}{8}$.

Practice 8

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int \sin(5x)\cos(3x)\,dx$ using a product-to-sum identity.

Solution

$\sin A\cos B=\tfrac12[\sin(A+B)+\sin(A-B)]$: $\sin5x\cos3x=\tfrac12[\sin8x+\sin2x]$.

Integrate: $\tfrac12\left(-\dfrac{\cos8x}{8}-\dfrac{\cos2x}{2}\right)$.

Answer: $-\dfrac{\cos8x}{16}-\dfrac{\cos2x}{4}+C$.

Practice 9

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int \tan^3x\sec^4x\,dx$ (tangent/secant version of the same odd-power strategy, using $\sec^2x=1+\tan^2x$).

Solution

Peel off $\sec^2x$ to pair with $dx$: $\int\tan^3x(1+\tan^2x)\sec^2x\,dx$.

Let $u=\tan x$: $\int u^3(1+u^2)\,du=\dfrac{u^4}{4}+\dfrac{u^6}{6}+C$.

Answer: $\dfrac{\tan^4x}{4}+\dfrac{\tan^6x}{6}+C$.

2 Trigonometric Substitution

Expressions like $\sqrt{a^2-x^2}$ resist ordinary substitution because there's no clean "derivative of the inside" sitting in the integral. Trig substitution replaces $x$ itself with a trig function, chosen so the square root collapses via a Pythagorean identity. After integrating, convert back to $x$ using a right-triangle diagram labelled from the original substitution.

Key idea
Expression containsSubstituteIdentity used
$\sqrt{a^2-x^2}$$x=a\sin\theta$$1-\sin^2\theta=\cos^2\theta$
$\sqrt{a^2+x^2}$$x=a\tan\theta$$1+\tan^2\theta=\sec^2\theta$
$\sqrt{x^2-a^2}$$x=a\sec\theta$$\sec^2\theta-1=\tan^2\theta$
Worked example

Example 1 — $\sqrt{a^2-x^2}$. Find $\int\sqrt{4-x^2}\,dx$.

$x=2\sin\theta$: $\int4\cos^2\theta\,d\theta=2\theta+\sin2\theta+C$; converting back: $2\arcsin(x/2)+\dfrac{x\sqrt{4-x^2}}{2}+C$.

Example 2 — $\sqrt{a^2+x^2}$. Find $\int\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{9+x^2}}\,dx$.

$x=3\tan\theta$: $\int\sec\theta\,d\theta=\ln|\sec\theta+\tan\theta|+C$; converting back: $\ln\left|\dfrac{\sqrt{9+x^2}+x}{3}\right|+C$.

Practice 10

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int \frac{x^2}{\sqrt{9-x^2}}\,dx$, converting the final answer fully back to $x$.

Solution

Let $x=3\sin\theta$: $\sqrt{9-x^2}=3\cos\theta$. $$9\int\sin^2\theta\,d\theta = \frac{9\theta}{2}-\frac{9\sin\theta\cos\theta}{2}$$

Convert back: $\theta=\arcsin(x/3)$; $\sin\theta\cos\theta=\dfrac{x\sqrt{9-x^2}}{9}$.

Answer: $\dfrac92\arcsin\!\left(\dfrac{x}{3}\right)-\dfrac{x\sqrt{9-x^2}}{2}+C$.

Practice 11

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int \frac{dx}{x^2\sqrt{x^2-4}}$ using the $\sqrt{x^2-a^2}$ substitution, converting fully back to $x$.

Solution

Let $x=2\sec\theta$: $\sqrt{x^2-4}=2\tan\theta$. $$\frac14\int\cos\theta\,d\theta = \frac14\sin\theta+C$$

From the triangle (hyp. $x$, adj. $2$, opp. $\sqrt{x^2-4}$): $\sin\theta=\dfrac{\sqrt{x^2-4}}{x}$.

Answer: $\dfrac{\sqrt{x^2-4}}{4x}+C$.

Practice 12

Set up and fully evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^{2} \sqrt{4+x^2}\,dx$ (definite integral requiring the $\sqrt{a^2+x^2}$ substitution, converting the bounds too).

Solution

Let $x=2\tan\theta$: $\sqrt{4+x^2}=2\sec\theta$. Bounds: $x=0\to\theta=0$; $x=2\to\theta=\pi/4$. $$4\int_0^{\pi/4}\sec^3\theta\,d\theta = 2\big[\sec\theta\tan\theta+\ln|\sec\theta+\tan\theta|\big]_0^{\pi/4}$$

At $\theta=\pi/4$: $\sqrt2+\ln(\sqrt2+1)$. At $\theta=0$: $0$.

Answer: $2\sqrt2+2\ln(\sqrt2+1)$.

3 Partial Fractions — Linear Factors

A rational function like $\dfrac{1}{x^2-1}$ can't be integrated directly, but it can be rewritten as a sum of simpler fractions, each with a single linear factor in the denominator — and each of those integrates immediately to a logarithm.

The setup. For $\dfrac{P(x)}{(x-a)(x-b)}$, propose $\dfrac{A}{x-a}+\dfrac{B}{x-b}$. Multiply through by the denominator, then substitute $x=a$ (isolating $A$) and $x=b$ (isolating $B$) — the cover-up method.

Improper fractions first. If the numerator's degree is $\ge$ the denominator's, do polynomial long division first, decomposing only the proper remainder.

Key idea

$$\frac{P(x)}{(x-a)(x-b)} = \frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}, \qquad \int \frac{A}{x-a}\,dx = A\ln|x-a|+C$$

Worked example

Example 1 — cover-up method. Find $\int\dfrac{1}{x^2-1}\,dx$.

$1=A(x+1)+B(x-1)$. At $x=1$: $A=\tfrac12$. At $x=-1$: $B=-\tfrac12$. Answer: $\dfrac12\ln\left|\dfrac{x-1}{x+1}\right|+C$.

Example 2 — three linear factors. Decompose $\dfrac{2x+1}{x(x-1)(x+2)}$.

At $x=0$: $A=-\tfrac12$. At $x=1$: $B=1$. At $x=-2$: $C=-\tfrac12$.

Practice 13

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int \frac{x^3}{x^2-1}\,dx$ (improper fraction — requires polynomial division before decomposing).

Solution

Divide first: $\dfrac{x^3}{x^2-1}=x+\dfrac{x}{x^2-1}$.

Decompose the remainder: $\dfrac{x}{(x-1)(x+1)}=\dfrac{1/2}{x-1}+\dfrac{1/2}{x+1}$.

Integrate all three pieces: $\dfrac{x^2}{2}+\tfrac12\ln|x-1|+\tfrac12\ln|x+1|+C$.

Answer: $\dfrac{x^2}{2}+\dfrac12\ln|x^2-1|+C$.

Practice 14

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_2^3 \frac{5x-1}{x(x-1)(x+1)}\,dx$ (definite integral, three distinct linear factors, cover-up method).

Solution

$5x-1=A(x-1)(x+1)+Bx(x+1)+Cx(x-1)$. At $x=0$: $A=1$. At $x=1$: $B=2$. At $x=-1$: $C=-3$.

Antiderivative: $\ln|x|+2\ln|x-1|-3\ln|x+1|$.

At $x=3$: $\ln3-4\ln2$. At $x=2$: $\ln2-3\ln3$. Subtract: $4\ln3-5\ln2$.

Answer: $4\ln3-5\ln2 \approx 0.9161$.

Practice 15

A tank-mixing scenario produces the integral $\displaystyle\int \frac{100}{(x+10)(x+20)}\,dx$. Decompose and evaluate it, then find the particular antiderivative satisfying $F(0)=0$.

Solution

$\dfrac{100}{(x+10)(x+20)}=\dfrac{10}{x+10}-\dfrac{10}{x+20}$ (cover-up at $x=-10,-20$).

$\int = 10\ln|x+10|-10\ln|x+20|+C = 10\ln\left|\dfrac{x+10}{x+20}\right|+C$.

Apply $F(0)=0$: $10\ln(0.5)+C=0 \implies C=10\ln2$.

Answer: $F(x)=10\ln\left|\dfrac{x+10}{x+20}\right|+10\ln2$.
