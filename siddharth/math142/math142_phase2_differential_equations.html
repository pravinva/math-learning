Introduction to Differential Equations & Separable DEs
What a DE actually asks, and the simplest technique for solving one
Every equation you've solved so far asks for a number. A differential equation asks for something bigger — an entire function — by describing a relationship between that function and its own rate of change.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

What a DE is. An equation involving a function and one or more of its derivatives, e.g. $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=2x$. A "solution" isn't a number — it's a function $y(x)$ that makes the equation true for every $x$. The order of a DE is the highest derivative appearing (first order: just $y'$; second order: up to $y''$).

General vs particular solutions. Just as $\int 2x\,dx=x^2+C$ has a whole family of antiderivatives, a first-order DE typically has a family of solutions differing by one constant — the general solution. Supplying one extra piece of information (an initial condition, like $y(0)=3$) pins down a single particular solution from that family.

Separable DEs — the idea. Some DEs can be algebraically rearranged so every $y$ (and $dy$) sits on one side, and every $x$ (and $dx$) sits on the other:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx}=g(x)h(y) \ \implies\ \frac{1}{h(y)}\,dy = g(x)\,dx \]

Once separated, integrate both sides independently — each side is now an ordinary single-variable integral, exactly the kind you already know how to solve.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \frac{dy}{dx}=g(x)h(y) \ \implies\ \int \frac{1}{h(y)}\,dy = \int g(x)\,dx \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — solve a separable DE

Solve $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=xy$.

\[ \frac{1}{y}\,dy = x\,dx \implies \int \frac1y\,dy = \int x\,dx \implies \ln|y| = \frac{x^2}{2}+C_1 \]
\[ y = e^{x^2/2+C_1} = e^{C_1}e^{x^2/2} = Ce^{x^2/2} \]
ANSWER $y=Ce^{x^2/2}$ (general solution)
Example 2 — find a particular solution

Solve $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=xy$ with $y(0)=5$.

Using the general solution from Example 1: $y(0)=Ce^0=C=5$.

ANSWER $y=5e^{x^2/2}$
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
Separating variables always produces an implicit relationship first (often with a $\ln$) — solving explicitly for $y$ (as in Example 1) is a separate algebra step afterward, and isn't always possible or necessary depending on what the question asks for.
W7
Separable DE Applications — Growth, Decay & Mixing
Turning real scenarios into separable equations and solving them
Many physical processes — population growth, radioactive decay, cooling, mixing — share the property that their rate of change depends on their current state, which is exactly what a separable DE describes. The hard part is rarely solving the equation; it's correctly writing down the equation from the words.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Exponential growth/decay. "Rate of change proportional to current amount" translates directly to $\dfrac{dy}{dt}=ky$, which you already solved in the previous topic: $y=y_0e^{kt}$, where $y_0$ is the initial amount and $k$ the growth ($k>0$) or decay ($k<0$) constant.

Newton's Law of Cooling. "Rate of temperature change proportional to the difference from ambient temperature" translates to $\dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-T_a)$, separable via the substitution-like observation that $T-T_a$ behaves like a single variable.

Mixing problems. A tank with inflow/outflow of a solution: set up a DE for the amount of substance $A(t)$ by balancing rate in minus rate out — $\dfrac{dA}{dt}=(\text{rate in})-(\text{rate out})$, where each rate is typically (concentration) $\times$ (flow rate), and the outflow concentration usually depends on $A(t)$ itself, making the equation separable (or, if the volume changes, linear — see the next topic).

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \frac{dy}{dt}=ky \implies y=y_0e^{kt}, \qquad \frac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-T_a) \implies T=T_a+(T_0-T_a)e^{-kt} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — exponential decay

A radioactive substance decays at a rate proportional to the amount present, with half-life 10 years. If there are 80g initially, find the amount after 20 years.

$y=80e^{kt}$. Half-life: $y(10)=40=80e^{10k}\implies e^{10k}=\tfrac12\implies k=\dfrac{\ln(1/2)}{10}$.

\[ y(20)=80e^{20k}=80\left(e^{10k}\right)^2=80\left(\frac12\right)^2=20 \]
ANSWER $20$g
Example 2 — Newton's Law of Cooling

A cup of coffee at $90°$C is placed in a $20°$C room. After 5 minutes it's $70°$C. Find its temperature after 15 minutes.

$T=20+70e^{-kt}$ (since $T_0-T_a=90-20=70$). At $t=5$: $70=20+70e^{-5k}\implies e^{-5k}=\tfrac{50}{70}=\tfrac57$.

\[ T(15) = 20+70(e^{-5k})^3 = 20+70\left(\frac57\right)^3 \approx 20+25.5 = 45.5°\text{C} \]
ANSWER $\approx 45.5°$C
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
Every one of these application types reduces to the same underlying DE shape ($y'=ky$ or $y'=-k(y-\text{constant})$) — once you recognise which shape a word problem matches, the solving is identical every time; the real skill being tested is the translation from words to equation.

First-Order Linear DEs — Integrating Factor Method
Solving DEs that can't be separated, by manufacturing a product rule
Not every first-order DE separates — $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+y=x$, for instance, has $x$ and $y$ hopelessly tangled together. The integrating factor method takes a completely different approach: multiply the whole equation by a cleverly chosen function so the left side becomes the derivative of a single product, which can then be integrated directly.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Standard form. Any first-order linear DE can be written as $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+p(x)y=q(x)$ — linear meaning $y$ and $y'$ each appear to the first power only, with no $y\cdot y'$ or similar mixing.

Manufacturing the integrating factor. The goal is to find a function $\mu(x)$ such that multiplying through by $\mu(x)$ makes the left side collapse into $\dfrac{d}{dx}[\mu(x)y]$ — i.e. exactly the product rule, run in reverse. Expanding $\dfrac{d}{dx}[\mu y]=\mu y'+\mu' y$ and comparing to $\mu y'+\mu p(x)y$ (the left side after multiplying through) shows this works exactly when $\mu'=\mu\,p(x)$ — a separable DE for $\mu$ itself! Solving it:

\[ \mu(x) = e^{\int p(x)\,dx} \]

Finishing the solve. Multiply the whole original equation by this $\mu(x)$; the left side is now guaranteed to be $\dfrac{d}{dx}[\mu y]$, so integrate both sides directly and solve for $y$:

\[ \mu(x)y = \int \mu(x)q(x)\,dx + C \implies y = \frac{1}{\mu(x)}\left[\int \mu(x)q(x)\,dx + C\right] \]
Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ y'+p(x)y=q(x), \qquad \mu(x)=e^{\int p(x)\,dx}, \qquad y=\frac{1}{\mu(x)}\left[\int \mu(x)q(x)\,dx+C\right] \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — apply the integrating factor method

Solve $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+y=x$.

Here $p(x)=1$, so $\mu(x)=e^{\int 1\,dx}=e^x$.

\[ \frac{d}{dx}[e^xy] = xe^x \implies e^xy = \int xe^x\,dx \]

Using integration by parts (from Phase 1) on the right side: $\int xe^x\,dx=xe^x-e^x+C$.

\[ e^xy = xe^x-e^x+C \implies y = x-1+Ce^{-x} \]
ANSWER $y=x-1+Ce^{-x}$
Example 2 — with an initial condition

Solve $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+\dfrac{2}{x}y=x$, $y(1)=1$ (for $x>0$).

$p(x)=2/x$; $\mu(x)=e^{\int(2/x)dx}=e^{2\ln x}=x^2$.

\[ \frac{d}{dx}[x^2y]=x^3 \implies x^2y=\frac{x^4}{4}+C \implies y=\frac{x^2}{4}+\frac{C}{x^2} \]

At $x=1,y=1$: $1=\tfrac14+C\implies C=\tfrac34$.

ANSWER $y=\dfrac{x^2}{4}+\dfrac{3}{4x^2}$
Note Study notes ▶
Watch out
The DE must be in the exact standard form $y'+p(x)y=q(x)$ (coefficient of $y'$ equal to $1$) before reading off $p(x)$ — if the original equation has a different leading coefficient, divide through by it first.
W8
First-Order Linear DE Applications
Mixing problems with changing volume, and other linear-DE scenarios
The separable mixing problems from earlier assumed the tank's volume stayed constant. Once inflow and outflow rates differ, the volume — and therefore the outflow concentration's denominator — changes with time, turning the equation genuinely linear rather than separable.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Mixing with changing volume. If inflow rate $r_{\text{in}}$ and outflow rate $r_{\text{out}}$ differ, the volume at time $t$ is $V(t)=V_0+(r_{\text{in}}-r_{\text{out}})t$ — no longer constant. The outflow concentration is $\dfrac{A(t)}{V(t)}$, so the DE becomes:

\[ \frac{dA}{dt} = (\text{inflow rate})\cdot(\text{inflow concentration}) - r_{\text{out}}\cdot\frac{A}{V(t)} \]

This is linear in $A$ (with a $t$-dependent coefficient from $V(t)$), not separable — exactly the shape the integrating factor method was built for.

Other common applications. Electrical circuits (RL circuits: $L\dfrac{dI}{dt}+RI=V(t)$ — an equation with the exact same linear structure), and any "rate in minus rate proportional to current amount" scenario more generally.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \frac{dA}{dt}+\frac{r_{\text{out}}}{V(t)}A = r_{\text{in}}\cdot c_{\text{in}}, \qquad \text{solve via integrating factor} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — constant-volume mixing (separable, reviewed as a linear DE)

A 200L tank starts with 50g of salt dissolved. Pure water flows in at 5L/min, and the mixed solution flows out at 5L/min. Find $A(t)$.

Volume constant at 200L. $\dfrac{dA}{dt}=0-5\cdot\dfrac{A}{200}=-\dfrac{A}{40}$ — this is both separable and linear with $p(t)=1/40$, $q(t)=0$; $\mu=e^{t/40}$.

\[ \frac{d}{dt}\left[e^{t/40}A\right]=0 \implies A=Ce^{-t/40} \]

At $t=0,A=50$: $C=50$.

ANSWER $A(t)=50e^{-t/40}$
Example 2 — set up a changing-volume mixing problem

A 100L tank starts with pure water. Brine at 3g/L flows in at 4L/min; the mixed solution flows out at 2L/min. Set up the DE for $A(t)$ (do not solve).

Volume: $V(t)=100+(4-2)t=100+2t$. Inflow rate of salt: $4\times3=12$g/min. Outflow rate: $2\cdot\dfrac{A}{100+2t}$.

\[ \frac{dA}{dt} = 12 - \frac{2A}{100+2t} \]
ANSWER $\dfrac{dA}{dt}+\dfrac{2}{100+2t}A=12$ — linear, ready for the integrating factor method
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
The hardest part of these problems is almost always setting up $V(t)$ correctly and getting the outflow term right — once the DE is correctly written in standard linear form, solving it is pure mechanics from the previous topic.

Second-Order DEs — Characteristic Equation & Three Root Cases
Guessing exponential solutions, and what happens for each type of root
A first-order linear DE with constant coefficients had solution $y=Ce^{kt}$. It's natural to guess that a second-order version might also have exponential solutions — and testing that guess turns the entire differential equation into an ordinary algebraic equation.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

The guess, and why it works. For $ay''+by'+cy=0$ (homogeneous, constant coefficients), try $y=e^{rx}$. Then $y'=re^{rx}$, $y''=r^2e^{rx}$, and substituting:

\[ ar^2e^{rx}+bre^{rx}+ce^{rx}=0 \implies e^{rx}(ar^2+br+c)=0 \]

Since $e^{rx}$ is never zero, the guess works exactly when $r$ satisfies the characteristic equation $ar^2+br+c=0$ — an ordinary quadratic, solvable with the quadratic formula (from Week 1). Every root $r$ gives one exponential solution $e^{rx}$.

Three cases, from the discriminant.

DiscriminantRootsGeneral solution
$b^2-4ac>0$Real, distinct: $r_1,r_2$$y=C_1e^{r_1x}+C_2e^{r_2x}$
$b^2-4ac=0$Repeated: $r$$y=(C_1+C_2x)e^{rx}$
$b^2-4ac<0$Complex: $\alpha\pm\beta i$$y=e^{\alpha x}(C_1\cos\beta x+C_2\sin\beta x)$

The repeated-root case needs an extra factor of $x$ because a single $e^{rx}$ alone isn't "enough" solutions for a second-order equation (which needs two independent building blocks) — $xe^{rx}$ can be verified directly to also solve the equation whenever $r$ is a repeated root. The complex case converts $e^{(\alpha+\beta i)x}$ into real functions using Euler's formula, $e^{i\theta}=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta$.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ ar^2+br+c=0 \quad\text{(characteristic equation, from trying }y=e^{rx}\text{)} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — real, distinct roots

Solve $y''-3y'+2y=0$.

\[ r^2-3r+2=0 \implies (r-1)(r-2)=0 \implies r=1,2 \]
ANSWER $y=C_1e^x+C_2e^{2x}$
Example 2 — complex roots

Solve $y''+4y=0$.

\[ r^2+4=0 \implies r^2=-4 \implies r=\pm2i \]

So $\alpha=0$, $\beta=2$.

ANSWER $y=C_1\cos(2x)+C_2\sin(2x)$ (the $e^{\alpha x}=e^0=1$ factor disappears)
Note Study notes ▶
Uni Bridge
The characteristic equation is a direct application of complex numbers (Phase 2 of the MATH141 tracker) to a calculus problem — recognising complex conjugate roots and converting them to real sinusoidal solutions via Euler's formula is exactly the kind of cross-topic connection the final exam likes to test.

Second-Order DE Applications — Simple Harmonic Motion & Initial Value Problems
Physical systems that literally are second-order DEs, plus using two conditions to pin down both constants
A mass on a spring, obeying Hooke's Law, has acceleration proportional to (negative) displacement — Newton's second law turns that physical statement directly into the complex-root second-order DE from the previous topic, and solving it predicts exactly how the mass oscillates.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Building the SHM equation. Hooke's Law: restoring force $F=-kx$ (proportional to displacement, opposite direction). Newton's second law: $F=ma=m\dfrac{d^2x}{dt^2}$. Setting these equal:

\[ m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} = -kx \implies \frac{d^2x}{dt^2}+\frac{k}{m}x=0 \]

This is exactly the complex-root case from the previous topic, with characteristic equation $r^2+\dfrac{k}{m}=0 \implies r=\pm i\sqrt{k/m}$. Writing $\omega=\sqrt{k/m}$ (the natural angular frequency):

\[ x(t) = C_1\cos(\omega t)+C_2\sin(\omega t) \]

Two initial conditions, two constants. A second-order DE's general solution has two arbitrary constants — so it takes two pieces of information to pin down a particular solution, typically an initial position $x(0)$ and an initial velocity $x'(0)$ (unlike first-order DEs, which needed only one condition).

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \frac{d^2x}{dt^2}+\omega^2x=0, \qquad x(t)=C_1\cos(\omega t)+C_2\sin(\omega t), \qquad \omega=\sqrt{k/m} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — solve an SHM initial value problem

A mass on a spring satisfies $x''+4x=0$, with $x(0)=3$ and $x'(0)=0$ (released from rest). Find $x(t)$.

$\omega=2$: $x(t)=C_1\cos2t+C_2\sin2t$. Differentiate: $x'(t)=-2C_1\sin2t+2C_2\cos2t$.

\[ x(0)=C_1=3, \qquad x'(0)=2C_2=0 \implies C_2=0 \]
ANSWER $x(t)=3\cos(2t)$
Example 2 — real distinct roots with two initial conditions

Solve $y''-y'-2y=0$, $y(0)=1$, $y'(0)=5$.

\[ r^2-r-2=(r-2)(r+1)=0 \implies r=2,-1, \qquad y=C_1e^{2x}+C_2e^{-x} \]

$y'=2C_1e^{2x}-C_2e^{-x}$. At $x=0$: $C_1+C_2=1$ and $2C_1-C_2=5$. Adding: $3C_1=6\implies C_1=2$, so $C_2=-1$.

ANSWER $y=2e^{2x}-e^{-x}$
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
Always differentiate the general solution before applying the second initial condition — a very common error is trying to use $y'(0)$ directly on the undifferentiated general solution.

30
Explain, using the product rule expansion, why $\mu(x)=e^{\int p(x)dx}$ is exactly the function that makes $\mu y'+\mu p(x)y$ collapse into $\frac{d}{dx}[\mu y]$.
W7
▶
Solution

Expanding $\frac{d}{dx}[\mu y]=\mu y'+\mu' y$ via the product rule, this matches $\mu y'+\mu p(x) y$ exactly when $\mu'=\mu p(x)$; solving that as a separable DE for $\mu$ gives $\ln\mu=\int p(x)dx$, i.e. $\mu=e^{\int p(x)dx}$ — so the integrating factor isn't an arbitrary trick, it's the unique function forced by wanting the product rule to run in reverse

Explain why a mixing problem with equal inflow and outflow rates is always separable, while one with unequal rates is genuinely linear but not separable.
W8
▶
Solution

Equal inflow/outflow rates keep the volume $V(t)$ constant, so the outflow term $\frac{r_{\text{out}}}{V}A$ has a constant coefficient — the DE is $\frac{dA}{dt}=\text{const}-\text{const}\cdot A$, which can be separated since both sides only involve $A$ and $t$ separately after rearranging. With unequal rates, $V(t)$ depends on $t$, making the coefficient of $A$ a genuine function of $t$ — the equation is still linear (degree 1 in $A$), but $A$ and $t$ can no longer be algebraically separated onto opposite sides
