Exam 2 Review: Differential Equations, Limits & Series

Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Separable DEs: $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=g(x)h(y) \implies \int\dfrac{1}{h(y)}dy=\int g(x)dx$; growth/decay $y=y_0e^{kt}$; cooling $T=T_a+(T_0-T_a)e^{-kt}$.
  • Integrating factor: $y'+p(x)y=q(x)$: $\mu=e^{\int p\,dx}$, $y=\dfrac1\mu\left[\int\mu q\,dx+C\right]$; RL circuits share this structure: $L I'+RI=V(t)$.
  • Second-order, constant coefficients: $ar^2+br+c=0$; real distinct $C_1e^{r_1x}+C_2e^{r_2x}$; repeated $(C_1+C_2x)e^{rx}$; complex $e^{\alpha x}(C_1\cos\beta x+C_2\sin\beta x)$. SHM: $\omega=\sqrt{k/m}$.
  • Indeterminate forms & L'Hôpital: $\lim\dfrac{f}{g}=\lim\dfrac{f'}{g'}$ for $\tfrac00$ or $\tfrac{\infty}{\infty}$; convert $0\cdot\infty,\infty-\infty$ algebraically; exponential forms via logarithm.
  • Series tests: Divergence (fastest check) → geometric/$p$-series formula → Integral/Comparison/Limit Comparison → Ratio/Root (factorials, exponentials). Telescoping sums collapse to $v_1-\lim v_{n+1}$.
  • Taylor/Maclaurin series: $P_n(x)=\sum\dfrac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k$; essential expansions for $e^x,\sin x,\cos x,\ln(1+x),\dfrac{1}{1-x}$; build new series via substitution/multiplication/term-by-term calculus.
  • Exam strategy: a DE question is usually recognise-the-type (separable vs. linear vs. second-order) then mechanical solving; a limits/series question is usually recognise-the-test then apply it correctly — in both cases, identifying the right tool is most of the battle.

0 Recap: Differential Equations (Weeks 7–9)

First-order DEs split into two recognisable shapes: separable ($y'=g(x)h(y)$) and linear ($y'+p(x)y=q(x)$) — correctly identifying which shape you're looking at determines the whole solution method. Second-order constant-coefficient DEs reduce entirely to solving a quadratic (the characteristic equation) and reading off one of three solution forms.

Key idea

$$\mu(x)=e^{\int p(x)dx}, \qquad ar^2+br+c=0, \qquad \omega=\sqrt{k/m}$$

Worked example

Exam-style. Solve $y'+2xy=2x$, $y(0)=3$, then separately solve $y''+4y'+13y=0$.

$\mu=e^{x^2}$: $\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^{x^2}y]=2xe^{x^2}\implies e^{x^2}y=e^{x^2}+C\implies y=1+Ce^{-x^2}$. At $x=0$: $3=1+C\implies C=2$: $y=1+2e^{-x^2}$.

$r=\dfrac{-4\pm\sqrt{16-52}}{2}=-2\pm3i$: $y=e^{-2x}(C_1\cos3x+C_2\sin3x)$.

Practice 1

Solve $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{2y}{x}+x$ (rewrite in standard linear form first).

Solution

Rearrange: $\dfrac{dy}{dx}-\dfrac{2}{x}y=x$. $\mu=e^{\int(-2/x)dx}=x^{-2}$.

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^{-2}y]=x^{-2}\cdot x=x^{-1}\implies x^{-2}y=\ln|x|+C\implies y=x^2(\ln|x|+C)$.

Answer: $y=x^2\ln|x|+Cx^2$.

Practice 2

A population satisfies $\dfrac{dP}{dt}=0.03P$, $P(0)=1000$. Find how long until the population doubles.

Solution

$P=1000e^{0.03t}$. Set $P=2000$: $2=e^{0.03t}\implies t=\dfrac{\ln2}{0.03}\approx23.1$ years.

Answer: approximately 23.1 years.

Practice 3

Solve $y''-2y'+5y=0$, $y(0)=1$, $y'(0)=3$.

Solution

$r=1\pm2i$: $y=e^x(C_1\cos2x+C_2\sin2x)$.

$y(0)=C_1=1$. $y'=e^x(C_1\cos2x+C_2\sin2x)+e^x(-2C_1\sin2x+2C_2\cos2x)$; $y'(0)=C_1+2C_2=3\implies C_2=1$.

Answer: $y=e^x(\cos2x+\sin2x)$.

Practice 4

A 100L tank has 20 kg of salt. Brine at 1 kg/L flows in at 3L/min; mixture flows out at 3L/min (constant volume). Find $A(t)$ and the steady-state amount.

Solution

$\dfrac{dA}{dt}=3-\dfrac{3A}{100}$. $\mu=e^{3t/100}$; $A=100+Ce^{-3t/100}$; $A(0)=20\implies C=-80$.

$A(t)=100-80e^{-3t/100}$. As $t\to\infty$, $A\to100$ kg.

Answer: $A(t)=100-80e^{-3t/100}$; steady-state 100 kg.

Practice 5

A spring-mass system has $m=2$ kg, $k=18$ N/m, released from $x(0)=0.3$ m with $x'(0)=0$. Find the maximum speed of the mass during its motion.

Solution

$\omega=\sqrt{18/2}=3$. $x(t)=0.3\cos3t$; $x'(t)=-0.9\sin3t$.

Maximum speed is the amplitude of $x'(t)$: $|{-0.9}|=0.9$ m/s (occurs when $\sin3t=\pm1$, i.e. as the mass passes through equilibrium).

Answer: $0.9$ m/s.

1 Recap: Limits, L'Hôpital's Rule & Series

Limits questions are almost always won or lost on correctly identifying the indeterminate form first. Series questions are won or lost on choosing the right test — check divergence first (fastest), match geometric/$p$-series directly, then reach for Integral/Comparison for awkward positive-term series or Ratio/Root for factorials and exponentials.

Key idea

$$\lim\frac{f}{g}=\lim\frac{f'}{g'}\ (\tfrac00,\tfrac{\infty}{\infty}), \qquad \rho=\lim\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|: \rho<1 \text{ conv.}, \rho>1 \text{ div.}$$

Worked example

Exam-style. Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{e^{2x}-1-2x}{x^2}$, then determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty\dfrac{n^2}{n!}$ converges.

$\dfrac00$; L'Hôpital twice: $\lim\dfrac{2e^{2x}-2}{2x}$ (still $\dfrac00$); again: $\lim\dfrac{4e^{2x}}{2}=2$.

Ratio Test: $\rho=\lim\left|\dfrac{(n+1)^2/(n+1)!}{n^2/n!}\right|=\lim\dfrac{(n+1)^2}{n^2(n+1)}=\lim\dfrac{n+1}{n^2}=0<1$: converges.

Practice 6

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\left(\frac{2x+1}{2x-1}\right)^x$ using the logarithm trick.

Solution

$1^\infty$ form. Let $y=\left(\dfrac{2x+1}{2x-1}\right)^x$; $\ln y=x\big[\ln(2x+1)-\ln(2x-1)\big]$, form $\infty\cdot0$.

Substitute $t=1/x\to0^+$: $2x+1=\dfrac{2+t}{t}$, $2x-1=\dfrac{2-t}{t}$, so $\ln(2x+1)-\ln(2x-1)=\ln(2+t)-\ln(2-t)$ (the $\ln t$ terms cancel).

$\ln y = \dfrac{\ln(2+t)-\ln(2-t)}{t}$, now form $\dfrac00$ as $t\to0$. L'Hôpital (differentiating with respect to $t$): $\lim_{t\to0}\left[\dfrac{1}{2+t}+\dfrac{1}{2-t}\right]=\dfrac12+\dfrac12=1$.

So $\ln y\to1 \implies y\to e^1=e$.

Answer: $e$ (verify numerically: at $x=1000$, $(2001/1999)^{1000}\approx2.71828$, matching $e$).

Practice 7

Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(n!)^2}{(3n)!}$ converges, using the Ratio Test.

Solution

$\rho=\lim\left|\dfrac{((n+1)!)^2/(3n+3)!}{(n!)^2/(3n)!}\right|=\lim\dfrac{(n+1)^2}{(3n+1)(3n+2)(3n+3)}$.

For large $n$: $\dfrac{n^2}{27n^3}\to0$. So $\rho=0<1$: converges.

Practice 8

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}\left(\cot x-\frac1x\right)$ (an $\infty-\infty$ form).

Solution

Combine: $\cot x-\dfrac1x=\dfrac{x\cos x-\sin x}{x\sin x}$, form $\dfrac00$.

L'Hôpital: $\lim\dfrac{\cos x-x\sin x-\cos x}{\sin x+x\cos x}=\lim\dfrac{-x\sin x}{\sin x+x\cos x}$, still $\dfrac00$; again: $\lim\dfrac{-\sin x-x\cos x}{\cos x+\cos x-x\sin x}=\dfrac{0}{2}=0$.

Answer: $0$.

Practice 9

Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=2}^\infty \frac{1}{n(\ln n)^2}$ converges, using the Integral Test.

Solution

$f(x)=\dfrac{1}{x(\ln x)^2}$, positive/decreasing for $x\ge2$. Let $u=\ln x$: $\int u^{-2}du=-1/u$, giving $\displaystyle\int_{\ln2}^\infty u^{-2}du=\dfrac{1}{\ln2}$, finite.

Answer: converges (by the Integral Test).

Practice 10

Find the Maclaurin series for $\dfrac{1-\cos x}{x^2}$ (simplify using the known cosine series first), and use it to evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{1-\cos x}{x^2}$.

Solution

$1-\cos x=\dfrac{x^2}{2}-\dfrac{x^4}{24}+\cdots$, so $\dfrac{1-\cos x}{x^2}=\dfrac12-\dfrac{x^2}{24}+\cdots$.

As $x\to0$, this series $\to\dfrac12$.

Answer: $\dfrac12$ (matches the standard L'Hôpital result for this classic limit).

2 Mixed Cross-Phase Problems (Weeks 7–12)

The Final Examination (and occasionally In-Session Exam 2 itself) can combine differential equations, limits, and series in a single problem. Read each multi-part question fully before starting — later parts often reuse an earlier result.

Key idea

No single formula — the skill here is recognising which tool (from Phase 2 or Phase 3) each part needs, and correctly carrying results between parts.

Worked example

Exam-style, multi-part. (a) Solve $y'+y=0$, $y(0)=1$. (b) Verify your solution matches the first three terms of the Maclaurin series for $e^{-x}$.

(a) Separable: $y=Ce^{-x}$; $y(0)=1\implies C=1$: $y=e^{-x}$.

(b) $e^{-x}=1-x+\dfrac{x^2}{2}-\cdots$ — matches the known Maclaurin expansion exactly, confirming both the DE solution and the series are describing the same function.

Practice 11

A radioactive substance decays according to $\dfrac{dy}{dt}=-0.05y$, $y(0)=200$. (a) Solve for $y(t)$. (b) Use the Maclaurin series for $e^x$ to estimate $y(1)$ using only the first four terms, and compare with the exact value.

Solution

(a) $y(t)=200e^{-0.05t}$.

(b) $e^{-0.05}\approx1-0.05+\dfrac{0.05^2}{2}-\dfrac{0.05^3}{6}=1-0.05+0.00125-0.0000208\approx0.951229$.

$y(1)\approx200(0.951229)\approx190.246$. Exact value: $200e^{-0.05}\approx190.2456$ — matches to 3 decimal places with just 4 series terms.

Answer: $y(t)=200e^{-0.05t}$; $y(1)\approx190.25$.

Practice 12

A spring-mass system satisfies $x''+\omega^2x=0$. (a) If $\omega=2$, $x(0)=1$, $x'(0)=0$, solve for $x(t)$. (b) Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{t\to0}\dfrac{x(t)-1}{t^2}$ using either L'Hôpital's Rule or the Maclaurin series for cosine.

Solution

(a) $x(t)=\cos(2t)$.

(b) Using the Maclaurin series: $\cos(2t)=1-\dfrac{(2t)^2}{2}+\cdots=1-2t^2+\cdots$, so $\dfrac{x(t)-1}{t^2}=\dfrac{-2t^2+\cdots}{t^2}\to-2$ as $t\to0$.

Answer: $x(t)=\cos2t$; limit $=-2$.

Practice 13

Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty y_n$ converges, where $y_n$ is the solution to $\dfrac{dy}{dn}=-\dfrac{y}{n}$ (treating $n$ continuously) with $y(1)=1$, evaluated at each positive integer $n$ (i.e. find $y(n)$ as a function of $n$ first, then test the resulting series).

Solution

Solve the DE: separable, $\dfrac1y\,dy=-\dfrac1n\,dn \implies \ln|y|=-\ln n+C_1 \implies y=\dfrac{K}{n}$. At $n=1,y=1$: $K=1$, so $y(n)=\dfrac1n$.

The series becomes $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac1n$ — the harmonic series, known to diverge (by the Integral Test, Week 11).

Answer: diverges (this DE-to-series connection produces the harmonic series exactly).

Practice 14

An RL circuit has current $I(t)=2(1-e^{-5t})$ (from a constant-voltage source). (a) Find the Maclaurin series for $I(t)$ using the known series for $e^x$ (up to the $t^2$ term). (b) Use it to estimate $I(0.01)$ and compare with the exact value.

Solution

(a) $e^{-5t}=1-5t+\dfrac{25t^2}{2}-\cdots$, so $1-e^{-5t}=5t-\dfrac{25t^2}{2}+\cdots$, giving $I(t)=2\left(5t-\dfrac{25t^2}{2}\right)=10t-25t^2+\cdots$.

(b) $I(0.01)\approx10(0.01)-25(0.01)^2=0.1-0.0025=0.0975$.

Exact: $I(0.01)=2(1-e^{-0.05})=2(1-0.951229)=2(0.048771)=0.097542$ — matches closely.

Answer: series estimate $\approx0.0975$; exact $\approx0.09754$.

Practice 15

Solve the second-order DE $y''-y=0$, $y(0)=1$, $y'(0)=1$, and show that your solution can be rewritten using the exponential form, then verify it matches the sum of the Maclaurin series for $e^x$ alone (not a combination of $e^x$ and $e^{-x}$).

Solution

$r^2-1=0\implies r=\pm1$: $y=C_1e^x+C_2e^{-x}$.

$y(0)=C_1+C_2=1$; $y'=C_1e^x-C_2e^{-x}$, $y'(0)=C_1-C_2=1$.

Adding: $2C_1=2\implies C_1=1$; subtracting: $2C_2=0\implies C_2=0$.

Answer: $y=e^x$ (the $e^{-x}$ term vanishes entirely since $C_2=0$) — and indeed $e^x=\sum x^n/n!$ is exactly the Maclaurin series memorised in Week 12, confirming the two representations describe the same function.
