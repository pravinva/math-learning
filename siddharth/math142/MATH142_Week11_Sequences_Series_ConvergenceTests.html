Sequences & Series: Convergence Tests, Telescoping Sums & Remainder Estimation

Sequences are the foundation everything else in this phase sits on — a series is literally defined as the limit of a sequence of partial sums. This week covers sequence convergence, the geometric series and Divergence/Integral Tests, Comparison tests, and the Ratio/Root Tests, plus telescoping sums and remainder (error) estimation for approximating an infinite sum. Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Sequence convergence: $\{a_n\}$ converges to $L$ if $\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n=L$; every bounded, monotonic sequence converges (even without knowing $L$ exactly).
  • Series as partial sums: $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n \equiv \lim_{n\to\infty}S_n$, where $S_n=a_1+\cdots+a_n$.
  • Geometric series: $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty ar^n=\dfrac{a}{1-r}$ for $|r|<1$; diverges for $|r|\ge1$.
  • Divergence Test: $\lim a_n\ne0 \implies \sum a_n$ diverges (test only proves divergence; the converse is false — the harmonic series has $\lim a_n=0$ but still diverges).
  • Integral Test: for positive, decreasing, continuous $f$ with $f(n)=a_n$: $\sum a_n$ converges $\iff \int_1^\infty f(x)\,dx$ converges. $p$-series $\sum1/n^p$ converges iff $p>1$.
  • Comparison / Limit Comparison: $0\le a_n\le b_n$ (direct); or $L=\lim a_n/b_n\in(0,\infty)$ (limit comparison) — both compare an unknown series to a known one.
  • Ratio / Root Test: $\rho=\lim|a_{n+1}/a_n|$ or $\lim\sqrt[n]{|a_n|}$: $\rho<1$ converges, $\rho>1$ diverges, $\rho=1$ inconclusive. Best for factorials/exponentials; useless (always gives 1) for $p$-series.
  • Telescoping series: if $a_n=v_n-v_{n+1}$, partial sums collapse to $S_n=v_1-v_{n+1}$; sum $=v_1-\lim v_{n+1}$ (use partial fractions to spot this form).
  • Remainder estimation ($R_n=S-s_n$): Integral method, $|R_n|\le\int_n^\infty f(x)dx$; Alternating series method, $|R_n|\le|u_{n+1}|$.

0 Sequences — Convergence & Limit Laws

A sequence is really just a function whose domain is restricted to the positive integers — $a_n$ instead of $f(x)$. Every limit idea already built (algebra of limits, indeterminate forms, L'Hôpital's Rule) transfers almost unchanged, just with $n\to\infty$ replacing $x\to\infty$.

Monotonic and bounded sequences. A key theorem: every bounded, monotonic sequence converges — this proves convergence even without finding the exact limit.

Squeeze Theorem. If $b_n\le a_n\le c_n$ and $\lim b_n=\lim c_n=L$, then $\lim a_n=L$ too.

Key idea

$$\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n=L \quad\text{(convergence)}, \qquad \text{bounded + monotonic} \implies \text{convergent}$$

Worked example

Example 1. Determine whether $a_n=\dfrac{2n+1}{n+3}$ converges.

$\lim_{n\to\infty}\dfrac{2+1/n}{1+3/n}=2$. Converges to $2$.

Example 2 — Squeeze Theorem. Show $a_n=\dfrac{\sin n}{n}\to0$.

$-\dfrac1n\le\dfrac{\sin n}{n}\le\dfrac1n$; both bounds $\to0$, so $a_n\to0$.

Practice 1

Determine whether $a_n=\dfrac{n^2}{2n^2+1}$ converges, and to what.

Solution

$\lim_{n\to\infty}\dfrac{1}{2+1/n^2}=\dfrac12$.

Answer: converges to $\dfrac12$.

Practice 2

Determine whether $a_n=\dfrac{(-1)^nn}{n+1}$ converges.

Solution

The terms oscillate: for even $n$, $a_n\to1$; for odd $n$, $a_n\to-1$. Since the sequence doesn't settle on a single value, it diverges.

Answer: diverges (oscillates between values approaching $1$ and $-1$).

Practice 3

Find $\lim_{n\to\infty}\dfrac{n!}{n^n}$ using the Squeeze Theorem (hint: bound the ratio using the fact that each factor in $n!/n^n$ except the first is at most $1$).

Solution

$\dfrac{n!}{n^n}=\dfrac{1}{n}\cdot\dfrac{2}{n}\cdots\dfrac{n}{n}$. Every factor from the second onward is at most $1$, so $0\le\dfrac{n!}{n^n}\le\dfrac1n\cdot1\cdot1\cdots1=\dfrac1n$.

Since $\dfrac1n\to0$ and the lower bound $0\to0$, by the Squeeze Theorem $\dfrac{n!}{n^n}\to0$.

Answer: converges to $0$.

Practice 4

Show that $a_n=\dfrac{2n}{n+5}$ is increasing and bounded above by $2$, and conclude it converges (find the limit too).

Solution

$f(x)=\dfrac{2x}{x+5}$; $f'(x)=\dfrac{2(x+5)-2x}{(x+5)^2}=\dfrac{10}{(x+5)^2}>0$, so increasing.

Bounded above: $\dfrac{2n}{n+5}<2$ since $2n<2(n+5)=2n+10$ always. By the bounded-monotonic theorem, converges.

$\lim_{n\to\infty}\dfrac{2n}{n+5}=\lim\dfrac{2}{1+5/n}=2$.

Answer: converges to $2$.

Practice 5

Determine whether $a_n=n\sin\left(\dfrac1n\right)$ converges, using L'Hôpital's Rule on the corresponding function limit.

Solution

Rewrite as $\dfrac{\sin(1/n)}{1/n}$, matching the classic $\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{\sin x}{x}=1$ with $x=1/n\to0$ as $n\to\infty$.

Answer: converges to $1$.

Practice 6

Determine whether $a_n=\left(1+\dfrac{2}{n}\right)^n$ converges, connecting it to the standard limit defining $e$.

Solution

This is the sequence version of $\lim_{n\to\infty}\left(1+\dfrac{k}{n}\right)^n=e^k$ with $k=2$.

Answer: converges to $e^2$.

Solution

$\dfrac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}=\dfrac{3^{n+1}/(n+1)!}{3^n/n!}=\dfrac{3}{n+1}$. For $n\ge3$, this ratio is $\le\dfrac34<1$, meaning $a_{n+1}

Practice 8

Determine whether $a_n=\dfrac{\ln(n^2)}{n}$ converges, using L'Hôpital's Rule on the corresponding function limit.

Solution

$\dfrac{\ln(n^2)}{n}=\dfrac{2\ln n}{n}$, form $\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$. L'Hôpital: $\lim\dfrac{2/n}{1}=0$.

Answer: converges to $0$.

Practice 9

Explain why $a_n=\cos(n\pi)$ does not converge, despite being bounded (between $-1$ and $1$), reconciling this with the bounded-monotonic theorem.

Solution

$\cos(n\pi)=(-1)^n$, which oscillates forever between $-1$ and $1$ without settling — it diverges.

This doesn't contradict the bounded-monotonic theorem because that theorem requires BOTH boundedness AND monotonicity; $(-1)^n$ is bounded but not monotonic (it's neither always increasing nor always decreasing), so the theorem simply doesn't apply.

Practice 10

Determine whether $a_n=\sqrt{n+1}-\sqrt{n}$ converges, using rationalisation.

Solution

Multiply by the conjugate: $\dfrac{(n+1)-n}{\sqrt{n+1}+\sqrt n}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{n+1}+\sqrt n}$.

As $n\to\infty$, the denominator $\to\infty$, so the whole expression $\to0$.

Answer: converges to $0$.

1 Series — Geometric Series, Divergence & Integral Tests, Telescoping Sums

The infinite series $\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n$ is defined as $\lim_{n\to\infty}S_n$, where $S_n=a_1+\cdots+a_n$ is the $n$-th partial sum. If this limit exists (finite), the series converges; otherwise it diverges.

Geometric series: $\sum_{n=0}^\infty ar^n=\dfrac{a}{1-r}$ for $|r|<1$ (diverges for $|r|\ge1$).

Divergence Test: if $\lim a_n\ne0$, $\sum a_n$ diverges. Warning: the converse is false — $\lim a_n=0$ does NOT guarantee convergence (the harmonic series $\sum1/n$ is the classic counterexample: terms $\to0$, but the series still diverges).

Integral Test: for $f$ positive, decreasing, continuous, with $f(n)=a_n$: $\sum a_n$ converges iff $\int_1^\infty f(x)dx$ converges.

Telescoping series. If $a_n=v_n-v_{n+1}$ (often revealed via partial fractions), the partial sum collapses: $S_n=(v_1-v_2)+(v_2-v_3)+\cdots+(v_n-v_{n+1})=v_1-v_{n+1}$, so the sum is $v_1-\lim_{n\to\infty}v_{n+1}$.

Key idea

$$\sum_{n=0}^\infty ar^n=\frac{a}{1-r}\ (|r|<1), \qquad \lim a_n\ne0 \implies \text{diverges}, \qquad \sum a_n \text{ conv.} \iff \int_1^\infty f(x)dx \text{ conv.}$$ $$\text{Telescoping: } a_n=v_n-v_{n+1} \implies \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n = v_1-\lim_{n\to\infty}v_{n+1}$$

Worked example

Example 1 — geometric series. Evaluate $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty\left(\frac13\right)^n$.

$a=1,r=\tfrac13$: $\dfrac{1}{1-1/3}=\dfrac32$.

Example 2 — telescoping series. Evaluate $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac{1}{n(n+1)}$.

Partial fractions: $\dfrac{1}{n(n+1)}=\dfrac1n-\dfrac{1}{n+1}$, so $v_n=\dfrac1n$. Sum $=v_1-\lim v_{n+1}=1-0=1$.

Practice 11

Evaluate $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty \left(\frac{-2}{5}\right)^n$.

Solution

$a=1,r=-\tfrac25$, $|r|<1$: $\dfrac{1}{1-(-2/5)}=\dfrac{1}{7/5}=\dfrac57$.

Answer: $\dfrac57$.

Practice 12

Use the Divergence Test on $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{3n^2+1}{n^2+5}$.

Solution

$\lim_{n\to\infty}\dfrac{3n^2+1}{n^2+5}=3\ne0$ — diverges.

Practice 13

Use the Integral Test to determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{n}{n^2+1}$ converges.

Solution

$f(x)=\dfrac{x}{x^2+1}$ is positive, and (checking $f'(x)$) decreasing for $x>1$.

$\displaystyle\int_1^\infty\frac{x}{x^2+1}dx=\lim_{t\to\infty}\left[\tfrac12\ln(x^2+1)\right]_1^t=\lim_{t\to\infty}\tfrac12\ln(t^2+1)=\infty$ — diverges.

Answer: diverges (by the Integral Test).

Practice 14

Evaluate the telescoping series $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt n}-\frac{1}{\sqrt{n+1}}\right)$.

Solution

Directly in telescoping form with $v_n=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt n}$: sum $=v_1-\lim v_{n+1}=1-0=1$.

Answer: $1$.

Practice 15

Evaluate $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{2}{n(n+2)}$ (telescoping, but requires partial fractions with a gap of 2 — the terms don't cancel adjacently).

Solution

$\dfrac{2}{n(n+2)}=\dfrac{A}{n}+\dfrac{B}{n+2}$; $2=A(n+2)+Bn$. At $n=0$: $A=1$. At $n=-2$: $B=-1$.

$S_N=\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^N\left(\frac1n-\frac{1}{n+2}\right)$. Writing out terms: $\left(1-\tfrac13\right)+\left(\tfrac12-\tfrac14\right)+\left(\tfrac13-\tfrac15\right)+\cdots+\left(\tfrac1N-\tfrac{1}{N+2}\right)$.

Since each term skips 2 rather than 1, surviving terms are the first two positive ($1,\tfrac12$) and the last two negative ($-\tfrac{1}{N+1},-\tfrac{1}{N+2}$), everything else cancels: $S_N=1+\tfrac12-\tfrac{1}{N+1}-\tfrac{1}{N+2}$.

As $N\to\infty$: $S_N\to1+\tfrac12=\tfrac32$.

Answer: $\dfrac32$.

Practice 16

Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n(\ln n)^3}$ (for $n\ge2$) converges, using the Integral Test with $u=\ln x$.

Solution

$f(x)=\dfrac{1}{x(\ln x)^3}$ is positive and decreasing for $x\ge2$. Let $u=\ln x,du=dx/x$: $\displaystyle\int_{\ln2}^\infty u^{-3}du=\lim_{t\to\infty}\left[-\dfrac{1}{2u^2}\right]_{\ln2}^t=0+\dfrac{1}{2(\ln2)^2}$, finite.

Answer: converges (by the Integral Test).

Practice 17

A ball dropped from 8m rebounds to 75% of its previous height each bounce. Find the total vertical distance travelled, using a geometric series.

Solution

Total distance $=8+2(6+4.5+3.375+\cdots)=8+2\cdot\dfrac{6}{1-0.75}=8+2(24)=8+48=56$m.

Answer: $56$ m.

Practice 18

Evaluate $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \ln\left(\frac{n}{n+1}\right)$, recognising it as a telescoping series in disguise (using properties of logarithms).

Solution

$\ln\left(\dfrac{n}{n+1}\right)=\ln n-\ln(n+1)$, so $v_n=\ln n$.

Sum $=v_1-\lim_{n\to\infty}v_{n+1}=\ln1-\lim_{n\to\infty}\ln(n+1)=0-\infty=-\infty$.

Answer: diverges (to $-\infty$) — a good reminder that not every telescoping-looking series has a finite sum; here $\lim v_{n+1}$ itself doesn't exist as a finite number.

Practice 19

Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{n^2}{2^n}$ converges, using the Integral Test (or state why the Ratio Test, covered next, would actually be more natural here).

Solution

While the Integral Test could technically be applied (checking $f(x)=x^2/2^x$ is eventually decreasing), differentiating $x^22^{-x}$ is messy, and the resulting integral $\int x^22^{-x}dx$ requires repeated integration by parts.

The Ratio Test (next topic) is far more natural for a series with $2^n$ in the denominator — it would be the recommended approach in practice, even though this question asks specifically about the Integral Test to reinforce recognising when it's the wrong tool to reach for.

Practice 20

Evaluate $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{(2n-1)(2n+1)}$ using partial fractions to reveal telescoping structure.

Solution

$\dfrac{1}{(2n-1)(2n+1)}=\dfrac{A}{2n-1}+\dfrac{B}{2n+1}$; $1=A(2n+1)+B(2n-1)$. At $n=1/2$: $A=\tfrac12$. At $n=-1/2$: $B=-\tfrac12$.

$S_N=\tfrac12\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^N\left(\frac{1}{2n-1}-\frac{1}{2n+1}\right)$; this telescopes with $v_n=\dfrac{1}{2n-1}$: $S_N=\tfrac12\left(1-\dfrac{1}{2N+1}\right)\to\tfrac12(1-0)=\tfrac12$.

Answer: $\dfrac12$.

2 Series — Comparison & Limit Comparison Tests

When a series closely resembles a known convergent or divergent series, comparing term-by-term (or via a ratio) often settles the question without any new integration.

Direct Comparison Test. If $0\le a_n\le b_n$: $\sum b_n$ converges $\implies \sum a_n$ converges; $\sum a_n$ diverges $\implies \sum b_n$ diverges.

Limit Comparison Test. $L=\lim\dfrac{a_n}{b_n}$. If $L$ is finite and positive, $\sum a_n$ and $\sum b_n$ behave identically (both converge or both diverge) — generally easier than direct comparison, since it avoids needing an exact inequality.

Choosing a comparison series. For rational-function-like terms, compare to the $p$-series matching the dominant powers of $n$.

Key idea

$$0\le a_n\le b_n:\ \sum b_n \text{ conv.} \implies \sum a_n \text{ conv.} \qquad L=\lim\frac{a_n}{b_n}\in(0,\infty) \implies \text{same behaviour}$$

Worked example

Example 1 — Direct Comparison. Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty\dfrac{1}{n^2+1}$ converges.

$\dfrac{1}{n^2+1}\le\dfrac{1}{n^2}$; $\sum1/n^2$ converges ($p=2>1$). By Direct Comparison, converges.

Example 2 — Limit Comparison. Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty\dfrac{n}{n^3+2}$ converges, comparing to $\sum1/n^2$.

$L=\lim\dfrac{n/(n^3+2)}{1/n^2}=\lim\dfrac{n^3}{n^3+2}=1$, finite/positive. Since $\sum1/n^2$ converges, so does this series.

Practice 21

Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{\sqrt{n^3+1}}$ converges, using Direct Comparison with $\dfrac{1}{n^{3/2}}$.

Solution

$\sqrt{n^3+1}>\sqrt{n^3}=n^{3/2}$, so $\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{n^3+1}}<\dfrac{1}{n^{3/2}}$.

$\sum1/n^{3/2}$ is a $p$-series with $p=3/2>1$: converges. By Direct Comparison, the given series converges too.

Practice 22

Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=2}^\infty \frac{1}{n-1}$ converges, using Direct Comparison or Limit Comparison with the harmonic series $\sum1/n$.

Solution

Limit comparison: $L=\lim_{n\to\infty}\dfrac{1/(n-1)}{1/n}=\lim\dfrac{n}{n-1}=1$, finite and positive.

Since $\sum1/n$ (harmonic) diverges, the given series diverges too.

Practice 23

Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{\sqrt n+1}{n^2-n}$ converges, using Limit Comparison with an appropriately chosen $p$-series.

Solution

Dominant behaviour for large $n$: numerator $\sim\sqrt n=n^{1/2}$, denominator $\sim n^2$, so the whole term behaves like $n^{1/2-2}=n^{-3/2}$ — compare to $\sum1/n^{3/2}$.

$L=\lim\dfrac{(\sqrt n+1)/(n^2-n)}{1/n^{3/2}}=\lim\dfrac{n^{3/2}(\sqrt n+1)}{n^2-n}=\lim\dfrac{n^2+n^{3/2}}{n^2-n}=1$ (dividing through by $n^2$).

Since $\sum1/n^{3/2}$ converges ($p=3/2>1$), the given series converges too.

Practice 24

For $n\ge2$: $2^n-n\ge2^{n-1}$ (check $n=2$: $4-2=2=2^1$; and the gap grows for larger $n$), so $\dfrac{1}{2^n-n}\le\dfrac{1}{2^{n-1}}$.

Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{5^n}{n\cdot4^n}$ converges, using Limit Comparison with the geometric series $\sum(5/4)^n$.

Solution

$L=\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{5^n/(n4^n)}{(5/4)^n}=\lim\frac{5^n}{n4^n}\cdot\frac{4^n}{5^n}=\lim\frac1n=0$.

Practice 28

Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n!+n}$ converges, using Direct Comparison with $\dfrac{1}{n!}$.

Solution

Since $n!+n>n!$ for $n\ge2$: $\dfrac{1}{n!+n}<\dfrac{1}{n!}$.

$\sum1/n!$ is known to converge (it's the series for $e$, minus the $n=0$ term) — by Direct Comparison, the given series converges too.

Practice 29

Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=2}^\infty \frac{1}{n\sqrt{\ln n}}$ converges, using Limit Comparison with $\sum\dfrac1n$ and explaining why the comparison is inconclusive, then identify the correct test.

Solution

$L=\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1/(n\sqrt{\ln n})}{1/n}=\lim\frac{1}{\sqrt{\ln n}}=0$.

Since $L=0$ here, and $\sum1/n$ diverges, the Limit Comparison Test gives no conclusion (as in the earlier edge-case question) — this particular comparison doesn't settle the question.

Correct approach: the Integral Test applies directly: $f(x)=\dfrac{1}{x\sqrt{\ln x}}$ is positive and decreasing; substituting $u=\ln x$ gives $\int u^{-1/2}du=2\sqrt u\to\infty$ — diverges.

Practice 30

Dominant powers: numerator $\sim2n^3$, denominator $\sim n^5$, so the term behaves like $2n^{-2}$ — compare to $\sum1/n^2$.

Finite and positive; since $\sum1/n^2$ converges, the given series converges too.

3 Series — Ratio & Root Tests, and Choosing the Right Test

For series built from factorials or $n$-th powers, the Ratio and Root Tests look directly at how fast consecutive terms shrink.

Ratio Test. $\rho=\lim\left|\dfrac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|$. $\rho<1$: converges absolutely; $\rho>1$: diverges; $\rho=1$: inconclusive. Best suited to factorials (enormous cancellation, e.g. $(n+1)!/n!=n+1$).

Root Test. $\rho=\lim\sqrt[n]{|a_n|}$. Same conclusion structure; natural when the whole term is already raised to the $n$-th power.

Choosing among all the tests — a decision guide: check the Divergence Test first (fastest); if the series matches geometric or $p$-series exactly, use that formula directly; for series of positive terms with no obvious comparison, try Ratio/Root (factorials, exponentials, $n$-th powers) or Integral Test (otherwise); for series with mixed/negative signs, check absolute convergence first, then consider the Alternating Series Test if it's genuinely alternating.

Worked example

Example 1 — Ratio Test. Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty\dfrac{n!}{2^n}$ converges.

$\rho=\lim\left|\dfrac{(n+1)!/2^{n+1}}{n!/2^n}\right|=\lim\dfrac{n+1}{2}=\infty>1$ — diverges.

Example 2 — Root Test. Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty\left(\dfrac{n}{2n+1}\right)^n$ converges.

$\rho=\lim\sqrt[n]{\left(\dfrac{n}{2n+1}\right)^n}=\lim\dfrac{n}{2n+1}=\dfrac12<1$ — converges.

$\rho=\lim\sqrt[n]{\left(\dfrac{n^2+1}{2n^2+3}\right)^n}=\lim\dfrac{n^2+1}{2n^2+3}=\dfrac12<1$ — converges.

Practice 36

Determine which single test is most appropriate for each of the following series, and state the conclusion (do not show all working, just identify test + answer): (a) $\sum\dfrac{1}{n^3}$, (b) $\sum\dfrac{n!}{5^n}$, (c) $\sum\left(\dfrac{n}{n+1}\right)^{n^2}$, (d) $\sum\dfrac{1}{n^2+3n}$.

Solution

(a) $p$-series formula directly, $p=3>1$: converges.

(b) Ratio Test (factorial): $\rho=\lim\dfrac{n+1}{5}=\infty>1$: diverges.

(c) Root Test (whole term to the power $n$... here it's $n^2$, so rewrite as $\left[\left(\frac{n}{n+1}\right)^n\right]^n$; taking the $n$-th root of the $n$-th root effectively gives $\rho=\lim\left(\frac{n}{n+1}\right)^n=e^{-1}<1$): converges.

(d) Limit Comparison with $\sum1/n^2$ (or Direct Comparison): converges.

Practice 37

Apply the Ratio Test to $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{n!}{n^n}$, and identify the standard limit that appears mid-calculation.

Solution

$\rho=\lim\left|\dfrac{(n+1)!/(n+1)^{n+1}}{n!/n^n}\right|=\lim\dfrac{(n+1)n^n}{(n+1)^{n+1}}=\lim\dfrac{n^n}{(n+1)^n}=\lim\left(\dfrac{n}{n+1}\right)^n=\lim\left(1+\dfrac1n\right)^{-n}$.

This is the reciprocal of the standard limit defining $e$: $\left(1+\tfrac1n\right)^n\to e$, so $\left(1+\tfrac1n\right)^{-n}\to\dfrac1e$.

$\rho=\dfrac1e\approx0.368<1$ — converges.

Practice 38

Explain why the Ratio Test always gives $\rho=1$ (inconclusive) for any $p$-series $\sum1/n^p$, regardless of the value of $p$, and what this implies about test selection.

Solution

$\rho=\lim\left|\dfrac{1/(n+1)^p}{1/n^p}\right|=\lim\left(\dfrac{n}{n+1}\right)^p=1^p=1$ for any fixed $p$ — the ratio of consecutive terms always approaches $1$ for power-law (polynomial-rate) series, since the Ratio Test is fundamentally measuring geometric-like decay, which $p$-series don't exhibit.

This means the Ratio Test can never distinguish a convergent $p$-series ($p>1$) from a divergent one ($p\le1$) — whenever a series has this polynomial "shape," reach for the Integral Test or a Comparison Test instead.

Practice 39

Apply the Ratio Test to $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(n!)^2}{(2n)!}$.

Solution

$\rho=\lim\left|\dfrac{((n+1)!)^2/(2n+2)!}{(n!)^2/(2n)!}\right|=\lim\dfrac{(n+1)^2}{(2n+2)(2n+1)}$.

For large $n$: $\dfrac{n^2}{4n^2}=\dfrac14$. So $\rho=\dfrac14<1$ — converges.

Practice 40

A series has terms $a_n=\dfrac{n^n}{n!\,3^n}$. Apply the Ratio Test, using the standard limit for $e$ along the way, to determine convergence.

Solution

$\rho=\lim\left|\dfrac{(n+1)^{n+1}/((n+1)!3^{n+1})}{n^n/(n!3^n)}\right|=\lim\dfrac{(n+1)^{n+1}}{(n+1)3^{n+1}}\cdot\dfrac{3^n}{n^n}=\lim\dfrac{(n+1)^n}{3n^n}=\dfrac13\lim\left(\dfrac{n+1}{n}\right)^n=\dfrac13\lim\left(1+\dfrac1n\right)^n=\dfrac{e}{3}$.

$e\approx2.718$, so $\rho\approx0.906<1$ — converges (though close to the boundary, illustrating why exact limits like $e$ matter here rather than rough approximations).
