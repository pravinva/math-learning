First-Order Linear DE Applications: Changing-Volume Mixing & RL Circuits

The separable mixing problems from last week assumed constant tank volume. Once inflow and outflow rates differ, the volume — and the outflow concentration's denominator — changes with time, turning the equation genuinely linear rather than separable. RL circuits share exactly the same linear structure. Exam-level worked examples and practice throughout. Solutions stay hidden until you click.

Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Changing-volume mixing: if inflow rate $r_{in}$ and outflow rate $r_{out}$ differ, $V(t)=V_0+(r_{in}-r_{out})t$; the DE becomes $¥dfrac{dA}{dt}+¥dfrac{r_{out}}{V(t)}A = r_{in}¥cdot c_{in}$ — linear, not separable, since the coefficient of $A$ now depends on $t$.
  • RL circuits: $L¥dfrac{dI}{dt}+RI=V(t)$ — the exact same linear structure as a mixing problem, with $I$ playing the role of $A$.
  • Steady-state behaviour: as $t¥to¥infty$, the transient term (multiplying $e^{-kt}$ for some $k>0$) decays to zero, leaving whatever constant/steady term remains — this is the long-run circuit current or mixing concentration.
  • Solution method: always the same integrating-factor machinery from Week 7 — the challenge here is correctly setting up $V(t)$ and the outflow term from the word problem, not the algebra of solving once it's set up.

0 Mixing with Changing Volume & RL Circuits

If inflow rate $r_{in}$ and outflow rate $r_{out}$ differ, the volume at time $t$ is $V(t)=V_0+(r_{in}-r_{out})t$ — no longer constant. The outflow concentration is $¥dfrac{A(t)}{V(t)}$, so the DE becomes linear in $A$ (with a $t$-dependent coefficient), exactly the shape the integrating factor method was built for.

RL circuits. By Kirchhoff's voltage law, $L¥dfrac{dI}{dt}+RI=V(t)$ — an equation with the exact same linear structure as mixing, just with different physical labels.

Key idea

$$¥frac{dA}{dt}+¥frac{r_{out}}{V(t)}A = r_{in}¥cdot c_{in}, ¥qquad L¥frac{dI}{dt}+RI=V(t)$$

Worked example

Example 1 — constant volume (reviewed as linear). A 200L tank starts with 50g salt. Pure water flows in at 5L/min, mixture flows out at 5L/min. Find $A(t)$.

$¥dfrac{dA}{dt}=-¥dfrac{A}{40}$; $¥mu=e^{t/40}$; $A=Ce^{-t/40}$; $A(0)=50¥implies A(t)=50e^{-t/40}$.

Example 2 — changing volume, setup only. A 100L tank starts with pure water. Brine at 3g/L flows in at 4L/min; mixture flows out at 2L/min. Set up the DE for $A(t)$.

$V(t)=100+2t$. $¥dfrac{dA}{dt}=12-¥dfrac{2A}{100+2t}$, i.e. $¥dfrac{dA}{dt}+¥dfrac{2}{100+2t}A=12$ — linear, ready for the integrating factor.

Practice 1

A 100L tank starts with pure water. Brine at 3g/L flows in at 4L/min; mixture flows out at 2L/min (as in the worked example). Solve the full DE for $A(t)$.

Solution

$V(t)=100+2t$. DE: $¥dfrac{dA}{dt}+¥dfrac{2}{100+2t}A=12$, i.e. $¥dfrac{dA}{dt}+¥dfrac{1}{50+t}A=12$.

$¥mu=e^{¥int¥frac{1}{50+t}dt}=e^{¥ln(50+t)}=50+t$.

$¥dfrac{d}{dt}¥big[(50+t)A¥big]=12(50+t) ¥implies (50+t)A=6(50+t)^2+C$.

At $t=0,A=0$: $50(0)=6(2500)+C¥cdot1 ¥implies 0=15000+C ¥implies C=-15000$.

Answer: $A(t)=6(50+t)-¥dfrac{15000}{50+t}$.

Practice 2

An RL circuit has $L=2$ H, $R=10¥ ¥Omega$, connected to a constant voltage source $V=20$ V, with $I(0)=0$. Solve for $I(t)$ and find the steady-state current.

Solution

$2¥dfrac{dI}{dt}+10I=20 ¥implies ¥dfrac{dI}{dt}+5I=10$.

$¥mu=e^{5t}$; $¥dfrac{d}{dt}[e^{5t}I]=10e^{5t} ¥implies e^{5t}I=2e^{5t}+C ¥implies I=2+Ce^{-5t}$.

At $t=0,I=0$: $0=2+C ¥implies C=-2$: $I(t)=2(1-e^{-5t})$.

As $t¥to¥infty$, $e^{-5t}¥to0$, so $I¥to2$ A.

Answer: $I(t)=2(1-e^{-5t})$; steady-state current is 2 A.

Practice 3

A 500L tank starts with 100 kg of salt. Brine at 2 kg/L flows in at 5L/min; the mixture flows out at 8L/min. Set up the DE for $A(t)$, noting the tank will empty at some finite time — find that time.

Solution

$V(t)=500+(5-8)t=500-3t$. The tank empties when $V=0$: $500-3t=0 ¥implies t=¥dfrac{500}{3}¥approx166.7$ min.

DE: $¥dfrac{dA}{dt}=5(2)-8¥cdot¥dfrac{A}{500-3t}=10-¥dfrac{8A}{500-3t}$, valid for $0¥le t<¥dfrac{500}{3}$.

Answer: $¥dfrac{dA}{dt}+¥dfrac{8}{500-3t}A=10$; the model is only valid up to $t=¥dfrac{500}{3}$ min, when the tank empties.

Practice 4

An RL circuit has $L=4$ H, $R=8¥ ¥Omega$, with a time-varying voltage source $V(t)=16e^{-2t}$, and $I(0)=1$ A. Solve for $I(t)$.

Solution

$4¥dfrac{dI}{dt}+8I=16e^{-2t} ¥implies ¥dfrac{dI}{dt}+2I=4e^{-2t}$.

Practice 5

A 250L tank starts with 40 kg salt. Brine at 0.5 kg/L flows in at 3L/min; mixture flows out at 5L/min. Find $A(t)$ (the DE will be linear with a shrinking volume).

Solution

$V(t)=250-2t$. DE: $¥dfrac{dA}{dt}=3(0.5)-5¥cdot¥dfrac{A}{250-2t}=1.5-¥dfrac{5A}{250-2t}$.

Standard form: $¥dfrac{dA}{dt}+¥dfrac{5}{250-2t}A=1.5$.

$¥mu=e^{¥int¥frac{5}{250-2t}dt}$. Let $w=250-2t,dw=-2dt$: $¥int¥dfrac{5}{w}¥cdot¥left(-¥dfrac{dw}{2}¥right)=-¥dfrac52¥ln|w|$, so $¥int¥dfrac{5}{250-2t}dt=-¥dfrac52¥ln(250-2t)$ (taking $250-2t>0$).

$¥mu=e^{-¥frac52¥ln(250-2t)}=(250-2t)^{-5/2}$.

$¥dfrac{d}{dt}¥big[(250-2t)^{-5/2}A¥big]=1.5(250-2t)^{-5/2}$.

Integrating the right side (using the same $w$-substitution): $¥displaystyle¥int1.5(250-2t)^{-5/2}dt = 1.5¥cdot¥dfrac{(250-2t)^{-3/2}}{3}=0.5(250-2t)^{-3/2}+C$ (signs work out from the chain rule on $w=250-2t$).

$(250-2t)^{-5/2}A = 0.5(250-2t)^{-3/2}+C ¥implies A=0.5(250-2t)+C(250-2t)^{5/2}$.

At $t=0,A=40$: $40=0.5(250)+C(250)^{5/2} ¥implies 40=125+C(250)^{5/2} ¥implies C=¥dfrac{40-125}{250^{5/2}}=¥dfrac{-85}{250^{5/2}}$.

Answer: $A(t)=0.5(250-2t)-¥dfrac{85}{250^{5/2}}(250-2t)^{5/2}$ (a genuinely involved exam-level setup — the key skill tested is correct DE setup and integrating-factor mechanics, even when the resulting algebra is heavy).

Practice 6

An RL circuit has $L=1$ H, $R=4¥ ¥Omega$, $V(t)=e^{-t}$, $I(0)=2$. Solve for $I(t)$.

Solution

$¥dfrac{dI}{dt}+4I=e^{-t}$.

$¥mu=e^{4t}$; $¥dfrac{d}{dt}[e^{4t}I]=e^{4t}¥cdot e^{-t}=e^{3t} ¥implies e^{4t}I=¥dfrac13e^{3t}+C$.

$I=¥dfrac13e^{-t}+Ce^{-4t}$. At $t=0,I=2$: $2=¥dfrac13+C ¥implies C=¥dfrac53$.

Practice 7

Explain why the mixing-with-changing-volume DE is linear but generally NOT separable, contrasting it with the constant-volume case, using the specific form of the coefficient of $A$.

Solution

In the constant-volume case, $¥dfrac{dA}{dt}=(¥text{const})-(¥text{const})¥cdot A$, which can be separated since both sides only involve $A$ and $t$ separately once rearranged — $¥dfrac{dA}{¥text{const}-¥text{const}¥cdot A}=dt$.

With changing volume, the coefficient of $A$ is $¥dfrac{r_{out}}{V(t)}$, a genuine function of $t$ (not a constant) — the DE is $¥dfrac{dA}{dt}+f(t)A=¥text{const}$, which is linear (degree 1 in $A$) but the presence of both $A$ and an independently-varying function of $t$ multiplying it means $A$ and $t$ cannot be algebraically separated onto opposite sides of the equation.

Practice 8

A capacitor-free RL circuit reaches 90% of its steady-state current after 2 seconds, with $L=5$ H. If $R=10¥ ¥Omega$, verify this is consistent with the circuit's time constant $¥tau=L/R$.

Solution

Time constant: $¥tau=L/R=5/10=0.5$ s.

For $I(t)=I_{ss}(1-e^{-t/¥tau})$, reaching 90% means $1-e^{-t/¥tau}=0.9 ¥implies e^{-t/¥tau}=0.1 ¥implies t=¥tau¥ln10=0.5(2.3026)¥approx1.151$ s.

Answer: the circuit reaches 90% of steady-state after approximately 1.15 s, not 2 s — so the stated 2-second observation is not consistent with $¥tau=0.5$ s; either the component values or the observation must be re-checked (a good example of using the model to sanity-check reported data).

Practice 9

A tank has $V(t)=100+t$ (inflow exceeds outflow by 1 L/min), with outflow rate 3 L/min and inflow concentration 2 kg/L. Write the full standard-form linear DE for $A(t)$ (do not solve).

Solution

Rate in: $(1+3)¥times2=8$ kg/min (total inflow rate is outflow $+$ net volume increase $=3+1=4$ L/min... let's use given inflow rate directly: since $V(t)=100+t$ implies $r_{in}-r_{out}=1$ and $r_{out}=3$, so $r_{in}=4$ L/min). Rate in $=4¥times2=8$ kg/min. Rate out $=3¥cdot¥dfrac{A}{100+t}$.

Practice 10

Explain the physical meaning of the steady-state current $I=V/R$ in an RL circuit driven by a constant voltage $V$, in terms of what happens to the inductor's behaviour once the transient has died away.

Solution

An inductor opposes changes in current (via the term $L¥,dI/dt$), but once the current has settled into a constant value ($dI/dt=0$), the inductor contributes no voltage drop at all — the circuit then behaves exactly like a plain resistor connected directly to the voltage source, obeying simple Ohm's law: $V=IR ¥implies I=V/R$. The inductor's influence is entirely a transient effect, shaping how the current approaches this steady value, not what the steady value itself is.

In this block, always identify the physical meaning of each coefficient before solving (rate in/out, resistance/inductance, or long-run level).
