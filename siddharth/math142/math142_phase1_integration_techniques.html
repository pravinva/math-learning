Integration by Parts
The product rule, run in reverse
The product rule for derivatives tells you how to differentiate a product of two functions. Running that rule backwards gives a technique for integrating products that substitution alone can't handle — like $\int x\cos x\,dx$, where nothing is "the derivative of something else" in the substitution sense.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Deriving the formula. Start from the product rule: $(uv)'=u'v+uv'$. Integrate both sides with respect to $x$:

\[ uv = \int u'v\,dx + \int uv'\,dx \]

Rearranging gives the integration by parts formula. In practice it's written using $du=u'\,dx$ and $dv=v'\,dx$:

\[ \int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du \]

Choosing $u$ and $dv$. The goal is for $\int v\,du$ to be easier than the original integral. A common mnemonic for what to pick as $u$ (differentiate) is LIATE — Logarithmic, Inverse trig, Algebraic, Trigonometric, Exponential, roughly in order of preference, since each category tends to simplify (or at least not worsen) when differentiated.

Repeated application — the tabular method. When $u$ needs several rounds of differentiation before it vanishes (e.g. $u=x^2$), doing integration by parts three separate times is repetitive. The tabular method organises this into two columns — derivatives of $u$ down one side, integrals of $dv$ down the other — with alternating signs, collapsing several rounds of the formula into one table.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — basic integration by parts

Find $\int x\cos x\,dx$.

Let $u=x$ (differentiates to something simpler), $dv=\cos x\,dx$. Then $du=dx$, $v=\sin x$.

\[ \int x\cos x\,dx = x\sin x - \int \sin x\,dx = x\sin x + \cos x + C \]
ANSWER $x\sin x+\cos x+C$
Example 2 — repeated application via the tabular method

Find $\int x^2e^x\,dx$.

Tabular method: differentiate $x^2\to2x\to2\to0$; integrate $e^x\to e^x\to e^x\to e^x$; alternate signs $+,-,+$.

\[ \int x^2e^x\,dx = x^2e^x - 2xe^x + 2e^x + C \]
ANSWER $e^x(x^2-2x+2)+C$
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
If, after applying the formula once, $\int v\,du$ looks exactly as hard as the original integral (not simpler), you likely picked $u$ and $dv$ the wrong way around — swap them and try again.

Trigonometric Integrals
Products and powers of sine and cosine
Integrals like $\int \sin^3x\cos^2x\,dx$ look intimidating, but a small set of Pythagorean-identity tricks converts almost any power/product of sine and cosine into a form substitution can finish.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Odd power present. If either $\sin x$ or $\cos x$ appears to an odd power, peel off one factor to pair with $dx$, then convert the remaining even power using $\sin^2x+\cos^2x=1$ — this sets up a clean substitution.

Both powers even. Use the half-angle identities $\sin^2x=\dfrac{1-\cos2x}{2}$, $\cos^2x=\dfrac{1+\cos2x}{2}$ to reduce the powers, at the cost of a doubled angle — repeat as needed until an odd power appears or the integral becomes elementary.

Products of sines/cosines of different angles. Product-to-sum identities (e.g. $\sin A\cos B=\tfrac12[\sin(A+B)+\sin(A-B)]$) convert a product into a sum, which integrates term by term.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \sin^2x+\cos^2x=1, \qquad \sin^2x=\frac{1-\cos2x}{2}, \qquad \cos^2x=\frac{1+\cos2x}{2} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — odd power

Find $\int \sin^3x\,dx$.

\[ \int \sin^3x\,dx = \int \sin x(1-\cos^2x)\,dx \]

Let $u=\cos x$, $du=-\sin x\,dx$.

\[ = -\int(1-u^2)\,du = -u+\frac{u^3}{3}+C = -\cos x+\frac{\cos^3x}{3}+C \]
ANSWER $-\cos x+\dfrac{\cos^3x}{3}+C$
Example 2 — both powers even

Find $\int \sin^2x\,dx$.

\[ \int \frac{1-\cos2x}{2}\,dx = \frac{x}{2}-\frac{\sin2x}{4}+C \]
ANSWER $\dfrac{x}{2}-\dfrac{\sin2x}{4}+C$
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
Always check for an odd power first — that route is almost always shorter than the half-angle route, since it avoids introducing a doubled angle.

Trigonometric Substitution
Using trig identities to simplify square roots
Expressions like $\sqrt{a^2-x^2}$ resist ordinary substitution because there's no clean "derivative of the inside" sitting in the integral. Trig substitution sidesteps this by replacing $x$ itself with a trig function, chosen specifically so the square root simplifies via a Pythagorean identity.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

The three standard forms. Each pattern in the integrand suggests a specific substitution, chosen so that the corresponding Pythagorean identity collapses the square root:

Expression containsSubstituteIdentity used
$\sqrt{a^2-x^2}$$x=a\sin\theta$$1-\sin^2\theta=\cos^2\theta$
$\sqrt{a^2+x^2}$$x=a\tan\theta$$1+\tan^2\theta=\sec^2\theta$
$\sqrt{x^2-a^2}$$x=a\sec\theta$$\sec^2\theta-1=\tan^2\theta$

After integrating. The result is in terms of $\theta$ — convert back to $x$ using the original substitution (often with a right-triangle diagram labelled from $x=a\sin\theta$ etc., reading off the other trig ratios in terms of $x$).

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \sqrt{a^2-x^2}: x=a\sin\theta \qquad \sqrt{a^2+x^2}: x=a\tan\theta \qquad \sqrt{x^2-a^2}: x=a\sec\theta \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — the $\sqrt{a^2-x^2}$ case

Find $\int \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{4-x^2}}\,dx$.

Let $x=2\sin\theta$, $dx=2\cos\theta\,d\theta$. Then $\sqrt{4-x^2}=\sqrt{4-4\sin^2\theta}=2\cos\theta$.

\[ \int \frac{2\cos\theta}{2\cos\theta}\,d\theta = \int d\theta = \theta+C = \arcsin\left(\frac{x}{2}\right)+C \]
ANSWER $\arcsin\left(\dfrac{x}{2}\right)+C$ (matches the standard inverse-trig integral form)
Example 2 — the $\sqrt{a^2+x^2}$ case

Find $\int \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{9+x^2}}\,dx$.

Let $x=3\tan\theta$, $dx=3\sec^2\theta\,d\theta$. Then $\sqrt{9+x^2}=3\sec\theta$.

\[ \int \frac{3\sec^2\theta}{3\sec\theta}\,d\theta = \int \sec\theta\,d\theta = \ln|\sec\theta+\tan\theta|+C \]

From $x=3\tan\theta$: $\tan\theta=x/3$, $\sec\theta=\sqrt{9+x^2}/3$.

ANSWER $\ln\left|\dfrac{\sqrt{9+x^2}+x}{3}\right|+C$
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
These three substitutions each turn a square root into a single trig function via one specific Pythagorean identity — memorising which identity matches which square root pattern is faster than re-deriving the choice each time.

Partial Fractions — Linear Factors
Splitting rational functions with distinct linear denominators
A rational function like $\dfrac{1}{x^2-1}$ can't be integrated directly, but it can be rewritten as a sum of simpler fractions, each with a single linear factor in the denominator — and each of those integrates immediately to a logarithm.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

The setup. For a rational function with a denominator that factors into distinct linear factors, e.g. $\dfrac{P(x)}{(x-a)(x-b)}$, propose a decomposition:

\[ \frac{P(x)}{(x-a)(x-b)} = \frac{A}{x-a} + \frac{B}{x-b} \]

Finding $A$ and $B$. Multiply both sides by the original denominator to clear fractions, then either (a) substitute convenient values of $x$ (like $x=a$, which makes the $B$-term vanish, isolating $A$ directly), or (b) expand and match coefficients of like powers of $x$.

Why it's always possible. This is guaranteed by a theorem from algebra: any proper rational function (numerator degree less than denominator degree) can be written this way, uniquely, once the denominator is fully factored — you're not guessing that a decomposition exists, only solving for its specific coefficients.

Improper fractions first. If the numerator's degree is $\ge$ the denominator's, do polynomial long division first, decomposing only the proper remainder.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \frac{P(x)}{(x-a)(x-b)} = \frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}, \qquad \int \frac{A}{x-a}\,dx = A\ln|x-a|+C \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — cover-up method

Find $\int \dfrac{1}{x^2-1}\,dx$.

\[ \frac{1}{(x-1)(x+1)} = \frac{A}{x-1}+\frac{B}{x+1} \]

Multiply by $(x-1)(x+1)$: $1=A(x+1)+B(x-1)$. Let $x=1$: $1=2A\implies A=\tfrac12$. Let $x=-1$: $1=-2B\implies B=-\tfrac12$.

\[ \int\left(\frac{1/2}{x-1}-\frac{1/2}{x+1}\right)dx = \frac12\ln|x-1|-\frac12\ln|x+1|+C \]
ANSWER $\dfrac12\ln\left|\dfrac{x-1}{x+1}\right|+C$
Example 2 — three distinct linear factors

Decompose $\dfrac{2x+1}{x(x-1)(x+2)}$.

$2x+1=A(x-1)(x+2)+Bx(x+2)+Cx(x-1)$. At $x=0$: $1=-2A\implies A=-\tfrac12$. At $x=1$: $3=3B\implies B=1$. At $x=-2$: $-3=6C\implies C=-\tfrac12$.

ANSWER $-\dfrac{1/2}{x}+\dfrac{1}{x-1}-\dfrac{1/2}{x+2}$
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
The "cover-up" trick (substituting the root of one factor to instantly isolate its coefficient) only works cleanly for distinct linear factors — repeated or irreducible-quadratic factors (next topic) need the full coefficient-matching approach instead.

Partial Fractions — Repeated & Irreducible Quadratic Factors
Extending the decomposition to harder denominator types
Distinct linear factors were the simplest case. When a factor repeats, or when the denominator contains a quadratic that can't be factored over the reals, the decomposition needs extra terms — built by the same coefficient-matching idea, just with a richer set of unknowns.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Repeated linear factors. For $(x-a)^n$ in the denominator, include a term for every power from $1$ to $n$:

\[ \frac{P(x)}{(x-a)^n} \to \frac{A_1}{x-a}+\frac{A_2}{(x-a)^2}+\cdots+\frac{A_n}{(x-a)^n} \]

This is needed because the cover-up trick only pins down $A_n$ directly (substituting $x=a$ after multiplying through) — the other coefficients require comparing further terms, since a single fraction $\frac{A}{x-a}$ alone can't reproduce all the necessary behaviour near $x=a$.

Irreducible quadratic factors. A quadratic $ax^2+bx+c$ that doesn't factor over the reals (negative discriminant) gets a linear numerator, not just a constant:

\[ \frac{P(x)}{(x^2+bx+c)} \to \frac{Bx+C}{x^2+bx+c} \]

A constant numerator alone can't produce the $x$-term needed when you multiply through and match coefficients — the linear numerator provides that missing flexibility.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ (x-a)^n \to \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{A_k}{(x-a)^k}, \qquad \text{irreducible } x^2+bx+c \to \frac{Bx+C}{x^2+bx+c} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — repeated linear factor

Decompose $\dfrac{3x-1}{(x-1)^2}$.

\[ \frac{3x-1}{(x-1)^2} = \frac{A}{x-1}+\frac{B}{(x-1)^2} \]

$3x-1=A(x-1)+B$. At $x=1$: $2=B$. Match $x$-coefficients: $3=A$.

ANSWER $\dfrac{3}{x-1}+\dfrac{2}{(x-1)^2}$
Example 2 — irreducible quadratic factor

Decompose $\dfrac{x+1}{x(x^2+1)}$.

\[ \frac{x+1}{x(x^2+1)} = \frac{A}{x}+\frac{Bx+C}{x^2+1} \]

$x+1=A(x^2+1)+(Bx+C)x$. At $x=0$: $1=A$. Match $x^2$: $0=A+B\implies B=-1$. Match $x$: $1=C$.

ANSWER $\dfrac{1}{x}+\dfrac{-x+1}{x^2+1}$
Note Study notes ▶
Watch out
The $\dfrac{Bx+C}{x^2+bx+c}$ term typically needs splitting into two integrals once you integrate — one part matching $\int\dfrac{2x+b}{x^2+bx+c}dx$ (a logarithm, via substitution), the other needing the arctan form from the inverse trig integrals topic.

Improper Integrals
Infinite limits and discontinuous integrands, via limits
Ordinary definite integrals assume a finite interval and a well-behaved (bounded, continuous) integrand. When either assumption fails — an infinite limit of integration, or a vertical asymptote inside the interval — the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus can't be applied directly. Improper integrals patch this by wrapping the troublesome point in a limit.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Infinite limits of integration. Replace the infinite bound with a finite variable, integrate normally, then take the limit as that variable heads to infinity:

\[ \int_a^\infty f(x)\,dx = \lim_{t\to\infty}\int_a^t f(x)\,dx \]

If the limit exists (is a finite number), the improper integral converges to that value; if the limit is infinite or doesn't exist, it diverges.

Discontinuous integrands. If $f$ has an infinite discontinuity at some point $c$ within $[a,b]$, split the integral at $c$ and wrap each piece in a one-sided limit approaching $c$:

\[ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx = \lim_{t\to c^-}\int_a^t f(x)\,dx + \lim_{t\to c^+}\int_t^b f(x)\,dx \]

Both pieces must converge individually for the whole integral to converge.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \int_a^\infty f(x)\,dx = \lim_{t\to\infty}\int_a^t f(x)\,dx, \qquad \text{converges if this limit is finite} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — infinite limit, converges

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_1^\infty \dfrac{1}{x^2}\,dx$.

\[ \lim_{t\to\infty}\int_1^t x^{-2}\,dx = \lim_{t\to\infty}\left[-\frac1x\right]_1^t = \lim_{t\to\infty}\left(-\frac1t+1\right)=1 \]
ANSWER Converges to $1$
Example 2 — discontinuous integrand

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^1 \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{x}}\,dx$ (discontinuous at $x=0$).

\[ \lim_{t\to0^+}\int_t^1 x^{-1/2}\,dx = \lim_{t\to0^+}\left[2\sqrt{x}\right]_t^1 = \lim_{t\to0^+}(2-2\sqrt t)=2 \]
ANSWER Converges to $2$
Note Study notes ▶
Uni Bridge
Convergence/divergence of improper integrals is the direct analogue of convergence/divergence of series (Week 11), and several series convergence tests (the integral test in particular) work by comparing a series directly to an improper integral.

Numerical Integration — Trapezoidal Rule & Error Estimation
Approximating a definite integral when antiderivatives are hard (or impossible) to find
Not every integrand has an elementary antiderivative (e.g. $e^{-x^2}$). Numerical integration sidesteps this entirely by approximating the area under the curve directly — the trapezoidal rule does this by replacing each thin vertical strip's top edge with a straight line instead of the true curve.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Building the rule. Split $[a,b]$ into $n$ equal subintervals of width $h=\dfrac{b-a}{n}$, with endpoints $x_0,x_1,\dots,x_n$. On each subinterval, approximate the area under the curve by the area of a trapezoid (rather than a rectangle, as in a basic Riemann sum) — using the straight line connecting $(x_{i-1},f(x_{i-1}))$ and $(x_i,f(x_i))$ as the top edge. A single trapezoid's area is $\dfrac{h}{2}\big(f(x_{i-1})+f(x_i)\big)$.

Summing over all $n$ trapezoids, each interior function value gets counted twice (once as the right edge of one trapezoid, once as the left edge of the next), while the two endpoints are counted once each:

\[ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx \approx \frac{h}{2}\Big[f(x_0)+2f(x_1)+2f(x_2)+\cdots+2f(x_{n-1})+f(x_n)\Big] \]

Error bound. Since each trapezoid replaces a curved top with a straight line, the error depends on how much the function curves — measured by its second derivative:

\[ |E_T| \le \frac{(b-a)h^2}{12}\max|f''(x)| \]
Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ T_n = \frac{h}{2}\big[f(x_0)+2f(x_1)+\cdots+2f(x_{n-1})+f(x_n)\big], \qquad h=\frac{b-a}{n} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — apply the trapezoidal rule

Estimate $\displaystyle\int_0^2 x^2\,dx$ using $n=4$ trapezoids.

$h=0.5$; $x_0,\dots,x_4=0,0.5,1,1.5,2$; $f(x)=x^2$ gives $0,0.25,1,2.25,4$.

\[ T_4 = \frac{0.5}{2}\big[0+2(0.25)+2(1)+2(2.25)+4\big] = 0.25(11)=2.75 \]
ANSWER $T_4=2.75$ (true value $\int_0^2x^2dx=\tfrac83\approx2.667$)
Example 2 — error bound

Bound the error for Example 1, given $f''(x)=2$ throughout $[0,2]$.

\[ |E_T| \le \frac{(2-0)(0.5)^2}{12}(2) = \frac{2(0.25)(2)}{12}=\frac{1}{12}\approx0.083 \]
ANSWER $|E_T|\le\tfrac{1}{12}\approx0.083$ (actual error was $2.75-2.667=0.083$ — matches the bound almost exactly)
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
The coefficient pattern $1,2,2,\dots,2,1$ comes directly from each interior point being shared by two adjacent trapezoids — it's worth deriving this once by hand rather than memorising it as an arbitrary rule.

Numerical Integration — Simpson's Rule & Error Estimation
Fitting parabolas instead of straight lines, for a dramatically better approximation
The trapezoidal rule fits a straight line across each pair of points — but a parabola can match the curve's bending too, using barely more information. Simpson's Rule exploits this by fitting a parabola through each set of three consecutive points, and the resulting formula is often far more accurate for the same number of subintervals.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Building the rule. Take subintervals in pairs. Over each pair (three consecutive points $x_{i-1},x_i,x_{i+1}$), fit the unique parabola through those three points, and use its area as the approximation instead of two trapezoids. This requires $n$ to be even (so the points pair up completely).

The resulting formula. The parabola-fitting calculation (carried out once, in general) produces a specific weighting pattern — $1,4,2,4,2,\dots,4,1$ — where odd-indexed points get weight $4$ and even-indexed interior points get weight $2$:

\[ S_n = \frac{h}{3}\Big[f(x_0)+4f(x_1)+2f(x_2)+4f(x_3)+\cdots+4f(x_{n-1})+f(x_n)\Big] \]

Error bound. Since Simpson's Rule matches any parabola exactly (zero error), the error now depends on the fourth derivative — how much the function deviates from parabolic behaviour, not just linear behaviour:

\[ |E_S| \le \frac{(b-a)h^4}{180}\max|f^{(4)}(x)| \]

Since the error bound uses $h^4$ rather than $h^2$, halving $h$ shrinks the error by a factor of $16$, not just $4$ — this is why Simpson's Rule usually converges far faster than the trapezoidal rule.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ S_n = \frac{h}{3}\big[f(x_0)+4f(x_1)+2f(x_2)+\cdots+4f(x_{n-1})+f(x_n)\big], \qquad n \text{ even} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — apply Simpson's Rule

Estimate $\displaystyle\int_0^2 x^2\,dx$ using Simpson's Rule with $n=4$ (same data as the trapezoidal example).

$h=0.5$; $f$-values: $0,0.25,1,2.25,4$; weights: $1,4,2,4,1$.

\[ S_4 = \frac{0.5}{3}\big[0+4(0.25)+2(1)+4(2.25)+4\big] = \frac{0.5}{3}(16)=\frac{8}{3} \]
ANSWER $S_4=\dfrac83$ — exactly matches the true value, since $x^2$ is itself a parabola
Example 2 — why Simpson's Rule was exact here

Explain why $S_4$ gave the exact answer in Example 1, while $T_4$ (trapezoidal) did not.

Simpson's Rule fits a parabola through each set of three points. Since $f(x)=x^2$ is a parabola, the fitted parabola coincides exactly with the true curve on every subinterval — there's no approximation error to speak of, by construction.

ANSWER Simpson's Rule is exact for any polynomial of degree $\le3$, which includes all parabolas
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
Always double-check that $n$ is even before applying Simpson's Rule — an odd $n$ leaves one interval unpaired, and the standard formula simply doesn't apply.

Polar Coordinates — Plotting & Conversion
A second coordinate system, built from distance and angle instead of horizontal/vertical position
Cartesian coordinates locate a point by how far across and up. Polar coordinates locate the same point a completely different way — by how far from the origin, and in what direction — and some curves (spirals, roses, cardioids) become far simpler to describe this way.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

The idea. A point's polar coordinates $(r,\theta)$ record: $r$, the distance from the origin (the "pole"), and $\theta$, the angle measured counterclockwise from the positive $x$-axis (the "polar axis"). Plotting $(r,\theta)$ means: face along angle $\theta$, then walk $r$ units in that direction.

Deriving the conversion formulas. Drop a perpendicular from the point down to the $x$-axis, forming a right triangle with hypotenuse $r$ and angle $\theta$ at the origin — ordinary right-triangle trig gives:

\[ x=r\cos\theta, \qquad y=r\sin\theta \]

Going the other way (Cartesian to polar), Pythagoras gives $r$, and the angle comes from $\tan\theta=y/x$ (with quadrant care, exactly as with the argument of a complex number):

\[ r=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}, \qquad \tan\theta=\frac{y}{x} \]

Non-uniqueness. Unlike Cartesian coordinates, a single point has infinitely many valid polar representations — adding $2\pi$ to $\theta$ returns to the same direction, and allowing $r<0$ points in the opposite direction to $\theta$, landing on the same point as a positive $r$ with angle $\theta+\pi$.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ x=r\cos\theta, \quad y=r\sin\theta, \qquad r^2=x^2+y^2, \quad \tan\theta=\frac{y}{x} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — polar to Cartesian

Convert $\left(4,\dfrac{\pi}{3}\right)$ to Cartesian coordinates.

\[ x=4\cos\frac{\pi}{3}=4\left(\frac12\right)=2, \qquad y=4\sin\frac{\pi}{3}=4\left(\frac{\sqrt3}{2}\right)=2\sqrt3 \]
ANSWER $(2,2\sqrt3)$
Example 2 — Cartesian to polar

Convert $(-1,1)$ to polar coordinates (with $0\le\theta<2\pi$).

\[ r=\sqrt{1+1}=\sqrt2, \qquad \tan\theta=\frac{1}{-1}=-1 \]

Reference angle $\pi/4$; since $x<0,y>0$, we're in the second quadrant: $\theta=\pi-\pi/4=3\pi/4$.

ANSWER $\left(\sqrt2,\dfrac{3\pi}{4}\right)$
Note Study notes ▶
Uni Bridge
These conversion formulas are identical in spirit to converting a complex number between Cartesian form $a+bi$ and polar form $r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)$ — same right-triangle trig, same quadrant-checking process for the angle.

Polar Coordinates — Graphing Curves & Symmetry
Cardioids, roses, limaçons, and how to sketch them efficiently
A polar equation $r=f(\theta)$ describes a curve by giving the distance from the origin as a function of angle — plotting a table of $(\theta,r)$ values and connecting them reveals shapes (hearts, flowers, spirals) that would be far messier to describe with a single Cartesian equation $y=f(x)$.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Building a sketch from a table. Choose several values of $\theta$ (typically multiples of $\pi/6$ or $\pi/4$), compute $r$ at each, then plot and connect. This always works but is slow — recognising standard families speeds things up dramatically.

Areas — Cartesian Extension & Polar Regions
Area between curves revisited, then a genuinely new formula for polar regions
Cartesian area-between-curves used thin vertical rectangles. A polar region isn't naturally swept out by rectangles at all — it's swept out by thin circular sectors, radiating from the origin as $\theta$ varies, which leads to a completely different-looking (but equally derivable) area formula.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Cartesian area, extended. The area-between-curves formula from MATH141 ($A=\int_a^b(f-g)dx$) extends naturally to regions bounded by more than two curves, or requiring integration with respect to $y$ instead of $x$ when curves are more naturally expressed as $x=g(y)$ — same idea, rotated $90°$.

Building the polar area formula. A thin sector of a circle with radius $r$ and angle $d\theta$ has area $\dfrac12r^2\,d\theta$ (this is just the "area of a circle" formula $\pi r^2$, scaled down: a full circle is angle $2\pi$, so a slice of angle $d\theta$ is the fraction $\dfrac{d\theta}{2\pi}$ of the full area $\pi r^2$, giving $\dfrac{d\theta}{2\pi}\cdot\pi r^2=\dfrac12r^2d\theta$). Summing (integrating) these thin sectors from $\theta=\alpha$ to $\theta=\beta$ gives the total area swept out by the polar curve:

\[ A = \frac12\int_\alpha^\beta r^2\,d\theta \]

Area between two polar curves. Exactly the same "outer minus inner" idea as Cartesian area-between-curves, just with $r^2$ playing the role of height: $A=\dfrac12\int_\alpha^\beta(r_{\text{outer}}^2-r_{\text{inner}}^2)\,d\theta$.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ A = \frac12\int_\alpha^\beta r^2\,d\theta \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — area enclosed by a cardioid

Find the area enclosed by $r=1+\cos\theta$.

The full cardioid is traced as $\theta$ goes from $0$ to $2\pi$.

\[ A = \frac12\int_0^{2\pi}(1+\cos\theta)^2\,d\theta = \frac12\int_0^{2\pi}\left(1+2\cos\theta+\cos^2\theta\right)d\theta \]

Using $\cos^2\theta=\tfrac{1+\cos2\theta}{2}$ from the trig integrals topic:

\[ = \frac12\left[\theta+2\sin\theta+\frac{\theta}{2}+\frac{\sin2\theta}{4}\right]_0^{2\pi} = \frac12(2\pi+\pi) = \frac{3\pi}{2} \]
ANSWER $\dfrac{3\pi}{2}$
Example 2 — area of one petal of a rose

Find the area of one petal of $r=\cos(2\theta)$.

One petal is traced as $\theta$ ranges over an interval where $r\ge0$ starts and ends at $0$: $-\pi/4\le\theta\le\pi/4$.

\[ A = \frac12\int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\cos^2(2\theta)\,d\theta = \frac12\left[\frac{\theta}{2}+\frac{\sin4\theta}{8}\right]_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4} = \frac{\pi}{8} \]
ANSWER $\dfrac{\pi}{8}$
Note Study notes ▶
Watch out
Always identify the correct $\theta$-interval that traces the region exactly once before integrating — tracing the same petal or loop twice (or including an extra loop) silently doubles or corrupts the area, and the formula itself won't warn you.

Volumes of Revolution — Disk/Washer & Shell Methods
Two different ways to slice a solid of revolution
Rotating a 2D region around an axis sweeps out a 3D solid. There are two natural ways to slice that solid into simple pieces to sum up its volume — thin disks/washers perpendicular to the axis, or thin cylindrical shells parallel to it — and the right choice depends entirely on which slicing direction gives simpler cross-sections for the specific region.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Disk/washer method — slicing perpendicular to the axis. Rotating the region under $y=f(x)$ (from $x=a$ to $b$) about the $x$-axis sweeps out disks of radius $f(x)$ and thickness $dx$. Each disk's volume is $\pi[f(x)]^2\,dx$ (a cylinder's volume, $\pi r^2 h$, with $h=dx$); summing (integrating) gives:

\[ V = \pi\int_a^b [f(x)]^2\,dx \]

If the region is between two curves (an inner and outer radius), each slice is a washer (a disk with a hole) rather than a solid disk: $V=\pi\int_a^b\big([R(x)]^2-[r(x)]^2\big)dx$.

Shell method — slicing parallel to the axis. Sometimes solving for $x$ in terms of $y$ (needed for the disk method around a vertical axis) is awkward. The shell method instead sums up thin cylindrical shells at each $x$, each with radius $x$, height $f(x)$, and thickness $dx$ — "unrolled," a thin shell is approximately a rectangular slab of area (circumference $\times$ height) $=2\pi x\cdot f(x)$, times thickness $dx$:

\[ V = 2\pi\int_a^b x\,f(x)\,dx \]

Choosing a method. Pick whichever method avoids having to solve the boundary curve for the "wrong" variable — if the region is naturally $y=f(x)$ and you're rotating about the $y$-axis, shells (which keep everything in terms of $x$) are usually easier than washers (which would need $x=g(y)$).

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \text{Disk/washer: } V=\pi\int_a^b\big([R(x)]^2-[r(x)]^2\big)dx \qquad \text{Shell: } V=2\pi\int_a^b x\,f(x)\,dx \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — disk method

Find the volume when the region under $y=\sqrt{x}$, $0\le x\le4$, is rotated about the $x$-axis.

\[ V = \pi\int_0^4 (\sqrt{x})^2\,dx = \pi\int_0^4 x\,dx = \pi\left[\frac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^4 = 8\pi \]
ANSWER $8\pi$
Example 2 — shell method

Find the volume when the region under $y=x^2$, $0\le x\le2$, is rotated about the $y$-axis.

\[ V = 2\pi\int_0^2 x\cdot x^2\,dx = 2\pi\int_0^2 x^3\,dx = 2\pi\left[\frac{x^4}{4}\right]_0^2 = 8\pi \]
ANSWER $8\pi$ (shells were far more direct here than solving $y=x^2$ for $x$ and using washers)
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
Sketch the region and the axis of rotation before choosing a method — the fastest way to decide between disk/washer and shells is visualising which slicing direction (perpendicular or parallel to the axis) gives cross-sections you can already describe in terms of the variable you have.

Arc Length
Measuring the true length of a curve, not just the width of its interval
The straight-line distance formula from MATH141 measures the distance between two points — but a curved path between them is longer than that straight line. Arc length adapts the same Pythagoras idea to an infinite sum of infinitesimally short straight segments that hug the curve.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Building the formula. Approximate the curve $y=f(x)$ between $x$ and $x+dx$ by the straight segment connecting $(x,f(x))$ to $(x+dx,f(x+dx))$. By Pythagoras, that tiny segment's length is $\sqrt{dx^2+dy^2}$. Factor out $dx$:

\[ \sqrt{dx^2+dy^2} = \sqrt{1+\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2}\,dx \]

Summing (integrating) these tiny segments over the whole curve gives the total arc length:

\[ L = \int_a^b \sqrt{1+[f'(x)]^2}\,dx \]

Connection to earlier topics. These integrals frequently contain a square root of a sum of squares — precisely the pattern that trig substitution (this phase, earlier) was built to handle. Arc length problems are a natural place that technique gets used in practice, not just as an abstract exercise.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ L = \int_a^b \sqrt{1+[f'(x)]^2}\,dx \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — arc length of a simple curve

Find the arc length of $y=\dfrac{2}{3}x^{3/2}$ from $x=0$ to $x=3$.

\[ f'(x)=x^{1/2}, \qquad 1+[f'(x)]^2=1+x \]
\[ L = \int_0^3 \sqrt{1+x}\,dx = \left[\frac{2}{3}(1+x)^{3/2}\right]_0^3 = \frac23\big(8-1\big) = \frac{14}{3} \]
ANSWER $\dfrac{14}{3}$
Example 2 — set up an arc length integral needing trig substitution

Set up (do not evaluate) the arc length of $y=x^2$ from $x=0$ to $x=1$, and identify the substitution needed.

\[ f'(x)=2x, \qquad L = \int_0^1 \sqrt{1+4x^2}\,dx \]

This matches the $\sqrt{a^2+x^2}$ pattern (with $a=\tfrac12$ after factoring) from the trig substitution topic — it would be solved with $2x=\tan\theta$.

ANSWER $\int_0^1\sqrt{1+4x^2}\,dx$, solvable via $2x=\tan\theta$
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
Arc length integrals are notorious for being simple to set up but hard to evaluate by hand — recognising when a problem is deliberately constructed to simplify nicely (as in Example 1) versus when it genuinely needs trig substitution (Example 2) will save you time figuring out which tool to reach for.
