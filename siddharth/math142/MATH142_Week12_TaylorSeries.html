0 Power Series & Taylor Polynomials

A power series centred at $a$ has the form $\sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n(x-a)^n$. For each fixed $x$, this becomes an ordinary numerical series, so the Ratio Test can be applied directly (treating $x$ as a parameter) to find the values of $x$ for which the series converges — producing an interval with radius of convergence $R$.

Building a Taylor polynomial. Demanding $P(a)=f(a),P'(a)=f'(a),\dots$ up to degree $n$, and using the fact that the $k$-th derivative of $(x-a)^k$ at $x=a$ is $k!$ (with all lower-degree terms vanishing), gives $P_n(x)=\sum_{k=0}^n\dfrac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k$ — the polynomial that best matches $f$'s value and its first $n$ derivatives at $x=a$.

Key idea

$$P_n(x) = \sum_{k=0}^n \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k$$

Worked example

Example 1 — radius of convergence. Find $R$ for $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{x^n}{n!}$.

$\rho=\lim\left|\dfrac{x^{n+1}/(n+1)!}{x^n/n!}\right|=\lim\dfrac{|x|}{n+1}=0<1$ for every $x$: $R=\infty$.

Example 2 — build $P_3(x)$. Find $P_3(x)$ for $f(x)=\cos x$ at $a=0$.

$f(0)=1,f'(0)=0,f''(0)=-1,f'''(0)=0$: $P_3(x)=1-\dfrac{x^2}{2}$.

Practice 1

Find the radius of convergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(x-2)^n}{n^2+1}$.

Solution

$\rho=\lim\left|\dfrac{(x-2)^{n+1}/((n+1)^2+1)}{(x-2)^n/(n^2+1)}\right|=|x-2|\lim\dfrac{n^2+1}{(n+1)^2+1}=|x-2|(1)$.

Require $|x-2|<1$: $R=1$ (centred at $a=2$).

Practice 2

Find the radius of convergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty n!\,(x+1)^n$.

Solution

$\rho=\lim\left|\dfrac{(n+1)!(x+1)^{n+1}}{n!(x+1)^n}\right|=\lim(n+1)|x+1|=\infty$ for any $x\ne-1$.

Converges only at $x=-1$: $R=0$.

Practice 3

Find $P_2(x)$ for $f(x)=\tan x$ centred at $a=0$.

Solution

$f(0)=0$; $f'(x)=\sec^2x\implies f'(0)=1$; $f''(x)=2\sec^2x\tan x\implies f''(0)=0$.

Answer: $P_2(x)=x$ (the quadratic term vanishes since $\tan x$ is an odd function).

Practice 4

Find $P_3(x)$ for $f(x)=e^{-x}$ centred at $a=0$.

Solution

$f^{(k)}(x)=(-1)^ke^{-x}$, so $f^{(k)}(0)=(-1)^k$.

Answer: $P_3(x)=1-x+\dfrac{x^2}{2}-\dfrac{x^3}{6}$.

Practice 5

Find $P_2(x)$ for $f(x)=\sqrt{x}$ centred at $a=4$.

Solution

$f(4)=2$; $f'(x)=\tfrac12x^{-1/2}\implies f'(4)=\tfrac14$; $f''(x)=-\tfrac14x^{-3/2}\implies f''(4)=-\tfrac{1}{32}$.

Answer: $P_2(x)=2+\dfrac14(x-4)-\dfrac{1}{64}(x-4)^2$.

Practice 6

Find the radius of convergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{4^n\,n}$.

Solution

$\rho=\lim\left|\dfrac{x^{2n+2}/(4^{n+1}(n+1))}{x^{2n}/(4^nn)}\right|=\dfrac{x^2}{4}\lim\dfrac{n}{n+1}=\dfrac{x^2}{4}$.

Require $\dfrac{x^2}{4}<1\implies x^2<4\implies|x|<2$: $R=2$.

Practice 7

Find $P_4(x)$ for $f(x)=\ln(1+x)$ centred at $a=0$.

Solution

$f(0)=0$; $f'(x)=(1+x)^{-1}\implies f'(0)=1$; $f''(x)=-(1+x)^{-2}\implies f''(0)=-1$; $f'''(x)=2(1+x)^{-3}\implies f'''(0)=2$; $f^{(4)}(x)=-6(1+x)^{-4}\implies f^{(4)}(0)=-6$.

Answer: $P_4(x)=x-\dfrac{x^2}{2}+\dfrac{2x^3}{6}-\dfrac{6x^4}{24}=x-\dfrac{x^2}{2}+\dfrac{x^3}{3}-\dfrac{x^4}{4}$.

Practice 8

Find the interval of convergence (radius AND endpoints checked) for $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{x^n}{n}$.

Solution

$\rho=\lim\left|\dfrac{x^{n+1}/(n+1)}{x^n/n}\right|=|x|\lim\dfrac{n}{n+1}=|x|$. Require $|x|<1$: $R=1$.

Check endpoints: at $x=1$: $\sum1/n$, the harmonic series — diverges. At $x=-1$: $\sum(-1)^n/n$ — this is an alternating series with terms $\to0$ decreasingly, so it converges (conditionally).

Answer: interval of convergence is $[-1,1)$ — radius $R=1$, but only the left endpoint is included.

Practice 9

Explain why a Taylor polynomial $P_n(x)$ of degree $n$ requires exactly $n+1$ pieces of information (the value and first $n$ derivatives at $x=a$) to be uniquely determined, connecting this to the number of unknown coefficients in a general degree-$n$ polynomial.

Solution

A general polynomial of degree $n$ has the form $c_0+c_1(x-a)+c_2(x-a)^2+\cdots+c_n(x-a)^n$ — exactly $n+1$ unknown coefficients $c_0,\dots,c_n$. Demanding that the polynomial and its first $n$ derivatives all match $f$'s corresponding values at $x=a$ provides exactly $n+1$ independent equations (since evaluating the $k$-th derivative at $x=a$ isolates $c_k$ alone, thanks to the vanishing of lower-order terms' higher derivatives and the not-yet-existing higher-order terms) — matching the $n+1$ unknowns exactly, guaranteeing a unique solution.

Practice 10

Find the radius of convergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{2^n(x-1)^n}{n+1}$.

Solution

$\rho=\lim\left|\dfrac{2^{n+1}(x-1)^{n+1}/(n+2)}{2^n(x-1)^n/(n+1)}\right|=2|x-1|\lim\dfrac{n+1}{n+2}=2|x-1|$.

Require $2|x-1|<1\implies|x-1|<\tfrac12$: $R=\dfrac12$ (centred at $a=1$).

1 Taylor & Maclaurin Series — Common Expansions & Applications

Letting the Taylor polynomial's degree run to infinity gives the Taylor series, which often reproduces a function exactly. When centred at $a=0$, it's called a Maclaurin series.

Building new series efficiently. Substituting into a known series (rather than computing derivatives from scratch) is almost always faster and less error-prone — memorise the handful of essential expansions and build new ones via substitution, multiplication, or term-by-term differentiation/integration.

Error/approximation. For alternating series (like $\sin x$'s expansion), truncating at degree $n$ leaves an error bounded by the size of the first omitted term.

Key idea

$$e^x = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{x^n}{n!}, \quad \sin x = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^nx^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}, \quad \cos x = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^nx^{2n}}{(2n)!}$$

Worked example

Example 1 — approximate. Estimate $\sin(0.1)$ using the first two nonzero terms of its Maclaurin series.

$\sin x\approx x-\dfrac{x^3}{6}$: $\sin(0.1)\approx0.1-\dfrac{0.001}{6}\approx0.099833$.

Example 2 — build from a known series. Find the Maclaurin series for $e^{-x^2}$.

Substitute $-x^2$ into $e^x=\sum x^n/n!$: $e^{-x^2}=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\dfrac{(-1)^nx^{2n}}{n!}$.

Practice 11

Find the Maclaurin series for $\cos(x^2)$ using the known series for $\cos x$.

Solution

Substitute $x^2$ for $x$ in $\cos x=\sum\dfrac{(-1)^nx^{2n}}{(2n)!}$:

Answer: $\cos(x^2)=\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{(-1)^nx^{4n}}{(2n)!}$.

Practice 12

Find the Maclaurin series for $x^2e^x$ using the known series for $e^x$.

Solution

Multiply every term of $e^x=\sum x^n/n!$ by $x^2$:

Answer: $x^2e^x=\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{x^{n+2}}{n!}$.

Practice 13

Estimate $\cos(0.3)$ using the first three terms of its Maclaurin series, and compare with the true value $\approx0.9553$.

Solution

$\cos x\approx1-\dfrac{x^2}{2}+\dfrac{x^4}{24}$: $\cos(0.3)\approx1-\dfrac{0.09}{2}+\dfrac{0.0081}{24}=1-0.045+0.0003375\approx0.9553$.

Answer: $\approx0.9553$, matching the true value to 4 decimal places.

Practice 14

Find the Maclaurin series for $\arctan(x)$ by integrating the known series for $\dfrac{1}{1+x^2}$ term by term (obtained by substituting $-x^2$ into $\dfrac{1}{1-x}=\sum x^n$).

Solution

$\dfrac{1}{1+x^2}=\dfrac{1}{1-(-x^2)}=\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty(-x^2)^n=\sum_{n=0}^\infty(-1)^nx^{2n}$.

Integrating term by term (since $\arctan x=\int_0^x\frac{1}{1+t^2}dt$): $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{(-1)^nx^{2n+1}}{2n+1}$.

Answer: $\arctan x=\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{(-1)^nx^{2n+1}}{2n+1}=x-\frac{x^3}{3}+\frac{x^5}{5}-\cdots$.

Practice 15

Using the alternating-series error bound, find how many terms of the Maclaurin series for $e^{-1}$ (i.e. $\sum(-1)^n/n!$) are needed to guarantee an error under $0.0001$.

Solution

The error after truncating is bounded by the first omitted term's magnitude, $\dfrac{1}{n!}$ (for the term at index $n$, since this series is alternating with terms $(-1)^n/n!$, though strictly the general alternating-series bound requires checking the terms are decreasing in magnitude, which factorial-denominator terms are).

Require $\dfrac{1}{n!}<0.0001$: $6!=720\implies\tfrac{1}{720}\approx0.00139$ (not small enough); $7!=5040\implies\tfrac{1}{5040}\approx0.000198$ (not quite); $8!=40320\implies\tfrac{1}{40320}\approx0.0000248$ (small enough).

Answer: need terms up to $n=8$ (i.e. 9 terms, $n=0$ to $8$) to guarantee error under $0.0001$.

Practice 16

Find the first four nonzero terms of the Maclaurin series for $\sin x\cos x$ two ways: (a) directly multiplying the two known series, (b) using the identity $\sin x\cos x=\tfrac12\sin(2x)$ and substituting into the sine series. Confirm they agree.

Solution

(a) $\sin x\approx x-\tfrac{x^3}{6}$, $\cos x\approx1-\tfrac{x^2}{2}$. Product (keeping terms up to $x^3$): $x\cdot1+x\cdot\left(-\tfrac{x^2}{2}\right)+\left(-\tfrac{x^3}{6}\right)\cdot1=x-\tfrac{x^3}{2}-\tfrac{x^3}{6}=x-\tfrac{2x^3}{3}$.

(b) $\tfrac12\sin(2x)=\tfrac12\left(2x-\dfrac{(2x)^3}{6}\right)=\tfrac12\left(2x-\dfrac{8x^3}{6}\right)=x-\dfrac{2x^3}{3}$.

Answer: both methods give $x-\dfrac{2x^3}{3}+\cdots$, confirming agreement.

Practice 17

Find the Maclaurin series for $\ln(1-x^2)$ using the known series for $\ln(1+x)$ (substitute $-x^2$).

Solution

$\ln(1+u)=\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac{(-1)^{n+1}u^n}{n}$; substitute $u=-x^2$: $\ln(1-x^2)=\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac{(-1)^{n+1}(-x^2)^n}{n}=\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac{(-1)^{n+1}(-1)^nx^{2n}}{n}$.

$(-1)^{n+1}(-1)^n=(-1)^{2n+1}=-1$ for every $n$: $\ln(1-x^2)=-\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac{x^{2n}}{n}$.

Answer: $\ln(1-x^2)=-x^2-\dfrac{x^4}{2}-\dfrac{x^6}{3}-\cdots$.

Practice 18

Use the Maclaurin series for $e^x$ to evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{e^x-1-x}{x^2}$ (an alternative to L'Hôpital's Rule, connecting Week 10 and Week 12).

Solution

$e^x=1+x+\dfrac{x^2}{2}+\dfrac{x^3}{6}+\cdots$, so $e^x-1-x=\dfrac{x^2}{2}+\dfrac{x^3}{6}+\cdots$.

$\dfrac{e^x-1-x}{x^2}=\dfrac12+\dfrac{x}{6}+\cdots \to \dfrac12$ as $x\to0$.

Answer: $\dfrac12$ (matching what L'Hôpital's Rule, applied twice, would also give — Taylor series are often a faster alternative for this type of limit).

Practice 19

Estimate $\displaystyle\int_0^1 \frac{\sin x}{x}\,dx$ (an integral with no elementary antiderivative) using the first three terms of the Maclaurin series for $\sin x$, integrated term by term.

Solution

$\dfrac{\sin x}{x}=1-\dfrac{x^2}{6}+\dfrac{x^4}{120}-\cdots$ (dividing the sine series by $x$).

$\displaystyle\int_0^1\left(1-\frac{x^2}{6}+\frac{x^4}{120}\right)dx = \left[x-\frac{x^3}{18}+\frac{x^5}{600}\right]_0^1 = 1-\frac{1}{18}+\frac{1}{600}$.

$=1-0.05556+0.001667\approx0.9461$.

Answer: $\approx0.9461$ (this is the well-known "sine integral" $\text{Si}(1)$, which has no elementary closed form — series expansion is the standard practical way to evaluate it).

Practice 20

Find the Maclaurin series for $\dfrac{1}{(1-x)^2}$ by differentiating the known series for $\dfrac{1}{1-x}$ term by term.

Solution

$\dfrac{1}{1-x}=\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n$. Differentiating both sides with respect to $x$: $\dfrac{1}{(1-x)^2}=\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty nx^{n-1}$.

Answer: $\dfrac{1}{(1-x)^2}=\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty nx^{n-1}=1+2x+3x^2+4x^3+\cdots$.
