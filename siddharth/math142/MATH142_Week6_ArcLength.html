Arc Length

The straight-line distance formula measures the distance between two points, but a curved path between them is longer than that straight line. Arc length adapts Pythagoras to an infinite sum of infinitesimally short straight segments hugging the curve — the last topic before Exam 1. Exam-level worked examples and practice throughout. Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Arc length formula: $L=\displaystyle\int_a^b \sqrt{1+[f'(x)]^2}\,dx$.
  • Derivation: approximate the curve between $x$ and $x+dx$ by a straight segment; by Pythagoras its length is $\sqrt{dx^2+dy^2}=\sqrt{1+(dy/dx)^2}\,dx$; sum (integrate) over the whole curve.
  • Connection to trig substitution: $1+[f'(x)]^2$ frequently has the form $a^2+u^2$ once simplified — exactly the pattern trig substitution (Week 1) was built to handle.
  • Exam pattern: problems are often deliberately constructed so $1+[f'(x)]^2$ is a perfect square (via a specific combination like $x^n\pm\frac{1}{cx^n}$) — recognising this shortcut avoids a much harder integral.

0 Arc Length

Approximate the curve $y=f(x)$ between $x$ and $x+dx$ by the straight segment connecting $(x,f(x))$ to $(x+dx,f(x+dx))$. By Pythagoras, that tiny segment's length is $\sqrt{dx^2+dy^2}$. Factoring out $dx$:

$$\sqrt{dx^2+dy^2} = \sqrt{1+\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2}\,dx$$

Summing (integrating) these tiny segments over the whole curve gives the total arc length. These integrals frequently contain a square root of a sum of squares — precisely the pattern trig substitution was built to handle, so arc length is a natural place that technique gets used in practice.

Exam shortcut. Many textbook/exam problems are deliberately constructed so that $1+[f'(x)]^2$ simplifies to a perfect square — typically arising from a function of the form $y=\dfrac{x^{n+1}}{2(n+1)}+\dfrac{x^{1-n}}{2(1-n)}$ or similar combinations, where $f'(x)$ takes the form $x^n-\dfrac{1}{4x^n}$ (or similar), so that $1+[f'(x)]^2=\left(x^n+\dfrac{1}{4x^n}\right)^2$. Recognising this pattern saves substantial work.

Key idea

$$L = \int_a^b \sqrt{1+[f'(x)]^2}\,dx$$

Worked example

Example 1 — simple curve. Find the arc length of $y=\dfrac23x^{3/2}$ from $x=0$ to $x=3$.

$f'(x)=x^{1/2}$; $1+[f'(x)]^2=1+x$. $L=\displaystyle\int_0^3\sqrt{1+x}\,dx = \left[\dfrac23(1+x)^{3/2}\right]_0^3=\dfrac23(8-1)=\dfrac{14}{3}$.

Example 2 — setup needing trig substitution. Set up the arc length of $y=x^2$ from $x=0$ to $x=1$.

$f'(x)=2x$; $L=\displaystyle\int_0^1\sqrt{1+4x^2}\,dx$ — matches the $\sqrt{a^2+x^2}$ pattern, solvable via $2x=\tan\theta$.

Practice 1

Find the arc length of $y=\dfrac{x^3}{3}+\dfrac{1}{4x}$ from $x=1$ to $x=2$ (designed so $1+[f'(x)]^2$ is a perfect square).

Solution

$f'(x)=x^2-\dfrac{1}{4x^2}$; $1+[f'(x)]^2=1+x^4-\dfrac12+\dfrac{1}{16x^4}=x^4+\dfrac12+\dfrac{1}{16x^4}=\left(x^2+\dfrac{1}{4x^2}\right)^2$.

$L=\displaystyle\int_1^2\left(x^2+\dfrac{1}{4x^2}\right)dx=\left[\dfrac{x^3}{3}-\dfrac{1}{4x}\right]_1^2=\left(\dfrac83-\dfrac18\right)-\left(\dfrac13-\dfrac14\right)$.

$=\dfrac83-\dfrac18-\dfrac13+\dfrac14 = \dfrac{7}{3}+\dfrac18 = \dfrac{56}{24}+\dfrac{3}{24}=\dfrac{59}{24}$.

Answer: $\dfrac{59}{24}\approx2.458$.

Practice 2

Find the arc length of $y=\cosh x$ from $x=0$ to $x=1$ (using the identity $\cosh^2x-\sinh^2x=1$).

Solution

$f'(x)=\sinh x$; $1+[f'(x)]^2=1+\sinh^2x=\cosh^2x$ (using $\cosh^2x-\sinh^2x=1$).

$L=\displaystyle\int_0^1\sqrt{\cosh^2x}\,dx=\int_0^1\cosh x\,dx=\big[\sinh x\big]_0^1=\sinh1-0$.

Answer: $\sinh(1)\approx1.175$.

Practice 3

Find the arc length of $y=\ln(\sin x)$ from $x=\pi/4$ to $x=\pi/2$.

Solution

$f'(x)=\cot x$; $1+[f'(x)]^2=1+\cot^2x=\csc^2x$.

$L=\displaystyle\int_{\pi/4}^{\pi/2}\sqrt{\csc^2x}\,dx=\int_{\pi/4}^{\pi/2}\csc x\,dx = \big[-\ln|\csc x+\cot x|\big]_{\pi/4}^{\pi/2}$.

At $\pi/2$: $\csc=1,\cot=0$: $-\ln1=0$. At $\pi/4$: $\csc=\sqrt2,\cot=1$: $-\ln(\sqrt2+1)$.

Subtract: $0-\big(-\ln(\sqrt2+1)\big)=\ln(\sqrt2+1)$.

Answer: $\ln(\sqrt2+1)\approx0.8814$.

Practice 4

Find the arc length of $y=\dfrac{x^2}{2}-\dfrac{\ln x}{4}$ from $x=1$ to $x=2$ (another perfect-square design, this time mixing a power and a logarithm).

Solution

$f'(x)=x-\dfrac{1}{4x}$; $1+[f'(x)]^2=1+x^2-\dfrac12+\dfrac{1}{16x^2}=x^2+\dfrac12+\dfrac{1}{16x^2}=\left(x+\dfrac{1}{4x}\right)^2$.

$L=\displaystyle\int_1^2\left(x+\dfrac{1}{4x}\right)dx=\left[\dfrac{x^2}{2}+\dfrac{\ln x}{4}\right]_1^2 = \left(2+\dfrac{\ln2}{4}\right)-\left(\dfrac12+0\right)=\dfrac32+\dfrac{\ln2}{4}$.

Answer: $\dfrac32+\dfrac{\ln2}{4}\approx1.673$.

Practice 5

Find the arc length of $y=\dfrac{x^4}{4}+\dfrac{1}{8x^2}$ from $x=1$ to $x=2$.

Solution

$f'(x)=x^3-\dfrac{1}{4x^3}$; $1+[f'(x)]^2=1+x^6-\dfrac12+\dfrac{1}{16x^6}=x^6+\dfrac12+\dfrac{1}{16x^6}=\left(x^3+\dfrac{1}{4x^3}\right)^2$.

$L=\displaystyle\int_1^2\left(x^3+\dfrac{1}{4x^3}\right)dx=\left[\dfrac{x^4}{4}-\dfrac{1}{8x^2}\right]_1^2=\left(4-\dfrac{1}{32}\right)-\left(\dfrac14-\dfrac18\right)$.

$=4-\dfrac{1}{32}-\dfrac18=4-\dfrac{1}{32}-\dfrac{4}{32}=4-\dfrac{5}{32}=\dfrac{123}{32}$.

Answer: $\dfrac{123}{32}=3.84375$.

Practice 6

Set up (do not evaluate) the arc length integral for $y=e^x$ from $x=0$ to $x=1$, and identify what technique would be needed to actually evaluate it.

Solution

$f'(x)=e^x$; $L=\displaystyle\int_0^1\sqrt{1+e^{2x}}\,dx$.

This doesn't match any of the three standard trig-substitution patterns directly (the "$x^2$" role is played by $e^x$, not $x$ itself) — a substitution like $u=e^x$ first (giving $du=e^x dx$, so $dx=du/u$) converts it to $\displaystyle\int\dfrac{\sqrt{1+u^2}}{u}\,du$, which then matches the $\sqrt{a^2+u^2}$ trig-substitution pattern with $u=\tan\theta$.

Answer: $\displaystyle\int_0^1\sqrt{1+e^{2x}}\,dx$; requires the substitution $u=e^x$ followed by trig substitution $u=\tan\theta$.

Practice 7

Find the arc length of the line segment $y=3x-2$ from $x=0$ to $x=4$ using the arc length formula, and verify it matches the plain distance formula between the endpoints.

Solution

$f'(x)=3$ (constant). $L=\displaystyle\int_0^4\sqrt{1+9}\,dx=\sqrt{10}\int_0^4dx=4\sqrt{10}$.

Check: endpoints are $(0,-2)$ and $(4,10)$. Distance $=\sqrt{(4-0)^2+(10-(-2))^2}=\sqrt{16+144}=\sqrt{160}=4\sqrt{10}$. ✓ Matches.

Answer: $4\sqrt{10}\approx12.65$.

Practice 8

Set up the arc length integral for one full arch of the sine curve, $y=\sin x$ from $x=0$ to $x=\pi$, and explain why this integral cannot be evaluated using any elementary antiderivative (i.e. it's a case demanding numerical methods, connecting back to Weeks 3's Simpson's Rule).

Solution

$f'(x)=\cos x$; $L=\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi}\sqrt{1+\cos^2x}\,dx$.

This is an example of an elliptic integral — a well-known class of integrals proven not to have a closed-form elementary antiderivative. Numerical integration (Simpson's Rule, Week 3) is the practical way to evaluate it, since no combination of algebraic, trigonometric, exponential, or logarithmic functions can express its antiderivative exactly.

Practice 9

Find the arc length of $y=\dfrac{(2x+1)^{3/2}}{3}$ from $x=0$ to $x=4$.

Solution

$f'(x)=(2x+1)^{1/2}$; $1+[f'(x)]^2=1+(2x+1)=2x+2$.

$L=\displaystyle\int_0^4\sqrt{2x+2}\,dx = \left[\dfrac{1}{3}(2x+2)^{3/2}\right]_0^4=\dfrac13\big(10^{3/2}-2^{3/2}\big)$.

$10^{3/2}=31.623$, $2^{3/2}=2.828$: $\dfrac13(31.623-2.828)=\dfrac13(28.795)\approx9.598$.

Answer: $\dfrac{10^{3/2}-2^{3/2}}{3}\approx9.60$.

Practice 10

The upper semicircle $y=\sqrt{r^2-x^2}$ has arc length integral $L=\displaystyle\int_{-r}^{r}\dfrac{r}{\sqrt{r^2-x^2}}\,dx$, which is improper (undefined at both endpoints). Evaluate it using the limit techniques from Week 2, and confirm it matches half the circle's circumference.

Solution

Handle both endpoints via limits (or use the symmetric antiderivative directly, since $\arcsin$ is continuous and bounded on the open interval, making this a removable-type improper integral): $$L = r\Big[\arcsin\Big(\frac{x}{r}\Big)\Big]_{-r}^{r} = r\left(\frac{\pi}{2}-\left(-\frac{\pi}{2}\right)\right)=\pi r$$

Answer: $L=\pi r$, exactly half the full circle's circumference $2\pi r$ — confirming the formula against basic geometry.
