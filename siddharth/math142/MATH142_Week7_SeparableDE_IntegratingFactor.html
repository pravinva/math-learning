0 Introduction to DEs & Separable DEs

Every equation you've solved so far asks for a number. A differential equation asks for something bigger — an entire function — by describing a relationship between that function and its own rate of change. A solution is a function $y(x)$ that makes the equation true for every $x$.

Order. The highest derivative appearing: first order involves only $y'$; second order involves up to $y''$.

General vs. particular solutions. A first-order DE typically has a whole family of solutions differing by one constant (the general solution). One initial condition (like $y(0)=3$) pins down a single particular solution.

Separable DEs. If a DE can be rearranged so every $y$ (with $dy$) sits on one side and every $x$ (with $dx$) sits on the other, integrate both sides independently — each side becomes an ordinary single-variable integral.

Key idea

$$\frac{dy}{dx}=g(x)h(y) \ \implies\ \int \frac{1}{h(y)}\,dy = \int g(x)\,dx$$

Worked example

Example 1. Solve $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=xy$.

$\dfrac1y\,dy=x\,dx \implies \ln|y|=\dfrac{x^2}{2}+C_1 \implies y=Ce^{x^2/2}$.

Example 2. Solve $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=xy$ with $y(0)=5$.

$y(0)=Ce^0=C=5 \implies y=5e^{x^2/2}$.

Practice 1

State the order of the DE $y'''-2y''+y=\sin x$, and explain what "order" measures.

Solution

The order is the highest derivative present, which here is $y'''$: third order.

Order measures how many times the unknown function must be differentiated to state the equation — it's a structural property of the DE itself, independent of the specific solution.

Practice 2

Solve $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{y}{x}$ (for $x>0$).

Solution

$\dfrac1y\,dy=\dfrac1x\,dx \implies \ln|y|=\ln|x|+C_1 \implies y=Cx$.

Answer: $y=Cx$.

Practice 3

Solve $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=y^2\cos x$ with $y(0)=2$.

Solution

$\dfrac{1}{y^2}dy=\cos x\,dx \implies -\dfrac1y=\sin x+C$.

At $x=0,y=2$: $-\tfrac12=0+C \implies C=-\tfrac12$.

$-\dfrac1y=\sin x-\dfrac12 \implies y=\dfrac{-1}{\sin x-\tfrac12}=\dfrac{2}{1-2\sin x}$.

Answer: $y=\dfrac{2}{1-2\sin x}$.

Practice 4

Solve $(1+x^2)\dfrac{dy}{dx}=xy$.

Solution

$\dfrac1y\,dy=\dfrac{x}{1+x^2}\,dx$.

Right side via $u=1+x^2$: $\dfrac12\ln(1+x^2)$. So $\ln|y|=\dfrac12\ln(1+x^2)+C_1$.

Answer: $y=C\sqrt{1+x^2}$.

Practice 5

Solve $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=e^{x+y}$ (rewrite $e^{x+y}=e^xe^y$ first).

Solution

$e^{-y}\,dy=e^x\,dx \implies -e^{-y}=e^x+C_1 \implies e^{-y}=-e^x-C_1=K-e^x$ (relabelling the constant).

$-y=\ln(K-e^x) \implies y=-\ln(K-e^x)$.

Answer: $y=-\ln(K-e^x)$ for a constant $K$.

Practice 6

Solve $\sec x\,\dfrac{dy}{dx}=y^3$, $y(0)=1$.

Solution

Rewrite: $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=y^3\cos x$. Separate: $y^{-3}dy=\cos x\,dx$.

$-\dfrac{1}{2y^2}=\sin x+C$. At $x=0,y=1$: $-\tfrac12=0+C \implies C=-\tfrac12$.

$-\dfrac{1}{2y^2}=\sin x-\dfrac12 \implies \dfrac{1}{2y^2}=\dfrac12-\sin x \implies y^2=\dfrac{1}{1-2\sin x}$.

Answer: $y=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-2\sin x}}$ (taking the positive root, consistent with $y(0)=1>0$).

Practice 7

Verify by direct substitution that $y=Ce^{x^2/2}$ satisfies $y'=xy$, and explain why this check matters even after separating variables correctly.

Solution

$y'=Ce^{x^2/2}\cdot x=x\cdot Ce^{x^2/2}=xy$. ✓ Confirmed.

This check matters because the separation-and-integration process, while algebraically valid, can occasionally introduce or lose solutions (e.g. dividing by an expression that could be zero) — directly substituting the candidate solution back into the original DE is the definitive way to confirm correctness, independent of how it was derived.

Practice 8

Solve the DE $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{2xy}{x^2+1}$, $y(0)=3$.

Solution

$\dfrac1y\,dy=\dfrac{2x}{x^2+1}\,dx \implies \ln|y|=\ln(x^2+1)+C_1 \implies y=C(x^2+1)$.

At $x=0,y=3$: $3=C(1) \implies C=3$.

Answer: $y=3(x^2+1)$.

Practice 9

Solve $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{y\ln y}{x}$ (for $x>0,y>1$).

Solution

$\dfrac{1}{y\ln y}\,dy=\dfrac1x\,dx$. Left side: let $u=\ln y,du=\tfrac1y dy$: $\int\dfrac1u\,du=\ln|u|=\ln|\ln y|$.

$\ln|\ln y|=\ln|x|+C_1 \implies \ln y=Cx \implies y=e^{Cx}$.

Answer: $y=e^{Cx}$.

Practice 10

Explain, using a specific example, why a separable DE's solution is typically found first in implicit form (like $\tfrac{y^2}{2}=\tfrac{x^2}{2}+C$) rather than immediately solved explicitly for $y$, and give a case where solving explicitly is genuinely impossible.

Solution

Separating and integrating $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{x}{y}$ gives $\int y\,dy=\int x\,dx \implies \dfrac{y^2}{2}=\dfrac{x^2}{2}+C$ directly — this implicit relationship is what the integration itself produces; solving for $y=\pm\sqrt{x^2+C'}$ is a separate algebraic step afterward (and requires choosing a sign based on context).

Example where explicit solving is impossible: a DE like $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{x}{y+\sin y}$ separates to $\int(y+\sin y)\,dy=\int x\,dx \implies \dfrac{y^2}{2}-\cos y=\dfrac{x^2}{2}+C$ — this implicit equation cannot be algebraically solved for $y$ in terms of $x$ using elementary functions, so the implicit form is the final, complete answer.

1 Separable DE Applications — Growth, Decay & Mixing

Many physical processes — population growth, radioactive decay, cooling, mixing — share the property that their rate of change depends on their current state, which is exactly what a separable DE describes. The hard part is rarely solving the equation; it's correctly writing down the equation from the words.

Exponential growth/decay. "Rate of change proportional to current amount" translates to $\dfrac{dy}{dt}=ky$, solved as $y=y_0e^{kt}$.

Newton's Law of Cooling. "Rate of temperature change proportional to the difference from ambient temperature" translates to $\dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-T_a)$.

Mixing problems (constant volume): $\dfrac{dA}{dt}=(\text{rate in})-(\text{rate out})$, where each rate is (concentration)$\times$(flow rate).

Key idea

$$\frac{dy}{dt}=ky \implies y=y_0e^{kt}, \qquad \frac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-T_a) \implies T=T_a+(T_0-T_a)e^{-kt}$$

Worked example

Example 1 — exponential decay. A substance decays with half-life 10 years, starting at 80g. Find the amount after 20 years.

$y(10)=40=80e^{10k}\implies e^{10k}=\tfrac12$. $y(20)=80(e^{10k})^2=80(\tfrac12)^2=20$g.

Example 2 — Newton's Cooling. Coffee at $90°$C in a $20°$C room reaches $70°$C after 5 min. Find its temperature after 15 min.

$T=20+70e^{-kt}$; $e^{-5k}=\tfrac{50}{70}=\tfrac57$. $T(15)=20+70(\tfrac57)^3\approx45.5°$C.

Practice 11

A bacteria culture triples every 4 hours. If it starts at 200 organisms, find the population after 10 hours.

Solution

$y=200e^{kt}$; $y(4)=600=200e^{4k} \implies e^{4k}=3$.

$e^{10k}=(e^{4k})^{10/4}=3^{2.5}=3^2\cdot3^{0.5}=9\sqrt3\approx15.588$.

$y(10)=200(15.588)\approx3117.7$.

Answer: approximately 3118 organisms.

Practice 12

A radioactive isotope has a half-life of 5730 years (carbon-14). A sample contains 30% of its original carbon-14. Find its approximate age.

Solution

$y=y_0e^{kt}$ with $e^{5730k}=\tfrac12 \implies k=\dfrac{\ln(1/2)}{5730}$.

$0.30=e^{kt} \implies kt=\ln(0.30) \implies t=\dfrac{\ln(0.30)}{k}=\dfrac{\ln(0.30)}{\ln(0.5)/5730}=5730\cdot\dfrac{\ln0.30}{\ln0.5}$.

$\ln0.30\approx-1.204$, $\ln0.5\approx-0.693$: $t\approx5730\times1.737\approx9955$ years.

Answer: approximately 9955 years old.

Practice 13

A metal rod at $200°$C is placed in a $25°$C water bath. After 2 minutes it's $120°$C. Find its temperature after 5 minutes.

Solution

$T=25+175e^{-kt}$; at $t=2$: $120=25+175e^{-2k} \implies e^{-2k}=\dfrac{95}{175}=\dfrac{19}{35}$.

$e^{-k}=\left(\dfrac{19}{35}\right)^{1/2}\approx0.7375$; $e^{-5k}=(e^{-k})^5\approx0.7375^5\approx0.2185$.

$T(5)=25+175(0.2185)\approx25+38.2=63.2°$C.

Answer: approximately $63.2°$C.

Practice 14

A 300L tank starts with 60g of dissolved salt. Pure water flows in and the mixture flows out, both at 4L/min. Find $A(t)$, and how long until only 5g of salt remains.

Solution

Volume constant at 300L: $\dfrac{dA}{dt}=-\dfrac{4A}{300}=-\dfrac{A}{75}$.

$A=Ce^{-t/75}$; $A(0)=60=C$: $A(t)=60e^{-t/75}$.

Set $A=5$: $5=60e^{-t/75} \implies e^{-t/75}=\dfrac{1}{12} \implies -\dfrac{t}{75}=\ln\dfrac{1}{12}=-\ln12$.

$t=75\ln12\approx75(2.485)\approx186.4$ minutes.

Answer: $A(t)=60e^{-t/75}$; reaches 5g after approximately 186.4 minutes.

Practice 15

A population of fish in a lake grows according to $\dfrac{dP}{dt}=kP$, starting at 500 and reaching 800 after 3 years. Find when the population will reach 2000.

Solution

$800=500e^{3k} \implies e^{3k}=1.6 \implies k=\dfrac{\ln1.6}{3}$.

$2000=500e^{kt} \implies e^{kt}=4 \implies kt=\ln4 \implies t=\dfrac{\ln4}{k}=\dfrac{3\ln4}{\ln1.6}$.

$\ln4\approx1.386$, $\ln1.6\approx0.470$: $t\approx\dfrac{3(1.386)}{0.470}\approx8.85$ years.

Answer: approximately 8.85 years.

Practice 16

A thermometer reading $70°$F is placed outside where the temperature is $10°$F. After 1 minute it reads $55°$F. Set up and solve for $T(t)$, then find how long until it reads $15°$F.

Solution

$T=10+60e^{-kt}$; at $t=1$: $55=10+60e^{-k} \implies e^{-k}=\dfrac{45}{60}=0.75$.

Set $T=15$: $15=10+60e^{-kt} \implies e^{-kt}=\dfrac{5}{60}=\dfrac1{12}$.

Since $e^{-k}=0.75$: $e^{-kt}=(e^{-k})^t=0.75^t=\dfrac1{12} \implies t\ln0.75=\ln(1/12) \implies t=\dfrac{\ln(1/12)}{\ln0.75}$.

$\ln(1/12)\approx-2.485$, $\ln0.75\approx-0.2877$: $t\approx8.64$ minutes.

Answer: $T(t)=10+60(0.75)^t$; reaches $15°$F after approximately 8.64 minutes.

Practice 17

A tank contains 500L of brine with concentration 0.05 kg/L. Pure water flows in and the mixture flows out, both at 10L/min. Find the time required for the concentration to drop to 0.01 kg/L.

Solution

Initial salt: $A_0=500(0.05)=25$ kg. $\dfrac{dA}{dt}=-\dfrac{10A}{500}=-\dfrac{A}{50}$.

$A(t)=25e^{-t/50}$. Since volume stays constant at 500L, concentration $c(t)=A(t)/500=0.05e^{-t/50}$.

Set $c=0.01$: $0.01=0.05e^{-t/50} \implies e^{-t/50}=0.2 \implies t=-50\ln0.2=50\ln5\approx80.5$ minutes.

Answer: approximately 80.5 minutes.

Practice 18

Explain why the differential equation for Newton's Law of Cooling, $\dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-T_a)$, correctly predicts that a hot object cools faster when it's much hotter than its surroundings, and slows its rate of cooling as it approaches ambient temperature.

Solution

The rate of change $\dfrac{dT}{dt}$ is directly proportional to $(T-T_a)$, the gap between the object's current temperature and the ambient temperature. When $T$ is much larger than $T_a$, this gap is large, making $|\dfrac{dT}{dt}|$ large — rapid cooling. As $T$ approaches $T_a$, the gap $(T-T_a)$ shrinks toward zero, so $\dfrac{dT}{dt}$ shrinks too — the object cools more and more slowly, asymptotically approaching (but never mathematically reaching, in finite time) the ambient temperature.

Practice 19

A cup of tea cools from $95°$C to $80°$C in 3 minutes in a $20°$C room. Find how much additional time is needed to cool from $80°$C to $65°$C, and explain (without recomputing $k$ explicitly) why this second interval should take longer than the first 3 minutes.

Solution

Conceptual reasoning first: since the cooling rate depends on the gap to ambient temperature, and the gap $(80-20)=60$ is smaller than the initial gap $(95-20)=75$, the cooling will proceed more slowly during the second interval — so it should take longer than 3 minutes to drop the same 15 degrees.

Calculation: $T=20+75e^{-kt}$; at $t=3$: $80=20+75e^{-3k} \implies e^{-3k}=\dfrac{60}{75}=0.8 \implies e^{-k}=0.8^{1/3}\approx0.9283$.

Time from $95°$C to $65°$C: $65=20+75e^{-kt} \implies e^{-kt}=\dfrac{45}{75}=0.6 \implies t=\dfrac{\ln0.6}{\ln0.9283}\approx\dfrac{-0.5108}{-0.0744}\approx6.87$ min (total, from the start).

Additional time (from $80°$C, at $t=3$, to $65°$C): $6.87-3=3.87$ minutes.

Answer: approximately 3.87 additional minutes — longer than the first 3 minutes, confirming the conceptual prediction.

Practice 20

A tank has 200L of pure water. Brine with concentration 3 g/L flows in at 2L/min, and the mixture flows out at the same rate (constant volume). Find $A(t)$, and the long-term (steady-state) amount of salt in the tank.

Solution

$\dfrac{dA}{dt}=2(3)-2\cdot\dfrac{A}{200}=6-\dfrac{A}{100}$.

This is separable (and linear): $\dfrac{dA}{6-A/100}=dt$. Let $u=6-A/100$: $du=-\dfrac{1}{100}dA$, so $-100\ln|u|=t+C_1$.

$u=Ke^{-t/100}$, i.e. $6-\dfrac{A}{100}=Ke^{-t/100} \implies A=600-100Ke^{-t/100}$.

At $t=0,A=0$: $0=600-100K \implies K=6$. So $A(t)=600-600e^{-t/100}=600(1-e^{-t/100})$.

As $t\to\infty$, $e^{-t/100}\to0$, so $A\to600$ g.

Answer: $A(t)=600(1-e^{-t/100})$; steady-state amount is 600 g (matching the equilibrium where inflow concentration $\times$ volume $=3\times200=600$ g).

2 First-Order Linear DEs — the Integrating Factor Method

Not every first-order DE separates — $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+y=x$ has $x$ and $y$ hopelessly tangled. The integrating factor method multiplies the whole equation by a cleverly chosen function $\mu(x)$ so the left side collapses into $\dfrac{d}{dx}[\mu(x)y]$ — the product rule, run in reverse.

Standard form: $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+p(x)y=q(x)$.

Finding $\mu$. Expanding $\dfrac{d}{dx}[\mu y]=\mu y'+\mu'y$ and comparing to $\mu y'+\mu p(x)y$ shows this works exactly when $\mu'=\mu p(x)$, giving $\mu(x)=e^{\int p(x)dx}$.

Key idea

$$y'+p(x)y=q(x), \qquad \mu(x)=e^{\int p(x)\,dx}, \qquad y=\frac{1}{\mu(x)}\left[\int \mu(x)q(x)\,dx+C\right]$$

Worked example

Example 1. Solve $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+y=x$.

$p(x)=1$, $\mu=e^x$. $\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^xy]=xe^x \implies e^xy=xe^x-e^x+C \implies y=x-1+Ce^{-x}$.

Example 2. Solve $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+\dfrac2xy=x$, $y(1)=1$.

$\mu=x^2$; $\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^2y]=x^3 \implies x^2y=\dfrac{x^4}{4}+C$; at $x=1,y=1$: $C=\tfrac34$. $y=\dfrac{x^2}{4}+\dfrac{3}{4x^2}$.

Practice 21

Solve $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+3y=e^{2x}$.

Solution

$\mu=e^{3x}$. $\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^{3x}y]=e^{5x} \implies e^{3x}y=\dfrac15e^{5x}+C \implies y=\dfrac15e^{2x}+Ce^{-3x}$.

Answer: $y=\dfrac15e^{2x}+Ce^{-3x}$.

Practice 22

Solve $\dfrac{dy}{dx}-\dfrac{2}{x}y=x^2$, $y(1)=2$ (for $x>0$).

Solution

$p(x)=-2/x$; $\mu=e^{\int(-2/x)dx}=e^{-2\ln x}=x^{-2}$.

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^{-2}y]=x^{-2}\cdot x^2=1 \implies x^{-2}y=x+C \implies y=x^3+Cx^2$.

At $x=1,y=2$: $2=1+C \implies C=1$.

Answer: $y=x^3+x^2$.

Practice 23

Solve $x\dfrac{dy}{dx}+2y=\sin x$ (for $x>0$) — first rewrite in standard form.

Solution

Divide by $x$: $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+\dfrac2xy=\dfrac{\sin x}{x}$.

$\mu=e^{\int(2/x)dx}=x^2$. $\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^2y]=x^2\cdot\dfrac{\sin x}{x}=x\sin x$.

$x^2y=\displaystyle\int x\sin x\,dx = -x\cos x+\sin x+C$ (by parts, from Phase 1).

Answer: $y=\dfrac{-x\cos x+\sin x+C}{x^2}$.

Practice 24

Solve $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+2xy=xe^{-x^2}$ (note: $q(x)$ matches $\mu(x)^{-1}$, so the right side simplifies substantially).

Solution

$\mu=e^{\int2x\,dx}=e^{x^2}$.

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^{x^2}y]=e^{x^2}\cdot xe^{-x^2}=x \implies e^{x^2}y=\dfrac{x^2}{2}+C$.

Answer: $y=\left(\dfrac{x^2}{2}+C\right)e^{-x^2}$.

Practice 25

Solve $\cos x\,\dfrac{dy}{dx}+y\sin x=1$, $0

Solution

Divide by $\cos x$: $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+y\tan x=\sec x$.

$\mu=e^{\int\tan x\,dx}=e^{-\ln|\cos x|}=\dfrac{1}{\cos x}=\sec x$.

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[y\sec x]=\sec x\cdot\sec x=\sec^2x \implies y\sec x=\tan x+C$.

Answer: $y=\cos x(\tan x+C)=\sin x+C\cos x$.

Practice 26

Solve $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+y=xe^{-x}$, $y(0)=0$.

Solution

$\mu=e^x$. $\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^xy]=e^x\cdot xe^{-x}=x \implies e^xy=\dfrac{x^2}{2}+C$.

At $x=0,y=0$: $0=0+C \implies C=0$.

Answer: $y=\dfrac{x^2}{2}e^{-x}$.

Practice 27

Verify by direct substitution that $y=x-1+Ce^{-x}$ solves $y'+y=x$ (the worked example above), confirming the integrating factor method's result.

Solution

$y'=1-Ce^{-x}$.

$y'+y=(1-Ce^{-x})+(x-1+Ce^{-x})=1-Ce^{-x}+x-1+Ce^{-x}=x$. ✓ The $Ce^{-x}$ terms cancel exactly, confirming the solution matches the original equation regardless of $C$.

Practice 28

Solve $\dfrac{dy}{dx}-y=2e^x$, $y(0)=3$ (careful: $q(x)$ has the same exponential as the homogeneous solution, so the right side won't simplify to a clean power of $x$ without care).

Solution

$\mu=e^{-x}$. $\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^{-x}y]=e^{-x}\cdot2e^x=2 \implies e^{-x}y=2x+C$.

At $x=0,y=3$: $3=0+C \implies C=3$.

Answer: $y=(2x+3)e^x$.

Practice 29

Solve the boundary-layer-style equation $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+\dfrac{1}{x+1}y=\dfrac{1}{(x+1)^2}$, $y(0)=1$.

Solution

$\mu=e^{\int\frac{1}{x+1}dx}=e^{\ln(x+1)}=x+1$.

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[(x+1)y]=(x+1)\cdot\dfrac{1}{(x+1)^2}=\dfrac{1}{x+1} \implies (x+1)y=\ln|x+1|+C$.

At $x=0,y=1$: $1=\ln1+C=C \implies C=1$.

Answer: $y=\dfrac{\ln(x+1)+1}{x+1}$.

Practice 30

Explain, using the product rule, why the integrating factor $\mu(x)=e^{\int p(x)dx}$ is the unique function (up to a constant multiple) that makes the left side of $y'+p(x)y=q(x)$ collapse into a single derivative $\dfrac{d}{dx}[\mu y]$, and why any constant multiple of $\mu$ works equally well.

Solution

Expanding $\dfrac{d}{dx}[\mu y]=\mu y'+\mu'y$ via the product rule, this matches $\mu y'+\mu p(x)y$ (the left side after multiplying the whole DE by $\mu$) precisely when $\mu'=\mu p(x)$ — this is itself a separable DE in $\mu$, whose general solution is $\mu=Ce^{\int p(x)dx}$ for any nonzero constant $C$.

Any such constant multiple works equally well because multiplying the entire original DE by a nonzero constant $C$ doesn't change which function $y$ solves it — it's conventional to simply take $C=1$ (the simplest choice) rather than carry an unnecessary constant through the whole calculation.

Practice 40

(a) For $M' = 4-0.2M$, find equilibrium mass. (b) Solve with $M(0)=0$.

Solution

Equilibrium from $0=4-0.2M$: $M^*=20$.

$M=20+Ce^{-0.2t}$, and $M(0)=0\Rightarrow C=-20$, so $M=20(1-e^{-0.2t})$.

Practice 41

(a) Solve $y'+y=\sin x$. (b) Identify homogeneous and particular parts.

Solution

$\mu=e^x$, $(e^xy)'=e^x\sin x$.

$\int e^x\sin x\,dx=\frac{e^x}{2}(\sin x-\cos x)$.

$y=\frac12(\sin x-\cos x)+Ce^{-x}$.

Practice 42

(a) Solve separable $y'=(x+1)(y-2)$. (b) Apply $y(0)=5$.

Solution

$\frac{dy}{y-2}=(x+1)dx\Rightarrow \ln|y-2|=x^2/2+x+C$.

$y=2+Ce^{x^2/2+x}$. IC gives $C=3$, so $y=2+3e^{x^2/2+x}$.

Practice 43

(a) Determine if $y'=\frac{x+y}{x}$ is separable. (b) Solve as linear if possible.

Solution

Not separable directly; rewrite $y'-\frac1x y=1$.

$\mu=e^{\int-1/x dx}=x^{-1}$, so $(y/x)'=1/x$.

$y/x=\ln x+C\Rightarrow y=x\ln x+Cx$ ($x>0$).

Practice 44

(a) Solve $y'=-ky$ and show doubling-time formula if $k<0$. (b) If $k=-0.08$, find doubling time.

Solution

$y=y_0e^{-kt}$. For growth $k<0$, write rate $r=-k>0$: $y=y_0e^{rt}$.

$2y_0=y_0e^{rt_d}\Rightarrow t_d=\ln2/r=\ln2/(-k)$. With $k=-0.08$, $t_d=\ln2/0.08\approx8.66$.

Practice 45

(a) Solve $y'+\frac{1}{2}y=6$. (b) With $y(0)=10$, when does $y$ first drop below 7?

Solution

$y=12+Ce^{-t/2}$; $10=12+C\Rightarrow C=-2$, so $y=12-2e^{-t/2}$.

$12-2e^{-t/2}<7\Rightarrow e^{-t/2}>2.5$ impossible. It never drops below 7; it rises toward 12.

Practice 46

(a) Solve $y'=\frac{1+x^2}{1+y^2}$. (b) Give implicit general solution.

Solution

Separate: $(1+y^2)dy=(1+x^2)dx$.

$y+y^3/3=x+x^3/3+C$ (implicit).

Practice 47

(a) Linear DE $y'+py=q$ with constants $p,q>0$: write long-time limit. (b) Justify in one line.

Solution

Solution $y=\frac{q}{p}+Ce^{-pt}$.

As $t\to\infty$, exponential term vanishes, so limit is $q/p$.

Practice 48

(a) Solve $x^2y'+xy=1$ ($x>0$). (b) Apply $y(1)=0$.

Solution

$y'+\frac1x y=\frac1{x^2}$, $\mu=x$.

$(xy)'=1/x$, so $xy=\ln x+C$, $y=\frac{\ln x+C}{x}$.

$y(1)=0\Rightarrow C=0$, so $y=\frac{\ln x}{x}$.

Practice 49

(a) Solve $y'=\frac{3y}{2x}$, $y(1)=4$. (b) Predict monotonicity for $x>0$.

Solution

$\frac{dy}{y}=\frac{3}{2}\frac{dx}{x}\Rightarrow y=Cx^{3/2}$. IC gives $C=4$.

$y=4x^{3/2}$, increasing for $x>0$.

Practice 50

(a) Solve $y'+4y=e^{-4x}$. (b) Explain resonance-like overlap with homogeneous exponent.

Solution

$\mu=e^{4x}$, so $(e^{4x}y)'=1\Rightarrow e^{4x}y=x+C$.

$y=(x+C)e^{-4x}$. Forcing matches homogeneous rate, so particular gains extra $x$ factor.

Practice 51

(a) Show $y\equiv0$ solves $y'=y(1-y)$. (b) Is $y\equiv1$ also solution?

Solution

Substitute constants: if $y=0$, RHS $0(1-0)=0$, so yes.

If $y=1$, RHS $1(1-1)=0$, so also yes.

Practice 52

(a) Solve $y'=(x-2)^2(y+1)$. (b) Apply $y(2)=0$.

Solution

$\frac{dy}{y+1}=(x-2)^2dx\Rightarrow \ln|y+1|=(x-2)^3/3+C$.

$y+1=Ce^{(x-2)^3/3}$. IC gives $1=C$, so $y=e^{(x-2)^3/3}-1$.

Practice 53

(a) Solve $y'+\frac{3}{x}y=x^2$ ($x>0$). (b) Find $y$ if $y(1)=2$.

Solution

$\mu=x^3$, $(x^3y)'=x^5$.

$x^3y=x^6/6+C\Rightarrow y=x^3/6+C/x^3$.

$2=1/6+C\Rightarrow C=11/6$, so $y=x^3/6+11/(6x^3)$.

Practice 54

(a) For $y'=ky$, derive formula from data $(t_1,y_1)$ and $(t_2,y_2)$ for $k$. (b) If $y_2=3y_1$ and $t_2-t_1=5$, compute $k$.

Solution

$y=Ce^{kt}$ gives $\frac{y_2}{y_1}=e^{k(t_2-t_1)}\Rightarrow k=\frac{1}{t_2-t_1}\ln\frac{y_2}{y_1}$.

$k=\frac{1}{5}\ln3$.

Practice 55

(a) Solve $y'+y=\cos x$ with $y(0)=0$. (b) Find long-time behavior qualitatively.

Solution

$\mu=e^x$, $(e^xy)'=e^x\cos x$.

$\int e^x\cos x dx=\frac{e^x}{2}(\sin x+\cos x)$.

$y=\frac12(\sin x+\cos x)+Ce^{-x}$. IC gives $C=-1/2$, so $y=\frac12(\sin x+\cos x-e^{-x})$.

Transient $e^{-x}$ dies; periodic part remains.

Practice 56

(a) Explain why integrating factor is chosen so $(\mu y)'=\mu y'+\mu p y$. (b) State ODE for $\mu$.

Solution

Need $\mu' y$ term to equal $\mu p y$, so product rule matches DE structure.

Hence $\mu'=\mu p$, so $\mu=e^{\int p\,dx}$.

Practice 57

(a) Solve $y'=\frac{x}{y}$ with $y(0)=2$. (b) Choose correct branch.

Solution

$y\,dy=x\,dx\Rightarrow y^2/2=x^2/2+C\Rightarrow y^2=x^2+C'$.

IC: $4=C'$, so $y=\pm\sqrt{x^2+4}$. With $y(0)=2>0$, choose $y=\sqrt{x^2+4}$.

Practice 58

(a) Solve $y'-\frac{y}{x}=x^2$, $x>0$. (b) Verify by substitution.

Solution

$\mu=e^{\int-1/x dx}=x^{-1}$.

$(y/x)'=x$, so $y/x=x^2/2+C\Rightarrow y=x^3/2+Cx$.

Practice 59

(a) Tank starts with 100 L, 10 g salt; inflow 2 L/min at 4 g/L, outflow 2 L/min. Solve $A(t)$. (b) Limit as $t\to\infty$.

Solution

Constant volume: $A'=8-\frac{2}{100}A=8-\frac{A}{50}$.

$A=400+Ce^{-t/50}$; $A(0)=10\Rightarrow C=-390$.

$A(t)=400-390e^{-t/50}$, limit $400$ g (concentration 4 g/L).

Practice 60

(a) Capstone classification: decide method for each DE: (i) $y'=\frac{x^2+1}{y}$, (ii) $y'+xy=x$, (iii) $y'=y+x$. (b) Name first step for each.

Solution

(i) Separable: move $y$ left, $x$ right. (ii) Linear: compute $\mu=e^{\int xdx}=e^{x^2/2}$. (iii) Linear: rewrite $y'-y=x$, use $\mu=e^{-x}$.
