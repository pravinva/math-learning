Areas (Cartesian & Polar) and Volumes of Revolution

Area-between-curves revisited with a genuinely new formula for polar regions, then two different ways to slice a solid of revolution into simple pieces. Exam-level worked examples and practice throughout. Solutions stay hidden until you click.

0 / 21 practice solutions revealed
Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Polar area: $A=\dfrac12\displaystyle\int_\alpha^\beta r^2\,d\theta$ — built from summing thin circular sectors, each of area $\tfrac12r^2\,d\theta$.
  • Area between two polar curves: $A=\dfrac12\displaystyle\int_\alpha^\beta \big(r_{outer}^2-r_{inner}^2\big)\,d\theta$ — same "outer minus inner" idea as Cartesian area-between-curves.
  • Disk/washer method (slicing perpendicular to the axis): $V=\pi\displaystyle\int_a^b \big([R(x)]^2-[r(x)]^2\big)\,dx$.
  • Shell method (slicing parallel to the axis): $V=2\pi\displaystyle\int_a^b x\,f(x)\,dx$.
  • Choosing a method: pick whichever avoids solving the boundary curve for the "wrong" variable — if the region is naturally $y=f(x)$ and you're rotating about the $y$-axis, shells (which keep everything in terms of $x$) are usually easier than washers (which need $x=g(y)$).

0 Areas — Cartesian Extension & Polar Regions

Cartesian area-between-curves used thin vertical rectangles. A polar region isn't naturally swept out by rectangles at all — it's swept out by thin circular sectors, radiating from the origin as $\theta$ varies, leading to a completely different-looking (but equally derivable) area formula.

Building the polar area formula. A thin sector of radius $r$ and angle $d\theta$ has area $\tfrac12r^2\,d\theta$ (a fraction $\tfrac{d\theta}{2\pi}$ of the full circle's area $\pi r^2$). Summing (integrating) these thin sectors gives the total area swept out.

Key idea

$$A = \frac12\int_\alpha^\beta r^2\,d\theta$$

Worked example

Example 1. Find the area enclosed by the cardioid $r=1+\cos\theta$.

$A=\tfrac12\int_0^{2\pi}(1+\cos\theta)^2\,d\theta=\tfrac12\int_0^{2\pi}(1+2\cos\theta+\cos^2\theta)\,d\theta = \tfrac12(2\pi+\pi)=\dfrac{3\pi}{2}$.

Example 2. Find the area of one petal of $r=\cos(2\theta)$.

One petal spans $\theta\in[-\pi/4,\pi/4]$ (where $r\ge0$): $A=\tfrac12\int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\cos^2(2\theta)\,d\theta=\dfrac{\pi}{8}$.

Practice 1

Find the exact area of the region that lies inside the circle $r=3$ but outside the cardioid $r=2+2\cos\theta$ (first find the intersection angles, and be careful about which curve is actually "inside" in which angular range).

Solution

Intersections: $3=2+2\cos\theta\implies\cos\theta=\tfrac12\implies\theta=\pm\dfrac{\pi}{3}$.

Identify the correct range first. At $\theta=0$: cardioid $r=4$, circle $r=3$ — the cardioid is bigger here, so the circle sits entirely inside the cardioid for $|\theta|<\pi/3$, and the "inside circle, outside cardioid" region is empty there. At $\theta=\pi$: cardioid $r=0$, circle $r=3$ — now the circle is bigger, so the desired region exists for $\pi/3<\theta<2\pi-\pi/3$ (i.e. everywhere except near $\theta=0$).

Set up, using symmetry about $\theta=\pi$: $$A = 2\cdot\frac12\int_{\pi/3}^{\pi}\Big[3^2-(2+2\cos\theta)^2\Big]\,d\theta = \int_{\pi/3}^{\pi}\left(3-8\cos\theta-2\cos2\theta\right)d\theta$$ (using $4\cos^2\theta=2+2\cos2\theta$ to simplify $9-4-8\cos\theta-4\cos^2\theta$).

Antiderivative: $\big[3\theta-8\sin\theta-\sin2\theta\big]_{\pi/3}^{\pi}$.

At $\theta=\pi$: $3\pi-0-0=3\pi$. At $\theta=\pi/3$: $\pi-8\cdot\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2}-\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2}=\pi-\tfrac{9\sqrt3}{2}$.

Subtract: $3\pi-\left(\pi-\tfrac{9\sqrt3}{2}\right)=2\pi+\dfrac{9\sqrt3}{2}$.

Answer: $2\pi+\dfrac{9\sqrt3}{2}\approx14.08$. (Always check which curve is actually the outer/inner one across the full angular range — assuming it stays the same everywhere, as in a naive symmetric-around-zero setup, is the most common error on this exact question type.)

Practice 2

Find the total area enclosed by the rose $r=3\sin(2\theta)$ (a 4-petal rose, since $n=2$ is even).

Solution

One petal spans $\theta\in[0,\pi/2]$ (where $\sin2\theta\ge0$). Area of one petal: $A_1=\tfrac12\int_0^{\pi/2}9\sin^2(2\theta)\,d\theta$.

$\sin^2(2\theta)=\dfrac{1-\cos4\theta}{2}$: $A_1=\dfrac92\int_0^{\pi/2}\dfrac{1-\cos4\theta}{2}\,d\theta = \dfrac94\left[\theta-\dfrac{\sin4\theta}{4}\right]_0^{\pi/2}=\dfrac94\cdot\dfrac{\pi}{2}=\dfrac{9\pi}{8}$.

Total area (4 petals): $4\times\dfrac{9\pi}{8}=\dfrac{9\pi}{2}$.

Answer: $\dfrac{9\pi}{2}$.

Practice 3

Find the area of the region inside $r=2\sin\theta$ and inside $r=1$ simultaneously (the smaller lens-shaped overlap region), identifying intersection angles first.

Solution

Intersections: $2\sin\theta=1 \implies \sin\theta=\tfrac12 \implies \theta=\dfrac{\pi}{6},\dfrac{5\pi}{6}$.

Identify inner curve on each range: at $\theta=\pi/2$ (between the intersections): $r_{2\sin\theta}=2$, $r_1=1$ — so $r=1$ is the inner (smaller) boundary between $\pi/6$ and $5\pi/6$. Outside this range (down to $\theta=0$ and up from $\theta=\pi$, within where $2\sin\theta\ge0$, i.e. $[0,\pi]$), $2\sin\theta<1$, so the circle $2\sin\theta$ is the inner boundary there.

Set up the area as three pieces: $$A = \frac12\int_0^{\pi/6}(2\sin\theta)^2d\theta + \frac12\int_{\pi/6}^{5\pi/6}(1)^2d\theta + \frac12\int_{5\pi/6}^{\pi}(2\sin\theta)^2d\theta$$

By symmetry the first and third integrals are equal. Evaluating: $\int(2\sin\theta)^2d\theta=\int(2-2\cos2\theta)d\theta=2\theta-\sin2\theta$; from $0$ to $\pi/6$: $\dfrac{\pi}{3}-\dfrac{\sqrt3}{2}$, so each end piece contributes $\tfrac12\left(\dfrac{\pi}{3}-\dfrac{\sqrt3}{2}\right)$. Middle piece: $\tfrac12\left(\dfrac{5\pi}{6}-\dfrac{\pi}{6}\right)=\tfrac12\cdot\dfrac{2\pi}{3}=\dfrac{\pi}{3}$.

Answer: $A = 2\cdot\tfrac12\left(\dfrac{\pi}{3}-\dfrac{\sqrt3}{2}\right)+\dfrac{\pi}{3} = \dfrac{\pi}{3}-\dfrac{\sqrt3}{2}+\dfrac{\pi}{3}=\dfrac{2\pi}{3}-\dfrac{\sqrt3}{2}$.

1 Volumes of Revolution — Disk/Washer & Shell Methods

Rotating a 2D region around an axis sweeps out a 3D solid. There are two natural ways to slice that solid into simple pieces to sum up its volume: thin disks/washers perpendicular to the axis, or thin cylindrical shells parallel to it. The right choice depends on which slicing direction gives simpler cross-sections for the specific region.

Disk/washer. Rotating the region under $y=f(x)$ about the $x$-axis sweeps out disks of radius $f(x)$: volume $\pi[f(x)]^2\,dx$. If the region is between two curves, each slice is a washer (a disk with a hole): $V=\pi\int([R(x)]^2-[r(x)]^2)\,dx$.

Shell method. Summing thin cylindrical shells at each $x$, each with radius $x$, height $f(x)$, thickness $dx$ — "unrolled," a thin shell is approximately a rectangular slab of area $2\pi x\cdot f(x)$.

Key idea

$$\text{Disk/washer: } V=\pi\int_a^b\big([R(x)]^2-[r(x)]^2\big)dx \qquad \text{Shell: } V=2\pi\int_a^b x\,f(x)\,dx$$

Worked example

Example 1 — disk method. Find the volume when the region under $y=\sqrt{x}$, $0\le x\le4$, is rotated about the $x$-axis.

$V=\pi\int_0^4 x\,dx = \pi\left[\dfrac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^4=8\pi$.

Example 2 — shell method. Find the volume when the region under $y=x^2$, $0\le x\le2$, is rotated about the $y$-axis.

$V=2\pi\int_0^2 x^3\,dx=2\pi\left[\dfrac{x^4}{4}\right]_0^2=8\pi$.

Practice 4

The region bounded by $y=\sqrt{x}$ and $y=x^2$ (for $0\le x\le1$) is rotated about the $x$-axis. Find the volume using the washer method, being careful to identify which curve is the outer radius.

Solution

On $[0,1]$, $\sqrt x \ge x^2$ (check at $x=0.5$: $\sqrt{0.5}\approx0.707 > 0.25$), so $y=\sqrt x$ is the outer curve, $y=x^2$ the inner.

$V=\pi\int_0^1\Big[(\sqrt x)^2-(x^2)^2\Big]dx = \pi\int_0^1(x-x^4)\,dx = \pi\left[\dfrac{x^2}{2}-\dfrac{x^5}{5}\right]_0^1=\pi\left(\dfrac12-\dfrac15\right)=\dfrac{3\pi}{10}$.

Answer: $\dfrac{3\pi}{10}$.

Practice 5

The region bounded by $y=\sqrt{x}$ and $y=x^2$ (same region as above) is instead rotated about the $y$-axis. Find the volume using the shell method.

Solution

Shell radius $=x$; shell height $=$ (outer curve $-$ inner curve) $=\sqrt x - x^2$.

$V=2\pi\int_0^1 x(\sqrt x-x^2)\,dx = 2\pi\int_0^1(x^{3/2}-x^3)\,dx = 2\pi\left[\dfrac{2}{5}x^{5/2}-\dfrac{x^4}{4}\right]_0^1=2\pi\left(\dfrac25-\dfrac14\right)$.

$\dfrac25-\dfrac14=\dfrac{8-5}{20}=\dfrac{3}{20}$: $V=2\pi\cdot\dfrac{3}{20}=\dfrac{3\pi}{10}$.

Answer: $\dfrac{3\pi}{10}$ (matches the disk/washer result from the previous question, rotated about a different axis but by coincidence giving the same numeric volume here — always worth double-checking such a match isn't a computational error, though in this case it is simply how the specific numbers worked out).

Practice 6

The region bounded by $y=4-x^2$ and the $x$-axis (for $-2\le x\le2$) is rotated about the vertical line $x=3$ (not the $y$-axis). Set up and evaluate the volume using the shell method with an adjusted radius.

Solution

Since the axis of rotation is $x=3$ rather than $x=0$, the shell radius for a shell at position $x$ is the distance to $x=3$: radius $=3-x$ (positive throughout since $x\le2<3$).

Shell height $=4-x^2$ (the curve's height above the $x$-axis).

$$V=2\pi\int_{-2}^{2}(3-x)(4-x^2)\,dx$$

Expand: $(3-x)(4-x^2)=12-3x^2-4x+x^3$.

$\displaystyle\int_{-2}^2(12-3x^2-4x+x^3)\,dx$: the odd-power terms ($-4x$ and $x^3$) integrate to zero over the symmetric interval $[-2,2]$, leaving $\displaystyle\int_{-2}^2(12-3x^2)\,dx=2\int_0^2(12-3x^2)\,dx=2\left[12x-x^3\right]_0^2=2(24-8)=32$.

$V=2\pi(32)=64\pi$.

Answer: $64\pi$.

Practice 7

The region under $y=e^{-x}$ for $0\le x\le1$ is rotated about the $y$-axis. Explain why the shell method is the natural choice here (rather than washers), then set up (and evaluate) the resulting integral using integration by parts.

Solution

Why shells: $y=e^{-x}$ cannot be solved for $x$ in terms of $y$ using elementary functions in a form convenient for the washer method ($x=-\ln y$ is possible here actually, but shells avoid the extra inversion step and keep the setup in terms of the given $x$-based function directly — generally the deciding factor for exponential/logarithmic boundary curves).

$$V=2\pi\int_0^1 x\,e^{-x}\,dx$$

By parts: $u=x,dv=e^{-x}dx$: $du=dx,v=-e^{-x}$.

$\displaystyle\int_0^1 xe^{-x}dx = \big[-xe^{-x}\big]_0^1+\int_0^1e^{-x}dx = -e^{-1}+\big[-e^{-x}\big]_0^1 = -e^{-1}+(-e^{-1}+1)=1-2e^{-1}$.

Answer: $V=2\pi(1-2e^{-1})\approx2.31$.

Practice 8

Find the area enclosed by one loop of $r=2+\cos\theta$ and verify whether an inner loop exists before integrating.

Solution

Since $2+\cos\theta\ge1>0$, there is no inner loop.

Total enclosed area: $A=\frac12\int_0^{2\pi}(2+\cos\theta)^2d\theta=\frac12(8\pi+\pi)=\frac{9\pi}{2}$.

Practice 9

Find the area common to $r=2$ and $r=2\cos\theta$.

Solution

Inside both means radial bound is $\min(2,2\cos\theta)$ where $\cos\theta\ge0$ i.e. $\theta\in[-\pi/2,\pi/2]$.

Since $2\cos\theta\le2$ there, area is $\frac12\int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2}(2\cos\theta)^2d\theta=2\int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2}\cos^2\theta d\theta=\pi$.

Practice 10

Find the area between $r=3\sin\theta$ and $r=\sin\theta$ for $\theta\in[0,\pi]$.

Solution

Outer minus inner in polar: $$A=\frac12\int_0^\pi\big((3\sin\theta)^2-(\sin\theta)^2\big)d\theta=4\int_0^\pi\sin^2\theta\,d\theta=4\cdot\frac{\pi}{2}=2\pi.$$

Practice 11

Rotate the region under $y=x^3$ from $x=0$ to $x=2$ about the $x$-axis. Compute with washers.

Solution

$V=\pi\int_0^2 (x^3)^2dx=\pi\int_0^2 x^6dx=\pi\left[\frac{x^7}{7}\right]_0^2=\frac{128\pi}{7}$.

Practice 12

Rotate the region under $y=\sqrt{4-x^2}$ for $0\le x\le2$ about the $x$-axis. Interpret geometrically and verify by integration.

Solution

This is a quarter circle radius 2; rotation gives a hemisphere radius 2, volume $\frac{2}{3}\pi r^3=\frac{16\pi}{3}$.

Washer check: $\pi\int_0^2(4-x^2)dx=\pi[4x-x^3/3]_0^2=\frac{16\pi}{3}$.

Practice 13

Rotate the region between $y=x$ and $y=x^2$ on $[0,1]$ about the $x$-axis. Use washers.

Solution

Outer radius $R=x$, inner radius $r=x^2$.

$V=\pi\int_0^1(x^2-x^4)dx=\pi\left[\frac{x^3}{3}-\frac{x^5}{5}\right]_0^1=\frac{2\pi}{15}$.

Practice 14

Find the same volume as Practice 13 but by shells about the $y$-axis for region between $y=x$ and $y=x^2$ on $[0,1]$.

Solution

Shell radius $x$, height $x-x^2$.

$V=2\pi\int_0^1 x(x-x^2)dx=2\pi\int_0^1(x^2-x^3)dx=2\pi\left(\frac13-\frac14\right)=\frac{\pi}{6}$.

Practice 15

Region under $y=\ln x$ from $x=1$ to $x=e$ rotated about the $x$-axis. Compute volume.

Solution

$V=\pi\int_1^e (\ln x)^2dx$.

Using known antiderivative $x[(\ln x)^2-2\ln x+2]$, value is $\pi(e-2)$.

Practice 16

Set up (do not evaluate) both washer and shell integrals for rotating region under $y=x^2$ on $[0,3]$ about line $x=4$.

Solution

Shells (in $x$): radius $4-x$, height $x^2$: $$V=2\pi\int_0^3 (4-x)x^2\,dx.$$

Washers (in $y$): need $x=\sqrt y$, with horizontal slices from $x=\sqrt y$ to $x=3$: $$V=\pi\int_0^9\Big[(4-\sqrt y)^2-(4-3)^2\Big]dy.$$

Practice 17

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^3\theta\,d\theta$ and use it to find area enclosed by one loop of $r=2\sin\theta$ in $[0,\pi]$.

Solution

$\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^3\theta d\theta=\frac23$.

For $r=2\sin\theta$, enclosed loop on $[0,\pi]$: $$A=\frac12\int_0^\pi 4\sin^2\theta\,d\theta=2\cdot\frac{\pi}{2}=\pi.$$

Practice 18

Compute the volume generated by rotating region under $y=\frac1{1+x^2}$ on $[0,1]$ about the $y$-axis using shells.

Solution

$V=2\pi\int_0^1 \frac{x}{1+x^2}\,dx=2\pi\cdot\frac12\ln(1+x^2)\Big|_0^1=\pi\ln2$.

Practice 19

Challenge: region between $y=x$ and $y=2x$ on $[0,1]$ rotated about $x$-axis. Compare shell vs washer setup and evaluate via washers.

Solution

Washers are direct in $x$: $R=2x,\;r=x$.

$V=\pi\int_0^1[(2x)^2-x^2]dx=3\pi\int_0^1x^2dx=\pi$.

Practice 20

For $r=1-\cos\theta$, find enclosed area and compare to cardioid $r=1+\cos\theta$ area.

Solution

$A=\frac12\int_0^{2\pi}(1-\cos\theta)^2d\theta=\frac12(2\pi+\pi)=\frac{3\pi}{2}$.

Same as $1+\cos\theta$ by symmetry/phase shift.

Practice 21

Method-choice question: region under $y=e^{-x^2}$ on $[0,1]$ rotated about y-axis. Write the exact shell integral and state why washers are less natural.

Solution

Shells: $$V=2\pi\int_0^1 x e^{-x^2}dx=\pi(1-e^{-1}).$$

Washers require inverting $y=e^{-x^2}$ to $x=\sqrt{-\ln y}$ and splitting in $y$, which is less direct.
