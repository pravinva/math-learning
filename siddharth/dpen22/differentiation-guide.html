Beginner Glossary — Greek Letters & Symbols

Keep this nearby while studying. These symbols appear repeatedly across limits, differentiation, and integration.

Core Symbols + Standard Form

In mathematical logic and set theory, "for every" is expressed using the universal quantifier \(\forall\). It translates to "for all," "for each," or "for any". When applied to a specific set, it is combined with the set membership symbol \(\in\) to restrict the domain.

The Core Symbols
\(\forall\): The universal quantifier. It asserts that the following statement is true for every element in the given domain.
\(\in\): Set membership. It indicates that an element belongs to a specified set.
\(\subset\) or \(\subseteq\): Subset. Indicates that all elements of one set are completely contained within another.

How to Write "For Every" in a Set
To state that a condition is true for every element in a specific set, you combine the universal quantifier with the set membership symbol.

§3.1
First Principles & Basic Rules
Before using any rule, understand what a derivative actually is: the slope of the tangent to a curve at a point, found by making the secant gap shrink to zero.
What Is a Derivative?

Imagine drawing a straight line (a secant) between two points on a curve: \((x,\,f(x))\) and \((x+h,\,f(x+h))\). The slope of that secant is \(\dfrac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\). As you move the second point closer and closer to the first (i.e. as \(h\to0\)), the secant rotates toward the tangent line at \((x,f(x))\). The slope of that tangent is the derivative.

Geometrically: the derivative is the instantaneous slope of the curve. Physically: it is the instantaneous rate of change — velocity if \(f\) is position, for example.

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \]
This is the definition. Every differentiation rule is a shortcut derived from this limit. Understanding the definition tells you what you are computing, not just how.
The Three Notations — Use All Fluently

Three notations are used in different contexts, and you must switch between them without hesitation:

Lagrange: \(f'(x)\) or \(y'\)
Leibniz: \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) or \(\dfrac{d}{dx}[y]\)
Operator: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}[f(x)]\)

Leibniz notation \(dy/dx\) is particularly useful because it reminds you that the derivative is a ratio of infinitesimal changes — and it makes the chain rule look like fraction cancellation: \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{dy}{du}\cdot\dfrac{du}{dx}\).

The Power Rule and Basic Rules

The power rule is the workhorse for polynomials, surds, and negative powers. It applies to any real exponent — not just positive integers.

\[ \frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = nx^{n-1} \quad \text{for any real } n \]
Rules that build on the power rule
  • Constant rule: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}[c]=0\) — constants have zero rate of change
  • Scalar multiple: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}[cf]=cf'\) — constants factor out
  • Sum/difference: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}[f\pm g]=f'\pm g'\) — differentiate term by term
  • Rewrite first: \(\sqrt{x}=x^{1/2}\), \;\dfrac{1}{x^3}=x^{-3}\), \;\sqrt[4]{x^3}=x^{3/4}\) — convert before differentiating
The sum rule means differentiating a polynomial is just applying the power rule to each term independently. This only works for sums — never for products or quotients.

§3.2
Product, Quotient & Chain Rules
When functions are multiplied, divided, or composed, simple term-by-term differentiation fails. These three rules handle each situation exactly.
Why You Cannot Differentiate Term-by-Term

It is tempting to assume \(\dfrac{d}{dx}[f(x)\cdot g(x)] = f'(x)\cdot g'(x)\). This is wrong. A simple check: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}[x\cdot x]=\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^2]=2x\), but \(1\cdot1=1\neq2x\). The product rule is needed.

Product Rule

Think of \(u\) and \(v\) as two lengths of a rectangle. If both change, the area changes by \(u'v\) (one side growing while the other stays) plus \(uv'\) (the other side growing). This geometric intuition gives:

\[ \frac{d}{dx}[uv] = u'v + uv' \]
Method: Write down \(u,\;v,\;u',\;v'\) before substituting. Skipping this step causes sign errors every time.
Quotient Rule

The quotient rule is derived by applying the product rule to \(u\cdot v^{-1}\) and using the chain rule on \(v^{-1}\). The result — bottom times derivative of top, minus top times derivative of bottom, all over bottom squared:

\[ \frac{d}{dx}\!\left[\frac{u}{v}\right] = \frac{u'v - uv'}{v^2} \]
The order matters in the numerator: it is \(u'v - uv'\), not \(uv'-u'v\). A wrong sign here negates your entire answer.
Chain Rule — Differentiating Composite Functions

A composite function has the form \(f(g(x))\) — a function of a function. Examples: \(\sin(x^2)\), \(e^{3x}\), \((2x+1)^5\), \(\ln(\cos x)\). The chain rule says: differentiate the outer function (leaving the inner intact), then multiply by the derivative of the inner function.

This is where most errors happen — students differentiate the outer but forget to multiply by the inner derivative, or they differentiate the inner and forget the outer.

\[ \frac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x) \]
Recognising chain rule situations
  • Any \([{\rm expression}]^n\) where the expression isn't just \(x\) → chain rule
  • \(e^{{\rm anything}}\), \(\sin({\rm anything})\), \(\ln({\rm anything})\) → chain rule
  • Nested composites: apply chain rule layer by layer from outside in
  • Chain + product: when both structures appear, apply both simultaneously

§3.3
Trig, Exponential & Log Derivatives
The full derivative table — and understanding why each result holds. Combined with the chain rule, these cover every function you will encounter in engineering mathematics.
Derivatives of Trigonometric Functions

The sine and cosine derivatives can be proved from first principles using the standard limits \(\lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{\sin h}{h}=1\) and \(\lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{1-\cos h}{h}=0\). The key result is that differentiation cycles through the trig functions:

\(\sin x \to \cos x \to -\sin x \to -\cos x \to \sin x \to \cdots\) (period 4 derivatives)

The remaining four trig derivatives are derived by applying the quotient rule to their definitions.

\(f(x)\)\(f'(x)\)\(f(x)\)\(f'(x)\)
\(\sin x\)\(\cos x\)\(\cos x\)\(-\sin x\)
\(\tan x\)\(\sec^2 x\)\(\cot x\)\(-\csc^2 x\)
\(\sec x\)\(\sec x\tan x\)\(\csc x\)\(-\csc x\cot x\)
Derivatives of Exponential Functions

The number \(e\approx2.718\) is defined precisely so that \(\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^x]=e^x\) — it is the unique base for which the exponential function is its own derivative. This self-derivative property makes \(e^x\) appear throughout physics, engineering, and finance.

For other bases \(a>0,\,a\neq1\): writing \(a^x=e^{x\ln a}\) and applying the chain rule gives the factor \(\ln a\).

\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^x]=e^x\)
\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^{f(x)}]=f'(x)e^{f(x)}\)
\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[a^x]=a^x\ln a\)
Derivatives of Logarithmic Functions

The natural log \(\ln x\) has derivative \(1/x\) — this is the special case that fills the gap in the power rule, since \(\int x^{-1}\,dx\) is not \(x^0/0\). It answers the question "what function has derivative \(1/x\)?"

Logarithmic differentiation is a technique for functions of the form \(y=[f(x)]^{g(x)}\) (power and exponent both functions of \(x\), like \(y=x^x\)). Take \(\ln\) of both sides, differentiate implicitly, then solve for \(dy/dx\).

\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[\ln x]=\dfrac{1}{x}\)
\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[\ln f(x)]=\dfrac{f'(x)}{f(x)}\)
\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[\log_a x]=\dfrac{1}{x\ln a}\)

§3.4
Implicit Differentiation & Higher-Order Derivatives
When a curve cannot be expressed as \(y=f(x)\) — or when you need the rate of change of the rate of change — these techniques apply.
When to Use Implicit Differentiation

Some curves are defined by equations involving both \(x\) and \(y\) where solving for \(y\) is impossible or produces multiple branches. Examples: \(x^2+y^2=25\) (circle), \(x^3+y^3=6xy\) (folium of Descartes), \(\sin(xy)=x+y\).

The key insight is that every \(y\) in the equation is a function of \(x\). Differentiate both sides of the equation with respect to \(x\), applying the chain rule to every \(y\) term: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}[y^n]=ny^{n-1}\dfrac{dy}{dx}\).

Implicit differentiation procedure
  • Differentiate every term on both sides w.r.t. \(x\)
  • Every \(y\) term picks up a factor of \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) via the chain rule
  • Collect all \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) terms on one side of the equation
  • Factor out \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) and solve algebraically
  • The result may contain both \(x\) and \(y\) — that is normal and correct
Higher-Order Derivatives

The second derivative \(f''(x)=\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}\) is the derivative of the derivative — the rate of change of the slope. It tells you whether the curve is bending upward (concave up) or downward (concave down), and is central to classifying stationary points.

For \(y=e^{ax}\): each differentiation multiplies by \(a\), so \(y^{(n)}=a^ne^{ax}\). For \(y=\sin(ax)\): the pattern has period 4 — \(\sin\to a\cos\to-a^2\sin\to-a^3\cos\to a^4\sin\to\cdots\)

\(f''(x)=\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}\) — second derivative
\(f^{(n)}(x)=\dfrac{d^ny}{dx^n}\) — \(n\)-th derivative
The second derivative determines concavity: \(f''>0\) means the curve is concave up (like a cup); \(f''<0\) means concave down (like a cap). This will be central to classifying stationary points in §3.5.

§3.5
Stationary Points, Increasing/Decreasing & Concavity
A stationary point is where the slope is zero. But knowing the slope is zero tells you almost nothing on its own — you need the sign of the derivative on either side, or the second derivative, to know what type of point it is.
Increasing and Decreasing Intervals

The derivative tells you the slope of the tangent. If the tangent is sloping upward (\(f'>0\)), the function is increasing — moving right takes you to higher values. If the tangent slopes downward (\(f'<0\)), the function is decreasing.

To find these intervals: differentiate, set \(f'(x)=0\) to find the boundary points (critical points), then test the sign of \(f'\) in each resulting interval using any convenient test value.

\(f'(x)>0\) on \((a,b)\) → increasing on \((a,b)\)
\(f'(x)<0\) on \((a,b)\) → decreasing on \((a,b)\)
What Is a Stationary Point?
Core Definition

A stationary point of \(f\) is a point where \(f'(x)=0\) — that is, where the tangent line is perfectly horizontal. The function is momentarily "flat" there: it is neither increasing nor decreasing at that exact point.

A broader category is a critical point: where \(f'(x)=0\) or where \(f'(x)\) does not exist (corner, cusp, vertical tangent). All stationary points are critical points; not all critical points are stationary points.

Finding a stationary point tells you where the slope is zero. It does not tell you why the slope is zero. There are three fundamentally different reasons:

Type 1 — Local Maximum

The function rises to a peak and then falls away on the other side. The slope changes from positive → zero → negative. Visually, this is a hill. The value \(f(c)\) is greater than all nearby values.

This is the most common mistake: students find \(f'(c)=0\) and automatically write "local max/min" without checking. You must check the sign of \(f'\) on both sides (or use the second derivative test) to determine which type.
The Two Classification Tests
Test 1 — First Derivative Test (always works)

After finding a critical point \(c\), check the sign of \(f'(x)\) for values slightly less than \(c\) and slightly greater than \(c\). Construct a sign chart. The sign change (or lack thereof) tells you the type.

This test always works — it is the reliable method. Use it when the second derivative test fails or is inconvenient.

Test 2 — Second Derivative Test (quick, but can fail)

At a critical point \(c\) where \(f'(c)=0\):

\(f''(c)<0\) → local maximum (curve bends down)
\(f''(c)>0\) → local minimum (curve bends up)
\(f''(c)=0\) → inconclusive — use Test 1
Why \(f''(c)=0\) is inconclusive

Consider \(f(x)=x^4\) at \(x=0\): \(f'(0)=0\) and \(f''(0)=0\). Yet \(x=0\) is a local minimum. Now consider \(f(x)=x^3\) at \(x=0\): again \(f'(0)=0\) and \(f''(0)=0\), but \(x=0\) is a stationary inflection, not a minimum. The second derivative test cannot distinguish these cases — only the first derivative test can.

Concavity and Inflection Points

Concavity describes how the slope itself is changing. If the slope is increasing (the curve bends upward, like a cup), the function is concave up — and \(f''>0\). If the slope is decreasing (the curve bends downward, like a cap), the function is concave down — and \(f''<0\).

An inflection point is where the concavity changes — from concave up to concave down or vice versa. At an inflection point, \(f''=0\) (usually), but not every point where \(f''=0\) is an inflection point — you must verify that \(f''\) actually changes sign there.

Inflection points may or may not be stationary points. A stationary inflection is a point that is both (\(f'=0\) and \(f''\) changes sign). A non-stationary inflection has \(f'\neq0\).

\(f''(x)>0\) → concave up (cup shape)
\(f''(x)<0\) → concave down (cap shape)

§3.6
Optimisation & Curve Sketching
Differentiation's ultimate application: find the best value of something, or build a complete picture of any function from scratch. Both require the same toolkit — just applied in different order.
Optimisation — Five-Step Procedure

Optimisation means finding the maximum or minimum value of a quantity subject to a constraint. Every problem follows the same five steps — skip one and you will lose marks.

The 5-step procedure
  • Step 1 — Define variables. Draw a diagram. Label every relevant length, area, or quantity with a letter.
  • Step 2 — Write the objective function. Express the quantity you want to maximise or minimise as a function (it will initially have two or more variables).
  • Step 3 — Apply the constraint. Use any given equation (perimeter, volume, budget, etc.) to eliminate one variable. You must reduce to a single-variable function.
  • Step 4 — Differentiate, set to zero, solve. Find all critical points in the valid domain.
  • Step 5 — Verify and state the answer. Use the second derivative or endpoint comparison to confirm it is a max or min. State the answer with units and interpret it in context.
The most common error: Step 3 is skipped, leaving a function of two variables. You cannot differentiate \(A(x,y)\) and set it to zero — you must eliminate one variable first.
Curve Sketching — Full Analysis Checklist

A complete curve sketch uses every technique from this topic in a systematic sequence. The order matters — asymptotes must come before you draw branches, critical points before you draw peaks and troughs.

Checklist (in order)
  • Domain — where is \(f(x)\) defined? Exclude zeros of denominators, negatives under even roots.
  • Intercepts — set \(x=0\) for \(y\)-intercept; set \(y=0\) for \(x\)-intercepts (may require factoring or numerical methods).
  • Asymptotes — vertical: where denominator\(=0\); horizontal: limits as \(x\to\pm\infty\); oblique: long division if degree of numerator exceeds denominator by 1.
  • Sign of \(f'\) — find critical points, build sign chart, identify increasing/decreasing intervals.
  • Critical points — classify each as local max, local min, or stationary inflection.
  • Sign of \(f''\) — find inflection points, identify concave up/down intervals.
  • Sketch — plot key points, draw through with correct shape, direction, and concavity.

Complete a full curve analysis and sketch for \(f(x)=x^3-3x\): find domain, intercepts, critical points (with classification), concavity and inflection, then describe the sketch.
▶
COMPLETE ANALYSIS
1
Domain: all real numbers.
2
Intercepts: \(y(0)=0\). \(x(x^2-3)=0\): \(x=0,\pm\sqrt{3}\approx\pm1.73\).
3
Critical points: \(f'=3x^2-3=3(x-1)(x+1)=0\) at \(x=\pm1\). \(f(-1)=2\) (local max), \(f(1)=-2\) (local min) — confirmed by \(f''=6x\): \(f''(-1)=-6<0\), \(f''(1)=6>0\).
4
Inflection: \(f''=6x=0\) at \(x=0\). Sign changes → inflection at \((0,0)\). Concave down \(x<0\), concave up \(x>0\).
5
Ends: \(f\to-\infty\) as \(x\to-\infty\); \(f\to+\infty\) as \(x\to+\infty\). Classic S-shaped cubic.
ANSWER Local max \((-1,2)\), local min \((1,-2)\), inflection \((0,0)\), zeros at \(0,\pm\sqrt{3}\)

A window consists of a rectangle topped by a semicircle of radius \(r\). Total perimeter is 12 m. Find \(r\) to maximise the total area.
▶
1
Width \(=2r\), rectangle height \(=h\). Perimeter: \(2r+2h+\pi r=12 \Rightarrow h=\dfrac{12-r(2+\pi)}{2}\).
2
\(A=2rh+\tfrac{\pi r^2}{2}=2r\!\cdot\!\dfrac{12-r(2+\pi)}{2}+\tfrac{\pi r^2}{2}=12r-r^2(2+\pi)+\tfrac{\pi r^2}{2}=12r-r^2\!\left(2+\dfrac{\pi}{2}\right)\)
3
\(\dfrac{dA}{dr}=12-2r\!\left(2+\dfrac{\pi}{2}\right)=0 \Rightarrow r=\dfrac{12}{4+\pi}\approx1.68\) m.
ANSWER \(r=\dfrac{12}{4+\pi}\approx1.68\) m

Find the dimensions of the cheapest open-top cylindrical can (no lid) with volume \(250\pi\) cm³, given base costs \$3/cm² and the side costs \$1/cm².
▶
1
Constraint: \(\pi r^2h=250\pi \Rightarrow h=250/r^2\).
2
\(C=3\pi r^2+2\pi r\cdot\dfrac{250}{r^2}=3\pi r^2+\dfrac{500\pi}{r}\)
3
\(\dfrac{dC}{dr}=6\pi r-\dfrac{500\pi}{r^2}=0 \Rightarrow 6r^3=500 \Rightarrow r=\sqrt[3]{250/3}\approx4.57\) cm; \(h\approx11.97\) cm.
ANSWER \(r=\sqrt[3]{250/3}\approx4.57\) cm, \(h\approx11.97\) cm
7
Perform a full curve analysis of \(f(x)=\dfrac{x^2-4}{x-1}\), finding domain, asymptotes (including oblique), stationary points, and concavity.
▶
1
Domain: \(x\neq1\). Intercepts: \(x=\pm2\), \(y=4\). VA: \(x=1\).
2
Oblique asymptote: long division: \(x^2-4=(x-1)(x+1)-3\), so \(f(x)=x+1-\dfrac{3}{x-1}\). OA: \(y=x+1\).
3
\(f'=1+\dfrac{3}{(x-1)^2}>0\) always. No stationary points — strictly increasing on each branch.
4
\(f''=-\dfrac{6}{(x-1)^3}\). Concave up for \(x<1\), concave down for \(x>1\). No inflection (function not defined at \(x=1\)).
ANSWER VA: \(x=1\). Oblique asymptote: \(y=x+1\). Strictly increasing; concave up on \((-\infty,1)\), concave down on \((1,\infty)\).

A rectangle is inscribed in a circle of radius 5. Find the dimensions that maximise the rectangle's area.
▶
1
Let half-width \(=x\), half-height \(=\sqrt{25-x^2}\). Area \(A=4x\sqrt{25-x^2}\).
2
\(\dfrac{dA}{dx}=4\!\cdot\!\dfrac{25-2x^2}{\sqrt{25-x^2}}=0 \Rightarrow x^2=25/2 \Rightarrow x=5/\sqrt{2}\).
3
Both sides \(=2x=5\sqrt{2}\). Square with area 50.
ANSWER Square with side \(5\sqrt{2}\approx7.07\) m; area \(=50\) m²
P2
Sketch \(f(x)=\dfrac{1}{x^2-1}\): find domain, asymptotes, stationary points, and concavity.
▶
1
Domain: \(x\neq\pm1\). HA: \(y\to0\) as \(x\to\pm\infty\). VAs at \(x=\pm1\).
2
\(f'=\dfrac{-2x}{(x^2-1)^2}=0\) at \(x=0\). \(f(0)=-1\).
3
Sign of \(f'\): \(+\) for \(x<0\) (not at asymptotes), \(-\) for \(x>0\). So \(x=0\) is a local max at \((0,-1)\).
ANSWER VAs \(x=\pm1\), HA \(y=0\), local max \((0,-1)\).
P3
An object has position \(s(t)=\tfrac{1}{3}t^3-2t^2+3t+1\) m, \(t\geq0\). Find when it has maximum velocity and find that maximum velocity.
▶
1
Velocity: \(v=s'=t^2-4t+3\). Acceleration: \(a=v'=2t-4=0 \Rightarrow t=2\).
2
\(v''=2>0\) → local minimum of \(v\), not maximum. Check endpoints: \(v(0)=3\), \(v(2)=-1\). As \(t\to\infty\): \(v\to\infty\). So velocity has no maximum — it grows without bound.
3
Minimum velocity is \(v(2)=-1\) m/s (momentarily moving backwards). Maximum speed on \([0,3]\): \(v(0)=3\) m/s.
ANSWER Minimum velocity \(-1\) m/s at \(t=2\). Velocity is unbounded above — it has no maximum.
