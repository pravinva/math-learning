§2.1
Concept of a Limit
A limit is about approach, not arrival. \(f(a)\) may be undefined, or differ from the limit — what matters is the value \(f(x)\) gets arbitrarily close to as \(x\to a\).
Intuitive Definition

We write \(\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L\) when \(f(x)\) can be made as close to \(L\) as desired by taking \(x\) sufficiently close to \(a\) (but \(x\neq a\)). The value \(f(a)\) is irrelevant.

\[ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = L \;\text{ and }\; \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = L \]
One-sided limits
  • Left-hand limit \(\lim_{x\to a^-}\): \(x\) approaches \(a\) from values less than \(a\)
  • Right-hand limit \(\lim_{x\to a^+}\): \(x\) approaches \(a\) from values greater than \(a\)
  • Two-sided limit exists \(\iff\) both one-sided limits exist and are equal
  • If LHL \(\neq\) RHL: write "limit does not exist (DNE)" and state each one-sided value
Reading limits from graphs — three cases
  • Hole (removable): limit exists, \(f(a)\) undefined or \(\neq L\)
  • Jump: LHL \(\neq\) RHL — two-sided limit DNE
  • Vertical asymptote: one or both one-sided limits are \(\pm\infty\)
The limit \(\lim_{x\to a}f(x)\) has nothing to do with \(f(a)\). A function can have a limit at a point where it is undefined, and can be defined at a point where its value differs from the limit.

One-sided limits; finding parameters; absolute value
4
Find \(\lim_{x\to0^-}\dfrac{|x|}{x}\) and \(\lim_{x\to0^+}\dfrac{|x|}{x}\). Does the two-sided limit exist?
▶

For \(x<0\): \(|x|=-x\), so \(\dfrac{|x|}{x}=\dfrac{-x}{x}=-1\). LHL \(=-1\).
2
For \(x>0\): \(|x|=x\), so \(\dfrac{|x|}{x}=1\). RHL \(=1\).

LHL \(\neq\) RHL \(\Rightarrow\) two-sided limit DNE.

For \(f(x)=\begin{cases}3x-1 & x<2\\x^2+k & x\geq2\end{cases}\), find \(k\) so that \(\lim_{x\to2}f(x)\) exists.
▶

LHL: \(\lim_{x\to2^-}(3x-1)=5\)
2
RHL: \(\lim_{x\to2^+}(x^2+k)=4+k\)
3
For limit to exist: \(4+k=5 \Rightarrow k=1\)
ANSWER \(k=1\)

Evaluate \(\lim_{x\to3^+}\dfrac{x-3}{|x-3|}\) and \(\lim_{x\to3^-}\dfrac{x-3}{|x-3|}\).

For \(x>3\): \(|x-3|=x-3\), so \(\dfrac{x-3}{x-3}=1\). RHL \(=1\).

▶
1
By limit laws (substituting the known limits): numerator \(=6^2+(-3)=33\)
2
Denominator \(=6-(-3)=9\). Answer \(=33/9=11/3\).

LEVEL 3
Parameter problems; floor function; composite limits
8
For \(f(x)=\begin{cases}\sin(\pi x) & x<1\\ax^2+b & x\geq1\end{cases}\), find a relationship between \(a\) and \(b\) so that \(\lim_{x\to1}f(x)\) exists and equals \(f(1)\).
▶
1
LHL: \(\lim_{x\to1^-}\sin(\pi x)=\sin(\pi)=0\)
2
RHL and \(f(1)\): \(a(1)^2+b=a+b\). For limit to exist and equal \(f(1)\): \(a+b=0\).
ANSWER \(a+b=0\) (i.e. \(b=-a\)). Any pair satisfying this works, e.g. \(a=1,b=-1\).
9
For what values of \(a\) does \(\lim_{x\to a}\lfloor x\rfloor\) not exist? (Here \(\lfloor x\rfloor\) is the floor/greatest-integer function.)
▶
1
At non-integers: \(\lfloor x\rfloor\) is constant near \(a\), so LHL \(=\) RHL \(=\lfloor a\rfloor\). Limit exists.
2
At integers \(n\): approaching from left, \(\lfloor x\rfloor=n-1\). Approaching from right, \(\lfloor x\rfloor=n\). LHL \(\neq\) RHL.
ANSWER The limit DNE for all integer values of \(a\).
10
For \(f(x)=\begin{cases}ax+b & x<-1\\x^2 & -1\leq x\leq1\\cx-1 & x>1\end{cases}\), find \(a,b,c\) so that \(f\) is continuous at both \(x=-1\) and \(x=1\).
▶
1
At \(x=-1\): \(f(-1)=1\). LHL: \(-a+b=1\). — (i)
2
At \(x=1\): \(f(1)=1\). RHL: \(c-1=1 \Rightarrow c=2\).
3
From (i): \(-a+b=1\). This one equation has infinitely many solutions. We need an extra condition — e.g. if also continuous as \(x\to-\infty\), we might set \(a=1\): then \(b=2\).
ANSWER \(c=2\); \(-a+b=1\) (e.g. \(a=1,b=2\)).
11
If \(\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=4\) and \(\lim_{x\to a}g(x)=0\), what can you say about \(\lim_{x\to a}\dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)}\)?
▶
1
The quotient rule for limits requires the denominator limit to be non-zero. Here denominator \(\to0\).
2
Depending on the sign of \(g(x)\) near \(a\), the ratio may \(\to+\infty\), \(-\infty\), or have different one-sided limits — the two-sided limit DNE (or is infinite).
ANSWER The limit DNE (or is \(\pm\infty\)) — the quotient rule does not apply when the denominator limit is zero.
12
Evaluate \(\lim_{x\to2^-}\dfrac{1}{x-2}\) and \(\lim_{x\to2^+}\dfrac{1}{x-2}\). What does this tell you about the graph?
▶
1
For \(x\to2^-\): \(x-2\to0^-\), so \(\dfrac{1}{x-2}\to-\infty\).
2
For \(x\to2^+\): \(x-2\to0^+\), so \(\dfrac{1}{x-2}\to+\infty\).
3
The graph has a vertical asymptote at \(x=2\), going to \(-\infty\) on the left and \(+\infty\) on the right.
ANSWER LHL \(=-\infty\), RHL \(=+\infty\). Vertical asymptote at \(x=2\).

§2.2
Algebraic Evaluation of Limits
Five techniques in order of preference: direct substitution → limit laws → factoring → conjugate → standard trig limits. Try the simplest first; move on only when it fails.
The Five Methods
Decision flowchart
  • Step 1 — Direct substitution. Substitute \(x=a\). If you get a real number, done. If you get \(0/0\) or \(\infty/\infty\), proceed.
  • Step 2 — Limit laws. Break into sum/product/quotient, provided denominator \(\neq0\).
  • Step 3 — Factor & cancel. Factor numerator and/or denominator; cancel the common \((x-a)\) factor; substitute.
  • Step 4 — Conjugate. Multiply by the conjugate of the surd expression; simplify; substitute.
  • Step 5 — Standard trig limit. Rewrite to use \(\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{\sin x}{x}=1\).
\[ \lim_{x\to0}\frac{\sin x}{x}=1 \qquad \lim_{x\to0}\frac{\tan x}{x}=1 \qquad \lim_{x\to0}\frac{1-\cos x}{x}=0 \qquad \lim_{x\to0}\frac{1-\cos x}{x^2}=\tfrac{1}{2} \]
Getting \(0/0\) after direct substitution is an indeterminate form — it does not mean the limit is zero. It means more work is required. Getting \(4/0\) (non-zero over zero) means the limit is \(\pm\infty\) or DNE.
Interactive — \(f(x)=\dfrac{x^2-a^2}{x-a}=x+a\) — factoring resolves the hole

Conjugate method; standard trig limits
5
Evaluate \(\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{\sqrt{x+9}-3}{x}\).
Multiply by conjugate \(\dfrac{\sqrt{x+9}+3}{\sqrt{x+9}+3}\).
▶

\(\dfrac{\sqrt{x+9}-3}{x}\cdot\dfrac{\sqrt{x+9}+3}{\sqrt{x+9}+3}=\dfrac{(x+9)-9}{x(\sqrt{x+9}+3)}=\dfrac{x}{x(\sqrt{x+9}+3)}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{x+9}+3}\)

\(\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{x+9}+3}=\dfrac{1}{3+3}=\dfrac{1}{6}\)

Evaluate \(\lim_{x\to4}\dfrac{2-\sqrt{x}}{4-x}\).
▶

Note \(4-x=-(x-4)=-({\sqrt{x}}-2)(\sqrt{x}+2)\). So \(\dfrac{2-\sqrt{x}}{4-x}=\dfrac{-({\sqrt{x}}-2)}{-({\sqrt{x}}-2)(\sqrt{x}+2)}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{x}+2}\)

\(\lim_{x\to4}\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{x}+2}=\dfrac{1}{4}\)

Evaluate \(\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{\sin(5x)}{3x}\).
▶

Use the identity \(1-\cos x=2\sin^2(x/2)\).

As \(x\to0\): \(x/2\to0\), so \(\left(\dfrac{\sin(x/2)}{x/2}\right)^2\to1^2=1\). Limit \(=\dfrac{1}{2}\).

9
Evaluate \(\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{\tan(3x)}{\sin(5x)}\).
▶
1
Rewrite: \(\dfrac{\tan(3x)}{\sin(5x)}=\dfrac{\tan(3x)}{3x}\cdot\dfrac{5x}{\sin(5x)}\cdot\dfrac{3x}{5x}=\dfrac{\tan(3x)}{3x}\cdot\dfrac{5x}{\sin(5x)}\cdot\dfrac{3}{5}\)
2
As \(x\to0\): both standard limits go to \(1\). Answer \(=1\cdot1\cdot\dfrac{3}{5}=\dfrac{3}{5}\).
ANSWER \(\dfrac{3}{5}\)

Harder algebra; general forms; combined methods

▶
1
\(\dfrac{\sin(ax)}{\sin(bx)}=\dfrac{\sin(ax)}{ax}\cdot\dfrac{bx}{\sin(bx)}\cdot\dfrac{a}{b}\)

Evaluate \(\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{\tan x - \sin x}{x^3}\).
▶
1
\(\tan x-\sin x=\dfrac{\sin x}{\cos x}-\sin x=\sin x\!\left(\dfrac{1}{\cos x}-1\right)=\dfrac{\sin x(1-\cos x)}{\cos x}\)

§2.3
Limits at Infinity, Asymptotes & Continuity
As \(x\to\pm\infty\) every power of \(1/x\) vanishes. Three conditions must all hold for continuity. The squeeze theorem handles limits where direct evaluation is impossible.
Limits at Infinity

Divide numerator and denominator by the highest power of \(x\) in the denominator. Every term \(c/x^n\to0\) as \(x\to\pm\infty\) for \(n>0\).

Degrees of \(P\) vs \(Q\) in \(P(x)/Q(x)\)Horizontal asymptote
\(\deg P < \deg Q\)\(y=0\)
\(\deg P = \deg Q\)\(y=\) ratio of leading coefficients
\(\deg P > \deg Q\)No horizontal asymptote (\(\to\pm\infty\))
Three Conditions for Continuity at \(x=a\)
① \(f(a)\) is defined
② \(\lim_{x\to a}f(x)\) exists
③ \(\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=f(a)\)
Failing any one of the three conditions makes \(f\) discontinuous at \(a\). State which condition fails when classifying a discontinuity.
Squeeze Theorem
\[\text{If } g(x)\leq f(x)\leq h(x) \text{ near } a \text{ and } \lim_{x\to a}g(x)=\lim_{x\to a}h(x)=L, \text{ then } \lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L\]
Classic pattern: bound \(f(x)\) using \(-|{\rm something}|\leq f(x)\leq|{\rm something}|\) when the oscillating part is bounded but the decaying factor forces the product to zero.
Interactive — \(f(x)=\dfrac{ax+b}{cx+d}\): horizontal and vertical asymptotes
\(a\) 2.0
\(b\) 1.0
\(c\) 1.0
\(d\) -2.0
