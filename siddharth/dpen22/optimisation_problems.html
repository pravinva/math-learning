Optimisation Problems

Six fully worked examples spanning the major problem types, followed by twenty practice word problems with a hidden answer key — try each one before revealing the answer.

Formulas worth knowing cold

These are the building blocks almost every optimisation word problem is assembled from. None of them involve calculus — they're just geometry and basic relations — but you need to reach for the right one instantly to set up the objective function and constraint.

Rectangles & boxes
Perimeter of a rectangle: $P=2l+2w$
Area of a rectangle: $A=lw$
Volume of a rectangular box: $V=lwh$
Surface area of a rectangular box: $S=2lw+2lh+2wh$

Circles, semicircles & cylinders
Circumference: $C=2\pi r$
Area of a circle: $A=\pi r^2$
Area of a semicircle: $A=\dfrac12\pi r^2$; arc length of a semicircle: $\pi r$
Volume of a cylinder: $V=\pi r^2h$
Total surface area of a cylinder: $S=2\pi r^2+2\pi rh$ (two circular ends $+$ the curved side)

Cones & spheres
Volume of a cone: $V=\dfrac13\pi r^2h$
Volume of a sphere: $V=\dfrac43\pi r^3$
Surface area of a sphere: $S=4\pi r^2$

Triangles & distances
Area of a triangle: $A=\dfrac12bh$
Pythagorean theorem (right triangles, and any straight-line distance problem): $a^2+b^2=c^2$
Distance between two points: $d=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}$

Economics quantities
Revenue: $R=pq$ (price $\times$ quantity)
Profit: $P=R-C$ (revenue minus cost)
Average cost: $\bar C(q)=\dfrac{C(q)}{q}$

The general five-step method

Every optimisation word problem, regardless of the setting, reduces to the same procedure. The hard part is almost always step 2 — translating words into a function — not the calculus itself.

The five steps

  1. Draw a diagram and label variables. Give every changing quantity a letter. A picture makes the relationships between quantities visible.
  2. Write the objective function — the quantity you're maximising or minimising — in terms of your variables.
  3. Find a constraint equation that relates your variables (a fixed perimeter, a fixed volume, a fixed cost), and use it to eliminate all but one variable from the objective function, so you end up with a function of a single variable.
  4. Differentiate, set the derivative to zero, and solve for the critical point(s). This gives the candidate value(s) for the optimum.
  5. Verify it's actually a max or min (using the second derivative test, or by checking the sign of the first derivative either side of the critical point, or by checking endpoints of a restricted domain), then answer the original question in context — with correct units.

Step 5 is easy to skip under exam pressure, but it matters: a critical point is only a candidate until you've confirmed which kind it is.

Recognising the constraint

Almost every optimisation problem hides its constraint in a phrase like "with 200m of fencing," "must hold 1 litre," or "costs \$6/m2 to build." That phrase is the equation that lets you eliminate a variable. Find it first, before worrying about the objective function's derivative.

Type 1 · Fixed perimeter, maximise area

The classic fencing problem

A farmer has 200 m of fencing and wants to enclose a rectangular field. What dimensions maximise the enclosed area?

Worked Example 1
maximise area given fixed perimeter
variables Let $x$ and $y$ be the side lengths of the rectangle.
constraint Perimeter: $2x+2y=200 \Rightarrow y=100-x$
objective function $A(x)=xy=x(100-x)=100x-x^2$, for $0 < x < 100$
differentiate & solve $A'(x)=100-2x=0 \Rightarrow x=50$
verify $A''(x)=-2<0$ for all $x$, so this is a maximum (the graph of $A$ is a downward parabola).
answer in context $y=100-50=50$. The rectangle is a $50\text{ m}\times50\text{ m}$ square.
Answer: $50\text{ m}\times50\text{ m}$ (a square), maximum area $= 2500\text{ m}^2$
Type 2 · One side free (river problem)

Fencing along a straight river

A rancher wants to fence a rectangular field bordering a straight river, so no fence is needed along the river side. With 300 m of fencing, what dimensions maximise the enclosed area?

Worked Example 2
maximise area, one side unfenced
variables Let $x$ = the side parallel to the river, $y$ = each of the two sides perpendicular to it.
constraint Only three sides need fencing: $x+2y=300 \Rightarrow x=300-2y$
objective function $A(y)=xy=(300-2y)y=300y-2y^2$, for $0 < y < 150$
differentiate & solve $A'(y)=300-4y=0 \Rightarrow y=75$
verify $A''(y)=-4<0$, confirming a maximum.
answer in context $x=300-2(75)=150$.
Answer: $150\text{ m}$ (parallel to river) $\times\ 75\text{ m}$ (perpendicular), maximum area $= 11{,}250\text{ m}^2$
Type 3 · Minimise material (container)

The cheapest cylindrical can

A cylindrical can must hold 1000 cm³ (1 litre). Find the radius and height that minimise the amount of material used (the total surface area).

Worked Example 3
minimise surface area of a can
variables Let $r$ = radius, $h$ = height.
constraint Volume: $\pi r^2 h = 1000 \Rightarrow h=\dfrac{1000}{\pi r^2}$
objective function Surface area (top + bottom + side): $S(r)=2\pi r^2+2\pi r h = 2\pi r^2 + 2\pi r\cdot\dfrac{1000}{\pi r^2}=2\pi r^2+\dfrac{2000}{r}$
differentiate & solve $S'(r)=4\pi r-\dfrac{2000}{r^2}=0 \Rightarrow 4\pi r^3=2000 \Rightarrow r^3=\dfrac{500}{\pi}$
verify $S''(r)=4\pi+\dfrac{4000}{r^3}>0$ always, confirming a minimum.
answer in context $r=\left(\dfrac{500}{\pi}\right)^{1/3}\approx5.42\text{ cm}$, and $h=\dfrac{1000}{\pi r^2}\approx10.84\text{ cm}$ — notice $h\approx2r$, the classic result for a minimal-material can.
Answer: $r\approx5.42\text{ cm}$, $h\approx10.84\text{ cm}$ (height ≈ diameter)
Type 4 · Maximise volume (box from a sheet)

The open-top box

A square piece of cardboard, 12 inches on each side, has equal squares of side $x$ cut from each corner. The sides are then folded up to form an open-top box. Find $x$ that maximises the box's volume.

Worked Example 4
maximise volume of a folded box
variables $x$ = side length of the cut squares (this becomes the box's height). The base becomes $(12-2x)$ on each side.
constraint Already built into the geometry — no separate equation needed here.
objective function $V(x)=x(12-2x)^2 = 4x^3-48x^2+144x$, for $0<x<6$
differentiate & solve $V'(x)=12x^2-96x+144=12(x^2-8x+12)=12(x-2)(x-6)=0 \Rightarrow x=2$ or $x=6$
verify $x=6$ makes the base side zero (a degenerate box, $V=0$), so it's rejected. $V''(x)=24x-96$; at $x=2$, $V''=-48<0$, confirming a maximum.
answer in context $V(2)=2(12-4)^2=2(64)=128$
Answer: cut squares of side $x=2$ inches, maximum volume $=128\text{ in}^3$
Type 5 · Minimise distance

Closest point on a curve

Find the point on the parabola $y=x^2$ that is closest to the point $(0,3)$.

Worked Example 5
minimise distance to a fixed point
variables Let $(x, x^2)$ be a general point on the parabola.
objective function Minimising distance is the same as minimising distance squared (avoids an ugly square root): $D(x)=x^2+(x^2-3)^2 = x^4-5x^2+9$
differentiate & solve $D'(x)=4x^3-10x=2x(2x^2-5)=0 \Rightarrow x=0$ or $x^2=\dfrac52$
verify $D(0)=9$ (distance $=3$). $D\left(\pm\sqrt{5/2}\right)=\left(\dfrac52\right)^2-5\left(\dfrac52\right)+9=\dfrac{11}{4}=2.75$ (distance $\approx1.66$) — smaller, so $x=0$ is a local max and $x=\pm\sqrt{5/2}$ give the minimum.
answer in context The closest points are $\left(\pm\sqrt{5/2},\ 5/2\right)$.
Answer: closest points $\left(\pm\sqrt{5/2},\dfrac52\right)\approx(\pm1.58,\ 2.5)$, minimum distance $=\dfrac{\sqrt{11}}{2}\approx1.66$
Type 6 · Maximise profit (economics)

Maximising profit from a price-demand model

A company's price-demand function is $p=50-0.5q$ (dollars per unit), and its cost function is $C(q)=200+10q$ (dollars). Find the production quantity $q$ that maximises profit.

Worked Example 6
maximise profit $=$ revenue $-$ cost
objective function Revenue $R(q)=pq=(50-0.5q)q=50q-0.5q^2$. Profit $P(q)=R(q)-C(q)=50q-0.5q^2-200-10q=40q-0.5q^2-200$
differentiate & solve $P'(q)=40-q=0 \Rightarrow q=40$
verify $P''(q)=-1<0$, confirming a maximum.
answer in context $P(40)=40(40)-0.5(1600)-200=1600-800-200=600$. Price: $p=50-0.5(40)=\$30$.
Answer: produce $q=40$ units at $\$30$ each, maximum profit $=\$600$

Practice 1 · Fixed perimeter
A rectangular garden is to be enclosed using 80 metres of fencing. Find the dimensions of the garden that will maximise the enclosed area, and state the maximum area.
variables $x,y$ = side lengths.
constraint $2x+2y=80 \Rightarrow y=40-x$
objective function $A(x)=x(40-x)=40x-x^2$, $0<x<40$
differentiate & solve $A'(x)=40-2x=0 \Rightarrow x=20$
verify $A''(x)=-2<0$, confirming a maximum.
Answer: $20\text{ m}\times20\text{ m}$ square; maximum area $=400\text{ m}^2$
Practice 2 · Fixed area, minimise perimeter
A rectangular plot of land is to be fenced so that it encloses an area of 900 square metres. Find the dimensions of the plot that will minimise the amount of fencing required, and state the minimum length of fencing needed.
variables $x,y$ = side lengths.
constraint $xy=900 \Rightarrow y=\dfrac{900}{x}$
objective function $P(x)=2x+2y=2x+\dfrac{1800}{x}$, $x>0$
differentiate & solve $P'(x)=2-\dfrac{1800}{x^2}=0 \Rightarrow x^2=900 \Rightarrow x=30$
verify $P''(x)=\dfrac{3600}{x^3}>0$ for $x>0$, confirming a minimum.
Answer: $30\text{ m}\times30\text{ m}$ square; minimum perimeter $=120\text{ m}$
Practice 3 · River fencing
A farmer has 400 metres of fencing available to enclose a rectangular plot of land that borders a straight river, so that no fencing is needed along the side facing the river. Find the dimensions of the plot that maximise the enclosed area, and state the maximum area.
variables $x$ = side parallel to the river, $y$ = each perpendicular side.
constraint $x+2y=400 \Rightarrow x=400-2y$
objective function $A(y)=(400-2y)y=400y-2y^2$, $0<y<200$
differentiate & solve $A'(y)=400-4y=0 \Rightarrow y=100$
verify $A''(y)=-4<0$, confirming a maximum.
Answer: $200\text{ m}$ (parallel to river) $\times100\text{ m}$; maximum area $=20{,}000\text{ m}^2$
Practice 4 · Divided rectangular plot
A rectangular plot of land is to be enclosed using 600 metres of fencing, and is also to be divided into two equal rectangular sections by an additional length of fencing built parallel to one pair of sides. Find the dimensions of the outer rectangle that maximise the total enclosed area, and state the maximum area.
variables $x$ = length of each of the two outer long sides, $y$ = length of each of the three cross-pieces (two ends plus one internal divider).
constraint $2x+3y=600 \Rightarrow x=\dfrac{600-3y}{2}$
objective function $A(y)=xy=\dfrac{(600-3y)y}{2}=300y-1.5y^2$, $0<y<200$
differentiate & solve $A'(y)=300-3y=0 \Rightarrow y=100$
verify $A''(y)=-3<0$, confirming a maximum.
answer in context $x=\dfrac{600-300}{2}=150$
Answer: outer sides $150\text{ m}$, cross-pieces $100\text{ m}$ each; maximum area $=15{,}000\text{ m}^2$
Practice 5 · Box, open top
An open-top box with a square base is to be constructed so that it has a volume of 32,000 cubic centimetres. Find the dimensions of the box that minimise the total amount of material used (that is, minimise the combined surface area of the base and the four sides), and state the minimum surface area.
variables $x$ = base side, $h$ = height.
constraint $x^2h=32000 \Rightarrow h=\dfrac{32000}{x^2}$
objective function Base $+$ four sides, no top: $S(x)=x^2+4xh=x^2+\dfrac{128000}{x}$
differentiate & solve $S'(x)=2x-\dfrac{128000}{x^2}=0 \Rightarrow x^3=64000 \Rightarrow x=40$
verify $S''(x)=2+\dfrac{256000}{x^3}>0$, confirming a minimum.
answer in context $h=\dfrac{32000}{1600}=20$
Answer: base $40\text{ cm}\times40\text{ cm}$, height $20\text{ cm}$; minimum surface area $=4800\text{ cm}^2$
Practice 6 · Can, mixed material cost
A cylindrical can is to be manufactured so that it holds 500 cubic centimetres. The circular top and bottom of the can are made from material costing 2 cents per square centimetre, while the curved side of the can is made from material costing 1 cent per square centimetre. Find the radius and height of the can that minimise the total cost of the material used.
variables $r$ = radius, $h$ = height.
constraint $\pi r^2h=500 \Rightarrow h=\dfrac{500}{\pi r^2}$
objective function Top $+$ bottom cost $4\pi r^2$ (two circles at 2¢ each); the side costs $2\pi rh$ (at 1¢): $C(r)=4\pi r^2+2\pi rh=4\pi r^2+\dfrac{1000}{r}$
differentiate & solve $C'(r)=8\pi r-\dfrac{1000}{r^2}=0 \Rightarrow r^3=\dfrac{1000}{8\pi}=\dfrac{125}{\pi} \Rightarrow r=\left(\dfrac{125}{\pi}\right)^{1/3}$
verify $C''(r)=8\pi+\dfrac{2000}{r^3}>0$, confirming a minimum.
answer in context $r\approx3.415\text{ cm}$, $h=\dfrac{500}{\pi r^2}\approx13.65\text{ cm}$
Answer: $r\approx3.42\text{ cm}$, $h\approx13.65\text{ cm}$
Practice 7 · Closest point, line
Find the point on the straight line given by the equation $y=2x+1$ that lies closest to the point $(4,2)$, and state the minimum distance between them.
variables A general point on the line: $(x,\ 2x+1)$.
objective function Minimise distance squared: $D(x)=(x-4)^2+(2x+1-2)^2=(x-4)^2+(2x-1)^2=5x^2-12x+17$
differentiate & solve $D'(x)=10x-12=0 \Rightarrow x=1.2$
verify $D''(x)=10>0$, confirming a minimum.
answer in context $y=2(1.2)+1=3.4$; minimum distance $=\sqrt{D(1.2)}=\sqrt{9.8}\approx3.13$
Answer: point $(1.2,\ 3.4)$; minimum distance $\approx3.13$
Practice 8 · Norman window
A window is to be built in the shape of a rectangle topped by a semicircle (this shape is known as a Norman window), using a total of 20 feet of framing material around its outer edge. Find the width and the height of the rectangular part of the window that maximise the total area of the window, and state the maximum area.
variables $x$ = width of the window (the semicircle's diameter), $h$ = height of the rectangular part. Radius $r=x/2$.
constraint Perimeter $=$ two vertical sides $+$ base... but the base is covered by the semicircle's diameter, so only the straight bottom is not included — actually the perimeter here is the two vertical sides, the bottom, and the curved arc: $x+2h+\pi r = x+2h+\dfrac{\pi x}{2}=20 \Rightarrow h=10-\dfrac{x}{2}-\dfrac{\pi x}{4}$
objective function $A(x)=xh+\dfrac12\pi r^2 = xh+\dfrac{\pi x^2}{8} = 10x-\dfrac{x^2}{2}-\dfrac{\pi x^2}{4}+\dfrac{\pi x^2}{8}=10x-\dfrac{x^2}{2}-\dfrac{\pi x^2}{8}$
differentiate & solve $A'(x)=10-x-\dfrac{\pi x}{4}=0 \Rightarrow x\left(1+\dfrac{\pi}{4}\right)=10 \Rightarrow x=\dfrac{40}{4+\pi}$
verify $A''(x)=-1-\dfrac{\pi}{4}<0$, confirming a maximum.
answer in context $x\approx5.60\text{ ft}$, $h\approx2.80\text{ ft}$ — note $h$ equals the radius $r\approx2.80\text{ ft}$.
Answer: width $\approx5.60\text{ ft}$, rectangle height $\approx2.80\text{ ft}$ (equal to the radius)
Practice 9 · Cone in a sphere
A right circular cone is to be inscribed inside a sphere of radius 6 units, with the apex of the cone touching the sphere and the circular rim of the cone's base also touching the surface of the sphere. Find the height and the base radius of the cone that maximise its volume, and state the maximum volume.
variables $h$ = height of the cone (measured from the sphere's lowest point), $r$ = base radius of the cone, $R=6$ = sphere radius.
constraint Placing the sphere's centre at the origin, a cross-section at height $h$ above the bottom of the sphere gives $r^2=R^2-(h-R)^2=h(2R-h)$
objective function $V(h)=\dfrac13\pi r^2h=\dfrac13\pi h^2(2R-h)=\dfrac13\pi(2Rh^2-h^3)$
differentiate & solve $V'(h)=\dfrac13\pi(4Rh-3h^2)=0 \Rightarrow h(4R-3h)=0 \Rightarrow h=\dfrac{4R}{3}$ (rejecting $h=0$)
verify $V''(h)=\dfrac13\pi(4R-6h)$; at $h=\frac{4R}{3}$, this is $\dfrac13\pi(4R-8R)=-\dfrac{4\pi R}{3}<0$, confirming a maximum.
answer in context With $R=6$: $h=8$; $r^2=8(12-8)=32 \Rightarrow r=4\sqrt2$; $V=\dfrac13\pi(32)(8)=\dfrac{256\pi}{3}$
Answer: height $=8$, radius $=4\sqrt2\approx5.66$; maximum volume $=\dfrac{256\pi}{3}\approx268.1$
Practice 10 · Cylinder in a sphere
A right circular cylinder is to be inscribed inside a sphere of radius 5 units, so that the circular rims at the top and bottom of the cylinder both touch the surface of the sphere. Find the height and the radius of the cylinder that maximise its volume, and state the maximum volume.
variables $h$ = half the cylinder's height, $r$ = cylinder's radius, $R=5$ = sphere radius.
constraint The half-height, radius, and sphere radius form a right triangle: $r^2+h^2=R^2 \Rightarrow r^2=R^2-h^2$
objective function Total cylinder height is $2h$: $V(h)=\pi r^2(2h)=2\pi h(R^2-h^2)=2\pi(R^2h-h^3)$
differentiate & solve $V'(h)=2\pi(R^2-3h^2)=0 \Rightarrow h^2=\dfrac{R^2}{3} \Rightarrow h=\dfrac{R}{\sqrt3}$
verify $V''(h)=-12\pi h<0$ for $h>0$, confirming a maximum.
answer in context With $R=5$: $h=\dfrac{5}{\sqrt3}$, total height $=\dfrac{10}{\sqrt3}\approx5.77$; $r^2=25-\dfrac{25}{3}=\dfrac{50}{3} \Rightarrow r\approx4.08$; $V\approx302.3$
Answer: total height $\approx5.77$, radius $\approx4.08$; maximum volume $\approx302.3$
Practice 11 · Rectangle under a parabola
A rectangle is to be positioned so that its base sits on the $x$-axis and its two upper corners touch the parabola given by $y=9-x^2$, with the rectangle symmetric about the $y$-axis. Find the dimensions of the rectangle that maximise its area, and state the maximum area.
variables Let the rectangle's top-right corner be $(x, 9-x^2)$, so the full width is $2x$ and the height is $9-x^2$.
objective function $A(x)=2x(9-x^2)=18x-2x^3$, $0<x<3$
differentiate & solve $A'(x)=18-6x^2=0 \Rightarrow x^2=3 \Rightarrow x=\sqrt3$
verify $A''(x)=-12x<0$ for $x>0$, confirming a maximum.
answer in context Width $=2\sqrt3$, height $=9-3=6$; area $=2\sqrt3\times6=12\sqrt3$
Answer: width $=2\sqrt3\approx3.46$, height $=6$; maximum area $=12\sqrt3\approx20.78$
Practice 12 · Rectangle in a semicircle
A rectangle is to be positioned inside a semicircle of radius 4 units, with its base lying along the diameter of the semicircle and its two upper corners touching the curved edge. Find the dimensions of the rectangle that maximise its area, and state the maximum area.
variables Let the rectangle's top-right corner sit on the circle: $(x,y)$ with $x^2+y^2=16$, so $y=\sqrt{16-x^2}$. Full width $=2x$, height $=y$.
objective function $A(x)=2x\sqrt{16-x^2}$. It's easier to maximise $A^2$: $A(x)^2=4x^2(16-x^2)=64x^2-4x^4$
differentiate & solve $\dfrac{d}{dx}(A^2)=128x-16x^3=16x(8-x^2)=0 \Rightarrow x^2=8 \Rightarrow x=2\sqrt2$ (taking the positive root)
verify $A^2$ rises from 0 then falls back to 0 as $x$ ranges over $(0,4)$, so this interior critical point is the maximum.
answer in context $y=\sqrt{16-8}=2\sqrt2$; width $=4\sqrt2$, height $=2\sqrt2$; area $=2(2\sqrt2)(2\sqrt2)=16$
Answer: width $=4\sqrt2\approx5.66$, height $=2\sqrt2\approx2.83$; maximum area $=16$
Practice 13 · Fixed product, minimise sum
Two positive numbers have a product equal to 100. Find the two numbers that minimise the sum of the numbers, and state the minimum possible sum.
variables $x,y$ = the two numbers.
constraint $xy=100 \Rightarrow y=\dfrac{100}{x}$
objective function $S(x)=x+\dfrac{100}{x}$, $x>0$
differentiate & solve $S'(x)=1-\dfrac{100}{x^2}=0 \Rightarrow x^2=100 \Rightarrow x=10$
verify $S''(x)=\dfrac{200}{x^3}>0$ for $x>0$, confirming a minimum.
answer in context $y=\dfrac{100}{10}=10$
Answer: both numbers equal $10$; minimum sum $=20$
Practice 14 · Ladder around a corner
Two straight hallways meet at a right angle: one hallway is 8 feet wide, and the other is 6 feet wide. Find the length of the longest ladder that could be carried flat around the corner from one hallway into the other, without tilting the ladder.
variables $\theta$ = the angle the ladder makes with the narrower hallway's wall. A ladder touching the inner corner and both outer walls has length $L(\theta)=\dfrac{8}{\sin\theta}+\dfrac{6}{\cos\theta}$.
the twist We're not minimising or maximising a single ladder's fit — we need the shortest ladder length $L(\theta)$ that still touches both walls at every angle, since that shortest value is the longest ladder guaranteed to clear the corner at all orientations.
differentiate & solve $L'(\theta)=-\dfrac{8\cos\theta}{\sin^2\theta}+\dfrac{6\sin\theta}{\cos^2\theta}=0 \Rightarrow 6\sin^3\theta=8\cos^3\theta \Rightarrow \tan^3\theta=\dfrac43 \Rightarrow \tan\theta=\left(\dfrac43\right)^{1/3}$
verify $L(\theta)\to\infty$ as $\theta\to0^+$ or $\theta\to(\pi/2)^-$, so this interior critical point must be the minimum of $L$.
answer in context $\theta\approx47.74^\circ$, giving $\sin\theta\approx0.741$, $\cos\theta\approx0.672$, so $L\approx\dfrac{8}{0.741}+\dfrac{6}{0.672}\approx19.73$
Answer: longest ladder $\approx19.73\text{ ft}$
Practice 15 · Underwater cable
A power plant is located on the shore of a lake. An island lies 3 kilometres directly out from the shoreline, and a second facility on the same shore is located 8 kilometres along the coast from the point on the shore closest to the island. Running cable underwater costs \$6000 per kilometre, while running cable over land along the shore costs \$4000 per kilometre. Find the point on the shore where the cable should leave the water so that the total cost of the cable is minimised.
variables $x$ = distance along the shore from the point closest to the island, to where the cable comes ashore ($0\le x\le8$).
objective function Underwater distance $=\sqrt{9+x^2}$ (by Pythagoras); land distance $=8-x$. $C(x)=6000\sqrt{9+x^2}+4000(8-x)$
differentiate & solve $C'(x)=\dfrac{6000x}{\sqrt{9+x^2}}-4000=0 \Rightarrow \dfrac{x}{\sqrt{9+x^2}}=\dfrac23 \Rightarrow 9x^2=4(9+x^2) \Rightarrow x^2=7.2 \Rightarrow x=\sqrt{7.2}$
verify $C''(x)=\dfrac{6000\cdot9}{(9+x^2)^{3/2}}>0$, confirming a minimum, and $x\approx2.68$ lies inside the valid range $[0,8]$.
Answer: bring the cable ashore $x=\sqrt{7.2}\approx2.68\text{ km}$ from the closest point to the island
Practice 16 · Revenue maximisation
A company finds that the price it can charge for one unit of its product depends on the number of units sold, according to the demand equation $p=100-2q$, where $p$ is the price in dollars and $q$ is the number of units sold. Find the number of units that maximises the company's total revenue, $R=pq$, and state the maximum revenue and the corresponding price.
objective function $R(q)=pq=(100-2q)q=100q-2q^2$
differentiate & solve $R'(q)=100-4q=0 \Rightarrow q=25$
verify $R''(q)=-4<0$, confirming a maximum.
answer in context $p=100-2(25)=50$; $R=25\times50=1250$
Answer: $q=25$ units, price $=\$50$; maximum revenue $=\$1250$
Practice 17 · Minimise average cost
A company's total cost of producing $q$ units of a product is given by $C(q)=0.01q^2+5q+900$ dollars. Find the level of production that minimises the average cost per unit, and state the minimum average cost.
objective function $\bar C(q)=\dfrac{C(q)}{q}=0.01q+5+\dfrac{900}{q}$
differentiate & solve $\bar C'(q)=0.01-\dfrac{900}{q^2}=0 \Rightarrow q^2=90000 \Rightarrow q=300$
verify $\bar C''(q)=\dfrac{1800}{q^3}>0$ for $q>0$, confirming a minimum.
answer in context $\bar C(300)=3+5+3=11$
Answer: $q=300$ units; minimum average cost $=\$11$ per unit
Practice 18 · Mixed-cost fencing
A rectangular block of land with an area of 1200 square metres is to be fenced on all four sides. Fencing along two opposite sides of the rectangle costs \$10 per metre, while fencing along the other two opposite sides costs \$15 per metre. Find the dimensions of the rectangle that minimise the total cost of the fencing, and state the minimum cost.
variables $x$ = length of each of the two sides fenced at \$10/m, $y$ = length of each of the two sides fenced at \$15/m.
constraint $xy=1200 \Rightarrow y=\dfrac{1200}{x}$
objective function $C(x)=2(10)x+2(15)y=20x+30\left(\dfrac{1200}{x}\right)=20x+\dfrac{36000}{x}$
differentiate & solve $C'(x)=20-\dfrac{36000}{x^2}=0 \Rightarrow x^2=1800 \Rightarrow x=30\sqrt2\approx42.43$
verify $C''(x)=\dfrac{72000}{x^3}>0$, confirming a minimum.
answer in context $y=\dfrac{1200}{42.43}\approx28.28$; $C\approx20(42.43)+30(28.28)\approx848.6+848.5\approx1697$
Answer: sides $\approx42.43\text{ m}$ (cheaper fencing) and $\approx28.28\text{ m}$ (pricier fencing); minimum cost $\approx\$1697$
Practice 19 · Poster margins
A poster is to be printed so that the printed material covers an area of 384 square centimetres, with a blank margin of 3 centimetres at the top and bottom, and a blank margin of 2 centimetres on each side. Find the dimensions of the printed area that minimise the total area of paper used for the whole poster (the printed area together with its margins), and state the overall dimensions of the poster.
variables $x,y$ = printed width and height.
constraint $xy=384 \Rightarrow y=\dfrac{384}{x}$
objective function Total poster width $=x+4$ (2 cm each side), total height $=y+6$ (3 cm top and bottom): $A(x)=(x+4)\left(\dfrac{384}{x}+6\right)=6x+\dfrac{1536}{x}+408$
differentiate & solve $A'(x)=6-\dfrac{1536}{x^2}=0 \Rightarrow x^2=256 \Rightarrow x=16$
verify $A''(x)=\dfrac{3072}{x^3}>0$, confirming a minimum.
answer in context $y=\dfrac{384}{16}=24$; total poster $=(16+4)\times(24+6)=20\times30$
Answer: printed area $16\text{ cm}\times24\text{ cm}$; total poster $20\text{ cm}\times30\text{ cm}$
Practice 20 · Box, mixed material cost
An open-top storage box with a square base is to be constructed so that it has a volume of 4 cubic metres. The material used for the base costs \$5 per square metre, while the material used for the four side walls costs \$3 per square metre. Find the dimensions of the box that minimise the total cost of the materials, and state the minimum cost.
variables $x$ = base side, $h$ = height.
constraint $x^2h=4 \Rightarrow h=\dfrac{4}{x^2}$
objective function Base costs \$5/m², the four sides cost \$3/m²: $C(x)=5x^2+4xh(3)=5x^2+12xh=5x^2+\dfrac{48}{x}$

Mining & Mechanical Engineering Set

Ten problems framed around equipment and site scenarios a mining or mechanical engineering student will recognise — storage tanks, ventilation ducts, haul trucks, and sheet-metal fabrication. Same five-step method throughout.

Formulas used in this set

Cylinder (tank, silo)
Volume: $V=\pi r^2h$  ·  Total surface area: $S=2\pi r^2+2\pi rh$

Rectangular duct / bin / plate
Cross-sectional area: $A=xy$  ·  Perimeter: $P=2x+2y$
Open-top box volume: $V=x^2h$ (square base)  ·  Open-top surface area: $S=x^2+4xh$

Distance
$d=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}$ — used for cable/pipe runs and closest-point problems

Cost-rate models (haul trucks, plant operations)
Cost per unit distance or time is often built from two competing terms: one that increases with speed or output (like fuel burn, $\propto v^2$), and one that decreases (like a fixed hourly cost spread over more distance, $\propto 1/v$). Minimising the sum $C(v)=av^2+\dfrac{b}{v}$ is the same algebra as every other "sum of an increasing and a decreasing term" problem in this set.

Average cost
$\bar C(q)=\dfrac{C(q)}{q}$ — total cost function divided by quantity produced

Worked 1 · Storage tank
Worked Example
A cylindrical water storage tank at a mine site is to be built so that it holds 200 cubic metres of water. Find the radius and height of the tank that minimise the amount of steel used to construct it (that is, minimise the total surface area of the tank), and state the minimum surface area.
variables $r$ = radius, $h$ = height.
constraint $\pi r^2h=200 \Rightarrow h=\dfrac{200}{\pi r^2}$
objective function $S(r)=2\pi r^2+2\pi rh=2\pi r^2+\dfrac{400}{r}$
differentiate & solve $S'(r)=4\pi r-\dfrac{400}{r^2}=0 \Rightarrow r^3=\dfrac{100}{\pi} \Rightarrow r=\left(\dfrac{100}{\pi}\right)^{1/3}\approx3.17\text{ m}$
verify $S''(r)=4\pi+\dfrac{800}{r^3}>0$, confirming a minimum.
answer in context $h=\dfrac{200}{\pi r^2}\approx6.34\text{ m}$ — again $h\approx2r$, the same ratio as any minimal-material closed cylinder.
Answer: $r\approx3.17\text{ m}$, $h\approx6.34\text{ m}$
Worked 2 · Ventilation duct
Worked Example
A rectangular ventilation duct at an underground mine is required to have a cross-sectional area of 2 square metres, in order to carry the required volume of air. Find the width and height of the duct's cross-section that minimise the perimeter (which determines the amount of sheet metal used, and the surface area exposed to air friction), and state the minimum perimeter.
variables $x,y$ = duct cross-section dimensions.
constraint $xy=2 \Rightarrow y=\dfrac{2}{x}$
objective function $P(x)=2x+2y=2x+\dfrac{4}{x}$
differentiate & solve $P'(x)=2-\dfrac{4}{x^2}=0 \Rightarrow x^2=2 \Rightarrow x=\sqrt2$
verify $P''(x)=\dfrac{8}{x^3}>0$, confirming a minimum.
answer in context $y=\dfrac{2}{\sqrt2}=\sqrt2$ — the most efficient cross-section is a square.
Answer: $\sqrt2\text{ m}\times\sqrt2\text{ m}\approx1.41\text{ m}\times1.41\text{ m}$ (square), minimum perimeter $=4\sqrt2\approx5.66\text{ m}$
Worked 3 · Ore bin
Worked Example
An open-top ore storage bin with a square base is to be built so that it holds 8 cubic metres of ore. Find the dimensions of the bin (the side length of the base, and the height) that minimise the amount of sheet metal used to construct it, and state the minimum surface area.
variables $x$ = base side, $h$ = height.
constraint $x^2h=8 \Rightarrow h=\dfrac{8}{x^2}$
objective function $S(x)=x^2+4xh=x^2+\dfrac{32}{x}$

Worked 4 · Haul truck economic speed
Worked Example
The operating cost per kilometre of running a haul truck at a mine site is modelled by $C(v)=0.005v^2+\dfrac{120}{v}$ dollars, where $v$ is the truck's speed in kilometres per hour. The first term represents fuel cost, which increases as speed increases, and the second term represents the fixed hourly cost of the truck and driver, spread over the distance travelled, which decreases as speed increases. Find the speed that minimises the cost per kilometre, and state the minimum cost per kilometre.
objective function Already given directly: $C(v)=0.005v^2+\dfrac{120}{v}$, $v>0$
differentiate & solve $C'(v)=0.01v-\dfrac{120}{v^2}=0 \Rightarrow v^3=\dfrac{120}{0.01}=12000 \Rightarrow v=(12000)^{1/3}\approx22.9\text{ km/h}$
verify $C''(v)=0.01+\dfrac{240}{v^3}>0$, confirming a minimum.
answer in context $C(22.9)\approx0.005(524)+\dfrac{120}{22.9}\approx2.62+5.24\approx\$7.86$ per km
Answer: optimal speed $\approx22.9\text{ km/h}$, minimum cost $\approx\$7.86$ per km

Practice 5 · Pipeline route
A pump station is located 4 kilometres (measured perpendicular to a straight ridge) from a processing plant, which lies along the ridge at a point 10 kilometres from the point on the ridge closest to the pump station. Drilling underground to lay pipe costs \$500,000 per kilometre, while trenching along the surface of the ridge costs \$200,000 per kilometre. Find the point along the ridge where the pipe should reach the surface so that the total cost of the pipeline is minimised.
variables $x$ = distance along the ridge, measured from the point closest to the pump station, to where the pipe reaches the surface ($0\le x\le10$).
objective function The underground section runs diagonally from the pump station to that surface point: length $=\sqrt{16+x^2}$ (Pythagoras, using the 4 km perpendicular distance). The remaining surface section runs along the ridge: length $=10-x$. $C(x)=500000\sqrt{16+x^2}+200000(10-x)$
differentiate & solve $C'(x)=\dfrac{500000x}{\sqrt{16+x^2}}-200000=0 \Rightarrow \dfrac{x}{\sqrt{16+x^2}}=\dfrac{2}{5} \Rightarrow 25x^2=4(16+x^2) \Rightarrow 21x^2=64 \Rightarrow x=\sqrt{64/21}$
verify $C''(x)=\dfrac{500000\cdot16}{(16+x^2)^{3/2}}>0$, confirming a minimum, and $x\approx1.75$ lies inside the valid range $[0,10]$.
Answer: reach the surface $x=\sqrt{64/21}\approx1.75\text{ km}$ along the ridge from the closest point
Practice 6 · Closest point, conveyor line
A control room is located at the point $(5,1)$ on a site map. A straight conveyor belt follows the path of the line given by $y=3x-2$. Find the point on the conveyor belt that lies closest to the control room, and state the minimum distance between them.
variables A general point on the conveyor line: $(x,\ 3x-2)$.
objective function Minimise distance squared: $D(x)=(x-5)^2+(3x-2-1)^2=(x-5)^2+(3x-3)^2=10x^2-28x+34$

verify $D''(x)=20>0$, confirming a minimum.

Practice 7 · Crushing plant average cost
A crushing plant's total operating cost is given by $C(q)=0.02q^2+8q+1250$ dollars per hour, where $q$ is the number of tonnes processed per hour. Find the production rate that minimises the average cost per tonne, and state the minimum average cost.
objective function $\bar C(q)=\dfrac{C(q)}{q}=0.02q+8+\dfrac{1250}{q}$

Practice 10 · Sheet-metal skip, folded box
A square sheet of metal measuring 20 centimetres on each side has an equal small square of side length $x$ cut from each of its four corners. The remaining sides are then folded upward to form an open-top storage skip. Find the value of $x$ that maximises the volume of the skip, and state the maximum volume.
variables $x$ = side length of each cut square (this becomes the skip's height). The base becomes $(20-2x)$ on each side.
objective function $V(x)=x(20-2x)^2=4x^3-80x^2+400x$, $0<x<10$
differentiate & solve $V'(x)=12x^2-160x+400=4(3x^2-40x+100)=4(3x-10)(x-10)=0 \Rightarrow x=\dfrac{10}{3}$ or $x=10$
verify $x=10$ makes the base side zero (a degenerate skip, $V=0$), so it's rejected. $V''(x)=24x-160$; at $x=\frac{10}{3}$, $V''=80-160=-80<0$, confirming a maximum.
answer in context $V\left(\dfrac{10}{3}\right)=\dfrac{10}{3}\left(20-\dfrac{20}{3}\right)^2=\dfrac{10}{3}\left(\dfrac{40}{3}\right)^2=\dfrac{16000}{27}$
Answer: $x=\dfrac{10}{3}\approx3.33\text{ cm}$; maximum volume $=\dfrac{16000}{27}\approx592.6\text{ cm}^3$
