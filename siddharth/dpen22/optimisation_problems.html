Optimisation Problems

Six fully worked examples spanning the major problem types, followed by twenty practice word problems with a hidden answer key — try each one before revealing the answer.

Formulas worth knowing cold

These are the building blocks almost every optimisation word problem is assembled from. None of them involve calculus — they're just geometry and basic relations — but you need to reach for the right one instantly to set up the objective function and constraint.

Rectangles & boxes
Perimeter of a rectangle: $P=2l+2w$
Area of a rectangle: $A=lw$
Volume of a rectangular box: $V=lwh$
Surface area of a rectangular box: $S=2lw+2lh+2wh$

Circles, semicircles & cylinders
Circumference: $C=2\pi r$
Area of a circle: $A=\pi r^2$
Area of a semicircle: $A=\dfrac12\pi r^2$; arc length of a semicircle: $\pi r$
Volume of a cylinder: $V=\pi r^2h$
Total surface area of a cylinder: $S=2\pi r^2+2\pi rh$ (two circular ends $+$ the curved side)

Cones & spheres
Volume of a cone: $V=\dfrac13\pi r^2h$
Volume of a sphere: $V=\dfrac43\pi r^3$
Surface area of a sphere: $S=4\pi r^2$

Triangles & distances
Area of a triangle: $A=\dfrac12bh$
Pythagorean theorem (right triangles, and any straight-line distance problem): $a^2+b^2=c^2$
Distance between two points: $d=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}$

Economics quantities
Revenue: $R=pq$ (price $\times$ quantity)
Profit: $P=R-C$ (revenue minus cost)
Average cost: $\bar C(q)=\dfrac{C(q)}{q}$

The general five-step method

Every optimisation word problem, regardless of the setting, reduces to the same procedure. The hard part is almost always step 2 — translating words into a function — not the calculus itself.

The five steps

  1. Draw a diagram and label variables. Give every changing quantity a letter. A picture makes the relationships between quantities visible.
  2. Write the objective function — the quantity you're maximising or minimising — in terms of your variables.
  3. Find a constraint equation that relates your variables (a fixed perimeter, a fixed volume, a fixed cost), and use it to eliminate all but one variable from the objective function, so you end up with a function of a single variable.
  4. Differentiate, set the derivative to zero, and solve for the critical point(s). This gives the candidate value(s) for the optimum.
  5. Verify it's actually a max or min (using the second derivative test, or by checking the sign of the first derivative either side of the critical point, or by checking endpoints of a restricted domain), then answer the original question in context — with correct units.

Step 5 is easy to skip under exam pressure, but it matters: a critical point is only a candidate until you've confirmed which kind it is.

Recognising the constraint

Almost every optimisation problem hides its constraint in a phrase like "with 200m of fencing," "must hold 1 litre," or "costs $6/m2 to build." That phrase is the equation that lets you eliminate a variable. Find it first, before worrying about the objective function's derivative.

Type 1 · Fixed perimeter, maximise area

The classic fencing problem

A farmer has 200 m of fencing and wants to enclose a rectangular field. What dimensions maximise the enclosed area?

Worked Example 1
maximise area given fixed perimeter
variables Let $x$ and $y$ be the side lengths of the rectangle.
constraint Perimeter: $2x+2y=200 \Rightarrow y=100-x$
objective function $A(x)=xy=x(100-x)=100x-x^2$, for $0 < x < 100$
differentiate & solve $A'(x)=100-2x=0 \Rightarrow x=50$
verify $A''(x)=-2<0$ for all $x$, so this is a maximum (the graph of $A$ is a downward parabola).
answer in context $y=100-50=50$. The rectangle is a $50\text{ m}\times50\text{ m}$ square.
Answer: $50\text{ m}\times50\text{ m}$ (a square), maximum area $= 2500\text{ m}^2$
Type 2 · One side free (river problem)

Fencing along a straight river

A rancher wants to fence a rectangular field bordering a straight river, so no fence is needed along the river side. With 300 m of fencing, what dimensions maximise the enclosed area?

Worked Example 2
maximise area, one side unfenced
variables Let $x$ = the side parallel to the river, $y$ = each of the two sides perpendicular to it.
constraint Only three sides need fencing: $x+2y=300 \Rightarrow x=300-2y$
objective function $A(y)=xy=(300-2y)y=300y-2y^2$, for $0 < y < 150$
differentiate & solve $A'(y)=300-4y=0 \Rightarrow y=75$
verify $A''(y)=-4<0$, confirming a maximum.
answer in context $x=300-2(75)=150$.
Answer: $150\text{ m}$ (parallel to river) $\times\ 75\text{ m}$ (perpendicular), maximum area $= 11{,}250\text{ m}^2$
Type 3 · Minimise material (container)

The cheapest cylindrical can

A cylindrical can must hold 1000 cm³ (1 litre). Find the radius and height that minimise the amount of material used (the total surface area).

Worked Example 3
minimise surface area of a can
variables Let $r$ = radius, $h$ = height.
constraint Volume: $\pi r^2 h = 1000 \Rightarrow h=\dfrac{1000}{\pi r^2}$
objective function Surface area (top + bottom + side): $S(r)=2\pi r^2+2\pi r h = 2\pi r^2 + 2\pi r\cdot\dfrac{1000}{\pi r^2}=2\pi r^2+\dfrac{2000}{r}$
differentiate & solve $S'(r)=4\pi r-\dfrac{2000}{r^2}=0 \Rightarrow 4\pi r^3=2000 \Rightarrow r^3=\dfrac{500}{\pi}$
verify $S''(r)=4\pi+\dfrac{4000}{r^3}>0$ always, confirming a minimum.
answer in context $r=\left(\dfrac{500}{\pi}\right)^{1/3}\approx5.42\text{ cm}$, and $h=\dfrac{1000}{\pi r^2}\approx10.84\text{ cm}$ — notice $h\approx2r$, the classic result for a minimal-material can.
Answer: $r\approx5.42\text{ cm}$, $h\approx10.84\text{ cm}$ (height ≈ diameter)
Type 4 · Maximise volume (box from a sheet)

The open-top box

A square piece of cardboard, 12 inches on each side, has equal squares of side $x$ cut from each corner. The sides are then folded up to form an open-top box. Find $x$ that maximises the box's volume.

Worked Example 4
maximise volume of a folded box
variables $x$ = side length of the cut squares (this becomes the box's height). The base becomes $(12-2x)$ on each side.
constraint Already built into the geometry — no separate equation needed here.
objective function $V(x)=x(12-2x)^2 = 4x^3-48x^2+144x$, for $0<x<6$
differentiate & solve $V'(x)=12x^2-96x+144=12(x^2-8x+12)=12(x-2)(x-6)=0 \Rightarrow x=2$ or $x=6$
verify $x=6$ makes the base side zero (a degenerate box, $V=0$), so it's rejected. $V''(x)=24x-96$; at $x=2$, $V''=-48<0$, confirming a maximum.
answer in context $V(2)=2(12-4)^2=2(64)=128$
Answer: cut squares of side $x=2$ inches, maximum volume $=128\text{ in}^3$
Type 5 · Minimise distance

Closest point on a curve

Find the point on the parabola $y=x^2$ that is closest to the point $(0,3)$.

Worked Example 5
minimise distance to a fixed point
variables Let $(x, x^2)$ be a general point on the parabola.
objective function Minimising distance is the same as minimising distance squared (avoids an ugly square root): $D(x)=x^2+(x^2-3)^2 = x^4-5x^2+9$
differentiate & solve $D'(x)=4x^3-10x=2x(2x^2-5)=0 \Rightarrow x=0$ or $x^2=\dfrac52$
verify $D(0)=9$ (distance $=3$). $D\left(\pm\sqrt{5/2}\right)=\left(\dfrac52\right)^2-5\left(\dfrac52\right)+9=\dfrac{11}{4}=2.75$ (distance $\approx1.66$) — smaller, so $x=0$ is a local max and $x=\pm\sqrt{5/2}$ give the minimum.
answer in context The closest points are $\left(\pm\sqrt{5/2},\ 5/2\right)$.
Answer: closest points $\left(\pm\sqrt{5/2},\dfrac52\right)\approx(\pm1.58,\ 2.5)$, minimum distance $=\dfrac{\sqrt{11}}{2}\approx1.66$
Type 6 · Maximise profit (economics)

Maximising profit from a price-demand model

A company's price-demand function is $p=50-0.5q$ (dollars per unit), and its cost function is $C(q)=200+10q$ (dollars). Find the production quantity $q$ that maximises profit.

Worked Example 6
maximise profit $=$ revenue $-$ cost
objective function Revenue $R(q)=pq=(50-0.5q)q=50q-0.5q^2$. Profit $P(q)=R(q)-C(q)=50q-0.5q^2-200-10q=40q-0.5q^2-200$
differentiate & solve $P'(q)=40-q=0 \Rightarrow q=40$
verify $P''(q)=-1<0$, confirming a maximum.
answer in context $P(40)=40(40)-0.5(1600)-200=1600-800-200=600$. Price: $p=50-0.5(40)=\$30$.
Answer: produce $q=40$ units at $\$30$ each, maximum profit $=\$600$

Practice 1 · Fixed perimeter
Rectangular garden, perimeter 80 m — maximise area
variables $x,y$ = side lengths.
constraint $2x+2y=80 \Rightarrow y=40-x$
objective function $A(x)=x(40-x)=40x-x^2$, $0<x<40$
differentiate & solve $A'(x)=40-2x=0 \Rightarrow x=20$
verify $A''(x)=-2<0$, confirming a maximum.
Answer: $20\text{ m}\times20\text{ m}$ square; maximum area $=400\text{ m}^2$
Practice 2 · Fixed area, minimise perimeter
Rectangular pen, area 900 m² — minimise fencing
variables $x,y$ = side lengths.
constraint $xy=900 \Rightarrow y=\dfrac{900}{x}$
objective function $P(x)=2x+2y=2x+\dfrac{1800}{x}$, $x>0$
differentiate & solve $P'(x)=2-\dfrac{1800}{x^2}=0 \Rightarrow x^2=900 \Rightarrow x=30$
verify $P''(x)=\dfrac{3600}{x^3}>0$ for $x>0$, confirming a minimum.
Answer: $30\text{ m}\times30\text{ m}$ square; minimum perimeter $=120\text{ m}$
Practice 3 · River fencing
400 m fencing, one side unfenced — maximise area
variables $x$ = side parallel to the river, $y$ = each perpendicular side.
constraint $x+2y=400 \Rightarrow x=400-2y$
objective function $A(y)=(400-2y)y=400y-2y^2$, $0<y<200$
differentiate & solve $A'(y)=400-4y=0 \Rightarrow y=100$
verify $A''(y)=-4<0$, confirming a maximum.
Answer: $200\text{ m}$ (parallel to river) $\times100\text{ m}$; maximum area $=20{,}000\text{ m}^2$
Practice 4 · Divided pen
600 m fencing, corral split into two equal pens — maximise area
variables $x$ = length of each of the two outer long sides, $y$ = length of each of the three cross-pieces (two ends plus one internal divider).
constraint $2x+3y=600 \Rightarrow x=\dfrac{600-3y}{2}$
objective function $A(y)=xy=\dfrac{(600-3y)y}{2}=300y-1.5y^2$, $0<y<200$
differentiate & solve $A'(y)=300-3y=0 \Rightarrow y=100$
verify $A''(y)=-3<0$, confirming a maximum.
answer in context $x=\dfrac{600-300}{2}=150$
Answer: outer sides $150\text{ m}$, cross-pieces $100\text{ m}$ each; maximum area $=15{,}000\text{ m}^2$
Practice 5 · Box, open top
Square base, volume 32,000 cm³ — minimise surface area
variables $x$ = base side, $h$ = height.
constraint $x^2h=32000 \Rightarrow h=\dfrac{32000}{x^2}$
objective function Base $+$ four sides, no top: $S(x)=x^2+4xh=x^2+\dfrac{128000}{x}$
differentiate & solve $S'(x)=2x-\dfrac{128000}{x^2}=0 \Rightarrow x^3=64000 \Rightarrow x=40$
verify $S''(x)=2+\dfrac{256000}{x^3}>0$, confirming a minimum.
answer in context $h=\dfrac{32000}{1600}=20$
Answer: base $40\text{ cm}\times40\text{ cm}$, height $20\text{ cm}$; minimum surface area $=4800\text{ cm}^2$
Practice 6 · Can, mixed material cost
Volume 500 cm³, top/bottom 2¢/cm², side 1¢/cm² — minimise cost
variables $r$ = radius, $h$ = height.
constraint $\pi r^2h=500 \Rightarrow h=\dfrac{500}{\pi r^2}$
objective function Top $+$ bottom cost $4\pi r^2$ (two circles at 2¢ each); the side costs $2\pi rh$ (at 1¢): $C(r)=4\pi r^2+2\pi rh=4\pi r^2+\dfrac{1000}{r}$
differentiate & solve $C'(r)=8\pi r-\dfrac{1000}{r^2}=0 \Rightarrow r^3=\dfrac{1000}{8\pi}=\dfrac{125}{\pi} \Rightarrow r=\left(\dfrac{125}{\pi}\right)^{1/3}$
verify $C''(r)=8\pi+\dfrac{2000}{r^3}>0$, confirming a minimum.
answer in context $r\approx3.415\text{ cm}$, $h=\dfrac{500}{\pi r^2}\approx13.65\text{ cm}$
Answer: $r\approx3.42\text{ cm}$, $h\approx13.65\text{ cm}$
Practice 7 · Closest point, line
Closest point on $y=2x+1$ to $(4,2)$
variables A general point on the line: $(x,\ 2x+1)$.
objective function Minimise distance squared: $D(x)=(x-4)^2+(2x+1-2)^2=(x-4)^2+(2x-1)^2=5x^2-12x+17$
differentiate & solve $D'(x)=10x-12=0 \Rightarrow x=1.2$
verify $D''(x)=10>0$, confirming a minimum.
answer in context $y=2(1.2)+1=3.4$; minimum distance $=\sqrt{D(1.2)}=\sqrt{9.8}\approx3.13$
Answer: point $(1.2,\ 3.4)$; minimum distance $\approx3.13$
Practice 8 · Norman window
Rectangle $+$ semicircle, perimeter 20 ft — maximise area
variables $x$ = width of the window (the semicircle's diameter), $h$ = height of the rectangular part. Radius $r=x/2$.
constraint Perimeter $=$ two vertical sides $+$ base... but the base is covered by the semicircle's diameter, so only the straight bottom is not included — actually the perimeter here is the two vertical sides, the bottom, and the curved arc: $x+2h+\pi r = x+2h+\dfrac{\pi x}{2}=20 \Rightarrow h=10-\dfrac{x}{2}-\dfrac{\pi x}{4}$
objective function $A(x)=xh+\dfrac12\pi r^2 = xh+\dfrac{\pi x^2}{8} = 10x-\dfrac{x^2}{2}-\dfrac{\pi x^2}{4}+\dfrac{\pi x^2}{8}=10x-\dfrac{x^2}{2}-\dfrac{\pi x^2}{8}$
differentiate & solve $A'(x)=10-x-\dfrac{\pi x}{4}=0 \Rightarrow x\left(1+\dfrac{\pi}{4}\right)=10 \Rightarrow x=\dfrac{40}{4+\pi}$
verify $A''(x)=-1-\dfrac{\pi}{4}<0$, confirming a maximum.
answer in context $x\approx5.60\text{ ft}$, $h\approx2.80\text{ ft}$ — note $h$ equals the radius $r\approx2.80\text{ ft}$.
Answer: width $\approx5.60\text{ ft}$, rectangle height $\approx2.80\text{ ft}$ (equal to the radius)
Practice 9 · Cone in a sphere
Cone inscribed in a sphere of radius 6 — maximise volume
variables $h$ = height of the cone (measured from the sphere's lowest point), $r$ = base radius of the cone, $R=6$ = sphere radius.
constraint Placing the sphere's centre at the origin, a cross-section at height $h$ above the bottom of the sphere gives $r^2=R^2-(h-R)^2=h(2R-h)$
objective function $V(h)=\dfrac13\pi r^2h=\dfrac13\pi h^2(2R-h)=\dfrac13\pi(2Rh^2-h^3)$
differentiate & solve $V'(h)=\dfrac13\pi(4Rh-3h^2)=0 \Rightarrow h(4R-3h)=0 \Rightarrow h=\dfrac{4R}{3}$ (rejecting $h=0$)
verify $V''(h)=\dfrac13\pi(4R-6h)$; at $h=\frac{4R}{3}$, this is $\dfrac13\pi(4R-8R)=-\dfrac{4\pi R}{3}<0$, confirming a maximum.
answer in context With $R=6$: $h=8$; $r^2=8(12-8)=32 \Rightarrow r=4\sqrt2$; $V=\dfrac13\pi(32)(8)=\dfrac{256\pi}{3}$
Answer: height $=8$, radius $=4\sqrt2\approx5.66$; maximum volume $=\dfrac{256\pi}{3}\approx268.1$
Practice 10 · Cylinder in a sphere
Cylinder inscribed in a sphere of radius 5 — maximise volume
variables $h$ = half the cylinder's height, $r$ = cylinder's radius, $R=5$ = sphere radius.
constraint The half-height, radius, and sphere radius form a right triangle: $r^2+h^2=R^2 \Rightarrow r^2=R^2-h^2$
objective function Total cylinder height is $2h$: $V(h)=\pi r^2(2h)=2\pi h(R^2-h^2)=2\pi(R^2h-h^3)$
differentiate & solve $V'(h)=2\pi(R^2-3h^2)=0 \Rightarrow h^2=\dfrac{R^2}{3} \Rightarrow h=\dfrac{R}{\sqrt3}$
verify $V''(h)=-12\pi h<0$ for $h>0$, confirming a maximum.
answer in context With $R=5$: $h=\dfrac{5}{\sqrt3}$, total height $=\dfrac{10}{\sqrt3}\approx5.77$; $r^2=25-\dfrac{25}{3}=\dfrac{50}{3} \Rightarrow r\approx4.08$; $V\approx302.3$
Answer: total height $\approx5.77$, radius $\approx4.08$; maximum volume $\approx302.3$
Practice 11 · Rectangle under a parabola
Rectangle inscribed under $y=9-x^2$ — maximise area
variables Let the rectangle's top-right corner be $(x, 9-x^2)$, so the full width is $2x$ and the height is $9-x^2$.
objective function $A(x)=2x(9-x^2)=18x-2x^3$, $0<x<3$
differentiate & solve $A'(x)=18-6x^2=0 \Rightarrow x^2=3 \Rightarrow x=\sqrt3$
verify $A''(x)=-12x<0$ for $x>0$, confirming a maximum.
answer in context Width $=2\sqrt3$, height $=9-3=6$; area $=2\sqrt3\times6=12\sqrt3$
Answer: width $=2\sqrt3\approx3.46$, height $=6$; maximum area $=12\sqrt3\approx20.78$
Practice 12 · Rectangle in a semicircle
Rectangle inscribed in a semicircle of radius 4 — maximise area
variables Let the rectangle's top-right corner sit on the circle: $(x,y)$ with $x^2+y^2=16$, so $y=\sqrt{16-x^2}$. Full width $=2x$, height $=y$.
objective function $A(x)=2x\sqrt{16-x^2}$. It's easier to maximise $A^2$: $A(x)^2=4x^2(16-x^2)=64x^2-4x^4$
differentiate & solve $\dfrac{d}{dx}(A^2)=128x-16x^3=16x(8-x^2)=0 \Rightarrow x^2=8 \Rightarrow x=2\sqrt2$ (taking the positive root)
verify $A^2$ rises from 0 then falls back to 0 as $x$ ranges over $(0,4)$, so this interior critical point is the maximum.
answer in context $y=\sqrt{16-8}=2\sqrt2$; width $=4\sqrt2$, height $=2\sqrt2$; area $=2(2\sqrt2)(2\sqrt2)=16$
Answer: width $=4\sqrt2\approx5.66$, height $=2\sqrt2\approx2.83$; maximum area $=16$

Practice 14 · Ladder around a corner
Hallways 8 ft and 6 ft wide — longest ladder that fits around the corner
variables $\theta$ = the angle the ladder makes with the narrower hallway's wall. A ladder touching the inner corner and both outer walls has length $L(\theta)=\dfrac{8}{\sin\theta}+\dfrac{6}{\cos\theta}$.
the twist We're not minimising or maximising a single ladder's fit — we need the shortest ladder length $L(\theta)$ that still touches both walls at every angle, since that shortest value is the longest ladder guaranteed to clear the corner at all orientations.
differentiate & solve $L'(\theta)=-\dfrac{8\cos\theta}{\sin^2\theta}+\dfrac{6\sin\theta}{\cos^2\theta}=0 \Rightarrow 6\sin^3\theta=8\cos^3\theta \Rightarrow \tan^3\theta=\dfrac43 \Rightarrow \tan\theta=\left(\dfrac43\right)^{1/3}$
verify $L(\theta)\to\infty$ as $\theta\to0^+$ or $\theta\to(\pi/2)^-$, so this interior critical point must be the minimum of $L$.
answer in context $\theta\approx47.74^\circ$, giving $\sin\theta\approx0.741$, $\cos\theta\approx0.672$, so $L\approx\dfrac{8}{0.741}+\dfrac{6}{0.672}\approx19.73$
Answer: longest ladder $\approx19.73\text{ ft}$
Practice 15 · Underwater cable
Island 3 km offshore, plant 8 km along shore — minimise cable cost
variables $x$ = distance along the shore from the point closest to the island, to where the cable comes ashore ($0\le x\le8$).
objective function Underwater distance $=\sqrt{9+x^2}$ (by Pythagoras); land distance $=8-x$. $C(x)=6000\sqrt{9+x^2}+4000(8-x)$
differentiate & solve $C'(x)=\dfrac{6000x}{\sqrt{9+x^2}}-4000=0 \Rightarrow \dfrac{x}{\sqrt{9+x^2}}=\dfrac23 \Rightarrow 9x^2=4(9+x^2) \Rightarrow x^2=7.2 \Rightarrow x=\sqrt{7.2}$
verify $C''(x)=\dfrac{6000\cdot9}{(9+x^2)^{3/2}}>0$, confirming a minimum, and $x\approx2.68$ lies inside the valid range $[0,8]$.
Answer: bring the cable ashore $x=\sqrt{7.2}\approx2.68\text{ km}$ from the closest point to the island
