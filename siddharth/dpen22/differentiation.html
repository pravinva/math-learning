Topic 1
The Derivative
What it is, what it means, and how to compute it from first principles
The derivative is the instantaneous rate of change of a function — the slope of the curve at a single point. Every differentiation rule is a shortcut for computing this limit. Understanding where the derivative comes from stops it feeling arbitrary.
DefinitionThe derivative — definition and notation▶

The derivative of \(f\) at \(x\), written \(f'(x)\), is defined by:

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} \]

All of these mean the same thing:

\[ f'(x) = \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{d}{dx}[f(x)] = \dot{y} \]

Geometric meaning: \(f'(a)\) is the slope of the tangent line to \(y=f(x)\) at the point \((a,f(a))\).

Differentiability implies continuity — but not vice versa. A function can be continuous but not differentiable (e.g. \(y=|x|\) at \(x=0\) has a corner — no unique tangent exists there).
PatternFour-step method for first principles▶
1
Find \(f(x+h)\): replace every \(x\) with \((x+h)\). Expand fully — do not skip.
2
Subtract \(f(x)\): the result must have \(h\) as a factor in every term.
3
Divide by \(h\): cancel \(h\) from the numerator. You must always be able to cancel it.
4
Take limit \(h\to 0\): substitute \(h=0\). The result is \(f'(x)\).
Step 1 is where most errors happen. \((x+h)^2 \neq x^2+h^2\). Expand fully: \((x+h)^2 = x^2+2xh+h^2\).
ExampleFour worked examples from first principles▶
Example 1 — Differentiate \(f(x)=x^2\)
1
\(f(x+h)=(x+h)^2=x^2+2xh+h^2\)
2
\(f(x+h)-f(x)=2xh+h^2=h(2x+h)\)
3
Divide by \(h\): \(2x+h\)
4
Limit: \(2x\)
ANSWER \(f'(x)=2x\)
Example 2 — Differentiate \(f(x)=x^3\)
1
\(f(x+h)=x^3+3x^2h+3xh^2+h^3\)
2
Subtract: \(h(3x^2+3xh+h^2)\)
3–4
Divide by \(h\), take limit: \(3x^2\)
ANSWER \(f'(x)=3x^2\)
Example 3 — Differentiate \(f(x)=\dfrac{1}{x}\)
1–2
\(\dfrac{1}{x+h}-\dfrac{1}{x} = \dfrac{-h}{x(x+h)}\)
3–4
Divide by \(h\), take limit: \(\dfrac{-1}{x^2}\)
ANSWER \(f'(x)=-\dfrac{1}{x^2}\)
Example 4 — Differentiate \(f(x)=\sqrt{x}\)
1–2
\(\sqrt{x+h}-\sqrt{x}\). Rationalise by multiplying by \(\dfrac{\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}}{\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}}\). Numerator becomes \(h\).
3–4
Divide by \(h\): \(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}}\). Limit: \(\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}\)
ANSWER \(f'(x)=\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}\)

Topic 2
Basic Differentiation Rules
The shortcuts — power rule, constant multiple, sum and difference
First principles works for everything but is slow. The rules are shortcuts derived from first principles once — now you use the results directly. The power rule is the most used rule in all of calculus. Know it without thinking.
DefinitionAll basic rules and the standard derivative table▶
POWER RULE
\[ \frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = nx^{n-1} \quad \text{for any real } n \]
CONSTANT, SUM, MULTIPLE
\[ \frac{d}{dx}[c]=0 \qquad \frac{d}{dx}[cf]=cf' \qquad \frac{d}{dx}[f\pm g]=f'\pm g' \]

All standard derivatives to memorise:

\(f(x)\)\(f'(x)\)\(f(x)\)\(f'(x)\)
\(x^n\)\(nx^{n-1}\)\(\sqrt{x}=x^{1/2}\)\(\frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}\)
\(e^x\)\(e^x\)\(a^x\)\(a^x\ln a\)
\(\ln x\)\(\frac{1}{x}\)\(\log_a x\)\(\frac{1}{x\ln a}\)
\(\sin x\)\(\cos x\)\(\cos x\)\(-\sin x\)
\(\tan x\)\(\sec^2 x\)\(\sec x\)\(\sec x\tan x\)
\(\cot x\)\(-\csc^2 x\)\(\csc x\)\(-\csc x\cot x\)
\(\sin^{-1}x\)\(\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\)\(\cos^{-1}x\)\(\frac{-1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\)
\(\tan^{-1}x\)\(\frac{1}{1+x^2}\)\(\frac{1}{x^n}\)\(\frac{-n}{x^{n+1}}\)
The power rule works for any real exponent — negative, fractional, zero. So \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^{-3}]=-3x^{-4}\) and \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^{2/3}]=\frac{2}{3}x^{-1/3}\).
PatternRewrite first, then differentiate▶

Convert non-standard forms into \(x^n\) before applying the rule:

\[ \frac{1}{x^3}=x^{-3} \qquad \sqrt[3]{x^2}=x^{2/3} \qquad \frac{2}{\sqrt{x}}=2x^{-1/2} \]
Common error: differentiating \(\frac{1}{x^2}\) as \(\frac{1}{2x}\). Rewrite as \(x^{-2}\) first: derivative is \(-2x^{-3}=-\frac{2}{x^3}\).
Power rule does NOT apply to \(e^x\). \(\frac{d}{dx}[e^x]=e^x\) — not \(xe^{x-1}\). eˣ is its own derivative.

1

2

Topic 3
Product & Quotient Rules
Differentiating products and fractions — the most error-prone rules
You cannot differentiate a product by differentiating each factor separately. The product rule and quotient rule are the correct procedures. The quotient rule has a specific order that is constantly confused — the memory aid below makes it automatic.
DefinitionBoth rules▶
PRODUCT RULE — \(f = uv\)
\[ f'= u'v + uv' \]
QUOTIENT RULE — \(f = u/v\)
\[ f'= \frac{u'v - uv'}{v^2} \]
Memory for quotient rule: "low d-high minus high d-low, over low squared." (low = denominator v, high = numerator u, d = differentiate.) The minus in the numerator is what students forget.
ExampleSix worked examples▶
Example 1 — Product: \(f(x)=x^2\sin x\)
1
\(u=x^2,\; v=\sin x.\quad u'=2x,\; v'=\cos x\)
2
\(f'=2x\sin x+x^2\cos x\)
ANSWER \(2x\sin x+x^2\cos x\)
Example 2 — Product: \(f(x)=e^x\ln x\)
1
\(u=e^x,\; v=\ln x.\quad u'=e^x,\; v'=\frac{1}{x}\)
2
\(f'=e^x\ln x+\frac{e^x}{x}=e^x\!\left(\ln x+\frac{1}{x}\right)\)
ANSWER \(e^x\!\left(\ln x+\dfrac{1}{x}\right)\)
Example 3 — Product: \(f(x)=x^3 e^x\)
1
\(u=x^3,\;v=e^x.\quad u'=3x^2,\;v'=e^x\)
2
\(f'=3x^2e^x+x^3e^x=x^2e^x(3+x)\)
ANSWER \(x^2e^x(x+3)\)
Example 4 — Quotient: \(f(x)=\dfrac{x^2+1}{x-3}\)
1
\(u=x^2+1,\;v=x-3.\quad u'=2x,\;v'=1\)
2
\(f'=\dfrac{2x(x-3)-(x^2+1)}{(x-3)^2}=\dfrac{x^2-6x-1}{(x-3)^2}\)
ANSWER \(\dfrac{x^2-6x-1}{(x-3)^2}\)
Example 5 — Quotient: \(f(x)=\dfrac{\sin x}{x}\)
1
\(u=\sin x,\;v=x.\quad u'=\cos x,\;v'=1\)
2
\(f'=\dfrac{x\cos x-\sin x}{x^2}\)
ANSWER \(\dfrac{x\cos x-\sin x}{x^2}\)
Example 6 — Derive \(\dfrac{d}{dx}[\tan x]\) from the quotient rule
1
\(\tan x=\dfrac{\sin x}{\cos x}.\quad u'=\cos x,\;v'=-\sin x\)
2
\(\dfrac{\cos^2 x+\sin^2 x}{\cos^2 x}=\dfrac{1}{\cos^2 x}=\sec^2 x\)
SHOWN \(\dfrac{d}{dx}[\tan x]=\sec^2 x\)
NoteThe two errors every student makes▶
\((uv)'\neq u'v'\). The product rule is \(u'v+uv'\). Multiplying derivatives gives a completely wrong answer.
Quotient numerator is \(u'v\) MINUS \(uv'\). Writing plus gives the product rule in the numerator — the minus sign is essential.
If you can simplify a fraction before differentiating, do it — you may avoid the quotient rule entirely. For example \(\frac{x^3+x}{x}\) simplifies to \(x^2+1\), trivially differentiable.

Topic 4
The Chain Rule
Differentiating composite functions — "differentiate outside, multiply by inside-derivative"
Most functions encountered in calculus are composed — one function inside another, like \(\sin(x^2)\) or \(e^{3x}\). The chain rule is the most widely used differentiation rule. Identifying "outer" and "inner" functions and handling them separately makes composite differentiation mechanical.
DefinitionThe chain rule▶

If \(y=f(g(x))\) — f applied to the inner function \(g(x)\) — then:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x) \]

In Leibniz notation, with \(y=f(u)\) and \(u=g(x)\):

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du}\cdot\frac{du}{dx} \]

In words: differentiate the outer function (leaving the inner unchanged), then multiply by the derivative of the inner function.

The chain rule is needed whenever the argument of a function is anything other than bare \(x\). If you see \(\sin(3x)\), \(e^{x^2}\), \(\sqrt{x+1}\), or \((2x-1)^5\) — chain rule is required.
PatternIdentifying outer and inner — and common forms▶
FunctionOuter \(f\)Inner \(g\)Derivative
\((3x+1)^5\)\(u^5\)\(3x+1\)\(5(3x+1)^4\cdot 3\)
\(\sin(x^2)\)\(\sin u\)\(x^2\)\(\cos(x^2)\cdot 2x\)
\(e^{3x-1}\)\(e^u\)\(3x-1\)\(e^{3x-1}\cdot 3\)
\(\ln(x^2+1)\)\(\ln u\)\(x^2+1\)\(\frac{2x}{x^2+1}\)
\(\sqrt{1-x^2}\)\(u^{1/2}\)\(1-x^2\)\(\frac{-x}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\)
\(\sin^3(x)=(\sin x)^3\)\(u^3\)\(\sin x\)\(3\sin^2 x\cdot\cos x\)
ExampleSix worked examples — chain rule alone and combined▶
Example 1 — \(f(x)=(x^2+1)^{10}\)
\[f'=10(x^2+1)^9\cdot 2x=20x(x^2+1)^9\]
ANSWER \(20x(x^2+1)^9\)
Example 2 — \(f(x)=\sin(3x^2-1)\)
\[f'=\cos(3x^2-1)\cdot 6x=6x\cos(3x^2-1)\]
ANSWER \(6x\cos(3x^2-1)\)
Example 3 — \(f(x)=\ln(x^3+2x)\)
\[f'=\frac{3x^2+2}{x^3+2x}\]
ANSWER \(\dfrac{3x^2+2}{x^3+2x}\)
Example 4 — Chain + product: \(f(x)=x^2e^{3x}\)
1
Product: \(u=x^2\), \(v=e^{3x}\). Need \(v'\) by chain rule: \(v'=3e^{3x}\)
2
\(f'=2xe^{3x}+3x^2e^{3x}=xe^{3x}(2+3x)\)
ANSWER \(xe^{3x}(3x+2)\)
Example 5 — Chain + quotient: \(f(x)=\dfrac{e^{2x}}{x^2+1}\)
1
\(u=e^{2x},\;v=x^2+1.\quad u'=2e^{2x},\;v'=2x\)
2
\(f'=\dfrac{2e^{2x}(x^2+1)-2xe^{2x}}{(x^2+1)^2}=\dfrac{2e^{2x}(x^2-x+1)}{(x^2+1)^2}\)
ANSWER \(\dfrac{2e^{2x}(x^2-x+1)}{(x^2+1)^2}\)
Example 6 — Nested chain: \(f(x)=\sin^3(2x)\)
1
Write as \((\sin 2x)^3\). Outer: \(u^3\), inner: \(\sin(2x)\)
2
\(f'=3(\sin 2x)^2\cdot\frac{d}{dx}[\sin 2x]=3\sin^2(2x)\cdot 2\cos(2x)\)
ANSWER \(6\sin^2(2x)\cos(2x)\)
NoteChain rule errors▶
Forgetting the inner derivative is the most common chain rule error. Always multiply by the derivative of what's inside. \(\frac{d}{dx}[\sin(3x)]=\cos(3x)\cdot 3\) — not just \(\cos(3x)\).
\(\sin^2(x)\) and \(\sin(x^2)\) are different. \(\sin^2 x=(\sin x)^2\) → derivative \(2\sin x\cos x=\sin 2x\). \(\sin(x^2)\) → derivative \(\cos(x^2)\cdot 2x\).

Topic 5
Derivatives of Trig, Exp & Log
Every transcendental derivative — and logarithmic differentiation
All the trig, exponential, and logarithmic derivatives are listed in the Topic 2 table. The chain rule then handles all composites. One extra technique — logarithmic differentiation — handles functions raised to variable powers, like \(x^{\sin x}\).
DefinitionLogarithmic differentiation — when to use it▶

Use logarithmic differentiation when \(y\) involves: (1) a variable base raised to a variable power, or (2) a complicated product/quotient that log laws simplify.

1
Take \(\ln\) of both sides: \(\ln y = \ln f(x)\)
2
Use log laws to expand the right side
3
Differentiate both sides: left side gives \(y'/y\) by the chain rule
4
Multiply both sides by \(y\) to solve for \(y'\)
ExampleSix worked examples▶
Example 1 — \(f(x)=e^{x^2+3x}\)
\[f'=(2x+3)e^{x^2+3x}\]
ANSWER \((2x+3)e^{x^2+3x}\)
Example 2 — \(f(x)=\ln(x^2+\sin x)\)
\[f'=\frac{2x+\cos x}{x^2+\sin x}\]
ANSWER \(\dfrac{2x+\cos x}{x^2+\sin x}\)
Example 3 — \(f(x)=\tan(e^x)\)
\[f'=\sec^2(e^x)\cdot e^x=e^x\sec^2(e^x)\]
ANSWER \(e^x\sec^2(e^x)\)
Example 4 — \(f(x)=\ln|\cos x|\)
1
Chain rule on \(\ln u\): \(\frac{1}{\cos x}\cdot(-\sin x)\)
2
\(=-\dfrac{\sin x}{\cos x}=-\tan x\)
ANSWER \(-\tan x\)
Example 5 — Logarithmic diff: \(y=x^{\cos x}\)
1
\(\ln y=\cos x\cdot\ln x\)
2
Differentiate: \(\dfrac{y'}{y}=-\sin x\ln x+\dfrac{\cos x}{x}\)
3
\(y'=x^{\cos x}\!\left(\dfrac{\cos x}{x}-\sin x\ln x\right)\)
ANSWER \(x^{\cos x}\!\left(\dfrac{\cos x}{x}-\sin x\ln x\right)\)
Example 6 — Logarithmic diff: \(y=\dfrac{(x+1)^2\sqrt{x-3}}{(x^2+1)^3}\)
1
\(\ln y=2\ln(x+1)+\tfrac{1}{2}\ln(x-3)-3\ln(x^2+1)\)
2
\(\dfrac{y'}{y}=\dfrac{2}{x+1}+\dfrac{1}{2(x-3)}-\dfrac{6x}{x^2+1}\)
3
\(y'=y\cdot\left[\dfrac{2}{x+1}+\dfrac{1}{2(x-3)}-\dfrac{6x}{x^2+1}\right]\)
ANSWER \(y'=\dfrac{(x+1)^2\sqrt{x-3}}{(x^2+1)^3}\cdot\left[\dfrac{2}{x+1}+\dfrac{1}{2(x-3)}-\dfrac{6x}{x^2+1}\right]\)
NoteThe two most important facts to remember▶
\(\frac{d}{dx}[\cos x]=-\sin x\) — the minus sign is essential. Forgetting it flips the sign of every downstream result.
For \(\frac{d}{dx}[\ln|x|]=\frac{1}{x}\) for all \(x\neq 0\). This extends the formula to negative x and is the correct antiderivative of \(1/x\).

Topic 6
Higher Derivatives
f′′, f′′′ — and what they tell you about shape
The second derivative is the derivative of the derivative — the rate of change of the rate of change. It tells you about concavity: whether the curve is bending upward or downward. This is the key tool for classifying stationary points and for curve sketching.
DefinitionNotation and meaning▶
\[ f''(x)=\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=\frac{d}{dx}[f'(x)] \qquad f'''(x)=\frac{d^3y}{dx^3}=\frac{d}{dx}[f''(x)] \]
Sign of \(f''(x)\)MeaningGraph shape
\(f''(x)>0\)Concave up (bends upward)Holds water ∪
\(f''(x)<0\)Concave down (bends downward)Spills water ∩
\(f''(x)=0\)Possible inflection pointCurvature changes
Point of inflection: where concavity changes from up to down or vice versa. Occurs where \(f''(x)=0\) AND the sign of \(f''\) changes across that point.
PatternSecond derivative test for stationary points▶

At a stationary point \(x=a\) where \(f'(a)=0\):

\(f''(a)\)Conclusion
\(f''(a)>0\)Local minimum (concave up at that point)
\(f''(a)<0\)Local maximum (concave down at that point)
\(f''(a)=0\)Test inconclusive — use first derivative test instead
If \(f''(a)=0\), the second derivative test fails. You must then use the first derivative test: check the sign of \(f'\) on each side of \(a\).

Topic 7
Tangents, Normals & Stationary Points
Using the derivative to find lines and classify turning points
The derivative gives the slope of the tangent at any point. From that slope you can write the equation of the tangent or normal line instantly. Stationary points — where the derivative is zero — are the turning points of the graph, and they require a test to determine whether they are maxima, minima, or neither.
DefinitionTangent lines, normal lines, and stationary points▶

Tangent line at point \((a, f(a))\) — slope \(= f'(a)\):

\[ y - f(a) = f'(a)(x-a) \]

Normal line at same point — perpendicular to tangent, slope \(= -\dfrac{1}{f'(a)}\):

\[ y - f(a) = -\frac{1}{f'(a)}(x-a) \]

Stationary point: a point where \(f'(x)=0\).

TypeFirst derivative testSecond derivative test
Local maxf′ changes + to −f′′ < 0
Local minf′ changes − to +f′′ > 0
Inflection pointf′ doesn't change signf′′ = 0 (and sign change in f′′)

Topic 8
Curve Sketching Using Calculus
Using f′ and f′′ together to produce an accurate sketch
Calculus gives you information that algebra alone cannot — the exact turning points, intervals of increase/decrease, and where concavity changes. Combined with axis intercepts and asymptotes, a complete picture of the function's behaviour emerges systematically.
DefinitionWhat f′ and f′′ tell you▶
Sign of \(f'(x)\)Graph behaviour
\(f'(x)>0\)f is increasing on that interval
\(f'(x)<0\)f is decreasing on that interval
\(f'(x)=0\)Stationary point — check type
Sign of \(f''(x)\)Graph behaviour
\(f''(x)>0\)Concave up (∪ shaped)
\(f''(x)<0\)Concave down (∩ shaped)
\(f''(x)=0\) and sign changesPoint of inflection
PatternSeven-step sketch method▶
1
Domain: any x-values excluded?
2
Intercepts: set y=0 (x-intercepts) and x=0 (y-intercept).
3
Asymptotes: vertical (denominator=0) and horizontal/oblique (limits at ±∞).
4
Stationary points: solve \(f'(x)=0\), find y-coordinates, classify using \(f''\) or sign table.
5
Increasing/decreasing: sign of \(f'\) on each interval between stationary points.
6
Inflection points: solve \(f''(x)=0\), verify sign changes in \(f''\).
7
Sketch: plot key points and connect with correct increasing/decreasing and concavity.
ExampleFull curve analysis of \(f(x)=x^3-3x^2-9x+5\)▶
1

2

Topic 9
Optimisation
Finding maximum and minimum values in real-world problems
Optimisation — finding the best possible value of something — is one of the most important applications of calculus. Fencing problems, volume problems, profit maximisation: all reduce to the same process. The key skill is setting up the equation before differentiating.
DefinitionGlobal vs local extrema▶

A global (absolute) maximum is the largest value f takes over its entire domain (or a given interval). A global minimum is the smallest.

To find global extrema on a closed interval \([a,b]\):

1
Find all stationary points in \((a,b)\) by solving \(f'(x)=0\).
2
Evaluate \(f\) at each stationary point and at both endpoints \(a\) and \(b\).
3
The largest value is the global max; the smallest is the global min.
Do not forget the endpoints. The global maximum may occur at x=a or x=b, not at a stationary point inside the interval.
PatternSix-step optimisation method for word problems▶
1
Draw and label a diagram with variables.
2
Write the quantity to optimise (area, volume, cost…) in terms of one variable — use a constraint to eliminate others.
3
State the domain of the variable (must be physically realistic).
4
Differentiate and set equal to zero. Solve for the critical point(s).
5
Verify it is a max/min using the second derivative test or by evaluating at endpoints.
6
Answer the question — substitute back to find the optimal value and state the answer in context.

NoteSetup is harder than the calculus▶
The hardest part is writing the formula to optimise in terms of one variable. Once that is done, the differentiation is often straightforward. Spend time on the setup before touching derivatives.
