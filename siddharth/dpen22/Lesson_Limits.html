0 Why formalise limits?

You already know that the derivative of $x^2$ is $2x$, and you got there by memorising the power rule, not by re-deriving it from scratch each time. That's completely fine, rules exist so you don't have to re-derive them constantly. But the power rule, product rule, chain rule, and every other differentiation shortcut you own are all consequences of a single formal definition, and that definition is built entirely out of a limit. Section 3 of this lesson shows you exactly that: plug $x^2$ into the formal machine, and $2x$ falls out, not as a rule you're told to trust, but as something you watch happen.

That's one reason to formalise limits properly. Here's the other, more practical one: limits are not just the hidden engine behind derivatives, they're an independent toolkit with real uses of their own, describing what happens near a vertical asymptote, what a function does as $x\to\infty$, whether a graph has a hole or a genuine break in it, and, once you have derivatives available, a genuinely new technique, L'Hôpital's Rule, for cracking limits that factoring alone can't touch. That last one, Section 9, is only teachable now, precisely because you already know how to differentiate. This lesson builds toward it deliberately.

1 From intuition to formal notation

The notation $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L$ asks a single question: as $x$ gets closer and closer to $a$ (from either side, without ever actually landing exactly on $a$), does $f(x)$ settle down and approach one specific number $L$? Note carefully what this does not ask: it says nothing about $f(a)$ itself, the function doesn't even need to be defined at $a$ for the limit to exist there. The limit is entirely about the function's behaviour near $a$, not at $a$.

Most of the limits you'll meet in practice, anywhere the function is a "nice," unbroken formula at the point in question, can be evaluated by direct substitution: just plug in $x=a$ and see what comes out. The interesting cases, the ones the rest of this lesson is built around, are exactly the places where direct substitution breaks down.

Key idea

$$\lim_{x\to a}f(x) = L$$

means $f(x)$ can be made as close as you like to $L$, simply by taking $x$ close enough to $a$ (on either side, $x\neq a$). If $f$ is defined by a single smooth formula throughout a neighbourhood of $a$ (a polynomial, a standard trig/exponential/log function, or any combination built from these without division by zero), then $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}f(x) = f(a)$: direct substitution works.

Worked example

Estimate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2}\dfrac{x^2-4}{x-2}$ numerically, using a table of values approaching $x=2$ from both sides, without simplifying the expression algebraically first.

$x$1.91.991.999→ 2 ←2.0012.012.1
$f(x)$3.93.993.999?4.0014.014.1

Notice $f(2)$ itself is undefined ($\frac00$, direct substitution fails), but from both sides the outputs are clearly homing in on $4$.

$$\lim_{x\to2}\frac{x^2-4}{x-2} = 4.$$

Section 5 shows the algebraic shortcut (factor and cancel) that reaches this same answer without a table. The table is worth doing once by hand, though, since it's the most direct way to see what "the limit" is actually asking for, entirely independent of any algebra trick.

Let $f(x)=\dfrac{x^2-4}{x-2}$ for $x\neq2$, with $f(2)$ left undefined. State $f(2)$ and $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2}f(x)$ separately, and explain why they can be different kinds of answers (one a genuine number, one "undefined").

Practice 9

Without a calculator, explain why $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{\sin x}x$ cannot be evaluated by direct substitution, even though the table in Practice 8 shows it clearly settling on a value.

Solution

Substituting $x=0$ directly gives $\dfrac{\sin0}0 = \dfrac00$, an undefined expression, division by zero is never a legitimate arithmetic step, regardless of what the numerator happens to be. This is the first example in the lesson of what Section 5 calls an indeterminate form: direct substitution fails outright, yet the limit itself still exists and is perfectly well-behaved, as the table confirms. The whole reason limits need techniques beyond "just substitute" is to handle exactly this gap, cases where the formula breaks but the function's actual behaviour doesn't.

Practice 10

Explain, in a sentence or two, why "the limit as $x\to a$" is fundamentally a statement about behaviour near $a$, and never a statement about the single value $f(a)$.

Solution

The formal idea (Section 0's motivation, made precise here) only ever asks what $f(x)$ does for $x$ close to $a$ but not equal to $a$, the point $a$ itself is explicitly excluded from the process of "getting closer." This is why a function can have a well-defined limit at a point where it isn't even defined (Practice 5), and, as Section 8 will show, also why a function can be defined at a point and have a limit there, yet still have those two values disagree, the third and most interesting kind of discontinuity.

2 Limit laws

Once you accept that $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}f(x)$ and $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}g(x)$ exist individually, a small set of rules tells you exactly how to combine them, sums, products, quotients, and powers all behave exactly the way you'd hope, and this is what lets you break a complicated expression into simple pieces rather than fighting the whole thing at once.

Key idea

If $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L$ and $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}g(x)=M$ both exist:

$$\lim_{x\to a}\big[f(x)\pm g(x)\big] = L\pm M, \qquad \lim_{x\to a}\big[cf(x)\big] = cL,$$

$$\lim_{x\to a}\big[f(x)g(x)\big] = LM, \qquad \lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac LM \ \ (M\neq0), \qquad \lim_{x\to a}\big[f(x)\big]^n = L^n.$$

Worked example

Given $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=3$ and $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}g(x)=5$, find $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}\big[2f(x)-3g(x)\big]$ and $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}\dfrac{f(x)g(x)}{g(x)+1}$.

$$\lim_{x\to a}\big[2f(x)-3g(x)\big] = 2(3)-3(5) = 6-15=-9.$$

$$\lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)g(x)}{g(x)+1} = \frac{3\times5}{5+1} = \frac{15}6 = \frac52.$$

Neither computation ever needed to know what $f$ or $g$ actually were as functions, only their limiting values, exactly the shortcut these laws are built to provide.

Practice 20

Explain why the quotient law explicitly requires $M\neq0$ (the denominator's limit can't be zero). What goes wrong in the argument if $M=0$?

Solution

The conclusion of the quotient law is $\dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)}\to\dfrac LM$, a specific finite number, and $\dfrac L0$ isn't a number at all, division by zero is undefined regardless of what $L$ is. If $M=0$, the ratio $f(x)/g(x)$ might still have a limit (this is exactly what Section 5's indeterminate-form techniques are built to find, when $L=0$ too), or it might blow up to $\pm\infty$ (Section 6), or fail to exist entirely, but the quotient law's clean, guaranteed answer $\frac LM$ simply doesn't apply anymore once $M=0$; a separate, more careful technique is needed instead of the blanket rule.

2S Squeeze theorem

Sometimes direct substitution fails and algebraic simplification does not remove the difficulty. In those cases, the squeeze theorem is often the cleanest method — but the key skill is choosing good upper and lower bounds.

Key idea

If $g(x)\le f(x)\le h(x)$ for all $x$ near $a$ (possibly excluding $x=a$), and

$$\lim_{x\to a}g(x)=\lim_{x\to a}h(x)=L,$$

then

$$\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L.$$

How to choose the two sides (practical checklist)

1) Look for a bounded factor first. Most common: $\sin(\cdot)$ or $\cos(\cdot)$, where $-1\le \sin(\cdot)\le1$ and $-1\le\cos(\cdot)\le1$.

2) Isolate that factor. Rewrite the expression as $f(x)=\big(\text{bounded part}\big)\big(\text{envelope part}\big)$.

3) Build bounds from the envelope. If the envelope is nonnegative, multiply inequalities directly. If sign is uncertain, switch to absolute values (this avoids accidental sign-flip mistakes): $$|f(x)|\le \text{envelope} \quad \Rightarrow \quad -\text{envelope}\le f(x)\le \text{envelope}.$$

4) Check both bounds go to same limit. Most often both go to $0$.

5) Conclude by squeeze theorem.

Fast bound templates to memorise

If you see $p(x)\sin(q(x))$ or $p(x)\cos(q(x))$, try $$-|p(x)|\le p(x)\sin(q(x))\le |p(x)|,\qquad -|p(x)|\le p(x)\cos(q(x))\le |p(x)|.$$

If you see absolute value, start from $$0\le |\,\text{expression}\,|.$$ Then find an upper bound that goes to $0$.

If you see $\dfrac{\sin(kx)}{x}$, rewrite as $$\frac{\sin(kx)}{x}=k\cdot\frac{\sin(kx)}{kx},$$ then use $\dfrac{\sin u}{u}\to1$.

Worked example 1 (full method)

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}x\sin\!\left(\frac1x\right)$.

Step A: bounded part. For all real $u$, $-1\le\sin u\le1$. Put $u=\frac1x$: $$-1\le\sin\!\left(\frac1x\right)\le1.$$

Step B: envelope choice. Here the multiplier is $x$, which changes sign, so don't multiply directly by $x$. Use absolute value: $$\left|\sin\!\left(\frac1x\right)\right|\le1 \ \Rightarrow\ \left|x\sin\!\left(\frac1x\right)\right|\le|x|.$$

Step C: convert to two-sided bounds. $$-|x|\le x\sin\!\left(\frac1x\right)\le |x|.$$

Step D: take limits of bounds. $$\lim_{x\to0}(-|x|)=0,\qquad \lim_{x\to0}|x|=0.$$

Both sides squeeze to $0$, so $$\lim_{x\to0}x\sin\!\left(\frac1x\right)=0.$$

Worked example 2 (nonnegative envelope)

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}x^2\cos\!\left(\frac{3}{x}\right)$.

Since $-1\le\cos(3/x)\le1$ and $x^2\ge0$, multiply all parts by $x^2$: $$-x^2\le x^2\cos\!\left(\frac{3}{x}\right)\le x^2.$$

Now let $x\to0$: $$\lim_{x\to0}(-x^2)=0,\qquad \lim_{x\to0}x^2=0.$$

Therefore, $$\lim_{x\to0}x^2\cos\!\left(\frac{3}{x}\right)=0.$$

Worked example 3 (classic trig squeeze)

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\sin x}{x}$.

For $x$ near $0$ (radians), a standard inequality is $$\cos x\le\frac{\sin x}{x}\le1,\qquad x\ne0.$$

Source of this inequality: on the unit circle, for $0<x<\frac{\pi}{2}$ (with $x$ in radians), geometry gives $$\sin x<x<\tan x.$$ From $\sin x<x$ and $x>0$, divide by $x$: $$\frac{\sin x}{x}<1.$$ From $x<\tan x=\frac{\sin x}{\cos x}$ and $\cos x>0$, rearrange: $$\cos x<\frac{\sin x}{x}.$$ So $$\cos x<\frac{\sin x}{x}<1.$$ (The same squeeze holds from the left side near $0$ as well.)

Radian condition: this geometry argument uses unit-circle arc length, where arc length equals angle only in radian measure.

Take limits: $$\lim_{x\to0}\cos x=1,\qquad \lim_{x\to0}1=1.$$

So by squeeze theorem: $$\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\sin x}{x}=1.$$

Worked example 4 (alternative method using a squeeze-derived fact)

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{1-\cos x}{x}$.

Use the identity $$1-\cos x=2\sin^2\left(\frac x2\right),$$ so $$\frac{1-\cos x}{x} =\frac{2\sin^2(x/2)}{x} =\sin\left(\frac x2\right)\cdot\frac{\sin(x/2)}{x/2}.$$

Now $x\to0$ gives $\sin(x/2)\to0$ and $\dfrac{\sin(x/2)}{x/2}\to1$, so the product limit is $0$.

Hence $$\lim_{x\to0}\frac{1-\cos x}{x}=0.$$

Foundation set (Squeeze 1-4): build the bounding habit on standard oscillatory forms.

Squeeze 1 (Foundation)

Use squeeze theorem to evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}x^3\sin\!\left(\frac1x\right)$.

Solution

$-1\le\sin(1/x)\le1$ and $x^3$ changes sign, so use absolute value: $$\left|x^3\sin(1/x)\right|\le |x|^3.$$ Hence $$-|x|^3\le x^3\sin(1/x)\le |x|^3.$$ Both bounds go to $0$, so the limit is $\boxed{0}$.

Squeeze 2 (Foundation)

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}|x|\cos\!\left(\frac{2}{x}\right)$.

Solution

Since $-1\le\cos(2/x)\le1$ and $|x|\ge0$, $$-|x|\le |x|\cos(2/x)\le |x|.$$ Both bounds go to $0$, so the limit is $\boxed{0}$.

Squeeze 3 (Foundation)

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{x^2}{1+x^2}\sin\!\left(\frac1x\right)$.

Solution

$-1\le\sin(1/x)\le1$ and $\dfrac{x^2}{1+x^2}\ge0$, so $$-\frac{x^2}{1+x^2}\le\frac{x^2}{1+x^2}\sin(1/x)\le\frac{x^2}{1+x^2}.$$ Now $\dfrac{x^2}{1+x^2}\to0$, so both bounds go to $0$ and the middle term also goes to $0$.

Squeeze 4 (Foundation)

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\left|x\sin\!\left(\frac{5}{x}\right)\right|$.

Solution

Because $|\sin(5/x)|\le1$, $$0\le\left|x\sin\!\left(\frac5x\right)\right|\le |x|.$$ As $x\to0$, right side goes to $0$, so the middle term also goes to $0$.

Intermediate set (Squeeze 5-8): combine squeeze with substitutions, identities, and chained limit laws.

Squeeze 5 (Intermediate)

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}x^2\!\left(2+\sin\!\frac1x\right)$.

Solution

First bound the bracket: since $-1\le\sin(1/x)\le1$, $$1\le 2+\sin(1/x)\le 3.$$ Multiply by $x^2\ge0$: $$x^2\le x^2\!\left(2+\sin\!\frac1x\right)\le 3x^2.$$ Both bounds go to $0$, so the limit is $0$.

Squeeze 6 (Intermediate)

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\sin(4x)}{x}$.

Solution

Write $$\frac{\sin(4x)}{x}=4\cdot\frac{\sin(4x)}{4x}.$$ Let $u=4x$. As $x\to0$, $u\to0$, and from squeeze theorem result $\lim_{u\to0}\frac{\sin u}{u}=1$. So the limit is $4\cdot1=\boxed{4}$.

Squeeze 7 (Intermediate)

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\sin(3x)}{\sin(5x)}$.

Solution

Multiply and divide strategically: $$\frac{\sin(3x)}{\sin(5x)} =\frac{\sin(3x)}{3x}\cdot\frac{5x}{\sin(5x)}\cdot\frac35.$$ As $x\to0$, first factor $\to1$ and second factor $\to1$ (from $\sin u/u\to1$), so limit is $$1\cdot1\cdot\frac35=\boxed{\frac35}.$$

Squeeze 8 (Intermediate)

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\left(1-\cos x\right)$ using squeeze-type bounds.

Solution

For all $x$, $-1\le\cos x\le1$, so $$0\le1-\cos x\le2.$$ This alone does not squeeze to one value. Use the sharper identity $$1-\cos x=2\sin^2(x/2)\le 2\left(\frac{x}{2}\right)^2=\frac{x^2}{2},$$ and clearly $1-\cos x\ge0$. So $$0\le1-\cos x\le\frac{x^2}{2}.$$ Both bounds $\to0$, so limit is $\boxed{0}$.

Exam-style set (Squeeze 9-12): proof-style bounds, one-sided checks, and abstract squeezing arguments.

Squeeze 9 (Exam style)

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{1-\cos x}{x}$ by first proving a valid absolute-value bound for the numerator.

Solution

From Squeeze 8, $0\le1-\cos x\le x^2/2$. Convert this into an absolute-value bound: $$\left|1-\cos x\right|=1-\cos x\le\frac{x^2}{2}.$$ Then $$0\le\left|\frac{1-\cos x}{x}\right|\le\frac{|x|}{2}.$$ Right side $\to0$, so the absolute value tends to $0$, hence $$\lim_{x\to0}\frac{1-\cos x}{x}=0.$$

Squeeze 10 (Exam style)

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\sin x}{|x|}$, or explain why it does not exist.

Solution

Use one-sided limits: $$\frac{\sin x}{|x|}= \begin{cases} \frac{\sin x}{x}, & x>0\\[2mm] -\frac{\sin x}{x}, & x<0 \end{cases}$$ so $$\lim_{x\to0^+}\frac{\sin x}{|x|}=1,\qquad \lim_{x\to0^-}\frac{\sin x}{|x|}=-1.$$ One-sided limits differ, so the two-sided limit does not exist.

Squeeze 11 (Exam style)

Suppose $|f(x)|\le x^2$ for all $x$ in some punctured neighbourhood of $0$. Prove that $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}f(x)=0$.

Solution

From $|f(x)|\le x^2$ we get the two-sided inequality $$-x^2\le f(x)\le x^2.$$ Both outer functions have limit $0$ as $x\to0$, so by squeeze theorem $$\lim_{x\to0}f(x)=0.$$

Squeeze 12 (Exam style)

Choose explicit lower and upper functions $g(x)$ and $h(x)$ such that $$g(x)\le 2x^2-x^2\cos\!\frac1x\le h(x),$$ and use them to evaluate the limit as $x\to0$.

Solution

Since $-1\le\cos(1/x)\le1$, multiply by $-x^2$ (note sign flip if doing in one move; easier use two-sided result): $$-x^2\le -x^2\cos(1/x)\le x^2.$$ Add $2x^2$: $$x^2\le 2x^2-x^2\cos(1/x)\le 3x^2.$$ Both bounds go to $0$, so the target limit is $\boxed{0}$.

3 The derivative, formally

Here is the payoff for everything so far. Every derivative rule you already know, power rule, whatever else, is a shortcut for one single limit. This section asks you to stop using the shortcut, just for a few problems, and watch the shortcut get rebuilt from scratch.

Key idea

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}h$$

This is called the difference quotient limit, and it's the formal definition every differentiation rule you've ever used is secretly computing in advance, once and for all, for an entire family of functions, so you never have to run the limit by hand again for that family.

Worked example

Find $f'(x)$ for $f(x)=x^2$ directly from the definition, and confirm it matches the power rule.

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{(x+h)^2-x^2}h.$$

Expand the numerator: $(x+h)^2-x^2 = x^2+2xh+h^2-x^2 = 2xh+h^2$.

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{2xh+h^2}h = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{h(2x+h)}h = \lim_{h\to0}(2x+h).$$

The $h$ in the denominator was never actually zero throughout this cancellation, $h\to0$ means "getting arbitrarily close," never "equal to," so dividing by $h$ was completely legal right up until the very last step, where substituting $h=0$ into the now-simplified expression $2x+h$ is finally safe.

$$f'(x) = 2x+0 = 2x,$$

exactly the power rule's answer for $x^2$, now derived rather than recalled.

Practice 21

Use the definition to find $f'(x)$ for $f(x)=x^3$, and confirm it matches the power rule.

Solution

$(x+h)^3-x^3 = 3x^2h+3xh^2+h^3$ (binomial expansion, dropping the $x^3$ terms that cancel).

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{3x^2h+3xh^2+h^3}h = \lim_{h\to0}\big(3x^2+3xh+h^2\big) = 3x^2.$$

Matches the power rule exactly.

Practice 22

Use the definition to find $f'(x)$ for $f(x)=5x+3$.

Solution

$f(x+h)-f(x) = 5(x+h)+3-(5x+3) = 5h$.

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{5h}h = \lim_{h\to0}5 = 5,$$

the constant slope of the line, exactly matching what "derivative of a linear function" should mean.

Practice 23

Use the definition to find $f'(x)$ for $f(x)=x^2+2x$.

Solution

$f(x+h)-f(x) = (x+h)^2+2(x+h) - (x^2+2x) = 2xh+h^2+2h$.

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{2xh+h^2+2h}h = \lim_{h\to0}(2x+h+2) = 2x+2.$$

Practice 24

Use the definition to find $f'(x)$ for $f(x)=\dfrac1x$ (this one needs a common denominator before anything cancels).

Solution

$$\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}h = \frac1h\left(\frac1{x+h}-\frac1x\right) = \frac1h\cdot\frac{x-(x+h)}{x(x+h)} = \frac1h\cdot\frac{-h}{x(x+h)} = \frac{-1}{x(x+h)}.$$

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{-1}{x(x+h)} = \frac{-1}{x^2}.$$

Matches $\dfrac{d}{dx}x^{-1} = -x^{-2}$ from the power rule (which extends to negative exponents too).

Practice 25

Use the definition to find $f'(x)$ for $f(x)=\sqrt x$ (needs the conjugate trick from Section 5, applied inside the difference quotient).

Solution

$$\frac{\sqrt{x+h}-\sqrt x}h.$$

Multiply top and bottom by the conjugate $\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt x$:

$$\frac{(\sqrt{x+h}-\sqrt x)(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt x)}{h(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt x)} = \frac{(x+h)-x}{h(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt x)} = \frac{h}{h(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt x)} = \frac1{\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt x}.$$

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0}\frac1{\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt x} = \frac1{2\sqrt x},$$

matching $\dfrac{d}{dx}x^{1/2} = \dfrac12x^{-1/2}$.

Practice 26

Use the definition directly (not the general formula) to find $f'(3)$ for $f(x)=x^2$, plugging $a=3$ in from the start rather than finding $f'(x)$ in general first.

Solution

$$f'(3) = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{(3+h)^2-9}h = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{9+6h+h^2-9}h = \lim_{h\to0}(6+h) = 6.$$

Matches the worked example's general formula, $f'(x)=2x$, evaluated at $x=3$: $2(3)=6$.

Practice 27

Explain why the definition insists $h\to0$ rather than simply setting $h=0$ directly from the start.

Solution

Setting $h=0$ immediately gives $\dfrac{f(x+0)-f(x)}0 = \dfrac00$, undefined, exactly the same problem Practice 9 hit with $\sin x/x$. The entire point of the difference quotient is to measure the average rate of change over a tiny but nonzero interval of width $h$, and then ask what that average rate approaches as the interval shrinks toward nothing, without the interval's width ever literally reaching zero mid-calculation. The limit process is precisely what lets $h$ get arbitrarily small (small enough that the algebra always cancels the troublesome $h$ in the denominator first, as in every worked example and practice problem above) without ever actually dividing by zero.

Practice 28

Use the definition to find $f'(x)$ for the constant function $f(x)=7$.

Solution

$f(x+h)-f(x) = 7-7=0$ for every $h$.

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0}\frac0h = \lim_{h\to0}0 = 0.$$

A constant function has zero slope everywhere, exactly as expected: it never rises or falls.

Practice 29

Use the definition to find $f'(x)$ for $f(x)=x^2-4x+1$.

Solution

$f(x+h)-f(x) = 2xh+h^2-4h$.

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0}(2x+h-4) = 2x-4.$$

Practice 30

Use the definition to find $f'(x)$ for $f(x)=x^4$ (binomial expansion required, same technique as Practice 21).

Solution

$(x+h)^4-x^4 = 4x^3h+6x^2h^2+4xh^3+h^4$.

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0}\big(4x^3+6x^2h+4xh^2+h^3\big) = 4x^3,$$

matching the power rule again. Practices 21 and 30 together make the pattern in the power rule, $\frac{d}{dx}x^n = nx^{n-1}$, visible directly: in both cases, every term of the binomial expansion except the very first ($nx^{n-1}h$) carries at least two factors of $h$, so every term except that one vanishes once $h\to0$, leaving exactly $nx^{n-1}$ behind.

4 One-sided limits and non-existence

Some functions approach different values depending on which direction you sneak up on them from. A one-sided limit only considers one direction of approach, and the ordinary (two-sided) limit only exists when both directions agree.

Key idea

$$\lim_{x\to a^-}f(x) \quad \text{(from the left, } xa\text{)}.$$

$$\lim_{x\to a}f(x) \text{ exists} \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \lim_{x\to a^-}f(x) = \lim_{x\to a^+}f(x).$$

If the two one-sided limits disagree (or either one fails to exist), the two-sided limit simply does not exist (DNE).

Worked example

Let $f(x) = \begin{cases}x+1 & x<2\\x^2 & x\ge2\end{cases}$. Determine whether $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2}f(x)$ exists.

From the left ($x<2$, using $f(x)=x+1$): $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2^-}f(x) = 2+1=3$.

From the right ($x\ge2$, using $f(x)=x^2$): $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2^+}f(x) = 2^2=4$.

$3\neq4$: the two one-sided limits disagree, so $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2}f(x)$ does not exist, even though both individual one-sided limits are perfectly well-defined numbers. This is a genuine jump discontinuity, revisited in Section 8.

Practice 31

Let $f(x)=\begin{cases}2x&x<1\\x+3&x\ge1\end{cases}$. Find $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to1^-}f(x)$ and $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to1^+}f(x)$, and state whether $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to1}f(x)$ exists.

Solution

Left: $2(1)=2$. Right: $1+3=4$. $2\neq4$, so $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to1}f(x)$ does not exist.

Practice 32

For $f(x)=\dfrac{|x|}x$, find $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^-}f(x)$ and $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}f(x)$. Does $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}f(x)$ exist?

Solution

For $x<0$: $|x|=-x$, so $f(x)=\dfrac{-x}x=-1$. $\ \displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^-}f(x)=-1$.

For $x>0$: $|x|=x$, so $f(x)=1$. $\ \displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}f(x)=1$.

$-1\neq1$: $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}f(x)$ does not exist. This is the classic sign function example, its graph genuinely jumps from $-1$ to $1$ right at $x=0$.

Practice 33

Let $f(x)=\begin{cases}x^2-1&x<3\\2x+2&x\ge3\end{cases}$. Verify that $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to3}f(x)$ exists, by checking both one-sided limits agree.

Solution

Left: $3^2-1=8$. Right: $2(3)+2=8$. Both give $8$, so $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to3}f(x)=8$ genuinely exists, the two pieces meet up exactly at the boundary, with no jump.

Practice 34

For the floor function $f(x)=\lfloor x\rfloor$ (rounds down to the nearest integer), find $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2^-}f(x)$ and $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2^+}f(x)$.

Solution

Just below $2$ (like $1.9,1.99,\dots$), the floor is $1$: $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2^-}\lfloor x\rfloor=1$.

At and just above $2$ (like $2,2.01,\dots$), the floor is $2$: $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2^+}\lfloor x\rfloor=2$.

$1\neq2$: the two-sided limit does not exist. The floor function jumps at every integer, this is why staircase-shaped graphs are the standard picture for jump discontinuities.

Practice 35

A graph shows a function approaching height $5$ as $x\to1$ from the left, and also approaching height $5$ from the right, but the function is defined to equal $8$ exactly at $x=1$ (an isolated point sitting above the rest of the curve). Does $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to1}f(x)$ exist? If so, what is it?

Solution

Yes, the limit exists and equals $5$. Both one-sided limits agree ($5$ from each side), and by Section 1's central point, the limit only cares about the function's behaviour near $x=1$, never the isolated value $f(1)=8$ actually assigned there. The limit is $5$ despite $f(1)=8$, a mismatch Section 8 classifies as a removable discontinuity.

Practice 36

Let $f(x)=\begin{cases}\sqrt x&x\ge0\\ \text{undefined}&x<0\end{cases}$. Explain why $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^-}f(x)$ doesn't exist, even though $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}f(x)$ does, and what that implies for the two-sided limit.

Solution

$f$ isn't defined at all for $x<0$, there are no output values to even examine as $x$ approaches $0$ from the left, so "the limit from the left" isn't asking about a well-posed situation, it fails to exist by default, not because of disagreement but because of absence. Meanwhile $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}f(x)=\sqrt0=0$ perfectly well. Since the two-sided limit requires both one-sided limits to exist and agree, and the left one doesn't even exist, $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}f(x)$ does not exist either, this is a slightly different failure mode from Practice 31–34's disagreement, but the same ultimate conclusion.

Practice 37

Explain in your own words why $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}f(x)$ existing requires both one-sided limits to exist and to be equal, rather than just one of those two conditions.

Solution

The two-sided limit is a claim about approaching $a$ from either direction and always landing on the same single number. If only one side's limit exists (Practice 36), there's no way to make a two-directional claim at all, half the picture is missing. If both sides exist but land on different numbers (Practice 31–34), the function is genuinely doing two different things depending on which direction you approach from, so there's no single number that correctly describes "the" behaviour near $a$; asserting one anyway would be simply false for whichever direction you didn't check. Both conditions together, existence and agreement, are exactly what's needed to make "the function approaches $L$ near $a$" an honest, unambiguous statement.

Practice 38

Let $f(x)=\begin{cases}x+1&x<2\\x^2&x\ge2\end{cases}$ (the worked example's function). Confirm $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2}f(x)$ doesn't exist by recomputing both one-sided limits yourself.

Solution

Left: $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2^-}(x+1) = 3$. Right: $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2^+}x^2 = 4$. $3\neq4$, confirming the two-sided limit does not exist, exactly as the worked example found.

Practice 39

Let $f(x)=\begin{cases}3-x&x<1\\2x&x\ge1\end{cases}$. Find both one-sided limits at $x=1$, and state whether $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to1}f(x)$ exists.

Solution

Left: $3-1=2$. Right: $2(1)=2$. Both give $2$: $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to1}f(x)=2$ exists.

Practice 40

Let $f(x)=\begin{cases}x^2+1&x<0\\x-1&x\ge0\end{cases}$. Find both one-sided limits at $x=0$, and state whether $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}f(x)$ exists.

Solution

Left: $0^2+1=1$. Right: $0-1=-1$. $1\neq-1$: $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}f(x)$ does not exist, a jump of size $2$ right at the boundary.

5 Indeterminate forms: factoring and conjugates

Whenever direct substitution produces $\dfrac00$, that's not the answer, it's a signal that the expression needs to be rewritten first, in a form where the troublesome factor of zero in the denominator cancels against a matching factor in the numerator before you substitute. Two techniques handle the overwhelming majority of these cases: factoring polynomials, and rationalising (multiplying by the conjugate) when a square root is involved.

Key idea

If direct substitution gives $\dfrac00$: factor both numerator and denominator and cancel the common factor causing the zero (works for polynomial-only expressions), or, if a square root is present, multiply by the conjugate of whichever piece has the root, to convert the root into a polynomial via the difference-of-squares identity $(\sqrt a-\sqrt b)(\sqrt a+\sqrt b)=a-b$.

Worked example

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to3}\dfrac{x^2-9}{x-3}$ (matching Section 1's table, now algebraically), and separately $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to9}\dfrac{\sqrt x-3}{x-9}$.

Direct substitution on the first: $\dfrac{0}0$, indeterminate. Factor the numerator: $x^2-9=(x-3)(x+3)$.

$$\lim_{x\to3}\frac{(x-3)(x+3)}{x-3} = \lim_{x\to3}(x+3) = 6.$$

Second one: direct substitution also gives $\dfrac00$. Multiply top and bottom by the conjugate $\sqrt x+3$:

$$\frac{(\sqrt x-3)(\sqrt x+3)}{(x-9)(\sqrt x+3)} = \frac{x-9}{(x-9)(\sqrt x+3)} = \frac1{\sqrt x+3}.$$

$$\lim_{x\to9}\frac{\sqrt x-3}{x-9} = \frac1{\sqrt9+3} = \frac16.$$

Practice 41

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2}\dfrac{x^2-4}{x-2}$.

Solution

$\dfrac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2} \to x+2 \to 4$.

Practice 42

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to1}\dfrac{x^2-1}{x^2-3x+2}$.

Solution

$x^2-1=(x-1)(x+1)$. $\ x^2-3x+2=(x-1)(x-2)$.

$$\lim_{x\to1}\frac{(x-1)(x+1)}{(x-1)(x-2)} = \lim_{x\to1}\frac{x+1}{x-2} = \frac2{-1} = -2.$$

Practice 43

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to-3}\dfrac{x^2+5x+6}{x+3}$.

Solution

$x^2+5x+6=(x+3)(x+2)$, so the limit is $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to-3}(x+2) = -1$.

Practice 44

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to4}\dfrac{\sqrt x-2}{x-4}$.

Solution

Multiply by the conjugate $\sqrt x+2$: $$\frac{(\sqrt x-2)(\sqrt x+2)}{(x-4)(\sqrt x+2)} = \frac{x-4}{(x-4)(\sqrt x+2)} = \frac1{\sqrt x+2}.$$

$$\lim_{x\to4}\frac1{\sqrt x+2} = \frac1{2+2} = \frac14.$$

Practice 45

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{\sqrt{x+9}-3}x$.

Solution

Conjugate: $\sqrt{x+9}+3$. $$\frac{(x+9)-9}{x(\sqrt{x+9}+3)} = \frac{x}{x(\sqrt{x+9}+3)} = \frac1{\sqrt{x+9}+3}.$$

$$\lim_{x\to0}\frac1{\sqrt{x+9}+3} = \frac1{3+3} = \frac16.$$

Practice 46

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to9}\dfrac{x-9}{\sqrt x-3}$ (the conjugate trick works even with the root in the denominator, applied the same way).

Solution

Multiply by $\dfrac{\sqrt x+3}{\sqrt x+3}$: $$\frac{(x-9)(\sqrt x+3)}{(\sqrt x-3)(\sqrt x+3)} = \frac{(x-9)(\sqrt x+3)}{x-9} = \sqrt x+3.$$

$$\lim_{x\to9}(\sqrt x+3) = 3+3=6.$$

Practice 47

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2}\dfrac{x^3-8}{x-2}$ (needs the difference-of-cubes factorisation, $a^3-b^3=(a-b)(a^2+ab+b^2)$).

Solution

$x^3-8 = (x-2)(x^2+2x+4)$.

$$\lim_{x\to2}(x^2+2x+4) = 4+4+4=12.$$

Practice 48

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to1}\dfrac{x^2-1}{\sqrt x-1}$ (needs both a factorisation and a conjugate step).

Solution

Factor the numerator: $x^2-1=(x-1)(x+1)$. Multiply by the conjugate $\sqrt x+1$ to handle the denominator:

$$\frac{(x-1)(x+1)(\sqrt x+1)}{(\sqrt x-1)(\sqrt x+1)} = \frac{(x-1)(x+1)(\sqrt x+1)}{x-1} = (x+1)(\sqrt x+1).$$

$$\lim_{x\to1}(x+1)(\sqrt x+1) = (2)(2) = 4.$$

Practice 49

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{(3+h)^2-9}h$ (this is literally a difference-quotient calculation, the same structure as Section 3, just with a specific number in place of a general $x$).

Solution

$(3+h)^2-9 = 9+6h+h^2-9 = 6h+h^2 = h(6+h)$.

$$\lim_{h\to0}\frac{h(6+h)}h = \lim_{h\to0}(6+h) = 6.$$

This is exactly $f'(3)$ for $f(x)=x^2$, matching Practice 26 precisely, the indeterminate-form technique here and the derivative-from-definition technique in Section 3 are the same skill, just applied to different-looking expressions.

Practice 50

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to5}\dfrac{x^2-25}{x^2-3x-10}$.

Solution

$x^2-25=(x-5)(x+5)$. $\ x^2-3x-10=(x-5)(x+2)$.

$$\lim_{x\to5}\frac{x+5}{x+2} = \frac{10}7.$$

6 Infinite limits and vertical asymptotes

When the denominator of a fraction approaches $0$ but the numerator does not, the fraction doesn't settle on a finite number, it grows without bound. Tracking the sign the denominator carries as it approaches zero from each side tells you whether the function shoots up to $+\infty$ or plunges to $-\infty$.

Key idea

If $f(x)\to c\neq0$ (finite, nonzero) and $g(x)\to0$ as $x\to a$, then $\dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)}\to\pm\infty$, with the sign determined by the signs of $f$ and $g$ near $a$ on that particular side. A vertical line $x=a$ is a vertical asymptote of $f$ when $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a^-}f(x)$ or $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a^+}f(x)$ (or both) is $\pm\infty$.

Worked example

Analyse $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2}\dfrac1{x-2}$ from both sides, and state the vertical asymptote.

As $x\to2^+$ ($x$ slightly bigger than $2$, like $2.01$): the denominator $x-2$ is a tiny positive number, so $\dfrac1{x-2}$ is a huge positive number.

$$\lim_{x\to2^+}\frac1{x-2} = +\infty.$$

As $x\to2^-$ ($x$ slightly smaller than $2$, like $1.99$): the denominator is a tiny negative number, so the fraction is a huge negative number.

$$\lim_{x\to2^-}\frac1{x-2} = -\infty.$$

The two sides disagree (one $+\infty$, one $-\infty$), so the ordinary two-sided limit doesn't exist, but there's still a vertical asymptote at $x=2$, the function is unbounded from both directions, even though it's unbounded in opposite ways.

Practice 51

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to3^+}\dfrac1{x-3}$.

Solution

Denominator is a tiny positive number as $x\to3^+$: $$\lim_{x\to3^+}\frac1{x-3} = +\infty.$$

Practice 52

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to3^-}\dfrac1{x-3}$.

Solution

Denominator is a tiny negative number as $x\to3^-$: $$\lim_{x\to3^-}\frac1{x-3} = -\infty.$$

Practice 53

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac1{x^2}$ (both sides at once, since the denominator is squared).

Solution

$x^2$ is always positive (for $x\neq0$), regardless of which side $x$ approaches $0$ from, so $\dfrac1{x^2}$ is always positive and grows without bound from both sides:

$$\lim_{x\to0}\frac1{x^2} = +\infty.$$

Unlike the worked example, both sides agree here (both $+\infty$), so it's meaningful to write the two-sided statement directly, even though the limit still isn't a finite number.

Practice 54

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to5^+}\dfrac x{x-5}$.

Solution

Numerator $\to5$ (positive, nonzero). Denominator is a tiny positive number as $x\to5^+$. Positive divided by tiny positive: $$\lim_{x\to5^+}\frac x{x-5} = +\infty.$$

Practice 55

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to-1}\dfrac1{(x+1)^2}$.

Solution

$(x+1)^2$ is always positive near $x=-1$ (squared, same reasoning as Practice 53), so both sides agree:

$$\lim_{x\to-1}\frac1{(x+1)^2} = +\infty.$$

Practice 56

Analyse $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to3}\dfrac{x+3}{x^2-9}$. Careful, factor first before deciding whether $x=3$ genuinely gives an infinite limit.

Solution

$x^2-9=(x-3)(x+3)$, so $\dfrac{x+3}{x^2-9} = \dfrac{x+3}{(x-3)(x+3)} = \dfrac1{x-3}$ for $x\neq-3$. The $(x+3)$ factor cancels, but only because $x=-3$ is a different point from the one being examined here.

At $x=3$: numerator of the simplified form is $1$ (nonzero), denominator $\to0$: this is a genuine vertical asymptote, with $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to3^+}=+\infty$ and $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to3^-}=-\infty$, exactly the worked example's pattern.

(By contrast, at the other zero of the original denominator, $x=-3$, the factor that caused it exactly cancelled, so that point is a removable hole, not an asymptote at all, a distinction developed fully in Practice 58 and Section 8.)

Practice 57

Find both vertical asymptotes of $f(x)=\dfrac{2x}{x^2-4}$, and determine the sign of $f$ (and hence $+\infty$ or $-\infty$) on each side of each asymptote.

Solution

$x^2-4=(x-2)(x+2)$, zero at $x=2$ and $x=-2$; the numerator $2x$ is nonzero at both, so both are genuine vertical asymptotes.

At $x=2$: $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2^+}f(x)=+\infty$ (numerator positive, denominator a tiny positive number), $\ \displaystyle\lim_{x\to2^-}f(x)=-\infty$.

At $x=-2$: $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to-2^+}f(x)=+\infty$, $\ \displaystyle\lim_{x\to-2^-}f(x)=-\infty$.

Both asymptotes share the identical left/right sign pattern here ($-\infty$ on the approach from below, $+\infty$ from above), worth noticing as a feature of this particular function's symmetry, not something to expect at every vertical asymptote in general.

Practice 58

Explain why $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac1x$ is correctly described as "does not exist," rather than being written as $\pm\infty$, even though both one-sided limits individually are infinite.

Solution

$\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}\frac1x=+\infty$ and $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^-}\frac1x=-\infty$, two individually well-understood infinite behaviours, but they disagree in sign, one side grows without bound in the positive direction, the other in the negative direction. Section 4's rule stands: a two-sided limit exists (even an infinite one, loosely speaking) only when both one-sided limits agree; here they emphatically don't, one is $+\infty$ and the other is $-\infty$, genuinely opposite behaviours, not two versions of the same trend. So the honest, precise statement is "does not exist," it would be misleading to write $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac1x=\infty$ as if there were one single unbounded trend, when there are actually two opposite ones on either side.

Practice 59

For $f(x)=\dfrac{x-1}{x^2-1}$, determine whether $x=1$ is a vertical asymptote or a removable hole, and find the actual behaviour there.

Solution

$x^2-1=(x-1)(x+1)$, so $f(x)=\dfrac{x-1}{(x-1)(x+1)} = \dfrac1{x+1}$ for $x\neq1$. The factor causing the zero at $x=1$ cancels completely.

$$\lim_{x\to1}f(x) = \lim_{x\to1}\frac1{x+1} = \frac12,$$

a genuine finite limit: $x=1$ is a removable hole (the graph has a gap there, but doesn't blow up), not a vertical asymptote. (By contrast, $x=-1$ is a true vertical asymptote, since $x+1\to0$ there with nothing left to cancel it.)

Practice 60

Explain the general procedure for telling whether a zero of a rational function's denominator is a genuine vertical asymptote or a removable hole, based on Practices 56 and 59.

Solution

Factor both the numerator and the denominator completely first, always, before drawing any conclusion about a zero of the denominator. If a factor cancels between numerator and denominator (Practice 59's $(x-1)$, or Practice 56's $(x+3)$ at the point $x=-3$), that zero is a removable hole: the simplified expression has a genuine finite limit there. If a factor of the denominator does not cancel against anything in the numerator (Practice 56's $(x-3)$, or either factor in Practice 57), that zero is a genuine vertical asymptote: the function blows up to $\pm\infty$ there. Never judge a denominator's zero as an asymptote by inspection alone, without checking first whether the numerator secretly shares that same factor.

7 Limits at infinity and horizontal asymptotes

A different question: what does a function do as $x$ grows without bound, in either direction? For a rational function (a polynomial over a polynomial), the answer only ever depends on the two leading terms, the rest of the polynomial becomes utterly irrelevant once $x$ is enormous. The standard technique makes this precise: divide every term, top and bottom, by the highest power of $x$ appearing anywhere in the expression.

Key idea

For a rational function $\dfrac{p(x)}{q(x)}$ as $x\to\pm\infty$: compare the degrees of $p$ and $q$.

Same degree: the limit is the ratio of the leading coefficients. Numerator's degree lower: the limit is $0$. Numerator's degree higher: the limit is $\pm\infty$ (no finite horizontal asymptote in that direction).

A horizontal asymptote at $y=L$ means $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}f(x)=L$ or $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to-\infty}f(x)=L$ (or both, possibly at two different heights).

Worked example

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{3x^2+1}{x^2-5}$.

Divide every term by $x^2$, the highest power present:

$$\frac{3x^2+1}{x^2-5} = \frac{3+\frac1{x^2}}{1-\frac5{x^2}}.$$

As $x\to\infty$, both $\dfrac1{x^2}$ and $\dfrac5{x^2}$ shrink toward $0$:

$$\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{3x^2+1}{x^2-5} = \frac{3+0}{1-0} = 3,$$

exactly the ratio of the two leading coefficients ($3$ and $1$), matching the key idea's "same degree" rule directly.

Practice 61

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{2x+1}{x-3}$.

Solution

Same degree (both linear); ratio of leading coefficients: $\dfrac21 = 2$.

Practice 62

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{5x^2}{x^3+1}$.

Solution

Denominator's degree ($3$) exceeds the numerator's ($2$): the limit is $0$.

Practice 63

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{x^3}{2x^2+1}$.

Solution

Numerator's degree ($3$) exceeds the denominator's ($2$): the limit is $+\infty$ (no horizontal asymptote in this direction).

Practice 64

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to-\infty}\dfrac{3x^2-2}{x^2+4}$.

Solution

Same degree; ratio of leading coefficients: $\dfrac31=3$. The rule works identically as $x\to-\infty$, the leading-term comparison doesn't care which direction $x$ is heading.

Practice 65

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{\sqrt{x^2+1}}x$ (careful: for $x>0$, $\sqrt{x^2}=x$, so this needs slightly more care than the polynomial cases above).

Solution

Divide inside the square root by $x^2$ (equivalently, factor $x^2$ out from under the root, using $x=\sqrt{x^2}$ since $x>0$ here):

$$\frac{\sqrt{x^2+1}}x = \frac{\sqrt{x^2\left(1+\frac1{x^2}\right)}}x = \frac{|x|\sqrt{1+\frac1{x^2}}}x = \frac{x\sqrt{1+\frac1{x^2}}}x = \sqrt{1+\frac1{x^2}}$$

(using $|x|=x$ since $x\to+\infty$ means $x>0$).

$$\lim_{x\to\infty}\sqrt{1+\frac1{x^2}} = \sqrt{1+0} = 1.$$

Practice 66

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to-\infty}\dfrac{\sqrt{x^2+1}}x$, and compare carefully with Practice 65.

Solution

Same setup as Practice 65, but now $x\to-\infty$ means $x<0$, so $|x|=-x$, not $x$:

$$\frac{\sqrt{x^2+1}}x = \frac{|x|\sqrt{1+\frac1{x^2}}}x = \frac{-x\sqrt{1+\frac1{x^2}}}x = -\sqrt{1+\frac1{x^2}}.$$

$$\lim_{x\to-\infty}\left(-\sqrt{1+\frac1{x^2}}\right) = -1.$$

The sign genuinely flips between the two directions, $1$ as $x\to\infty$, but $-1$ as $x\to-\infty$, purely because of the hidden $|x|$ lurking inside $\sqrt{x^2}$. This function has two different horizontal asymptotes, $y=1$ and $y=-1$, one for each direction, exactly the kind of case the key idea flagged as possible.

Practice 67

Find the horizontal asymptote of $f(x)=\dfrac{4x-1}{2x+3}$.

Solution

Same degree; ratio of leading coefficients: $\dfrac42=2$. Horizontal asymptote: $y=2$ (and by the same computation as $x\to-\infty$, this holds in both directions here, since it's a same-degree case, unlike Practices 65–66).

Practice 68

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{7x^5-2x}{x^5+3x^2-1}$.

Solution

Same degree (both degree $5$); ratio of leading coefficients: $\dfrac71=7$. Every other term, $-2x$, $3x^2$, $-1$, becomes utterly negligible next to the $x^5$ terms once $x$ is huge, exactly the point of the key idea's "only the leading terms matter" rule.

Practice 69

Determine the end behaviour of $f(x)=\dfrac{x^2-1}{x+1}$ as $x\to\infty$. (Simplify first, don't apply the degree-comparison rule blindly.)

Solution

$x^2-1=(x-1)(x+1)$, so $f(x) = \dfrac{(x-1)(x+1)}{x+1} = x-1$ for $x\neq-1$. The function is secretly just a line (with a hole at $x=-1$), not a genuine rational function once simplified.

$$\lim_{x\to\infty}(x-1) = +\infty.$$

No horizontal asymptote exists, even though the raw, unsimplified degrees (numerator $2$, denominator $1$) might tempt you to reach for the "numerator's degree is higher, so $\pm\infty$" rule directly, which happens to give the right qualitative answer here, but only because the simplification confirms it; always simplify first rather than trusting raw degree-counting on an unfactored expression.

Practice 70

Explain, using the "divide by the highest power" technique, why a rational function's limit at infinity only ever depends on the leading terms of the numerator and denominator, and never on any of the lower-degree terms.

Solution

Dividing every term by the highest power of $x$ present turns every non-leading term into something of the form $\dfrac{c}{x^k}$ for some $k\ge1$, and every one of those fractions shrinks to $0$ as $x\to\pm\infty$ (Practices 62's denominator terms, or the worked example's $\frac1{x^2}$ and $\frac5{x^2}$, are exactly this). Only the leading terms, the ones that turn into plain constants (no $x$ left at all) after the division, survive the limit untouched. So no matter how complicated the rest of the polynomial looks, once $x$ is large enough, only the highest-power terms are still contributing anything non-negligible to the ratio, everything else has already been dwarfed into irrelevance.

8 Continuity

A function is continuous at a point when there's no gap, jump, or blow-up there, informally, when you could trace the graph through that point without lifting your pen. Formally, this comes down to three separate conditions all holding simultaneously, and every discontinuity you've met so far in this lesson (Sections 1, 4, and 6) turns out to be exactly one of these three conditions failing.

Key idea

$f$ is continuous at $x=a$ when all three hold: (1) $f(a)$ is defined; (2) $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}f(x)$ exists; (3) $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=f(a)$.

If condition (2) fails because $f(a)$ is undefined but the limit itself exists (Section 1, Practice 5), it's a removable discontinuity. If condition (2) fails because the one-sided limits disagree (Section 4), it's a jump discontinuity. If condition (2) fails because the limit is infinite (Section 6), it's an infinite discontinuity.

Differentiable implies continuous, but not the reverse

If $f'(a)$ exists (Section 3's limit is a genuine finite number), then $f$ must be continuous at $a$: the existence of $\displaystyle\lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{f(a+h)-f(a)}h$ as a finite number forces $f(a+h)-f(a)\to0$ as $h\to0$ (otherwise the ratio itself couldn't settle on a finite value), which is exactly $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=f(a)$, continuity.

The reverse fails. Take $f(x)=|x|$ at $a=0$: continuous, since $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}|x|=0=f(0)$. But the derivative from the left is $\displaystyle\lim_{h\to0^-}\dfrac{|0+h|-0}h = \dfrac{-h}h=-1$, while from the right it's $\displaystyle\lim_{h\to0^+}\dfrac{h}h=1$. Different one-sided derivatives, so $f'(0)$ doesn't exist (Section 4's disagreement condition, now applied to the difference quotient itself, not to $f$ directly): the graph has a sharp corner at the origin, perfectly connected, but not smooth.

Practice 71

Verify $f(x)=x^2+1$ is continuous at $x=2$, checking all three conditions explicitly.

Solution

(1) $f(2)=4+1=5$, defined. (2) $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2}(x^2+1)=5$, exists (direct substitution, a polynomial). (3) $5=5$ ✓. All three conditions hold: continuous at $x=2$.

Practice 72

Let $f(x)=\begin{cases}x+2&x\le1\\3x&x>1\end{cases}$. Determine whether $f$ is continuous at $x=1$.

Solution

$f(1)=1+2=3$. $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to1^-}(x+2)=3$. $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to1^+}3x=3$. All three agree, $3=3=3$: continuous at $x=1$.

Practice 73

Find the value of $k$ that makes $f(x)=\begin{cases}kx+1&x<2\\x^2-1&x\ge2\end{cases}$ continuous at $x=2$.

Solution

$f(2)=4-1=3$ (using the right-hand piece, since $x=2$ falls in that case). For continuity, the left-hand limit must also equal $3$:

$$\lim_{x\to2^-}(kx+1) = 2k+1 = 3 \quad\Rightarrow\quad k=1.$$

Practice 74

Classify the discontinuity of $f(x)=\dfrac{x^2-4}{x-2}$ at $x=2$.

Solution

$f(2)$ is undefined (condition (1) fails), but $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2}f(x)=4$ exists (Section 1's worked example). Undefined at the point, but a perfectly good limit exists there: a removable discontinuity, a single-point hole in the graph.

Practice 75

Classify the discontinuity of $f(x)=\dfrac1{x-3}$ at $x=3$.

Solution

$\displaystyle\lim_{x\to3^+}f(x)=+\infty$, $\ \displaystyle\lim_{x\to3^-}f(x)=-\infty$ (Section 6): condition (2) fails because the limit is infinite. An infinite discontinuity, matching the vertical asymptote there.

Practice 76

Classify the discontinuity of $f(x)=\dfrac{|x|}x$ at $x=0$.

Solution

From Practice 32: $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^-}f(x)=-1$, $\ \displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}f(x)=1$, disagreeing finite one-sided limits: a jump discontinuity (the graph genuinely leaps from height $-1$ to height $1$).

Practice 77

Verify $f(x)=|x|$ is continuous at $x=0$ (condition-by-condition), but explain, referencing the "why" box, why this does not mean $f$ is differentiable there.

Solution

(1) $f(0)=0$, defined. (2) $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}|x|=0$, exists. (3) $0=0$ ✓. Continuous.

But continuity only requires the function itself to have matching one-sided limits, it says nothing about the difference quotient's one-sided limits, which is a separate, stricter question (differentiability). The "why" box already computed those separately: left derivative $-1$, right derivative $1$, disagreeing. A function can be perfectly connected (continuous) while still having a sharp corner (non-differentiable) at the very same point, connectedness and smoothness are genuinely different properties.

Practice 78

Let $f(x)=\begin{cases}x^2&x\le0\\2x&x>0\end{cases}$. Verify $f$ is continuous at $x=0$, then determine whether $f$ is differentiable there.

Solution

Continuity: $f(0)=0$. $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^-}x^2=0$. $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}2x=0$. All agree: continuous.

Differentiability: left derivative, using $f(x)=x^2$: $\displaystyle\lim_{h\to0^-}\dfrac{h^2-0}h = \lim_{h\to0^-}h=0$. Right derivative, using $f(x)=2x$: $\displaystyle\lim_{h\to0^+}\dfrac{2h-0}h = \lim_{h\to0^+}2=2$.

$0\neq2$: the one-sided derivatives disagree, so $f$ is not differentiable at $x=0$, despite being continuous there, another corner, just like $|x|$.

Practice 79

Can $f(x)=\dfrac{x^3-1}{x-1}$ be made continuous at $x=1$ by defining $f(1)$ appropriately? If so, find the value $f(1)$ would need.

Solution

$x^3-1=(x-1)(x^2+x+1)$ (difference-of-cubes factorisation), so $f(x)=x^2+x+1$ for $x\neq1$.

$$\lim_{x\to1}f(x) = 1+1+1=3.$$

Yes: defining $f(1)=3$ makes all three continuity conditions hold, this is precisely what "removable" means, the discontinuity can be patched by filling in a single, correctly-chosen point.

Practice 80

Using the derivative definition from Section 3, explain precisely why $f'(a)$ existing (as a finite number) forces $f$ to be continuous at $a$.

Solution

If $\displaystyle\lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{f(a+h)-f(a)}h = f'(a)$, a specific finite number, then for the ratio itself to settle on a finite value as $h\to0$ (rather than blowing up, since the denominator $h$ is shrinking to $0$), the numerator $f(a+h)-f(a)$ must also shrink to $0$ at a matching rate, if it didn't, the fraction would blow up to $\pm\infty$ instead of settling on $f'(a)$. Formally: $f(a+h)-f(a) = h\cdot\dfrac{f(a+h)-f(a)}h \to 0\cdot f'(a) = 0$ as $h\to0$ (product of something $\to0$ with something $\to$ a finite number). That's exactly $\displaystyle\lim_{h\to0}f(a+h)=f(a)$, i.e.\ $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=f(a)$: continuity, derived directly from differentiability's own definition, not assumed separately.

9 L'Hôpital's Rule

Everything in Section 5 relied on being able to factor or rationalise your way out of a $\frac00$ form. Plenty of genuinely important limits, ones involving $\ln x$, $e^x$, or trig functions mixed together, don't factor at all. L'Hôpital's Rule is the payoff for already knowing how to differentiate: for exactly the indeterminate forms $\frac00$ and $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$, you can replace the limit of a quotient with the limit of the quotient of derivatives, and that new limit is very often dramatically easier.

Key idea

If $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}g(x)=0$ (a $\frac00$ form), or both $\to\pm\infty$ (a $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$ form), and $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}\dfrac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}$ exists, then:

$$\lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x\to a}\frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}.$$

Crucially, this rule only applies to genuinely indeterminate forms. If direct substitution already gives a real number (not $\frac00$ or $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$), L'Hôpital's Rule simply doesn't apply, there's nothing indeterminate left to resolve. And if, after one application, the new limit is still indeterminate, you can apply the rule again, taking derivatives a second (or third) time.

Worked example

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\sin x/x$ using L'Hôpital's Rule (cross-checking Practice 8's numerical answer), then evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{1-\cos(2x)}{x^2}$, which needs the rule applied twice.

First one: direct substitution gives $\dfrac{\sin0}0=\dfrac00$, genuinely indeterminate, so L'Hôpital applies. Differentiate top and bottom separately: $\dfrac{d}{dx}\sin x=\cos x$, $\ \dfrac{d}{dx}x=1$.

$$\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\sin x}x = \lim_{x\to0}\frac{\cos x}1 = \cos0=1,$$

matching Practice 8's table exactly, now confirmed algebraically rather than numerically.

Second one: direct substitution gives $\dfrac{1-\cos0}0=\dfrac00$. Differentiate: $\dfrac{d}{dx}\big[1-\cos(2x)\big]=2\sin(2x)$, $\ \dfrac{d}{dx}x^2=2x$.

$$\lim_{x\to0}\frac{2\sin(2x)}{2x} = \lim_{x\to0}\frac{\sin(2x)}x.$$

Substituting $x=0$ here gives $\dfrac{\sin0}0=\dfrac00$ again, still indeterminate: apply L'Hôpital a second time. Differentiate again: $\dfrac{d}{dx}\sin(2x)=2\cos(2x)$, $\ \dfrac{d}{dx}x=1$.

$$\lim_{x\to0}\frac{2\cos(2x)}1 = 2\cos0 = 2.$$

So $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{1-\cos(2x)}{x^2}=2$, reached only by recognising, honestly, that the form was still $\frac00$ after the first application, and applying the rule again rather than stopping too early.

Practice 81

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{e^x-1}x$ using L'Hôpital's Rule.

Solution

Direct substitution: $\dfrac{e^0-1}0=\dfrac00$, indeterminate. Differentiate: $\dfrac{d}{dx}(e^x-1)=e^x$, $\ \dfrac{d}{dx}x=1$.

$$\lim_{x\to0}\frac{e^x}1 = e^0 = 1.$$

Practice 82

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{1-\cos x}{x^2}$ using L'Hôpital's Rule (you may need to apply it more than once, check carefully after each step).

Solution

$\dfrac{1-\cos0}0=\dfrac00$. Differentiate: $\dfrac{\sin x}{2x}$. Substituting $x=0$: $\dfrac{\sin0}0=\dfrac00$, still indeterminate, apply again: $\dfrac{d}{dx}\sin x=\cos x$, $\ \dfrac{d}{dx}2x=2$.

$$\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\cos x}2 = \frac12.$$

Practice 83

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{\ln x}x$ using L'Hôpital's Rule.

Solution

$\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$ form. Differentiate: $\dfrac{d}{dx}\ln x=\dfrac1x$, $\ \dfrac{d}{dx}x=1$.

$$\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{1/x}1 = \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac1x = 0.$$

A genuinely useful fact this confirms: $\ln x$ grows without bound, but so slowly that it becomes negligible compared to $x$ itself.

Practice 84

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{x-\sin x}{x^3}$ using L'Hôpital's Rule. This one genuinely needs three applications, don't stop early just because the algebra looks like it should be settling down.

Solution

$\dfrac{0-\sin0}0=\dfrac00$. First derivative: $\dfrac{1-\cos x}{3x^2}$. At $x=0$: $\dfrac{1-1}0=\dfrac00$, still indeterminate.

Second derivative: $\dfrac{\sin x}{6x}$. At $x=0$: $\dfrac{0}0$, still indeterminate, a third round is genuinely necessary here.

Third derivative: $\dfrac{\cos x}6$. At $x=0$: $\dfrac{\cos0}6 = \dfrac16$.

$$\lim_{x\to0}\frac{x-\sin x}{x^3} = \frac16.$$

This is the single most important habit L'Hôpital's Rule demands: check the form every single time before deciding you're done, never assume one or two rounds is automatically enough.

Practice 85

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{x^2}{e^x}$ using L'Hôpital's Rule (this one needs two applications).

Solution

$\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$. Differentiate: $\dfrac{2x}{e^x}$. As $x\to\infty$: still $\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$, apply again: $\dfrac{2}{e^x}$.

$$\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{2}{e^x} = 0,$$

since $e^x\to\infty$. This confirms $e^x$ eventually outpaces any power of $x$, not just $x$ itself, the exact opposite growth-rate relationship from Practice 83's $\ln x$ falling behind $x$.

Practice 86

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to1}\dfrac{\ln x}{x-1}$ using L'Hôpital's Rule.

Solution

$\dfrac{\ln1}0=\dfrac00$. Differentiate: $\dfrac{1/x}1 = \dfrac1x$.

$$\lim_{x\to1}\frac1x = 1.$$

Practice 87

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2}\dfrac{x^2-4}{x-2}$ using L'Hôpital's Rule, and confirm it matches Practice 41's factoring answer.

Solution

$\dfrac00$. Differentiate: $\dfrac{2x}1=2x$.

$$\lim_{x\to2}2x = 4,$$

matching Practice 41 exactly. For a simple polynomial ratio like this one, factoring and L'Hôpital are both about equally fast, the real value of L'Hôpital shows up on expressions (like Practices 83–86) that don't factor at all.

Practice 88

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}x\ln x$. This isn't in the $\frac00$ or $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$ form yet, it's a "$0\times\infty$" form, rewrite it as a quotient first before L'Hôpital can apply.

Solution

Rewrite $x\ln x = \dfrac{\ln x}{1/x}$: as $x\to0^+$, $\ln x\to-\infty$ and $\dfrac1x\to+\infty$, a genuine $\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$ (well, $\dfrac{-\infty}{\infty}$, the rule still applies) form now.

Differentiate: $\dfrac{d}{dx}\ln x = \dfrac1x$, $\ \dfrac{d}{dx}x^{-1} = -x^{-2}$.

$$\lim_{x\to0^+}\frac{1/x}{-1/x^2} = \lim_{x\to0^+}\left(-x\right) = 0.$$

A useful general lesson: L'Hôpital's Rule only ever applies directly to $\frac00$ or $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$; forms like $0\times\infty$, $\infty-\infty$, or $1^\infty$ all need to be algebraically massaged into one of the two standard forms first, before the rule can be used at all.

Practice 89

Explain why applying L'Hôpital's Rule to $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{x+1}{x+2}$ would be a misuse of the rule, even though differentiating top and bottom happens not to change the numeric answer for this particular pair of functions.

Solution

Direct substitution gives $\dfrac{0+1}{0+2} = \dfrac12$, a perfectly ordinary, non-indeterminate real number, no division-by-zero problem, nothing left unresolved. L'Hôpital's Rule is a tool for resolving indeterminate forms specifically; here there simply isn't one to resolve. (Differentiating anyway happens to give $\dfrac{d}{dx}(x+1)=1$ and $\dfrac{d}{dx}(x+2)=1$, so $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac11=1\neq\frac12$: applying the rule here would actually have produced the wrong answer, not merely an unnecessary one, a useful cautionary example that misusing the rule outside its actual conditions isn't just sloppy, it can be flatly incorrect.) Always check that the form is genuinely $\frac00$ or $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$ by direct substitution first, every single time, before reaching for derivatives.

Practice 90

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{3x^2+2x}{5x^2-1}$ two ways: using Section 7's "divide by the highest power" technique, and using L'Hôpital's Rule (two applications). Confirm they agree, and comment on which method felt faster.

Solution

Section 7's method: same degree, ratio of leading coefficients: $\dfrac35$.

L'Hôpital: $\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$ form. First derivative: $\dfrac{6x+2}{10x}$, as $x\to\infty$ still $\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$. Second derivative: $\dfrac6{10}=\dfrac35$.

Both methods agree: $\dfrac35$. For rational functions specifically, Section 7's degree-comparison trick is almost always faster, no differentiation required at all, just reading off leading coefficients. L'Hôpital's real advantage shows up on the non-polynomial cases from earlier in this section ($\ln x$, $e^x$, trig functions), exactly the situations where no purely algebraic shortcut like Section 7's exists at all.
