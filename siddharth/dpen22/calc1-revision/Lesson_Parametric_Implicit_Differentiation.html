Differentiation

Parametric and Implicit Differentiation

A from-scratch lesson on two situations where you cannot simply differentiate $y$ as a plain function of $x$: when $x$ and $y$ are tangled together in a single equation (implicit differentiation), and when both are separately controlled by a third variable (parametric differentiation). Both techniques, and their common application to related rates and tangent lines, are motivated before they're used. Solutions stay hidden until you click.

1 Implicit differentiation: the basics

Every derivative you have computed so far has come from an equation already solved for $y$: $y=x^2$, $y=\sin x$, and so on. But plenty of natural equations, $x^2+y^2=25$ (a circle), $x^3+y^3=6xy$ (a looping curve called the folium of Descartes), are not solved for $y$, and often cannot be solved for $y$ at all without splitting into multiple ugly cases or square roots. Implicit differentiation finds $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ directly from an equation like this, without ever solving for $y$ first.

Key idea

Differentiate both sides of the equation with respect to $x$, treating $y$ as an unspecified function of $x$. Every time you differentiate a term containing $y$, the chain rule produces a factor of $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ (often written $y'$), since you are really differentiating "the outside function, with respect to $y$, times $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$." Once every term is differentiated, collect all the $y'$ terms on one side and solve for $y'$ algebraically.

Why a $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ appears out of nowhere

It doesn't appear out of nowhere, it is the chain rule, exactly as you already know it, just applied to a $y$ you haven't been given an explicit formula for. Differentiating $y^2$ with respect to $x$ means differentiating $(y(x))^2$: by the chain rule, that is $2y(x)\cdot y'(x)$, i.e.\ $2y\,y'$. Compare this with differentiating $\sin(u(x))$, which everyone accepts gives $\cos(u)\cdot u'$, without needing to know $u$'s formula either. Implicit differentiation is not a new rule, it is the ordinary chain rule, applied to a function $y(x)$ whose explicit formula you are choosing not to write down (or cannot).

Worked example

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ if $x^2+y^2=25$.

Differentiate both sides with respect to $x$: $\dfrac{d}{dx}(x^2) + \dfrac{d}{dx}(y^2) = \dfrac{d}{dx}(25)$.

$2x + 2y\,y' = 0$ (the $y^2$ term picks up a $y'$ by the chain rule, exactly as in the "why" box; the right side, a constant, differentiates to $0$).

Solve for $y'$: $2y\,y' = -2x \Rightarrow y' = -\dfrac{x}y$.

Practice 1

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ if $x^2+y^2=9$.

Solution

Same structure as the worked example: $2x+2y\,y'=0 \Rightarrow y' = -\dfrac{x}y$.

Practice 2

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ if $xy+y^2=4$.

Solution

Differentiate $xy$ with the product rule: $\dfrac{d}{dx}(xy) = 1\cdot y + x\cdot y' = y+xy'$. Differentiate $y^2$: $2y\,y'$.

$$y+xy'+2y\,y' = 0 \Rightarrow y'(x+2y) = -y \Rightarrow y' = -\frac{y}{x+2y}.$$

Practice 3

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ if $x^3+y^3=6xy$ (the folium of Descartes).

Solution

Left side: $3x^2+3y^2y'$. Right side needs the product rule: $\dfrac{d}{dx}(6xy) = 6y+6xy'$.

$$3x^2+3y^2y' = 6y+6xy' \Rightarrow 3y^2y'-6xy' = 6y-3x^2 \Rightarrow y'(3y^2-6x)=6y-3x^2.$$

$$y' = \frac{6y-3x^2}{3y^2-6x} = \frac{2y-x^2}{y^2-2x}.$$

Practice 4

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ if $\sin(y)=x$.

Solution

$\cos(y)\,y' = 1 \Rightarrow y' = \dfrac1{\cos y}$.

This is worth noticing: $\sin(y)=x$ is exactly the equation defining $y=\arcsin x$, so this calculation is secretly deriving the formula for the derivative of $\arcsin x$ from scratch. (Using $\cos y=\sqrt{1-\sin^2y}=\sqrt{1-x^2}$, this becomes the familiar $y'=\dfrac1{\sqrt{1-x^2}}$.)

Practice 5

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ if $x^2y+y^3=x$.

Solution

$x^2y$ needs the product rule: $\dfrac{d}{dx}(x^2y) = 2xy+x^2y'$. Plus $\dfrac{d}{dx}(y^3)=3y^2y'$.

$$2xy+x^2y'+3y^2y' = 1 \Rightarrow y'(x^2+3y^2) = 1-2xy \Rightarrow y' = \frac{1-2xy}{x^2+3y^2}.$$

Practice 6

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ if $e^{xy}=x+y$.

Solution

Left side by the chain rule (inner function $xy$, needing the product rule inside): $\dfrac{d}{dx}\big(e^{xy}\big) = e^{xy}\cdot(y+xy')$. Right side: $1+y'$.

$$e^{xy}(y+xy') = 1+y' \Rightarrow ye^{xy}+xe^{xy}y' = 1+y' \Rightarrow y'(xe^{xy}-1) = 1-ye^{xy}.$$

$$y' = \frac{1-ye^{xy}}{xe^{xy}-1}.$$

Practice 7

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ if $x^2y^3=8$.

Solution

Product rule on the left: $\dfrac{d}{dx}(x^2y^3) = 2xy^3+x^2(3y^2y') = 2xy^3+3x^2y^2y'$.

$$2xy^3+3x^2y^2y' = 0 \Rightarrow y' = -\frac{2xy^3}{3x^2y^2} = -\frac{2y}{3x}.$$

Practice 8

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ if $\tan(xy)=x$.

Solution

Chain rule with product rule inside: $\dfrac{d}{dx}\tan(xy) = \sec^2(xy)\cdot(y+xy')$.

$$\sec^2(xy)(y+xy') = 1 \Rightarrow y\sec^2(xy)+x\sec^2(xy)y' = 1 \Rightarrow y' = \frac{1-y\sec^2(xy)}{x\sec^2(xy)}.$$

Multiplying top and bottom by $\cos^2(xy)$ gives the equivalent, tidier form $y' = \dfrac{\cos^2(xy)-y}x$.

2 Second derivatives (implicit)

Finding $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ implicitly is not a new technique, it is Section 1's technique applied twice: differentiate the original equation once to get $y'$ in terms of $x$ and $y$, then differentiate that expression again with respect to $x$ (remembering that $y$ is still secretly a function of $x$, so the chain rule keeps applying, and remembering to substitute your first-derivative result whenever a $y'$ shows up in the second round).

Key idea

Differentiate the equation for $y'$ found in the first round, treating $y$ as a function of $x$ (so any leftover $y$ still produces a $y'$ term when differentiated), then substitute the known expression for $y'$ back in to get an answer purely in terms of $x$ and $y$.

Worked example

Find $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ if $x^2+y^2=25$.

From the Section 1 worked example, $y' = -\dfrac{x}y$.

Differentiate again using the quotient rule (treating $y$ as a function of $x$):

$$y'' = -\frac{(1)(y) - x(y')}{y^2} = -\frac{y-xy'}{y^2}.$$

Substitute $y'=-\dfrac{x}y$: $y'' = -\dfrac{y-x\left(-\frac{x}y\right)}{y^2} = -\dfrac{y+\frac{x^2}y}{y^2} = -\dfrac{\frac{y^2+x^2}y}{y^2} = -\dfrac{x^2+y^2}{y^3}$.

Using the original equation $x^2+y^2=25$ to simplify: $y'' = -\dfrac{25}{y^3}$.

Practice 9

Find $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ if $xy=1$, and simplify using $y=\dfrac1x$ to check your answer against direct differentiation of $y=x^{-1}$.

Solution

$y+xy'=0 \Rightarrow y' = -\dfrac{y}x$.

Differentiate again (quotient rule): $y'' = -\dfrac{y'x-y}{x^2}$. Substitute $y'=-y/x$: $y'' = -\dfrac{-\frac{y}xx-y}{x^2} = -\dfrac{-y-y}{x^2} = \dfrac{2y}{x^2}$.

Using $y=1/x$: $y'' = \dfrac{2/x}{x^2} = \dfrac2{x^3}$, exactly matching $\dfrac{d^2}{dx^2}(x^{-1}) = 2x^{-3}$ computed directly.

Practice 10

Find $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ if $x^2-y^2=1$.

Solution

$2x-2yy'=0 \Rightarrow y'=\dfrac{x}y$.

$y'' = \dfrac{(1)y-x(y')}{y^2} = \dfrac{y-x\left(\frac{x}y\right)}{y^2} = \dfrac{\frac{y^2-x^2}y}{y^2} = \dfrac{y^2-x^2}{y^3}$.

Using $x^2-y^2=1 \Rightarrow y^2-x^2=-1$: $y'' = -\dfrac1{y^3}$.

Practice 11

Find $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ if $y^2=x^3$, and simplify your answer using the original equation.

Solution

$2yy'=3x^2 \Rightarrow y' = \dfrac{3x^2}{2y}$.

Quotient rule: $y'' = \dfrac{6x(2y)-3x^2(2y')}{4y^2} = \dfrac{12xy-6x^2y'}{4y^2}$. Substitute $y'=\dfrac{3x^2}{2y}$: numerator becomes $12xy-6x^2\left(\dfrac{3x^2}{2y}\right) = 12xy-\dfrac{9x^4}y = \dfrac{12xy^2-9x^4}y$.

$$y'' = \frac{12xy^2-9x^4}{4y^3}.$$

Using $y^2=x^3$, so $12xy^2=12x\cdot x^3=12x^4$: $y'' = \dfrac{12x^4-9x^4}{4y^3} = \dfrac{3x^4}{4y^3}$. Since $x^4=x\cdot x^3=xy^2$, this simplifies further to $y''=\dfrac{3xy^2}{4y^3}=\dfrac{3x}{4y}$.

Practice 12

Without redoing the full calculation, explain why $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ for a circle $x^2+y^2=r^2$ is always negative when $y>0$ (the top half of the circle), and connect this to the shape of that half of the graph.

Solution

By the same method as the worked example, $y'' = -\dfrac{r^2}{y^3}$ for any circle of radius $r$. When $y>0$, $y^3>0$ too, so $y''=-\dfrac{r^2}{y^3}$ is negative (a positive number divided by a positive number, then negated).

A negative second derivative means the curve is concave down, curving like the top of a dome, exactly the shape of the upper half of a circle. This matches the picture perfectly: the top semicircle bulges upward and then curves back down on both sides, concave down everywhere along its length.

Practice 13

Find $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ if $y=x^2+y^2$ (a shifted circle in disguise; don't try to solve for $y$ explicitly, just apply the implicit method directly).

Solution

$y' = 2x+2yy' \Rightarrow y'-2yy' = 2x \Rightarrow y'(1-2y)=2x \Rightarrow y' = \dfrac{2x}{1-2y}$.

Quotient rule: $y'' = \dfrac{2(1-2y) - 2x(-2y')}{(1-2y)^2} = \dfrac{2-4y+4xy'}{(1-2y)^2}$.

Substitute $y'=\dfrac{2x}{1-2y}$: $4xy' = \dfrac{8x^2}{1-2y}$, so

$$y'' = \frac{2-4y+\frac{8x^2}{1-2y}}{(1-2y)^2} = \frac{(2-4y)(1-2y)+8x^2}{(1-2y)^3}.$$

This is messier than the earlier examples, and that is a fair reflection of reality, not every implicit second derivative simplifies to something as clean as the circle's $-\dfrac{r^2}{y^3}$; sometimes the honest answer is simply a more complicated expression.

Practice 14

Explain in general terms why, whenever you find $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ implicitly, it is worth substituting the original equation back in at the end (as in the worked example and Practice 9-11), rather than leaving the answer in whatever form falls out of the quotient rule.

Solution

The raw output of differentiating twice typically contains both $x$ and $y$ mixed together in a way that looks more complicated than necessary, since it doesn't yet "know about" the constraint linking $x$ and $y$ in the first place. Substituting the original equation back in exploits that constraint to cancel or combine terms, often revealing a much simpler final answer (compare the worked example's raw $-\dfrac{x^2+y^2}{y^3}$ against the simplified $-\dfrac{25}{y^3}$). It is the same idea as simplifying a fraction using a known relationship between its parts, rather than presenting an answer in its most complicated, unreduced form.

3 Tangent and normal lines (implicit)

Once you can find $y'$ implicitly, finding the tangent line to a curve like $x^2+y^2=25$ at a given point is no different from finding a tangent line anywhere else: evaluate the slope at that point, then use the point-slope form of a line. The normal line (perpendicular to the tangent at that point) uses the negative reciprocal of that slope.

Key idea

At a point $(x_0,y_0)$ on the curve, find $y'$ implicitly and evaluate it at that point to get the tangent slope $m$. The tangent line is $y-y_0 = m(x-x_0)$. The normal line, perpendicular to the tangent, is $y-y_0 = -\dfrac1m(x-x_0)$.

Worked example

Find the tangent and normal lines to $x^2+y^2=25$ at $(3,4)$.

From Section 1, $y'=-\dfrac{x}y$. At $(3,4)$: $m=-\dfrac34$.

Tangent line: $y-4 = -\dfrac34(x-3)$.

Normal line: slope $-\dfrac1m = \dfrac43$, so $y-4=\dfrac43(x-3)$. (This particular normal line, for a circle, always passes straight through the centre, here the origin, since the radius to any point on a circle is always perpendicular to the tangent there, a fact you can check directly: the line $y-4=\frac43(x-3)$ does pass through $(0,0)$.)

Practice 15

Verify that $(1,2)$ lies on $x^2+xy+y^2=7$, then find the tangent line there.

Solution

Check: $1^2+1(2)+2^2 = 1+2+4=7$ ✓.

Implicit differentiation: $2x+(y+xy')+2yy' = 0 \Rightarrow y'(x+2y) = -2x-y \Rightarrow y' = -\dfrac{2x+y}{x+2y}$.

At $(1,2)$: $y' = -\dfrac{2+2}{1+4} = -\dfrac45$.

Tangent: $y-2 = -\dfrac45(x-1)$.

Practice 16

Find the tangent line to $y^2=4x$ at $(1,2)$.

Solution

$2yy'=4 \Rightarrow y' = \dfrac2y$. At $(1,2)$: $y'=\dfrac22=1$.

Tangent: $y-2 = 1(x-1)$, i.e.\ $y=x+1$.

Practice 17

Find the normal line to $x^2+y^2=25$ at $(-3,4)$.

Solution

$y'=-\dfrac{x}y$, so at $(-3,4)$: tangent slope $= -\dfrac{-3}4=\dfrac34$.

Normal slope $=-\dfrac1{3/4} = -\dfrac43$.

Normal line: $y-4=-\dfrac43(x-(-3)) = -\dfrac43(x+3)$. (Again, this should pass through the origin, the circle's centre: checking, at $x=0$, $y=4-\dfrac43(3)=4-4=0$ ✓.)

Practice 18

Verify that $(3,3)$ lies on the folium $x^3+y^3=6xy$, and find the tangent line there.

Solution

Check: $27+27=54$, and $6(3)(3)=54$ ✓.

From Practice 3, $y' = \dfrac{2y-x^2}{y^2-2x}$. At $(3,3)$: $y' = \dfrac{6-9}{9-6} = \dfrac{-3}3=-1$.

Tangent: $y-3 = -1(x-3)$, i.e.\ $y=-x+6$.

Practice 19

Find the point(s) on $x^2+y^2=25$ where the tangent line is horizontal, using the fact that a horizontal tangent needs $y'=0$.

Solution

$y' = -\dfrac{x}y = 0$ requires $x=0$ (with $y\neq0$). Substituting $x=0$ into $x^2+y^2=25$: $y^2=25 \Rightarrow y=\pm5$.

Horizontal tangents at $(0,5)$ and $(0,-5)$, the very top and bottom of the circle, exactly where you would expect a circle's tangent to be horizontal.

Practice 20

Find the point(s) on $x^2+y^2=25$ where the tangent line is vertical, and explain why the implicit formula $y'=-x/y$ signals this differently than it signalled the horizontal case in Practice 19.

Solution

A vertical tangent corresponds to an undefined (infinite) slope, which happens here when the denominator $y=0$ (with $x\neq0$, so the fraction is a genuine "nonzero over zero," not the indeterminate $0/0$). Setting $y=0$ in $x^2+y^2=25$ gives $x=\pm5$.

Vertical tangents at $(5,0)$ and $(-5,0)$. Notice the contrast with Practice 19: a horizontal tangent shows up as the numerator of $y'$ vanishing, while a vertical tangent shows up as the denominator vanishing, two structurally different conditions that are easy to mix up if you only check one of them.

4 Related rates

Related rates problems describe two or more quantities that are linked by an equation (a ladder's height and base distance, a balloon's radius and volume) and change together over time. Implicit differentiation, with respect to time $t$ rather than $x$, turns the linking equation into a relationship between the rates of change of those quantities, which is exactly what these problems ask you to find.

Key idea: the standard recipe

1. Draw a picture and label every changing quantity with a variable (not a specific number yet).

2. Write down an equation relating the variables, true at every instant in time (not just the instant you care about).

3. Differentiate that equation with respect to $t$ (implicitly, exactly as in Sections 1-2, except every variable is secretly a function of $t$ now, not just $y$ a function of $x$).

4. Only now substitute the specific numbers given for the instant you care about, and solve for the unknown rate.

Why the numbers must wait until step 4

If you substitute a specific numerical snapshot (say, "the ladder's base is $6$ feet from the wall") into the equation before differentiating, you have frozen the equation at a single instant, and differentiating a constant just gives $0$, destroying all the information about how things are changing. The equation from step 2 needs to stay general, expressed in terms of changing variables, all the way through the differentiation step, so that the chain rule can produce the $\dfrac{dx}{dt}$ and $\dfrac{dy}{dt}$ terms you actually need. Only after differentiating is it safe to plug in the specific snapshot you're interested in.

Worked example

A $10$-foot ladder leans against a wall. The base slides away from the wall at $2$ft/s. How fast is the top of the ladder sliding down the wall when the base is $6$ feet from the wall?

Let $x$ be the base's distance from the wall, $y$ the ladder's height on the wall. Pythagoras: $x^2+y^2=10^2=100$, true at every instant.

Differentiate with respect to $t$: $2x\dfrac{dx}{dt}+2y\dfrac{dy}{dt} = 0$.

At the instant in question, $x=6$; from $x^2+y^2=100$, $y=\sqrt{100-36}=8$. We are told $\dfrac{dx}{dt}=2$.

$$2(6)(2)+2(8)\frac{dy}{dt}=0 \Rightarrow 24+16\frac{dy}{dt}=0 \Rightarrow \frac{dy}{dt} = -\frac{24}{16}=-1.5\text{ ft/s}.$$

The negative sign means $y$ is decreasing, the top of the ladder is sliding down the wall, at $1.5$ ft/s, exactly matching physical intuition.

Practice 21

A spherical balloon is inflated so that its volume increases at $100$cm$^3$/s. Find how fast the radius is increasing when $r=5$cm. (Volume of a sphere: $V=\frac43\pi r^3$.)

Solution

Differentiate $V=\frac43\pi r^3$ with respect to $t$: $\dfrac{dV}{dt} = 4\pi r^2\dfrac{dr}{dt}$.

$100 = 4\pi(5)^2\dfrac{dr}{dt} = 100\pi\dfrac{dr}{dt} \Rightarrow \dfrac{dr}{dt} = \dfrac1\pi \approx 0.318\text{ cm/s}.$

Practice 22

A person $6$ feet tall walks away from a $15$-foot lamppost at $5$ft/s. Let $x$ be the person's distance from the lamppost and $s$ the length of their shadow. Using similar triangles, $\dfrac{15}{x+s}=\dfrac6s$, find how fast the shadow is lengthening.

Solution

Cross-multiplying the similar-triangles relationship first (to avoid differentiating a fraction unnecessarily): $15s = 6(x+s) = 6x+6s \Rightarrow 9s=6x \Rightarrow s=\dfrac23x$.

This relationship holds at every instant, so differentiate directly: $\dfrac{ds}{dt} = \dfrac23\dfrac{dx}{dt} = \dfrac23(5) = \dfrac{10}3 \approx 3.33$ft/s.

Notice the shadow's own tip moves even faster, at $\dfrac{dx}{dt}+\dfrac{ds}{dt} = 5+\dfrac{10}3=\dfrac{25}3$ft/s, but the question only asked about the shadow's length, which is what $\dfrac{ds}{dt}$ measures.

Practice 23

Water drains out of an inverted conical tank (vertex at the bottom) at $2$m$^3$/min. The tank has height $10$m and top radius $4$m, so at any water height $h$, the radius of the water's surface is $r=\dfrac25h$ (matching the tank's proportions), giving volume $V=\dfrac13\pi r^2h = \dfrac{4\pi}{75}h^3$. Find how fast the water level is dropping when $h=5$m.

Solution

Differentiate $V=\dfrac{4\pi}{75}h^3$ with respect to $t$: $\dfrac{dV}{dt} = \dfrac{12\pi}{75}h^2\dfrac{dh}{dt} = \dfrac{4\pi}{25}h^2\dfrac{dh}{dt}$.

Draining means $\dfrac{dV}{dt}=-2$ (volume decreasing). At $h=5$:

$$-2 = \frac{4\pi}{25}(25)\frac{dh}{dt} = 4\pi\frac{dh}{dt} \Rightarrow \frac{dh}{dt} = -\frac1{2\pi} \approx -0.159\text{ m/min}.$$

The water level is dropping at about $0.16$ m/min at that instant.

Practice 24

Car A leaves an intersection heading east at $40$mph; Car B leaves the same intersection at the same time, heading north at $30$mph. How fast is the distance between them increasing after $2$ hours?

Solution

Let $x=40t$ (Car A's distance), $y=30t$ (Car B's distance), $z=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ (distance between them). At $t=2$: $x=80$, $y=60$, $z=\sqrt{6400+3600}=\sqrt{10000}=100$.

Differentiate $z^2=x^2+y^2$ with respect to $t$: $2z\dfrac{dz}{dt} = 2x\dfrac{dx}{dt}+2y\dfrac{dy}{dt}$, so $\dfrac{dz}{dt} = \dfrac{x\frac{dx}{dt}+y\frac{dy}{dt}}z$.

$$\frac{dz}{dt} = \frac{80(40)+60(30)}{100} = \frac{3200+1800}{100} = \frac{5000}{100} = 50\text{ mph}.$$

Practice 25

Redo Practice 24's setup but at a general time $t$ (not just $t=2$), and show algebraically that $\dfrac{dz}{dt}=50$ for every $t>0$, not just at $t=2$. What does that tell you about how the two cars separate over time?

Solution

In general, $z=\sqrt{(40t)^2+(30t)^2} = \sqrt{1600t^2+900t^2} = \sqrt{2500t^2} = 50t$ (for $t>0$). So $\dfrac{dz}{dt}=50$ constantly, for every $t$, not just at $t=2$.

This makes sense: both cars move at constant speed in fixed, perpendicular directions, so the distance between them is always exactly $50t$ (a straightforward consequence of the $3$-$4$-$5$ triangle pattern scaled by $10t$: sides $40t,30t,50t$), growing at the constant combined rate $50$mph the entire time, not just momentarily. Related rates problems don't always have a constant answer, this one does specifically because both original speeds were constant and the paths were straight lines.

Practice 26

Explain, referencing the Section 4 "why" box, what would go wrong if you substituted $x=6$ into $x^2+y^2=100$ (the ladder problem) before differentiating, and then tried to differentiate the resulting equation $36+y^2=100$.

Solution

$36+y^2=100$ simplifies to $y^2=64$, a single frozen fact ($y=8$ at that instant), with no reference to $x$ left at all. Differentiating this with respect to $t$ gives $2y\dfrac{dy}{dt}=0$, which forces $\dfrac{dy}{dt}=0$, the wrong answer entirely (and obviously wrong, since the ladder is sliding). The mistake was substituting the snapshot value of $x$ before differentiating, which erased the variable $x$ (and its rate $\dfrac{dx}{dt}$) from the equation, so the chain rule had nothing left to connect the two rates through. The general relationship between the two rates has to be established while $x$ and $y$ are still both variables.

Practice 27

The area of a circle is $A=\pi r^2$. If the radius is increasing at a constant rate $\dfrac{dr}{dt}=3$cm/s, find $\dfrac{dA}{dt}$ when $r=10$cm, and explain why $\dfrac{dA}{dt}$ is not constant even though $\dfrac{dr}{dt}$ is.

Solution

$\dfrac{dA}{dt} = 2\pi r\dfrac{dr}{dt}$. At $r=10$: $\dfrac{dA}{dt} = 2\pi(10)(3) = 60\pi \approx 188.5$cm$^2$/s.

Since $\dfrac{dA}{dt}=2\pi r\dfrac{dr}{dt}$ depends explicitly on $r$, and $r$ itself keeps growing over time, $\dfrac{dA}{dt}$ grows too, even though the radius grows at a perfectly steady rate. A circle's area grows faster and faster (for constant radius growth) simply because there is more circumference to "sweep out" area with as the circle gets bigger, area depends on radius nonlinearly (as $r^2$), so a linear change in $r$ does not produce a linear change in $A$.

5 Parametric differentiation: the basics

A parametric curve describes both $x$ and $y$ as separate functions of a third variable $t$ (often thought of as time), rather than $y$ as a function of $x$ directly: $x=x(t)$, $y=y(t)$. This is a natural way to describe motion, at each moment $t$ you get a point $(x(t),y(t))$, and as $t$ varies the point traces out a curve, and it can describe shapes (like a full circle) that are not the graph of any single function $y=f(x)$ at all.

Key idea

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt},$$

provided $\dfrac{dx}{dt}\neq0$. Compute $\dfrac{dx}{dt}$ and $\dfrac{dy}{dt}$ separately (each is an entirely ordinary, single-variable derivative), then divide.

Why dividing the two derivatives works

This is the chain rule again, just rearranged. If $y$ can be regarded as a function of $x$ (at least locally, wherever the curve doesn't double back on itself), then by the chain rule $\dfrac{dy}{dt} = \dfrac{dy}{dx}\cdot\dfrac{dx}{dt}$, since $y$ depends on $t$ only through $x$. Dividing both sides by $\dfrac{dx}{dt}$ (assuming it's nonzero) gives exactly $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}$. So this formula is not a new rule to memorise separately, it is the chain rule solved for the one derivative ($dy/dx$) you cannot compute directly, in terms of two derivatives ($dy/dt$ and $dx/dt$) you can.

Worked example

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ if $x=t^2$, $y=t^3$.

$\dfrac{dx}{dt} = 2t$, $\ \dfrac{dy}{dt}=3t^2$.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{3t^2}{2t} = \frac{3t}2 \quad (t\neq0).$$

Notice the answer is still in terms of $t$, not $x$, which is completely normal and expected for a parametric curve: the slope at each point on the curve is naturally labelled by which value of $t$ produced that point, not by the $x$-coordinate directly (though you could substitute $t=\sqrt x$ if you specifically wanted the slope purely as a function of $x$).

Practice 28

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ if $x=\cos t$, $y=\sin t$.

Solution

$\dfrac{dx}{dt}=-\sin t$, $\ \dfrac{dy}{dt}=\cos t$.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{\cos t}{-\sin t} = -\cot t.$$

Practice 29

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ if $x=t+1$, $y=t^2-1$.

Solution

$\dfrac{dx}{dt}=1$, $\ \dfrac{dy}{dt}=2t$.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2t}1 = 2t.$$

Practice 30

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ if $x=e^t$, $y=e^{2t}$, and check your answer by eliminating $t$ to write $y$ directly as a function of $x$.

Solution

$\dfrac{dx}{dt}=e^t$, $\ \dfrac{dy}{dt}=2e^{2t}$.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2e^{2t}}{e^t} = 2e^t.$$

Eliminating $t$: since $x=e^t$, we get $y=e^{2t}=(e^t)^2=x^2$, so directly $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=2x$. Since $x=e^t$, $2x=2e^t$, exactly matching the parametric answer, confirming the two routes agree.

Practice 31

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ if $x=t-\sin t$, $y=1-\cos t$ (a cycloid, the path traced by a point on the rim of a rolling wheel).

Solution

$\dfrac{dx}{dt}=1-\cos t$, $\ \dfrac{dy}{dt}=\sin t$.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{\sin t}{1-\cos t}.$$

Practice 32

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ if $x=t^3$, $y=t^2$.

Solution

$\dfrac{dx}{dt}=3t^2$, $\ \dfrac{dy}{dt}=2t$.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2t}{3t^2} = \frac2{3t} \quad (t\neq0).$$

Practice 33

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ if $x=3t-1$, $y=2t+5$, and explain, without doing any further calculation, why this parametric curve must actually be a straight line.

Solution

$\dfrac{dx}{dt}=3$, $\ \dfrac{dy}{dt}=2$, so $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac23$, a plain number with no $t$ in it at all.

Since both $x$ and $y$ are linear (degree-$1$) functions of $t$, eliminating $t$ can only ever produce a linear relationship between $x$ and $y$, i.e.\ a straight line. The fact that $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ came out constant (not depending on $t$, and therefore not depending on position along the curve) is exactly the defining property of a straight line: the same slope everywhere.

Practice 34

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ if $x=t^2+1$, $y=2t^2-3$, and again predict (before eliminating $t$) what kind of curve this must be, based on the shape of $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$.

Solution

$\dfrac{dx}{dt}=2t$, $\ \dfrac{dy}{dt}=4t$, so $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{4t}{2t}=2$ (for $t\neq0$), again constant, again with no $t$ remaining.

By the same reasoning as Practice 33, this should also be a straight line, and indeed it is: eliminating $t$ using $x-1=t^2$ gives $y=2(x-1)-3=2x-5$, a line of slope $2$, exactly matching. (This is a slightly disguised case: both $x$ and $y$ are quadratic in $t$, not linear, but since they are both built from the same quadratic building block $t^2$, the nonlinearity cancels out of the ratio $dy/dx$ entirely.)

Practice 35

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ if $x=\ln t$, $y=t^2$ (for $t>0$).

Solution

$\dfrac{dx}{dt}=\dfrac1t$, $\ \dfrac{dy}{dt}=2t$.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2t}{1/t} = 2t^2.$$

6 Second derivatives (parametric)

Just as in Section 2, finding $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ for a parametric curve is not a brand-new technique, but the natural formula is easy to get wrong if you guess it rather than derive it: it is not simply "$\dfrac{d^2y/dt^2}{d^2x/dt^2}$," a very common and understandable mistake.

Key idea

$$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{\dfrac{d}{dt}\left(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\right)}{\dfrac{dx}{dt}}.$$

In words: first find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ as a function of $t$ (Section 5's method), then differentiate that entire expression with respect to $t$, and finally divide by $\dfrac{dx}{dt}$ again, the same denominator as before.

Why it isn't just $\dfrac{d^2y/dt^2}{d^2x/dt^2}$

$\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ means "the derivative, with respect to $x$, of $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$." Since $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ is itself a function of $t$ (Section 5's formula produces an expression in $t$), finding its derivative with respect to $x$ requires exactly the same chain-rule trick used to find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ in the first place: differentiate with respect to $t$, then divide by $\dfrac{dx}{dt}$ to convert that into a derivative with respect to $x$. There is no shortcut that skips this, treating $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ as a naive ratio of second $t$-derivatives silently assumes $x$ and $t$ are interchangeable in a way they simply are not, except in the special case $x=t$.

Worked example

Find $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ if $x=t^2$, $y=t^3$.

From the Section 5 worked example, $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{3t}2$.

Differentiate this with respect to $t$: $\dfrac{d}{dt}\left(\dfrac{3t}2\right) = \dfrac32$.

Divide by $\dfrac{dx}{dt}=2t$: $$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{3/2}{2t} = \frac3{4t}.$$

Practice 36

Find $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ if $x=\cos t$, $y=\sin t$ (continuing Practice 28).

Solution

From Practice 28, $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-\cot t$. Differentiate with respect to $t$: $\dfrac{d}{dt}(-\cot t) = \csc^2t$.

Divide by $\dfrac{dx}{dt}=-\sin t$:

$$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{\csc^2t}{-\sin t} = -\frac1{\sin^3t} = -\csc^3t.$$

Practice 37

Find $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ if $x=t^3$, $y=t^2$ (continuing Practice 32).

Solution

From Practice 32, $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac2{3t}$. Differentiate with respect to $t$: $\dfrac{d}{dt}\left(\dfrac2{3t}\right) = -\dfrac2{3t^2}$.

Divide by $\dfrac{dx}{dt}=3t^2$:

$$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{-2/(3t^2)}{3t^2} = -\frac2{9t^4}.$$

Practice 38

Using Practice 33 ($x=3t-1$, $y=2t+5$, with $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac23$ constant), find $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$, and explain why the answer had to come out this way.

Solution

$\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac23$ is a constant (no $t$ in it), so differentiating it with respect to $t$ gives $0$ immediately, regardless of what you then divide by: $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \dfrac0{dx/dt} = 0$.

This has to be $0$: a second derivative of $0$ everywhere means zero curvature, exactly the defining property of a straight line (Practice 33 already established this curve is a line), consistent with the concavity ideas from earlier calculus, a line neither curves upward nor downward.

Practice 39

Find $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ if $x=e^t$, $y=e^{2t}$ (continuing Practice 30).

Solution

From Practice 30, $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=2e^t$. Differentiate with respect to $t$: $\dfrac{d}{dt}(2e^t)=2e^t$.

Divide by $\dfrac{dx}{dt}=e^t$:

$$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{2e^t}{e^t} = 2.$$

Since Practice 30 found $y=x^2$ directly (eliminating $t$), this checks out immediately: $\dfrac{d^2}{dx^2}(x^2)=2$, exactly matching.

Practice 40

Find $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ if $x=t-\sin t$, $y=1-\cos t$ (the cycloid from Practice 31), and simplify as far as you can.

Solution

From Practice 31, $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{\sin t}{1-\cos t}$. Differentiate with respect to $t$ using the quotient rule:

$$\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\sin t}{1-\cos t}\right) = \frac{\cos t(1-\cos t) - \sin t(\sin t)}{(1-\cos t)^2} = \frac{\cos t-\cos^2t-\sin^2t}{(1-\cos t)^2} = \frac{\cos t-1}{(1-\cos t)^2} = \frac{-1}{1-\cos t}.$$

Divide by $\dfrac{dx}{dt}=1-\cos t$:

$$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{-1/(1-\cos t)}{1-\cos t} = \frac{-1}{(1-\cos t)^2}.$$

Since $(1-\cos t)^2>0$ whenever the curve is smooth (i.e.\ $\cos t\neq1$), this is always negative: the cycloid's arches are concave down everywhere, matching the dome-like shape traced by a point on a rolling wheel.

Practice 41

Find $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ if $x=t+1$, $y=t^2-1$ (continuing Practice 29).

Solution

From Practice 29, $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=2t$. Differentiate with respect to $t$: $\dfrac{d}{dt}(2t)=2$.

Divide by $\dfrac{dx}{dt}=1$: $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \dfrac21 = 2$, a positive constant, so this curve (a parabola, since $x=t+1,y=t^2-1$ eliminates to $y=(x-1)^2-1$) is concave up everywhere, exactly as expected for an upward-opening parabola.

7 Tangent lines and motion (parametric)

Everything from Section 3 (tangent and normal lines) carries over to parametric curves, evaluate $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ at the value of $t$ corresponding to the point you care about, then use point-slope form as usual. Parametric curves also come with a natural physical interpretation when $t$ represents time: $\left(\dfrac{dx}{dt},\dfrac{dy}{dt}\right)$ is the velocity vector of a moving point, and its magnitude is the point's speed.

Key idea

Tangent line at parameter value $t=t_0$: find the point $\big(x(t_0),y(t_0)\big)$ and the slope $m=\dfrac{dy}{dx}\Big|_{t=t_0}$, then use $y-y_0=m(x-x_0)$ as usual.

If $(x(t),y(t))$ describes a moving particle's position at time $t$, its speed is $\sqrt{\left(\dfrac{dx}{dt}\right)^2+\left(\dfrac{dy}{dt}\right)^2}$, the magnitude of the velocity vector $\left(\dfrac{dx}{dt},\dfrac{dy}{dt}\right)$, by the ordinary Pythagorean length of a vector.

A curve has a horizontal tangent where $\dfrac{dy}{dt}=0$ (and $\dfrac{dx}{dt}\neq0$), and a vertical tangent where $\dfrac{dx}{dt}=0$ (and $\dfrac{dy}{dt}\neq0$), mirroring Practice 19-20's implicit version exactly.

Worked example

Find the tangent line to $x=t^2$, $y=t^3$ at $t=1$, and find the speed of a particle following $x=\cos(2t)$, $y=\sin(2t)$.

At $t=1$: point is $(1,1)$. From the Section 6 worked example, $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{3t}2$, so at $t=1$: slope $=\dfrac32$.

Tangent line: $y-1 = \dfrac32(x-1)$.

For the circular motion: $\dfrac{dx}{dt}=-2\sin(2t)$, $\ \dfrac{dy}{dt}=2\cos(2t)$.

$$\text{speed} = \sqrt{(-2\sin2t)^2+(2\cos2t)^2} = \sqrt{4\sin^22t+4\cos^22t} = \sqrt4 = 2,$$

a constant speed of $2$, for every value of $t$, exactly what you would expect from a particle tracing a circle at a uniform rate: constant angular speed around a fixed-radius circle means constant linear speed too.

Practice 42

Find the tangent line to $x=\cos t$, $y=\sin t$ at $t=\dfrac{\pi}4$.

Solution

Point: $\left(\cos\dfrac{\pi}4,\sin\dfrac{\pi}4\right) = \left(\dfrac{\sqrt2}2,\dfrac{\sqrt2}2\right)$.

From Practice 28, $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-\cot t$. At $t=\dfrac{\pi}4$: $-\cot\dfrac{\pi}4 = -1$.

Tangent line: $y-\dfrac{\sqrt2}2 = -1\left(x-\dfrac{\sqrt2}2\right)$, i.e.\ $y = -x+\sqrt2$.

Practice 43

Find the horizontal and vertical tangent points of $x=t^2-4t$, $y=t^3-3t$.

Solution

$\dfrac{dx}{dt}=2t-4$, $\ \dfrac{dy}{dt}=3t^2-3$.

Horizontal ($dy/dt=0$): $3t^2-3=0 \Rightarrow t=\pm1$. At $t=1$: $(x,y)=(1-4,1-3)=(-3,-2)$. At $t=-1$: $(x,y)=(1+4,-1+3)=(5,2)$.

Vertical ($dx/dt=0$): $2t-4=0 \Rightarrow t=2$. At $t=2$: $(x,y)=(4-8,8-6)=(-4,2)$.

Practice 44

A particle's position is given by $x=3t^2$, $y=4t^2$. Find its speed at $t=1$, and explain why the direction of motion never changes (i.e.\ why the particle moves along a straight line, even though both $x$ and $y$ are nonlinear in $t$).

Solution

$\dfrac{dx}{dt}=6t$, $\ \dfrac{dy}{dt}=8t$. Speed $=\sqrt{(6t)^2+(8t)^2} = \sqrt{36t^2+64t^2}=\sqrt{100t^2}=10|t|$. At $t=1$: speed $=10$.

The velocity vector is $(6t,8t) = t(6,8)$: for every $t$, this points in the same fixed direction $(6,8)$ (or its opposite, for $t<0$), just scaled by a factor of $t$. A particle whose velocity always points along the same fixed direction must be moving along a straight line, even though its speed ($10|t|$) is changing, direction and speed are independent pieces of information, and here only the speed varies.

Practice 45

Find the tangent line to the cycloid $x=t-\sin t$, $y=1-\cos t$ at $t=\dfrac{\pi}2$.

Solution

Point: $\left(\dfrac{\pi}2-\sin\dfrac{\pi}2,\ 1-\cos\dfrac{\pi}2\right) = \left(\dfrac{\pi}2-1,\ 1\right)$.

From Practice 31, $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{\sin t}{1-\cos t}$. At $t=\dfrac{\pi}2$: $\dfrac{1}{1-0}=1$.

Tangent line: $y-1 = 1\left(x-\left(\dfrac{\pi}2-1\right)\right)$, i.e.\ $y = x - \dfrac{\pi}2+2$.

Practice 46

Find the speed of a particle at $t=2$ if $x=t^2$, $y=t^3$ (the running example from Sections 5-6, now interpreted as a motion path).

Solution

$\dfrac{dx}{dt}=2t$, $\ \dfrac{dy}{dt}=3t^2$. At $t=2$: $\dfrac{dx}{dt}=4$, $\dfrac{dy}{dt}=12$.

$$\text{speed} = \sqrt{4^2+12^2} = \sqrt{16+144} = \sqrt{160} = 4\sqrt{10} \approx 12.65.$$

Practice 47

Explain why a parametric curve can have a vertical tangent line (Section 7's key idea) even though "the slope is infinite" sounds like it shouldn't be describable at all, connecting your answer to how $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}$ behaves when $\dfrac{dx}{dt}=0$.

Solution

The formula $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}$ is a genuine division, and division by $0$ is undefined, so the formula itself cannot produce a numerical slope when $\dfrac{dx}{dt}=0$. But this is not a failure of the parametric description, it is exactly correct: at such a point the curve genuinely has a vertical tangent, and a vertical line has no finite slope to report in the first place (its "slope," if you insist on describing it, would be $\pm\infty$). The parametric approach handles this gracefully precisely because $x$ and $y$ are tracked as two separate functions of $t$; the point $(x(t_0),y(t_0))$ and its instantaneous direction of travel are both perfectly well defined even at a moment when $y$ cannot locally be written as any ordinary function of $x$.

Practice 48

A particle moves along $x=t^2-6t$, $y=t^2$. Find the value(s) of $t$ where the particle is moving straight up or straight down (vertical velocity direction, i.e.\ $\dfrac{dx}{dt}=0$), and find its position at that moment.

Solution

$\dfrac{dx}{dt}=2t-6=0 \Rightarrow t=3$.

At $t=3$: $x=9-18=-9$, $\ y=9$. Position $(-9,9)$.

Check this is a genuine vertical tangent (not a stationary point): $\dfrac{dy}{dt}=2t=6\neq0$ at $t=3$, confirming the particle is indeed still moving (straight up or down) at that instant, rather than momentarily stopped.
