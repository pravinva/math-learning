Q1

Solve the equations for $x$.

(a) $\log_4(2x+4) = 3$

(b) $2 = \log_x(x+6)$

Solution

(a) Raising 4 to the power of each side of the equation, $2x+4 = 4^3 = 64$. So $2x = 60$ and $x = 30$.

(b) Raising $x$ to the power of both sides, $x^2 = x+6$. So $x^2 - x - 6 = 0$ and this factorises as $(x-3)(x+2) = 0$ so $x = 3$ or $-2$. Logarithms with negative bases are not defined, so while $x=-2$ does satisfy $x^2-x-6=0$, it is not a solution to $2 = \log_x(x+6)$: the expression $\log_{-2}(-2+6)$ is not defined, so the equation $2 = \log_{-2}(-2+6)$ does not make sense.

Q2

Below is the structure of solid sodium chloride. If the distance between the centres of adjacent chlorine and sodium ions is 278 pm (picometers), what is the next shortest distance between such ions? i.e. what is the length of the red dashed line?

Solution

We use Pythagoras' theorem.

The solid lines on the bottom of the diagram have length 278, so the distance $c$ satisfies $c^2 = a^2+b^2 = 2\times 278^2$. Hence $c = \sqrt{2\times 278^2} = 278\sqrt2 \approx 393$pm.

Now we can use Pythagoras again: $e^2 = c^2+d^2$. We already know that $c^2 = 2\times(278)^2$ and $d^2 = 278^2$, so $e^2 = 2\times 278^2 + 278^2 = 3\times 278^2$. Hence $e = \sqrt{3\times 278^2} = 278\sqrt3 \approx 482$pm.

Q3

Radioactive material is known to decay at a rate given by the equation

$$M = M_0 e^{-kt}$$

where $M$ is the mass (kg) of radioactive material remaining, $M_0$ is the initial mass (kg), $k$ is the decay constant, and $t$ is the time (in years).

(a) If 10 kg reduces to 6 kg in $\frac14$ year, find the time for the material to decay to a mass of 2 kg.

(b) If half-life is the time taken for half of the amount of a material to decay, find the half life of this material.

Solution

(a) Here $M_0 = 10$ and $M=6$ when $t=\frac14$, so $6 = 10e^{-k\times 1/4}$. Dividing both sides by 10 we get $e^{-k/4} = 0.6$ and taking the natural logarithm of both sides, $-k/4 = \ln(0.6)$. Multiplying both sides by $-4$ we obtain $k = -4\ln(0.6) \approx -0.204$.

(b) The half-life is the value of $t$ for which $M=M_0/2$. That is $M_0e^{-(4\ln(0.6))t} = M_0/2$. (Note: stick with exact values for as long as possible rather than the decimal approximation for $k$.) Dividing both sides by $M_0$ we get $e^{-(4\ln(0.6))t} = \frac12$. Using log and index laws:

$$e^{-(-4\ln(0.6))t} = e^{\ln(0.6^4)t} = (e^{\ln(0.6^4)})^t = (0.6^4)^t = 0.1296^t.$$

So we must solve $0.1296^t = \frac12$. Taking logarithms to the base 0.1296, $t = \log_{0.1296}(1/2)$, and using the change-of-base law this is $\ln(1/2)/\ln(0.1296) \approx 0.339$.

Sanity check: the mass reduced by a factor of 0.6 in a quarter of a year, so after half a year it would reduce by another factor of 0.6 to 0.36 of its original. Since 0.5 lies between these two values, the half-life should sit between $\frac14$ and $\frac12$ — and 0.339 does.

Q4

Without using a calculator, find the general solution to

$$\tan^4 x - 2\sec^2 x - 1 = 0.$$

[You have to find all solutions on the real line.]

Solution

We use the identity $\sec^2(x) - \tan^2(x) = 1$, so $\sec^2(x) = 1+\tan^2(x)$. Rewriting the equation:

$$\tan^4(x) - 2(1+\tan^2(x)) - 1 = 0 \iff (\tan^2(x))^2 - 2\tan^2(x) - 3 = 0.$$

This is a quadratic in $\tan^2(x)$: putting $t=\tan^2(x)$ gives $t^2-2t-3=0$, which factorises as $(t-3)(t+1)=0$, so $t=3$ or $-1$. Since $t=\tan^2(x)$ must be positive, only $t=3$ works: $\tan^2(x)=3$ so $\tan(x)=\pm\sqrt3$.

From the $\pi/3$–$\pi/6$ right triangle, $\tan(\pi/3) = \frac{\sqrt3/2}{1/2} = \sqrt3$, giving a first-quadrant solution $x=\pi/3$. Since $\tan(-x)=-\tan x$, $x=-\pi/3$ solves $\tan x = -\sqrt3$. As $\tan x$ has period $\pi$, the general solution is:

$$x = \pi/3 + k\pi,\ k\in\mathbb Z, \quad\text{and}\quad x=-\pi/3+k\pi,\ k\in\mathbb Z.$$

Q5

Using the following picture, prove that $\sin(x+y) = \sin x \cos y + \cos x \sin y$.

Solution

We add a vertical line (in red) from the top of the blue edge of length 1 to the bottom black line, and label points and lengths as below.

Consider the triangle $\triangle ACE$. It has short sides $a$ and $b$ and long side of length 1. So $b=\sin(x)$ and $a=\cos(x)$.

To find $c$, use $\triangle CDE$: $b=\sin(x)$ is the hypotenuse, and $c$ is adjacent to angle $y$, so $\cos(y) = \frac{c}{\sin(x)}$, giving $c=\sin(x)\cos(y)$.

To find $d$, use $\triangle BCF$: $a=\cos(x)$ is the hypotenuse and $d$ is opposite angle $y$, so $\sin(y)=\frac{d}{\cos(x)}$, giving $d=\cos(x)\sin(y)$.

Since $ED$ and $FB$ are parallel, the red line $EF$ equals $DB = c+d$, so $e = \sin(x)\cos(y)+\cos(x)\sin(y)$.

In right triangle $\triangle AEF$, the hypotenuse $AE=1$ and the angle at $A$ is $x+y$, so

$$\sin(x+y) = \frac{e}{1} = \sin(x)\cos(y)+\cos(x)\sin(y).$$

Q6

Without using a grapher or calculator, plot the graph of $f(x) = -3\cos(-2x + \frac{\pi}{3})$. Give an explanation of how you obtain the graph.

Solution

Adding $\frac{\pi}{3}$ inside the argument shifts the graph left by $\pi/3$, so $f$ is the graph of $y=-3\cos(-2x)$ shifted left by $\pi/3$. Since $\cos(x)=\cos(-x)$, this equals $y=-3\cos(2x)$, the mirror across the $x$-axis of $y=3\cos(2x)$, which has amplitude 3 and period $\frac{2\pi}{2}=\pi$.

Q7

A fighter aircraft is approaching point $A$ along a straight line and at a constant altitude $h$. At 0:00 am, the angle of elevation of the airplane is $20^\circ$ and at 0:01 am it is $60^\circ$. What is the altitude $h$ of the airplane if the speed of the aircraft is constantly equal to 600 km/hour? (round answer to 2 decimal places).

Solution

In $\triangle AB'B$, $\cot(20^\circ) = \frac{|AB'|}{h}$. In $\triangle AC'C$, $\cot(60^\circ) = \frac{|AC'|}{h}$. Subtracting:

$$\cot(20^\circ)-\cot(60^\circ) = \frac{|AB'|}{h}-\frac{|AC'|}{h} = \frac{|B'C'|}{h}.$$

The aircraft travels at 600 km/h from $B$ to $C$ in 1 minute ($\frac{1}{60}$ hours), so $|B'C'|=|BC|=\frac{1}{60}\times 600=10$ km. So $\cot(20^\circ)-\cot(60^\circ) = \frac{10}{h}$, giving

$$h = \frac{10}{\cot(20^\circ)-\cot(60^\circ)}.$$

This is a job for a calculator: $h \approx 4.61$ km.

Q8

"Loudness" is measured in decibels. The level of sound $dB$ is defined as

$$dB = 10\log\left(\frac{I}{I_0}\right)$$

where $I$ is the sound intensity (watts/cm$^2$) and $I_0$ is a fixed reference value (usually the sound that can be barely perceived).

(a) Suppose $I_0 = 10^{-16}$ watts/cm$^2$. Determine the decibels of a sound with intensity $I=10^{-8}$ watts/cm$^2$.

(b) Prolonged exposure above 85 decibels can cause hearing damage. Suppose $I$ is the intensity of a gunshot — for what value of $I$ should you wear ear protection?

Solution

(a) $dB = 10\log(10^{-8}/10^{-16}) = 10\log(10^{8}) = 10\times 8 = 80$.

(b) Solve $85 = 10\log(I/I_0)$ for $I$:

$$85 = 10(\log(I)-\log(10^{-16})) = 10(\log(I)+16) = 10\log(I)+160.$$

Subtracting 160, $10\log(I) = -75$, so $\log(I) = -7.5$. Raising 10 to the power of each side, $I = 10^{-7.5} \approx 3.16\times 10^{-8}$ watts/cm$^2$.

Q9

When discharging, the voltage across the plates of a capacitor $V(t)$ is given by

$$V(t) = V_0 e^{-at}$$

where $a$ is a positive constant and $V_0>0$ is the initial voltage (in volts). Find $t$ for which $V(t)$ equals 50% of $V_0$. How about 75%? Can its voltage be dropped to 0? If so, how much time is needed?

Solution

Solve $V_0e^{-at} = \frac12 V_0$: dividing by $V_0$, $e^{-at}=\frac12$. Taking logs, $-at=\ln(1/2)$, so $t = \frac1a\ln(2)$.

For 1/4 of $V_0$: either solve directly, or notice that if the voltage halves in $\frac1a\ln(2)$ time, it halves again in another $\frac1a\ln(2)$, so reaching 1/4 takes $2\times\frac1a\ln(2) = \frac2a\ln(2)$ units of time.

Since $e^{-at} > 0$ for all finite $t$, the voltage never actually reaches exactly 0 — it only approaches it as $t\to\infty$.

Q10

The definition of pH is

$$\text{pH} = -\log_{10}[H^+] = \log_{10}\frac{1}{[H^+]}$$

where $[H^+]$ is the concentration of hydrogen ions measured in mol dm$^{-3}$.

(a) Without using your calculator, what is the pH of a solution containing a concentration of hydrogen ions of $10^{-5}$ mol dm$^{-3}$?

(b) If the pH value of a sample of orange juice is 3.5, what is the concentration of hydrogen ions?

(c) Plot the graph of the pH value against the concentration $[H^+]$.

Solution

(a) $\log_{10}(10^{-5}) = -5$ so the pH is $-\log_{10}(10^{-5}) = -(-5) = 5$.
