Engineering Dynamics: Kinematics
Kinematics is the study of motion — position, velocity and acceleration — without worrying about the forces that cause it. This guide covers motion in a straight line (rectilinear), the constant-acceleration equations, motion that changes erratically, and 2-D projectile motion. These are the mechanics half of ENGG100.

Topic 1 · Week 8
Position, Velocity & Acceleration
The three quantities that fully describe motion
All of kinematics rests on three quantities and the calculus that links them. Velocity is the rate of change of position; acceleration is the rate of change of velocity. Differentiate to go "down" the chain, integrate to go "up". Get these relationships clear and every problem becomes a matter of picking the right tool.
DefinitionThe calculus chain▶
\[ v = \frac{ds}{dt} \qquad\qquad a = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2s}{dt^2} \]

And the reverse, by integration:

\[ v = \int a\,dt \qquad\qquad s = \int v\,dt \]

There is also a very useful equation with no time in it:

\[ a\,ds = v\,dv \]
NoteDistance vs displacement, speed vs velocity▶
ScalarVector
Distance (total path travelled)Displacement (net change in position)
Speed (how fast)Velocity (how fast + direction)
Key exam idea: if an object reverses direction, distance > |displacement|. To find total distance you must locate every point where velocity = 0 (a turning point) and add the path lengths between them.
ExampleWorked example — differentiate to get v and a▶
Example — From position to velocity & acceleration

A particle moves with \(s = 2t^3 - 6t^2\) (m). Find \(v\) and \(a\) at \(t = 2\) s.

1
\(v = \dfrac{ds}{dt} = 6t^2 - 12t\). At t=2: \(v = 24 - 24 = 0\) m/s.
2
\(a = \dfrac{dv}{dt} = 12t - 12\). At t=2: \(a = 24 - 12 = 12\) m/s².
ANSWER v = 0 m/s, a = 12 m/s² (t = 2 s is a turning point since v = 0)

Topic 2 · Week 8
Constant Acceleration (SUVAT)
The four equations that solve most rectilinear problems
When acceleration is constant — free fall under gravity, a car braking steadily — you do not need calculus. Four standard equations (the SUVAT equations) relate displacement, velocities, acceleration and time. The whole skill is: list what you know, see what you want, pick the equation containing those four quantities.
DefinitionThe four SUVAT equations▶
\[ v = u + at \]
\[ s = ut + \tfrac12 a t^2 \]
\[ v^2 = u^2 + 2as \]
\[ s = \frac{u+v}{2}\,t \]
How to choose: each equation is missing one of the five variables. Pick the one that does not contain the variable you neither know nor want. e.g. no time given and time not wanted → use \(v^2=u^2+2as\).
NoteSign convention & gravity▶

Choose a positive direction and stick to it. For vertical motion, gravity gives \(a = g = 9.81\) m/s² downward (use \(-9.81\) if "up" is positive).

Watch signs: a ball thrown up has positive initial velocity but negative (downward) acceleration. Mixing signs is the most common SUVAT mistake.
ExampleTwo worked examples▶
Example 1 — Braking car

A car travelling at 20 m/s brakes at 4 m/s². How far does it travel before stopping?

1
Known: u = 20, v = 0, a = −4. Want: s. No time → use \(v^2 = u^2 + 2as\).
2
\(0 = 20^2 + 2(-4)s \Rightarrow 8s = 400 \Rightarrow s = 50\) m.
ANSWER s = 50 m
Example 2 — Dropped object

A stone is dropped from rest. How fast is it moving after 3 s? (g = 9.81)

\[ v = u + at = 0 + 9.81\times3 = 29.43\ \text{m/s} \]
ANSWER v ≈ 29.4 m/s downward
Quick Practice — Topic 2Click to reveal
A
A car accelerates from rest at 2 m/s² for 6 s. Final velocity?
▶
v = u + at = 0 + 2×6 = 12 m/s.
B
Same car — how far did it travel in those 6 s?
▶
s = ut + ½at² = 0 + ½(2)(6²) = 36 m.
C
Which SUVAT equation has no time in it?
▶
v² = u² + 2as. Use it when time is neither given nor wanted.
D
A ball is thrown up at 15 m/s. Time to reach the top? (g = 9.81)
▶
At the top v = 0: 0 = 15 − 9.81t → t = 15/9.81 ≈ 1.53 s.
Topic 3 · Week 8
Variable & Erratic Motion
When acceleration is not constant
Many real motions have changing acceleration — given as a function of time, or as a graph with different phases. Here SUVAT does not apply directly. Instead you use calculus (differentiate/integrate) or, for graphs, read areas and slopes. The key skill is finding critical points: where v = 0 (turning points, for total distance) and where a = 0 (max/min velocity).
PatternTotal distance: find where v = 0▶
1
Differentiate position to get \(v(t)\); set \(v = 0\) and solve for the critical times.
2
Evaluate position \(s\) at the start, at each critical time, and at the end.
3
Add up the magnitudes of the changes in \(s\) between consecutive points → total distance.
PatternMaximum velocity: find where a = 0▶

Velocity is maximum (or minimum) when its rate of change — the acceleration — is zero. So set \(a = dv/dt = 0\), solve for \(t\), then substitute back into \(v(t)\).

Same idea as calculus maxima: a maximum of \(v\) occurs where its derivative \(a\) is zero, exactly like finding a function's peak by setting its derivative to zero.
NotePiecewise (erratic) motion & graphs▶

When motion is described in stages (constant a for a while, then a different a), handle each stage separately and carry the final velocity/position of one stage as the initial condition of the next.

ExampleWorked example — total distance with a reversal▶
Example — Distance travelled in 0 ≤ t ≤ 4

A particle has \(s = t^2 - 6t\) (m). Find the total distance travelled from t = 0 to t = 4 s.

1
\(v = 2t - 6 = 0 \Rightarrow t = 3\) s (the turning point).
2
\(s(0) = 0\); \(s(3) = 9 - 18 = -9\); \(s(4) = 16 - 24 = -8\).
3
Leg 1 (0→3): \(|-9 - 0| = 9\) m. Leg 2 (3→4): \(|-8 - (-9)| = 1\) m.
4
Total distance = 9 + 1 = 10 m (displacement is only 8 m).
ANSWER Total distance = 10 m

▶

B

▶
Acceleration (a = dv/dt = 0).

▶

▶

Topic 4 · Week 9
Curvilinear & Projectile Motion
Motion in two dimensions — splitting into x and y
When motion is 2-D (a thrown ball, a launched projectile), the trick is that the horizontal and vertical motions are completely independent. Horizontally there is no acceleration (constant velocity); vertically there is gravity. Solve each direction with SUVAT, sharing only the time \(t\). This single insight cracks every projectile problem.
DefinitionSplitting the launch velocity▶

A projectile launched at speed \(u\) and angle \(\theta\) splits into:

\[ u_x = u\cos\theta \qquad\qquad u_y = u\sin\theta \]
DirectionAccelerationEquations
Horizontal (x)\(a_x = 0\)\(x = u_x t\) (constant velocity)
Vertical (y)\(a_y = -g\)SUVAT with \(u_y\), \(a=-g\)
PatternThe projectile solving recipe▶
1
Resolve \(u\) into \(u_x\) and \(u_y\).
2
Use the vertical equation to find the time of flight (e.g. when \(y\) returns to launch height, or hits the ground).
3
Substitute that time into the horizontal equation \(x = u_x t\) to get the range.
Useful results for launch and landing at the same height:
\[ t_{\text{flight}} = \frac{2u\sin\theta}{g} \quad H = \frac{u^2\sin^2\theta}{2g} \quad R = \frac{u^2\sin 2\theta}{g} \]
ExampleWorked example — full projectile▶
Example — Range of a launched ball

A ball is launched at 20 m/s at 30° above the horizontal from ground level. Find the time of flight and horizontal range. (g = 9.81)

1
\(u_x = 20\cos30° = 17.32\) m/s, \(u_y = 20\sin30° = 10\) m/s.
2
Time of flight: \(t = \dfrac{2u_y}{g} = \dfrac{2(10)}{9.81} = 2.04\) s.
3
Range: \(R = u_x t = 17.32 \times 2.04 = 35.3\) m.
ANSWER t ≈ 2.04 s, range ≈ 35.3 m

▶
