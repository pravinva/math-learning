0 Why Excel for an engineer?

Before the fancy stuff, a plain fact: whatever software your future employer eventually adopts, PowerBI, MATLAB, Python, dedicated mine-planning packages, someone is still going to hand you a spreadsheet on your first day. Production tallies, drill-and-blast logs, equipment maintenance schedules, cost breakdowns by pit, QAQC assay batches, almost all of it arrives as, or gets exported to, an Excel file. Being genuinely fast and accurate in Excel isn't a "soft skill," it's the difference between spending an afternoon on a report and spending fifteen minutes on it.

There's a second reason this lesson exists, one aimed specifically at where you're headed. Every core Excel idea, a table of rows and columns, a formula that references other cells instead of hardcoded numbers, a function that summarises a range, is the exact same idea you'll meet again in a pandas DataFrame, a numpy array, or a matplotlib chart, just with different syntax. Learning to think in these terms now, cell references instead of typed numbers, structured tables instead of scattered data, makes that later transition far smoother. This lesson closes with that bridge made explicit.

1 Workbooks, worksheets, and cell addresses

A workbook is the file itself, the thing you save and email. Inside it are one or more worksheets (tabs), each ruled into a grid of columns (lettered A through XFD, 16,384 in total) and rows (numbered 1 through 1,048,576). Where a column and row cross is a cell, and every cell has an address built from its column letter and row number, e.g. C11.

Key idea

Cell address = column letter + row number (e.g. B7, AA204). A range is a rectangular block of cells written as top-left:bottom-right, e.g. B3:D5. The active cell is whichever cell is currently selected and ready to receive typed input.

Worked example

A production worksheet has drill hole IDs in column A, starting at row 6, and assay grades (g/t gold) in column C. The third drill hole listed is in row 8. What is the cell address of its assay grade?

Row 6 is the first drill hole, so row 7 is the second, and row 8 is the third, that matches the stated position directly.

The assay grade lives in column C.

C8.

Practice 1

A haul truck log has truck IDs starting in cell A2, one truck per row. What is the cell address of the 6th truck's ID?

Solution

Row 2 is truck 1, so truck 6 is row $2+(6-1)=7$: A7.

Practice 2

List every cell contained in the range B3:D5.

Solution

B3, C3, D3, B4, C4, D4, B5, C5, D5, a $3\times3$ rectangular block of 9 cells.

Practice 3

Explain the difference between a workbook and a worksheet, using a mine-site example.

Solution

A workbook is the entire file, e.g. 2024_Production_Report.xlsx. A worksheet is one page (tab) inside it, e.g. that same workbook might have separate worksheets named North_Pit, South_Pit, and Summary, all living inside the one workbook file.

Practice 4

A blank new workbook opens with three worksheet tabs. What are they named by default, and why would you almost always rename them before sharing the file?

Solution

By default: Sheet1, Sheet2, Sheet3. These tell a reader nothing about what's inside them, renaming to something like North_Pit_Jan2024 means anyone opening the file (a supervisor, a contractor, future you in six months) can navigate it instantly without opening every tab to guess.

Practice 5

You're tracking production for three pits (North, South, East) across 12 months. Would you rather structure this as one worksheet per pit (with 12 rows of monthly data each), or one single worksheet with a "Pit" column? Give one genuine advantage of each approach.

Solution

Separate worksheets per pit: easier to hand one pit's data to its specific site supervisor without exposing the others, and formatting/layout can be tailored per pit.

One combined worksheet with a "Pit" column: far easier to sum, filter, chart, or compare across pits (total company-wide tonnage, for instance), since everything lives in one place a single formula or chart can reach. This second structure, "long" data with a category column, is also exactly the shape a pandas DataFrame or a database table expects, worth remembering for Section 9.

Practice 6

A worksheet can hold up to 1,048,576 rows. At what point would a dataset genuinely start to strain Excel, and what tool would a data scientist reach for instead?

Solution

In practice, Excel gets noticeably sluggish well before hitting the row limit, once a file has hundreds of thousands of rows of live formulas, or several million cells with heavy formatting, recalculation and file size become a real problem. A common threshold engineers actually hit: high-frequency sensor logs (vibration monitoring, drill rig telemetry) can generate that many rows in days, not months. At that point, the standard move is to load the data into pandas (Python) instead, which handles millions of rows without breaking a sweat, exactly the tool this lesson is quietly building toward.

Practice 7

What is the "active cell," and how can you tell which one it is just by looking at the screen?

Solution

The active cell is the one currently selected and ready to receive typed input. You can spot it because it's outlined/highlighted on the grid, and its column letter and row number are both highlighted in the column and row headers too, so you can confirm the address at a glance without needing to click into the cell first.

2 Entering data and writing formulas

Text and numbers get typed straight into a cell. A formula is different: it always starts with an equals sign =, and instead of typing a computed number yourself, you tell Excel which cells to combine and how. This single habit, referencing cells instead of typing final numbers, is the entire reason spreadsheets are useful rather than just fancy notepads.

Key idea

A formula begins with =, and uses cell references combined with operators: + (add), - (subtract), * (multiply), / (divide). For example, =B2*C2 multiplies whatever is currently in B2 by whatever is currently in C2, and updates automatically if either value later changes.

Worked example

Cell B2 holds tonnes hauled (2,450), cell C2 holds cost per tonne ($3.20). Write a formula in D2 for total haulage cost, and explain why =2450*3.20 typed directly would be a worse choice.

$$\texttt{D2: =B2*C2}$$

This computes $2450\times3.20 = \$7840.00$, exactly as typing the numbers directly would, on this one occasion.

The difference shows up next week, when the tonnage or rate changes: with =B2*C2, editing B2 or C2 updates D2 automatically. With =2450*3.20 hardcoded, D2 is silently wrong the moment either input changes, and nothing on screen warns you, the formula still "works," it's just answering last week's question.

Practice 8

Tonnes are in B2, rate per tonne in C2. Write the formula for total cost in D2.

Solution

=B2*C2

Practice 9

Budgeted cost is in B5, actual cost in C5. Write a formula in D5 for the variance (actual minus budget), and note whether a positive result means over- or under-budget.

Solution

=C5-B5. A positive result means actual exceeded budget, i.e.\ over-budget; a negative result means under-budget.

Practice 10

Cell B10 contains 850 (metres drilled), cell C10 contains 6 (hours). A formula in D10 reads =B10/C10. What value appears in D10, and what does it represent?

Solution

$850\div6\approx141.67$. This represents the drilling rate, roughly 141.67 metres per hour.

Practice 11

A haul truck's payload is 220 tonnes, entered in B3, and it completes 14 cycles in a shift, entered in C3. Write a formula for total tonnes hauled that shift, and compute the result.

Solution

=B3*C3, giving $220\times14=3080$ tonnes.

Practice 12

Mechanical: a component experiences a force of 4500 N (in B2) over a cross-sectional area of 0.02 m² (in C2). Write a formula for stress (force ÷ area) in D2, and compute the result in Pa.

Solution

=B2/C2, giving $4500\div0.02 = 225{,}000$ Pa (225 kPa).

Practice 13

Explain, in engineering terms, why "hardcoding" a computed number directly into a cell (rather than a formula referencing the inputs) is considered bad practice, even if it gives the right answer today.

Solution

A hardcoded number severs the link between the result and the inputs that produced it. If an input later changes, a corrected assay result, a revised cost rate, a hardcoded downstream cell won't update, and nothing visually flags the discrepancy, you get a wrong number that looks completely normal. This is exactly the same principle behind reproducibility in data science: a script that recomputes results from raw data every time is trustworthy in a way that a spreadsheet full of typed-in final numbers never can be, because the entire calculation chain is visible and automatically re-runs when anything upstream changes.

3 Filling a series

A series is a sequence of ordered entries in adjacent cells, drill hole IDs, shift numbers, dates. The fill handle (the small square at the bottom-right corner of a selected cell) lets Excel continue a pattern automatically once it can see the first one or two entries, saving both time and the transcription errors that come with typing long sequences by hand.

Key idea

Type the first entry (or first two, if a step size needs to be established), select it, then drag the fill handle across or down. Excel recognises common patterns automatically: numbers, dates, days of the week, months, and any two-cell arithmetic sequence.

Worked example

Cell A1 contains DH-001 (a drill hole ID). Dragging the fill handle down to A5, what appears in A2 through A5?

Excel recognises the trailing number in the text and increments it by 1 each row, leaving the text prefix unchanged.

A2: DH-002, A3: DH-003, A4: DH-004, A5: DH-005.

Practice 14

Cell A1 contains Q1. Predict A2, A3, A4 after dragging the fill handle.

Solution

Q2, Q3, Q4 (Excel recognises the quarter pattern and cycles it, similar to how it handles days and months).

Practice 15

Cell A1 contains Monday. Predict A2 through A4.

Solution

Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday.

Practice 16

Cell A1 contains 5 and A2 contains 10. Both are selected together and the fill handle is dragged to A5. Predict A3, A4, A5.

Solution

Selecting both seed cells tells Excel the step size: $10-5=5$. So A3=15, A4=20, A5=25. (Note: dragging from just A1=5 alone would instead just copy 5 repeatedly, or increment by 1 depending on version, this is exactly why selecting two seed cells matters whenever the step size isn't 1.)

Practice 17

You need a column labelled Shift 1 through Shift 10 for a fortnightly roster. Describe how you'd generate this with the fill handle rather than typing all ten.

Solution

Type Shift 1 into the first cell, select it, then drag the fill handle down through 10 rows. Excel recognises the trailing number and increments it each row, producing Shift 2, Shift 3, ... Shift 10 automatically, exactly the same mechanism as the drill hole ID worked example.

Practice 18

A1 contains 1-Jan, A2 contains 8-Jan (both selected together, then filled down). Predict A3, A4, A5.

Solution

Step size is 7 days (weekly): 15-Jan, 22-Jan, 29-Jan, a useful pattern for weekly production or inspection reports.

Practice 19

Beyond just saving time, give one QAQC-specific reason filling a series is better than manually typing 200 sequential sample IDs by hand.

Solution

Manual typing of a long sequential list is a genuine source of transcription error, a skipped number, a duplicated number, a typo, any of which can misalign a sample ID against its lab result and quietly corrupt an entire QAQC dataset. A filled series is generated mechanically from a known starting point and step size, so it's guaranteed internally consistent, there's no possibility of a human slip partway through 200 rows.

4 Managing worksheets

Once a workbook has more than one worksheet, being able to insert, delete, rename, copy, colour-code, group, and freeze them becomes genuinely important for keeping a large reporting file navigable.

Key idea

Rename: double-click the tab, type the new name. Copy/Move: right-click the tab → Move or Copy. Group: click the first tab, Shift+click the last, to apply the same formatting change to every grouped sheet at once. Freeze Panes: locks rows/columns in place so headers stay visible while scrolling through long data.

Worked example

You have a 500-row equipment maintenance log, with headers (Equipment ID, Hours, Last Service, Status) in row 4. You click in cell A5, then apply Freeze Panes. What stays visible when you scroll down?

Freeze Panes locks everything above and to the left of the currently selected cell.

Since A5 is selected, rows 1 through 4 (including the header row) freeze in place, and no columns freeze (since A5 is in column A, there's nothing to its left). Scrolling down 400 rows still shows the header row 4 pinned at the top, exactly what makes a long log usable.

Practice 20

State the two steps to rename a worksheet tab.

Solution

Double-click the tab name (this places it into edit mode), then type the new name and press Enter.

Practice 21

You have 12 monthly worksheets, all with an identical layout, and you want to bold and colour the header row on every single one without repeating the formatting 12 times. What Excel feature is built exactly for this?

Solution

Grouping worksheets. Click the first tab, hold Shift, click the last tab, to select all 12 at once, then any formatting or text change applied to one sheet in the group is applied identically to all of them simultaneously. Remember to ungroup afterward (click any tab outside the group), or further edits will keep applying to every sheet, not just the one you're looking at.

Practice 22

What happens if you delete a worksheet that contains data, and why does Excel treat this differently from deleting the contents of a single cell?

Solution

Excel asks for confirmation before deleting a worksheet that contains data, because worksheet deletion is permanent and cannot be undone, unlike almost everything else in Excel. Deleting a single cell's contents can be reversed with Undo (Ctrl+Z); deleting an entire worksheet cannot, once confirmed, that data is gone. Always save the workbook before deleting worksheets, precisely so a backup exists if the deletion turns out to be a mistake.

Practice 23

Freeze Panes is applied with the active cell at C4. Which rows and columns freeze?

Solution

Rows 1–3 freeze (everything above row 4), and column A and B freeze (everything to the left of column C). This is the standard setup for a spreadsheet with both a header row and a label column (e.g.\ dates down column A, categories across row 4) that both need to stay visible while scrolling in either direction.

Practice 24

Explain the difference between moving a worksheet to another workbook and copying it, and give a mine-site scenario where you'd specifically want to copy rather than move.

Solution

Moving removes the worksheet entirely from the original workbook and places it in the destination workbook; copying leaves the original untouched and places a duplicate in the destination. You'd copy, never move, if you needed to send a contractor their pit's data as a standalone file while keeping the full multi-pit master workbook intact on your own machine, moving would strip that pit's data out of your master file entirely, which is almost never what you actually want.

Practice 25 EXPLORE

Propose a worksheet tab colour-coding scheme for a workbook tracking 5 pits, where each pit's status is either "Active," "On Hold," or "Closed." Open Excel and try applying it to a few dummy tabs.

One reasonable approach

Green tabs for Active pits, yellow/orange for On Hold, red or grey for Closed, an at-a-glance visual status board without needing to open a single tab. There's no single correct scheme here, the point is that tab colour is a genuinely useful signal, not just decoration, and it costs nothing to set up (right-click tab → Tab colour, exactly as covered in the source manual's Changing Worksheet Tab Colours section).

5 Selecting ranges, rows, and columns

Almost every Excel operation, formatting, charting, applying a formula, starts with selecting the right cells first. Getting comfortable with the different selection techniques (click-drag, Shift-click, Ctrl-click, clicking a row/column header) saves real time once you're working with genuinely large datasets.

Key idea

Contiguous range: click the first cell, hold Shift, click the last cell (or click-drag). Non-adjacent cells/ranges: hold Ctrl while clicking each one. Entire row/column: click the row number or column letter header directly.

Worked example

You want to chart two non-adjacent columns: Month (column A) and Tonnage (column D), skipping B and C entirely. How do you select just those two columns together?

Click the column A header to select the entire column.

Hold Ctrl, then click the column D header.

Both columns A and D are now selected together, with B and C excluded, exactly what a chart needs when the data you want plotted isn't sitting in adjacent columns.

Practice 26

Describe two different ways to select the range A1:E1.

Solution

(1) Click A1, hold Shift, click E1. (2) Click A1, hold the left mouse button, and drag across to E1 before releasing.

Practice 27

Describe how to select columns B and D together, without selecting column C.

Solution

Click the column B header, hold Ctrl, then click the column D header. Ctrl-click adds a non-adjacent selection without disturbing what's already selected.

Practice 28

You accidentally select entire column C (instead of just the data range within it) and apply bold formatting. What, precisely, just happened to the worksheet, and why is this worth being careful about?

Solution

Every cell in column C, all 1,048,576 of them, all the way down, is now bold, not just the handful of rows that actually contain data. This usually causes no visible problem immediately (empty bold cells look the same as empty normal cells), but it can bloat the file size, and it can cause confusing behaviour later if you ever reference "the last used cell" in that column, since Excel may now think the column extends much further than your actual data does. Selecting the specific data range, rather than the whole column, avoids this entirely.

Practice 29

Give two separate methods for selecting an entire row 5.

Solution

(1) Click directly on the row 5 header (the number "5" at the left edge). (2) Click any cell in row 5, then press Shift+Space (Home+Shift in some references, but Shift+Space is the standard entire-row shortcut).

Practice 30

You have a 200-row assay dataset and need to select the entire "Grade_ppm" column of actual data quickly, without dragging the mouse the whole way down. Describe a fast keyboard-based technique.

Solution

Click the first data cell in the column, then press Ctrl+Shift+End (or Ctrl+Shift+Down Arrow) to extend the selection to the last used cell in that column/direction in one keystroke, instead of dragging or scrolling manually through 200 rows. This kind of shortcut is what actually separates someone comfortable in Excel from someone who technically knows the menus, worth practising deliberately.

Practice 31

Why do most Excel functions (SUM, AVERAGE, and the like, covered in Section 7) require a rectangular, contiguous range as their argument, rather than accepting an arbitrary scattered selection of cells?

Solution

A rectangular range (like B6:B8) can be described compactly with just two cell addresses, top-left and bottom-right, which is why range notation exists in the first place. A genuinely scattered, non-rectangular selection has no such compact description, so functions instead handle that case by accepting a comma-separated list of individual ranges as separate arguments (e.g.\ =SUM(B6:B8,B11:B13), exactly as the source manual's AVERAGE example does), rather than trying to invent notation for an arbitrary shape.

6 Number formatting: the veil over numbers

Every number in Excel has two separate things going on: the underlying value actually stored in the cell, and the display format that decides how it's shown on screen. Formatting never changes the real stored value, it only changes the "veil" placed over it, and forgetting this distinction is a classic source of reporting errors.

Key idea

A cell storing 0.02 can be displayed as 2% (Percentage format), or a cell storing 44104 can be displayed as 30-Sep-2020 (Date format), the underlying number never actually changes, only its on-screen appearance does.

Worked example

An ore grade is stored as the decimal value 0.0034. Formatted as a percentage, what does the cell display?

Percentage format multiplies the stored value by 100 and appends a % sign, purely for display.

0.34%. The stored value is still exactly 0.0034, that's what any formula referencing this cell will use in its calculation, regardless of how it's displayed.

Practice 32

A cell stores the value 12889.95. Formatted as currency, what does it display as?

Solution

$12,889.95 (or the equivalent in whatever currency symbol is configured, e.g.\ €12,889.95), with the thousands separator and two decimal places added automatically by the format.

Practice 33

A cell displays 1,250 after applying "0 decimal places" formatting to an underlying value of 1249.7. If a formula elsewhere sums this cell along with others, does it use 1,250 or 1249.7 in the actual calculation?

Solution

1249.7, the true stored value, not the rounded display. This is a genuinely common gotcha: individually rounded-looking cells can sum to a total that doesn't match what you'd get by adding up the displayed (rounded) numbers by hand, because Excel is always calculating from the real underlying values, never from what's shown on screen. If a report absolutely must have displayed figures add up exactly, the values need to be genuinely rounded with a function (like ROUND), not just display-formatted.

Practice 34

The date serial number 44104 is formatted as a Short Date. What does it display as, and what does that serial number actually represent internally?

Solution

Displays as 30-Sep-2020. Internally, Excel stores every date as a plain number counting days from a fixed starting point (January 1, 1900), so date arithmetic, "how many days between these two dates," "what's 30 days after this date," is just ordinary subtraction and addition under the hood.

Practice 35

Why would an engineer deliberately restrict a reported value to 2 or 3 decimal places, even when Excel is internally storing many more?

Solution

Significant figures: reporting a stress value as 245.847291 MPa falsely implies a precision the measurement instrument never actually delivered, most gauges and assays simply aren't accurate to six decimal places, so displaying that many digits is misleading rather than more informative. Formatting the display to match the genuine precision of the underlying measurement (e.g.\ 245.8 MPa) communicates honestly what's actually known, while the full-precision value can still be kept stored underneath for accurate downstream calculations.

Practice 36

What's the practical difference between Currency format and Accounting format for the same underlying number?

Solution

Both display a currency symbol and thousands separator, but Accounting format aligns the currency symbols and decimal points consistently down an entire column (making a column of dollar figures easy to scan and compare at a glance), and typically displays negative values in parentheses rather than with a minus sign, the layout convention traditionally used in financial statements.

Practice 37

A cost report shows five line items, each individually rounded to whole dollars for display, and a total at the bottom. A colleague notices the displayed total doesn't exactly equal the sum of the five displayed (rounded) line items. Is this necessarily an error? Explain.

Solution

Not necessarily an error, this is a direct, entirely expected consequence of Practice 33's rounding behaviour. The total is computed from the true underlying (unrounded) values, while each line item is independently rounded only for display; those two processes can legitimately produce a small mismatch (often called a "rounding residual"). It's worth flagging in a report footnote when it happens (so a reader doesn't mistake it for an actual arithmetic mistake), but the fix isn't to "correct" the total, it's to understand that displayed rounding and true summation are two separate operations that don't have to reconcile perfectly.

7 Functions: SUM, AVERAGE, MIN, MAX & errors

Functions are pre-built formulas for common operations, add a range, average a range, find the extremes. They're written as =NAME(arguments), and they save you from typing out long chains of + signs by hand (imagine adding 200 cells one at a time). Function names are not case-sensitive.

Key idea

=SUM(range) adds every value in the range. =AVERAGE(range) finds the arithmetic mean. =MIN(range) and =MAX(range) find the smallest and largest values. All four accept either a single contiguous range or a comma-separated list of several ranges.

Worked example

Five shift tonnages are entered in B2:B6: 1250, 1180, 1340, 1290, 1205. Find the total, the average, the minimum, and the maximum shift.

=SUM(B2:B6): $1250+1180+1340+1290+1205 = 6265$ tonnes total.

=AVERAGE(B2:B6): $6265\div5 = 1253$ tonnes average.

=MIN(B2:B6): $1180$ (the weakest shift). $\ $ =MAX(B2:B6): $1340$ (the strongest shift).

Four different questions about the same five numbers, each answered with one short function rather than five separate manual calculations.

Practice 38

Compute =SUM(B1:B4) by hand, given B1=340, B2=275, B3=410, B4=198.

Solution

$340+275+410+198 = 1223$.

Practice 39

Using the same four values (340, 275, 410, 198), compute =AVERAGE(B1:B4).

Solution

$1223\div4 = 305.75$.

Practice 40

Using the same four values, state =MIN(B1:B4) and =MAX(B1:B4).

Solution

MIN = 198, MAX = 410.

Practice 41

Six stress test readings (MPa) are recorded: 245.2, 248.1, 243.9, 250.3, 246.7, 244.5. Compute the average, and the range (max minus min), a common QC check for measurement consistency.

Solution

Sum $=245.2+248.1+243.9+250.3+246.7+244.5=1478.7$. Average $=1478.7\div6\approx246.45$ MPa.

Max $=250.3$, Min $=243.9$, Range $=250.3-243.9=6.4$ MPa. A tight range like this suggests consistent, well-controlled testing; a wide range would flag a measurement or process problem worth investigating.

Practice 42

A formula =B5/B6 displays #DIV/0!. What does this error mean, and what's the most likely cause?

Solution

Excel is being asked to divide by zero, mathematically undefined. Most likely cause: cell B6 (the divisor) is empty or genuinely contains 0, e.g.\ a "hours operated" cell that hasn't been filled in yet, feeding into an "output per hour" calculation.

Practice 43

A formula typed as =AVG(B3:B7) displays #NAME?. What's gone wrong?

Solution

AVG is not a real Excel function, the correct function name is AVERAGE. Excel found text in the formula it couldn't match to any legitimate function or named range, and flags it with #NAME?, exactly the =SOME(...) vs.\ =SUM(...) typo the source manual itself warns about.

Practice 44

A cell displays a row of hash symbols, ########, instead of a number. Is this an error in the formula? What's actually wrong, and how do you fix it?

Solution

No, the formula and its result are perfectly fine, this isn't a calculation error at all. The column is simply too narrow to display the full number, Excel refuses to show a truncated or misleading version of a number, so it shows hash marks instead. Fix: widen the column (drag the column border, or double-click it to auto-fit).

Practice 45

Cell B3 contains the word "three" (text) instead of the number 3. A formula =B2/B3 displays #VALUE!. Explain what this error means and how it differs from #DIV/0!.

Solution

#VALUE! means the formula encountered something that isn't a usable number where a number was expected, here, text ("three") sitting where the divisor should be. This is a different failure from #DIV/0!: dividing by zero is a valid number that just produces an undefined result, while dividing by text isn't even a valid arithmetic operation to attempt in the first place. The fix is to enter an actual numeric value (3, not "three") into B3.

8 Quick Analysis, tables, and charts

Raw numbers in a grid rarely communicate a trend as fast as a well-chosen chart does. Excel's Quick Analysis tool (the small icon that appears at the corner of a selected range) offers instant previews of formatting, totals, and chart types, but the real skill is knowing which chart type actually fits the question you're asking.

Key idea

Column/Bar: compare values across categories. Line: show a trend over time. Pie: show proportion of a whole (only when the parts genuinely sum to 100%). Scatter (XY): show the relationship between two numeric variables. A chart can live embedded in a worksheet (alongside the data) or on its own chart sheet (for a cleaner, full-page view when printing or presenting).

Worked example

You have monthly tonnage figures for a pit across a full year, and separately, a cost breakdown (fuel, labour, maintenance, other) for one specific month. Which chart type suits each, and why would swapping them be a mistake?

Monthly tonnage over a year is a trend over time, a line chart fits, the eye reads the slope and direction instantly.

The cost breakdown for one month is a proportion of a whole (fuel + labour + maintenance + other = 100% of that month's cost), a pie chart fits.

Swapping them would actively mislead a reader: a pie chart of 12 months of tonnage implies those 12 figures are parts of one whole (they're not, they're a sequence over time, and a pie chart hides the order entirely), while a line chart of four cost categories with no time axis implies a trend where none exists.

Practice 46

You have weekly ore tonnage figures for the last 26 weeks. What chart type is most appropriate, and why?

Solution

A line chart. Weekly figures over time are a trend, and a line chart makes the direction (rising, falling, plateauing) and any seasonal pattern immediately visible in a way a table of 26 numbers never could.

Practice 47

You have total operating cost for one quarter, broken into four categories that sum to 100% of spend. What chart type fits?

Solution

A pie chart, exactly because these four categories are genuine parts of one whole, the classic case a pie chart is built for.

Practice 48

You suspect older haul trucks (by age in years) tend to have more unplanned downtime (hours per month). You have both figures for 30 trucks. What chart type would reveal whether a relationship exists?

Solution

A scatter (XY) chart, with truck age on one axis and downtime hours on the other. Each truck becomes a single point, and if a genuine relationship exists, the points will visibly trend upward (or downward) rather than scattering randomly, exactly the tool for investigating a relationship between two numeric variables, which neither a column, line, nor pie chart is designed to show.

Practice 49

Explain the difference between an embedded chart and a chart sheet, and when you'd prefer each.

Solution

An embedded chart sits on top of a worksheet, alongside its source data, useful when you want the chart and the numbers visible together (e.g.\ a monthly report where both matter). A chart sheet gives the chart its own dedicated tab with no data cluttering the view, useful when the chart itself is the deliverable, printing a single clean chart for a presentation slide or a stand-alone summary page, rather than a working report.

Practice 50

What does converting a plain range of data into an official Excel Table add, beyond what an ordinary range already has?

Solution

A Table adds automatic filter buttons on every header, banded row shading for easier reading, and, critically, it automatically expands to include new rows appended at the bottom (a formula or chart referencing the table keeps working correctly as data grows, without needing the range manually re-selected). It also enables structured references (naming columns instead of just cell addresses), a small taste of the column-based thinking that a pandas DataFrame formalises much further.

Practice 51

Explain, with a concrete example, why using a pie chart for a genuinely time-based dataset (like 12 months of production) can actively mislead a reader, rather than just being a slightly worse choice.

Solution

A pie chart strips out ordering entirely, the twelve slices could be arranged in any sequence around the circle with no visual consequence, so a genuine trend (steadily rising production through the year, say) becomes completely invisible; the reader sees only twelve unordered proportions of an annual total. Worse, a pie chart implicitly frames the twelve months as competing "shares" of one fixed pie, which isn't really the right mental model for a sequence of independent monthly totals at all. This is a real, not just stylistic, distortion of what the data is actually saying, exactly why chart-type selection is a data-literacy skill, not a cosmetic one.

Practice 52

An Excel Table has columns Equipment_ID, Hours_Run, and Last_Service_Date. In one sentence, describe the pandas equivalent of this structure, and what the column names would become.

Solution

This Table is directly equivalent to a pandas DataFrame with three columns, and the column names (Equipment_ID, Hours_Run, Last_Service_Date) would become the DataFrame's column labels exactly as written, referenced in Python as df['Hours_Run'] the same way you'd click a column header in Excel, the underlying idea, rows are records and columns are variables, is genuinely identical across both tools.

Practice 53 EXPLORE

Sketch (on paper or in Excel) a 3-chart monthly dashboard for a single pit: what three charts would you include, and what would each one need to show to actually be useful to a site manager glancing at it for 30 seconds?

One reasonable approach

(1) A line chart of daily or weekly tonnage against a target line, the fastest way to see if the pit is tracking on plan. (2) A pie chart of cost breakdown by category for the month, where the money is actually going. (3) A column chart comparing this month's key figures against last month's, immediate context for whether things are improving or slipping. The real design principle: each chart should answer one specific question a manager actually has, not just "chart everything available," a dashboard with too many charts is often less useful than one with three well-chosen ones.

Use the fill series technique to generate survey chainage labels: 0+000, 0+020, 0+040, ... up to 0+200 (20 m intervals), a common civil/mining survey convention. Does Excel's built-in series recognition handle this pattern automatically, or would you need to type the first two entries to establish the step?

Solution

Chainage notation like 0+020 isn't one of Excel's automatically-recognised built-in series (unlike days, months, or a simple trailing number), so the reliable approach is to type the first two entries (0+000 and 0+020) as plain text, select both, and drag the fill handle, letting Excel infer the pattern from the two-cell step exactly as in Practice 16 and 18. Practice 58 EXPLORE

Imagine your 8-truck cycle-time table from Practice 54 as a CSV file loaded into Python with pandas.read_csv(). What would df.describe() return, and how does that compare to what you computed by hand with SUM/AVERAGE/MIN/MAX?

The bridge to data science

df.describe() returns, for every numeric column at once, exactly the summary statistics you've been computing one function at a time in Excel: count, mean (AVERAGE), min, max, standard deviation, and the quartiles, all in a single command, for every column simultaneously, rather than one =AVERAGE() formula per column typed by hand. What to expect
