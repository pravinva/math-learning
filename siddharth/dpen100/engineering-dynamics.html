Topic 1
Kinematics Fundamentals
position · velocity · acceleration · the three core relations

What is Kinematics?

Kinematics describes how objects move — position, velocity, acceleration — without asking why (forces come later in dynamics). For DPEN100, we focus on a particle: an object whose size and shape don't matter, only its position.

Position and Displacement

Choose a fixed reference point (origin) and a positive direction. Position $s$ locates the particle along the axis.

$$\Delta s = s_2 - s_1 \qquad \text{(displacement, can be negative)}$$

Displacement ≠ Distance. Distance is the total path length (always positive). Displacement is the net change in position (signed).

Concept Example

A particle moves from $s = 2$ m to $s = 8$ m then back to $s = 5$ m.

  • Distance travelled: $6 + 3 = 9$ m
  • Displacement: $5 - 2 = +3$ m

Velocity

Velocity is the rate of change of position with respect to time.

$$v_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t} \qquad v = \frac{ds}{dt} \quad \text{(instantaneous)}$$
  • $v > 0$: moving in the positive direction
  • $v < 0$: moving in the negative direction
  • $v = 0$: momentarily at rest (may reverse direction)
  • Speed $= |v|$ — always non-negative

Acceleration

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity.

$$a_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} \qquad a = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2s}{dt^2}$$
Key sign rule: When $a$ and $v$ have the same sign → particle is speeding up. When they have opposite signs → particle is slowing down. This is true regardless of which direction is positive.

The Three Fundamental Relations

These three equations are the backbone of all kinematics. Everything else is derived from them.

$$\boxed{v = \frac{ds}{dt}} \qquad \boxed{a = \frac{dv}{dt}} \qquad \boxed{a\,ds = v\,dv}$$

The third relation is found by eliminating $dt$:

$$a = \frac{dv}{dt} \implies a\,dt = dv \quad \text{and} \quad v = \frac{ds}{dt} \implies dt = \frac{ds}{v}$$

Substituting: $a\,\dfrac{ds}{v} = dv \implies a\,ds = v\,dv$

Use this third form whenever time is not involved in the problem.

Worked Example 1.1 — Find v and a from s(t)

A particle has position $s(t) = 2t^3 - 9t^2 + 12t - 4$ metres, $t \geq 0$.

Find: velocity and acceleration at $t = 2$ s. When does the particle momentarily stop?

Step 1 — Differentiate

$$v(t) = \frac{ds}{dt} = 6t^2 - 18t + 12$$

$$a(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} = 12t - 18$$

Step 2 — Evaluate at t = 2

$$v(2) = 6(4) - 18(2) + 12 = 24 - 36 + 12 = 0 \text{ m/s}$$

$$a(2) = 12(2) - 18 = 6 \text{ m/s}^2$$

Step 3 — When does it stop? (v = 0)

$$6t^2 - 18t + 12 = 0 \implies t^2 - 3t + 2 = 0 \implies (t-1)(t-2) = 0$$

Stops at $t = 1$ s and $t = 2$ s. At $t = 1$: $a(1) = 12 - 18 = -6$ m/s² (reverses). At $t = 2$: $a(2) = +6$ m/s² (reverses again).

Worked Example 1.2 — Use a·ds = v·dv

A particle starts at $s = 0$ with $v_0 = 2$ m/s. Its acceleration is $a = 3s$ m/s² where $s$ is in metres. Find the velocity when $s = 3$ m.

Use the third relation (no time involved)

$$a\,ds = v\,dv \implies 3s\,ds = v\,dv$$

$$\int_0^3 3s\,ds = \int_2^v v\,dv$$

$$\left[\frac{3s^2}{2}\right]_0^3 = \left[\frac{v^2}{2}\right]_2^v$$

$$\frac{3(9)}{2} = \frac{v^2 - 4}{2} \implies 13.5 = \frac{v^2-4}{2} \implies v^2 = 31 \implies v = 5.57 \text{ m/s}$$

Practice Problems
  1. A particle moves with $s(t) = t^4 - 4t^3$ m. Find: (a) velocity at $t = 3$ s; (b) acceleration at $t = 3$ s; (c) the time(s) when the particle is at rest.
  2. A particle has $v_0 = 5$ m/s and acceleration $a = 4s$ m/s² (s in metres, starting from $s=0$). Find $v$ when $s = 2$ m using the $a\,ds = v\,dv$ relation.
▶ Show Answers

1: $v = 4t^3 - 12t^2$. At $t=3$: $v = 108 - 108 = 0$ m/s. $a = 12t^2 - 24t$. At $t=3$: $a = 108 - 72 = 36$ m/s². At rest: $4t^2(t-3) = 0 \implies t = 0$ or $t = 3$ s.

2: $\int_0^2 4s\,ds = \int_5^v v\,dv \implies [2s^2]_0^2 = [\frac{v^2}{2}]_5^v \implies 8 = \frac{v^2 - 25}{2} \implies v^2 = 41 \implies v = 6.40$ m/s.

Topic 2
Graphical Kinematics — s-t, v-t, a-t Graphs
slopes · areas · erratic motion

The Three Graphs and Their Relationships

Since $v = ds/dt$ and $a = dv/dt$, the graphs are linked by slopes and areas:

From graphSlope givesArea under curve gives
$s$-$t$ graphvelocity $v$—
$v$-$t$ graphacceleration $a$displacement $\Delta s$
$a$-$t$ graph—change in velocity $\Delta v$
Area under v-t curve = displacement (not distance). Areas below the time axis are negative — they represent motion in the negative direction.

Reading the s-t Graph

  • Steep positive slope → high positive velocity (moving fast in + direction)
  • Horizontal → at rest ($v = 0$)
  • Negative slope → moving in negative direction
  • Curve curving upward → acceleration (slope is increasing)
  • Curve curving downward → deceleration (slope is decreasing)

Reading the v-t Graph

  • Positive slope → positive acceleration
  • Horizontal → constant velocity ($a = 0$)
  • Crosses zero axis → particle reverses direction
  • Constant slope (straight line) → constant acceleration → use SUVAT

Erratic Motion — Piecewise Graphs

When a-t is given as a piecewise graph, integrate each segment separately to build the v-t graph, then integrate v-t to get s-t. Always carry over values from the end of one segment to the start of the next.

Worked Example 2.1 — Build v-t from a-t

A particle starts at rest. The a-t graph is:

  • $0 \leq t \leq 4$ s: $a = 3$ m/s² (constant)
  • $4 \leq t \leq 8$ s: $a = -1.5$ m/s² (constant)
Phase 1 (0–4 s): Area under a-t = Δv

$\Delta v = 3 \times 4 = 12$ m/s. Since $v_0 = 0$: $v(4) = 12$ m/s

Phase 2 (4–8 s):

$\Delta v = -1.5 \times 4 = -6$ m/s. So $v(8) = 12 - 6 = 6$ m/s

Displacement: area under v-t (two trapezoids)

Phase 1: triangle → $\frac{1}{2}(4)(12) = 24$ m

Phase 2: trapezoid → $\frac{1}{2}(12 + 6)(4) = 36$ m

Total displacement $= 60$ m

Worked Example 2.2 — Particle reverses direction

v-t graph: straight line from $v = 6$ m/s at $t=0$ to $v = -4$ m/s at $t = 5$ s.

Find total distance (not displacement)

Line crosses zero at: $t^* = \frac{6}{6+4} \times 5 = 3$ s (by similar triangles)

Area $0 \to 3$: $\frac{1}{2}(3)(6) = +9$ m (positive displacement)

Area $3 \to 5$: $\frac{1}{2}(2)(4) = -4$ m (negative displacement)

Displacement $= 9 - 4 = +5$ m  |  Distance $= 9 + 4 = 13$ m

Practice Problems
  1. A v-t graph shows: $v = 0$ at $t=0$, rises linearly to $v = 20$ m/s at $t=4$ s, stays constant to $t=7$ s, then drops linearly to $v=0$ at $t=10$ s. Find: (a) acceleration in each phase; (b) total displacement; (c) total distance.
  2. An a-t graph shows $a = 2t$ for $0 \leq t \leq 3$ s. If $v_0 = 1$ m/s, find $v$ at $t = 3$ s using the area method.
▶ Show Answers

1a: Phase 1: $a = 20/4 = 5$ m/s². Phase 2: $a = 0$. Phase 3: $a = (0-20)/3 = -6.67$ m/s².

1b: $\frac{1}{2}(4)(20) + (3)(20) + \frac{1}{2}(3)(20) = 40 + 60 + 30 = 130$ m. No sign change, so distance = displacement = 130 m.

2: Area under $a = 2t$ from 0 to 3: $\int_0^3 2t\,dt = [t^2]_0^3 = 9$. So $v(3) = v_0 + 9 = 10$ m/s.

Topic 3
Constant Acceleration — SUVAT Equations
derivation · strategy · free fall · multi-body problems

Derivation from First Principles

If $a = \text{constant}$, integrate directly:

$$\int_{v_0}^{v} dv = a\int_0^t dt \implies \boxed{v = v_0 + at} \quad \cdots (1)$$

Substitute (1) into $ds/dt = v$ and integrate:

$$\int_{s_0}^{s} ds = \int_0^t (v_0 + at)\,dt \implies \boxed{s = s_0 + v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2} \quad \cdots (2)$$

Eliminate $t$ by solving (1) for $t = \frac{v - v_0}{a}$ and substituting into (2):

$$\boxed{v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(s - s_0)} \quad \cdots (3)$$

Average velocity (only valid for constant $a$):

$$\boxed{s - s_0 = \tfrac{1}{2}(v_0 + v)\,t} \quad \cdots (4)$$

The Five SUVAT Variables

$s$
displacement (m)
$u = v_0$
initial velocity (m/s)
$v$
final velocity (m/s)
$a$
acceleration (m/s²)
$t$
time (s)

Each equation uses 4 of the 5. Know 3 → find the other 2.

Equation Selection Guide

Missing variableUse equation
$s$ (displacement unknown)$v = v_0 + at$
$v$ (final vel unknown)$s = s_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2$
$t$ (time unknown)$v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(s-s_0)$
$a$ (acceleration unknown)$s = s_0 + \frac{1}{2}(v_0+v)t$
$v_0$ (initial vel unknown)any equation with known others
Only use SUVAT when $a$ is constant throughout the problem. If $a$ changes, split into phases or use integration.

Free Fall

Special case: only gravity acts. $|a| = g = 9.81$ m/s².

  • If upward is positive: $a = -9.81$ m/s²
  • If downward is positive: $a = +9.81$ m/s²
  • At maximum height: $v = 0$ (only momentarily, particle still has acceleration $g$ downward)
  • For symmetric launch/landing: time up = time down
Worked Example 3.1 — Braking Vehicle

A truck travelling at 90 km/h applies brakes and stops in 75 m. Find: (a) deceleration; (b) time to stop; (c) speed after 40 m.

Convert: 90 km/h = 25 m/s
(a) Use eq. (3) — no time involved

$$v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(s-s_0) \implies 0 = 625 + 2a(75) \implies a = -4.17 \text{ m/s}^2$$

(b) Use eq. (1)

$$0 = 25 + (-4.17)t \implies t = 6.0 \text{ s}$$

(c) Speed after 40 m

$$v^2 = 625 + 2(-4.17)(40) = 625 - 333.6 = 291.4 \implies v = 17.1 \text{ m/s}$$

Worked Example 3.2 — Vertical throw

A ball is thrown vertically upward at 18 m/s from a roof 30 m high. Taking upward as positive and origin at ground. Find: (a) max height above ground; (b) time to hit the ground; (c) speed at impact.

Set up: $s_0 = 30$ m, $v_0 = +18$ m/s, $a = -9.81$ m/s²
(a) At max height, $v = 0$

$$v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(s - s_0) \implies 0 = 324 + 2(-9.81)(s - 30)$$

$$s - 30 = \frac{324}{19.62} = 16.51 \text{ m} \implies s = 46.5 \text{ m above ground}$$

(b) When $s = 0$ (ground level)

$$0 = 30 + 18t - 4.905t^2 \implies 4.905t^2 - 18t - 30 = 0$$

$$t = \frac{18 \pm \sqrt{324 + 4(4.905)(30)}}{2(4.905)} = \frac{18 \pm \sqrt{912.6}}{9.81}$$

$$t = \frac{18 + 30.21}{9.81} = 4.91 \text{ s} \quad (\text{take positive root})$$

(c) Speed at impact

$$v = 18 + (-9.81)(4.91) = 18 - 48.2 = -30.2 \text{ m/s} \implies \text{speed} = 30.2 \text{ m/s}$$

Worked Example 3.3 — Two vehicles, when do they meet?

Car A starts from rest at $x = 0$ with $a = 2$ m/s². At the same instant, car B passes $x = 0$ at $v = 10$ m/s with $a = -0.5$ m/s² (braking). Find: (a) when A overtakes B; (b) the position.

Write position equations for each

$$s_A = \tfrac{1}{2}(2)t^2 = t^2$$

$$s_B = 10t + \tfrac{1}{2}(-0.5)t^2 = 10t - 0.25t^2$$

Set equal (they meet when $s_A = s_B$)

$$t^2 = 10t - 0.25t^2 \implies 1.25t^2 - 10t = 0 \implies t(1.25t - 10) = 0$$

$t = 0$ (start) and $t = 8$ s. A overtakes B at $t = \mathbf{8}$ s.

Position: $s_A(8) = 64$ m

Check B stops before $t = 8$: $v_B = 0$ at $t = 10/0.5 = 20$ s. B hasn't stopped yet. ✓

Practice Problems
  1. A motorcycle accelerates from 60 km/h to 100 km/h in 8 s. Find: (a) acceleration; (b) distance covered during this period.
  2. A stone is dropped from 80 m height. Find: (a) velocity just before impact; (b) time to fall.
  3. A car decelerates uniformly from 28 m/s and stops. If it travels 180 m in the last 6 s before stopping, find the deceleration.
  4. Two trains 500 m apart approach each other on parallel tracks. Train A: 30 m/s, decelerating at 1 m/s². Train B: 20 m/s, decelerating at 0.5 m/s². Do they collide?
▶ Show Answers

1: $\Delta v = 40/3.6 = 11.11$ m/s. $a = 11.11/8 = 1.39$ m/s². $s = \frac{1}{2}(60/3.6 + 100/3.6)(8) = \frac{1}{2}(16.67+27.78)(8) = 177.8$ m.

2: $v^2 = 0 + 2(9.81)(80) = 1569.6 \implies v = 39.6$ m/s. $t = v/g = 39.6/9.81 = 4.04$ s.

3: In last 6 s: $v = 0$, $s = 180$ m. Using $s = \frac{1}{2}(v_0+v)t$: $180 = \frac{1}{2}v_0(6) \implies v_0 = 60$ m/s at start of last 6 s phase. But car's initial speed was 28 m/s, so this scenario is impossible as stated. Assuming the problem means the car decelerates uniformly to rest and covers 180 m total: use $0 = 28^2 + 2a(180) \implies a = -2.18$ m/s².

4: When both stop: $v_A = 0$ at $t = 30$ s (dist = 450 m). $v_B = 0$ at $t = 40$ s (dist = 400 m). Total stopping distance = 850 m > 500 m → they collide.

Topic 4
Variable Acceleration — Integration
a(t) · a(s) · a(v) · choosing the right integral

When SUVAT Fails

If acceleration changes with time, position, or velocity, you must integrate. The three fundamental relations guide which integral to use.

Case 1: a = a(t)

Acceleration is given as a function of time. Integrate directly:

$$v(t) = v_0 + \int_{t_0}^{t} a(\tau)\,d\tau \qquad s(t) = s_0 + \int_{t_0}^{t} v(\tau)\,d\tau$$

Case 2: a = a(s)

Acceleration depends on position. Use $a\,ds = v\,dv$:

$$\int_{v_0}^{v} v\,dv = \int_{s_0}^{s} a(s)\,ds \implies \frac{v^2 - v_0^2}{2} = \int_{s_0}^{s} a(s)\,ds$$

Case 3: a = a(v)

Acceleration depends on velocity. Separate variables from $a = dv/dt$:

$$\int_{v_0}^{v} \frac{dv}{a(v)} = \int_0^t dt = t - t_0$$

To find position as a function of velocity, use $v\,dt = ds \implies dt = ds/v$:

$$\int_{v_0}^{v} \frac{v\,dv}{a(v)} = \int_{s_0}^{s} ds = s - s_0$$
Worked Example 4.1 — a(t)

A particle starts at rest at $s = 0$. Its acceleration is $a = 6t$ m/s² for $t \geq 0$. Find $v$ and $s$ at $t = 4$ s.

Integrate a(t) to get v(t)

$$v = 0 + \int_0^t 6\tau\,d\tau = [3\tau^2]_0^t = 3t^2$$

Integrate v(t) to get s(t)

$$s = 0 + \int_0^t 3\tau^2\,d\tau = [\tau^3]_0^t = t^3$$

At t = 4 s

$$v(4) = 3(16) = 48 \text{ m/s}, \quad s(4) = 64 \text{ m}$$

Worked Example 4.2 — a(s)

A particle has $a = -2s$ m/s² where $s$ is in metres. At $s = 0$, $v = 8$ m/s. Find $v$ when $s = 3$ m.

Use a·ds = v·dv

$$\int_0^3 (-2s)\,ds = \int_8^v v\,dv$$

$$[-s^2]_0^3 = \left[\frac{v^2}{2}\right]_8^v$$

$$-9 = \frac{v^2 - 64}{2} \implies v^2 = 64 - 18 = 46 \implies v = 6.78 \text{ m/s}$$

Worked Example 4.3 — a(v)

A particle has $a = -0.2v$ m/s² (air drag proportional to velocity). At $t = 0$, $v = 20$ m/s. Find $v(t)$.

Separate variables: a = dv/dt

$$-0.2v = \frac{dv}{dt} \implies \frac{dv}{v} = -0.2\,dt$$

$$\int_{20}^{v} \frac{d\nu}{\nu} = -0.2\int_0^t d\tau$$

$$[\ln \nu]_{20}^{v} = -0.2t \implies \ln v - \ln 20 = -0.2t$$

$$\ln\left(\frac{v}{20}\right) = -0.2t \implies v = 20e^{-0.2t} \text{ m/s}$$

Practice Problems
  1. A particle starts from rest at $s = 0$ with $a = 4t^2$ m/s². Find $v$ and $s$ at $t = 2$ s.
  2. A particle has $a = -4\sqrt{s}$ m/s² (s in metres). At $s = 0$, $v = 12$ m/s. Find $v$ when $s = 4$ m.
  3. A particle has $a = -kv^2$ (quadratic drag). If $v_0 = 30$ m/s and $k = 0.01$, find $v$ at $t = 5$ s.
▶ Show Answers

1: $v = \int_0^2 4t^2\,dt = [\frac{4t^3}{3}]_0^2 = \frac{32}{3} = 10.67$ m/s. $s = \int_0^2 \frac{4t^3}{3}\,dt = [\frac{t^4}{3}]_0^2 = \frac{16}{3} = 5.33$ m.

2: $\int_0^4 (-4\sqrt{s})\,ds = \int_{12}^v v\,dv \implies [-\frac{8s^{3/2}}{3}]_0^4 = \frac{v^2-144}{2} \implies -\frac{64}{3} = \frac{v^2-144}{2} \implies v^2 = 144 - \frac{128}{3} = \frac{304}{3} \implies v = 10.07$ m/s.

3: $\int_{30}^v \frac{dv}{-0.01v^2} = 5 \implies [\frac{1}{0.01v}]_{30}^v = 5 \implies \frac{100}{v} - \frac{100}{30} = 5 \implies \frac{100}{v} = 5 + 3.33 = 8.33 \implies v = 12$ m/s.

Topic 5
Curvilinear Motion — 2D Kinematics
position vectors · velocity · acceleration · Cartesian coordinates

Position Vector

A particle moving in 2D or 3D has position vector $\vec{r}(t)$ from origin O to the particle.

$$\vec{r} = x\,\hat{i} + y\,\hat{j} \quad \text{(2D)} \qquad \vec{r} = x\,\hat{i} + y\,\hat{j} + z\,\hat{k} \quad \text{(3D)}$$

Velocity and Acceleration Vectors

$$\vec{v} = \frac{d\vec{r}}{dt} = \dot{x}\,\hat{i} + \dot{y}\,\hat{j}$$ $$\vec{a} = \frac{d\vec{v}}{dt} = \ddot{x}\,\hat{i} + \ddot{y}\,\hat{j}$$

Magnitude (speed): $|\vec{v}| = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2}$

Direction: $\theta = \tan^{-1}(v_y / v_x)$

Key Principle

x and y motions are independent. Solve each component separately using 1D kinematics, then recombine. This is the foundation of projectile motion.

Path Curvature

The particle follows a curved path if the velocity vector changes direction (even if its magnitude stays constant). Acceleration has two roles:

  • Change speed — component along velocity (tangential)
  • Change direction — component perpendicular to velocity (normal)
Worked Example 5.1 — Velocity and acceleration from position

A particle moves with $\vec{r}(t) = (2t^2)\,\hat{i} + (3t - t^2)\,\hat{j}$ metres. Find velocity and acceleration at $t = 1$ s.

Differentiate once for velocity

$$\vec{v} = \frac{d\vec{r}}{dt} = (4t)\,\hat{i} + (3 - 2t)\,\hat{j}$$

At $t = 1$: $\vec{v}(1) = 4\,\hat{i} + 1\,\hat{j}$ m/s

Differentiate again for acceleration

$$\vec{a} = \frac{d\vec{v}}{dt} = 4\,\hat{i} - 2\,\hat{j} \text{ m/s}^2 \quad (\text{constant})$$

Speed and direction at t = 1

$$|\vec{v}| = \sqrt{16 + 1} = 4.12 \text{ m/s}, \quad \theta = \tan^{-1}(1/4) = 14°$$

Worked Example 5.2 — Independent x-y motion

A particle has constant $a_x = 2$ m/s², $a_y = -3$ m/s². At $t = 0$: $x = 0$, $y = 0$, $v_x = 5$ m/s, $v_y = 0$. Find position at $t = 4$ s.

x-direction (SUVAT)

$$x = 0 + 5(4) + \frac{1}{2}(2)(16) = 20 + 16 = 36 \text{ m}$$

y-direction (SUVAT)

$$y = 0 + 0 + \frac{1}{2}(-3)(16) = -24 \text{ m}$$

Position vector

$$\vec{r}(4) = 36\,\hat{i} - 24\,\hat{j} \text{ m}$$

Practice Problems
  1. A particle moves with $\vec{r} = (t^3 - 2t)\,\hat{i} + (4t^2)\,\hat{j}$ m. Find: (a) velocity at $t = 2$ s; (b) acceleration at $t = 2$ s; (c) when the particle crosses the y-axis.
  2. A particle has constant acceleration $\vec{a} = 3\,\hat{i} - 4\,\hat{j}$ m/s². At $t = 0$: $\vec{r} = \vec{0}$, $\vec{v} = 10\,\hat{i} + 5\,\hat{j}$ m/s. Find position at $t = 3$ s.
▶ Show Answers

1a: $\vec{v} = (3t^2 - 2)\,\hat{i} + 8t\,\hat{j} \implies \vec{v}(2) = 10\,\hat{i} + 16\,\hat{j}$ m/s.

1b: $\vec{a} = 6t\,\hat{i} + 8\,\hat{j} \implies \vec{a}(2) = 12\,\hat{i} + 8\,\hat{j}$ m/s².

1c: On y-axis: $x = 0 \implies t^3 - 2t = 0 \implies t(t^2 - 2) = 0 \implies t = 0, \pm\sqrt{2}$ s. For $t \geq 0$: $t = 1.41$ s.

2: $x = 0 + 10(3) + \frac{1}{2}(3)(9) = 30 + 13.5 = 43.5$ m. $y = 0 + 5(3) + \frac{1}{2}(-4)(9) = 15 - 18 = -3$ m. $\vec{r}(3) = 43.5\,\hat{i} - 3\,\hat{j}$ m.

Topic 6
Projectile Motion
horizontal · vertical · range · max height · time of flight

The Setup

A projectile is launched with initial speed $v_0$ at angle $\theta$ to the horizontal. Gravity acts downward. Air resistance is neglected.

Initial conditions
$$v_{0x} = v_0 \cos\theta, \quad v_{0y} = v_0 \sin\theta$$ $$a_x = 0, \quad a_y = -g = -9.81 \text{ m/s}^2 \quad (\text{taking upward as positive})$$

The Equations (x and y are independent)

Horizontal (x)Vertical (y)
$v_x = v_{0x}$ (constant)$v_y = v_{0y} - gt$
$x = v_{0x}\,t$$y = v_{0y}\,t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2$
—$v_y^2 = v_{0y}^2 - 2gy$

Key Results for Level Launch (same height landing)

Time of flight
$T = \frac{2v_0\sin\theta}{g}$
Max height
$H = \frac{v_0^2\sin^2\theta}{2g}$
Range
$R = \frac{v_0^2\sin 2\theta}{g}$
Maximum range: occurs at $\theta = 45°$ (for level ground). Complementary angles $\theta$ and $90° - \theta$ give the same range but different trajectories.

At Maximum Height

  • $v_y = 0$ (instantaneous stop in vertical motion)
  • $v_x$ unchanged → particle still has horizontal velocity
  • Time to max height: $t = \frac{v_{0y}}{g} = \frac{v_0\sin\theta}{g}$ (half the flight time)
Worked Example 6.1 — Projectile on level ground

A ball is kicked at 20 m/s at 35° to the horizontal. Find: (a) time of flight; (b) horizontal range; (c) max height.

Resolve initial velocity

$$v_{0x} = 20\cos 35° = 16.38 \text{ m/s}, \quad v_{0y} = 20\sin 35° = 11.47 \text{ m/s}$$

(a) Time of flight: when y = 0

$$0 = 11.47t - 4.905t^2 \implies t(11.47 - 4.905t) = 0 \implies t = 2.34 \text{ s}$$

(b) Horizontal range

$$R = v_{0x} \times T = 16.38 \times 2.34 = 38.3 \text{ m}$$

(c) Max height: when $v_y = 0$

$$v_y^2 = v_{0y}^2 - 2gH \implies 0 = 131.6 - 19.62H \implies H = 6.71 \text{ m}$$

Worked Example 6.2 — Projectile from a cliff

A stone is thrown horizontally at 15 m/s from the top of a 40 m cliff. Find: (a) time to hit the ground; (b) horizontal distance from cliff base; (c) speed at impact.

Set up: $v_{0x} = 15$ m/s, $v_{0y} = 0$, $y_0 = 40$ m
(a) When y = 0 (ground level)

$$0 = 40 + 0 - 4.905t^2 \implies t = \sqrt{\frac{40}{4.905}} = 2.86 \text{ s}$$

(b) Horizontal distance

$$x = 15 \times 2.86 = 42.9 \text{ m}$$

(c) Velocity components at impact

$$v_x = 15 \text{ m/s} \quad (\text{unchanged})$$

$$v_y = 0 - 9.81(2.86) = -28.1 \text{ m/s}$$

$$|\vec{v}| = \sqrt{15^2 + 28.1^2} = 31.9 \text{ m/s}$$

Worked Example 6.3 — Hitting a target at different height

A projectile is launched at 25 m/s at 40° from ground level. A wall 30 m away has height 12 m. Does the projectile clear the wall?

When x = 30 m, find y

$$v_{0x} = 25\cos 40° = 19.15 \text{ m/s}, \quad v_{0y} = 25\sin 40° = 16.07 \text{ m/s}$$

$$30 = 19.15t \implies t = 1.57 \text{ s}$$

Height at x = 30 m

$$y = 16.07(1.57) - 4.905(1.57)^2 = 25.2 - 12.1 = 13.1 \text{ m}$$

Yes, clears the wall by 1.1 m.

Practice Problems
  1. A ball is thrown at 18 m/s at 50° on level ground. Find: (a) max height; (b) range; (c) velocity (magnitude and direction) at max height.
  2. A projectile launched at 30° hits the ground 50 m away. Find the initial speed.
  3. A ball is kicked from ground level and reaches max height of 8 m. It lands 40 m away. Find: (a) initial speed; (b) launch angle.
▶ Show Answers

1a: $H = \frac{18^2\sin^2 50°}{2(9.81)} = \frac{324(0.587)}{19.62} = 9.70$ m.

1b: $R = \frac{18^2\sin 100°}{9.81} = \frac{324(0.985)}{9.81} = 32.5$ m.

1c: At max height: $v_y = 0$, $v_x = 18\cos 50° = 11.57$ m/s. Speed = 11.57 m/s, direction = horizontal.

2: $R = \frac{v_0^2\sin 60°}{9.81} = 50 \implies v_0 = \sqrt{\frac{50(9.81)}{0.866}} = 23.8$ m/s.

3a: $H = \frac{v_0^2\sin^2\theta}{2g} = 8$ and $R = \frac{v_0^2\sin 2\theta}{g} = 40$. Divide: $\frac{\sin^2\theta}{2\sin 2\theta} = \frac{8}{40} \implies \frac{\sin\theta}{4\cos\theta} = 0.2 \implies \tan\theta = 0.8 \implies \theta = 38.7°$. Then $v_0 = \sqrt{\frac{8(2)(9.81)}{\sin^2 38.7°}} = 20.2$ m/s.

Topic 7
Normal and Tangential Components
path coordinates · tangent · normal · radius of curvature

Why n-t Coordinates?

Sometimes it's easier to describe motion along a curved path using coordinates aligned with the path itself:

  • Tangential (t): along the direction of motion (velocity)
  • Normal (n): perpendicular to the path, pointing toward the centre of curvature

Velocity in n-t Coordinates

$$\vec{v} = v\,\hat{e}_t \qquad (\text{velocity is always tangent to the path})$$

where $v = |\vec{v}|$ is the speed (scalar) and $\hat{e}_t$ is the unit tangent vector.

Acceleration Components

$$\vec{a} = a_t\,\hat{e}_t + a_n\,\hat{e}_n$$ $$a_t = \frac{dv}{dt} \quad (\text{rate of change of speed})$$ $$a_n = \frac{v^2}{\rho} \quad (\text{centripetal acceleration, } \rho = \text{radius of curvature})$$
Tangential acceleration changes the magnitude of velocity (speed). Normal acceleration changes the direction of velocity. Even at constant speed, $a_n \neq 0$ if the path is curved.

Radius of Curvature

For a path $y = f(x)$:

$$\rho = \frac{\left[1 + \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2\right]^{3/2}}{\left|\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\right|}$$

For circular motion, $\rho = R$ (constant).

Worked Example 7.1 — Car on circular track

A car travels at constant speed $v = 20$ m/s around a circular track of radius 50 m. Find the magnitude of its acceleration.

Constant speed → $a_t = 0$
Normal acceleration

$$a_n = \frac{v^2}{\rho} = \frac{400}{50} = 8 \text{ m/s}^2$$

Total acceleration: $|\vec{a}| = a_n = 8$ m/s² (directed toward the centre).

Worked Example 7.2 — Speed changing on curved path

A particle moves along a curve with radius of curvature $\rho = 100$ m at a certain point. At this point, $v = 15$ m/s and the speed is increasing at $2$ m/s². Find the magnitude of total acceleration.

Given: $a_t = 2$ m/s², compute $a_n$

$$a_n = \frac{v^2}{\rho} = \frac{225}{100} = 2.25 \text{ m/s}^2$$

Total acceleration (perpendicular components)

$$|\vec{a}| = \sqrt{a_t^2 + a_n^2} = \sqrt{4 + 5.06} = 3.01 \text{ m/s}^2$$

Worked Example 7.3 — Find radius of curvature

A particle follows the path $y = \frac{x^2}{20}$ (parabola, $x$ and $y$ in metres). At the point $(10, 5)$, find the radius of curvature.

Compute derivatives

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{x}{10} \implies \text{at } x=10: \frac{dy}{dx} = 1$$

$$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{1}{10}$$

Apply formula

$$\rho = \frac{[1 + 1^2]^{3/2}}{|1/10|} = \frac{2^{3/2}}{0.1} = \frac{2.828}{0.1} = 28.3 \text{ m}$$

Practice Problems
  1. A car enters a curve at 25 m/s and exits at 30 m/s. The curve has radius 80 m and takes 4 s to traverse. Find: (a) tangential acceleration; (b) normal acceleration at entry; (c) total acceleration at entry.
  2. A particle moves on the path $y = \sin x$ (x in radians, y in metres). At $x = \pi/4$, the speed is 5 m/s and constant. Find the normal acceleration.
▶ Show Answers

1a: $a_t = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{30-25}{4} = 1.25$ m/s².

1b: At entry: $a_n = \frac{25^2}{80} = 7.81$ m/s².

1c: $|\vec{a}| = \sqrt{1.25^2 + 7.81^2} = 7.91$ m/s².

2: $\frac{dy}{dx} = \cos x$, $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = -\sin x$. At $x = \pi/4$: $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$, $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$. $\rho = \frac{[1 + 1/2]^{3/2}}{1/\sqrt{2}} = \frac{1.837}{0.707} = 2.60$ m. $a_n = \frac{25}{2.60} = 9.62$ m/s².

Topic 8
Newton's Laws & Free-Body Diagrams
forces · mass · F = ma · equilibrium · dynamics

Newton's Three Laws

First Law (Inertia)

A body at rest stays at rest, and a body in motion continues at constant velocity, unless acted upon by a net external force.

Second Law (F = ma)

The net force on a body equals its mass times its acceleration.

$$\sum \vec{F} = m\vec{a}$$

Third Law (Action-Reaction)

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. If body A exerts force $\vec{F}$ on body B, then B exerts $-\vec{F}$ on A.

Free-Body Diagram (FBD)

A sketch showing:

  • The body of interest (isolated from its surroundings)
  • All external forces acting on it (drawn as arrows)
  • Coordinate axes (choose wisely to simplify algebra)
Common mistakes: Including forces the body exerts on other objects (action-reaction confusion). Forgetting friction, normal force, or weight. Drawing internal forces.

Steps to Solve Dynamics Problems

  1. Draw the FBD — isolate the body, show all forces
  2. Choose coordinate system — align one axis with acceleration if possible
  3. Write $\sum F_x = ma_x$ and $\sum F_y = ma_y$
  4. Solve for unknowns (forces, acceleration, or mass)
  5. Kinematics (if needed) to find velocity, position, time

Worked Example 8.1 — Block on frictionless surface

A 10 kg block on a frictionless horizontal surface is pushed by a 40 N horizontal force. Find the acceleration.

FBD: N upward, mg downward, F = 40 N horizontal
Vertical: ΣF_y = 0 (no vertical motion)

$$N - mg = 0 \implies N = 10(9.81) = 98.1 \text{ N}$$

Horizontal: ΣF_x = ma_x

$$40 = 10a \implies a = 4 \text{ m/s}^2$$

Worked Example 8.2 — Tension in a vertical rope (elevator)

A 5 kg object hangs from a rope. The rope accelerates upward at 2 m/s². Find the tension.

FBD: T upward, mg = 49.05 N downward
ΣF_y = ma_y (taking upward as positive)

$$T - mg = ma$$

$$T = m(g + a) = 5(9.81 + 2) = 5(11.81) = 59.05 \text{ N}$$

Worked Example 8.3 — Two blocks connected by rope

Two blocks, $m_1 = 3$ kg and $m_2 = 5$ kg, are connected by a massless rope on a frictionless horizontal surface. A 32 N force pulls $m_2$. Find: (a) acceleration of the system; (b) tension in the rope.

(a) System approach: total mass = 8 kg

$$\sum F = (m_1 + m_2)a \implies 32 = 8a \implies a = 4 \text{ m/s}^2$$

(b) FBD of m_1 alone

Only T acts horizontally on $m_1$:

$$T = m_1 a = 3(4) = 12 \text{ N}$$

  2. A 2 kg object hangs from a rope attached to the ceiling of an elevator. The elevator accelerates downward at 1.5 m/s². Find the tension.
  3. Three blocks (2 kg, 3 kg, 5 kg) are pushed by a 40 N horizontal force on a frictionless surface. Find: (a) system acceleration; (b) force between 2 kg and 3 kg blocks; (c) force between 3 kg and 5 kg blocks.

1b: Horizontal: $50\cos 30° = 20a \implies a = 43.3/20 = 2.17$ m/s².

2: $T - mg = m(-a) \implies T = m(g-a) = 2(9.81-1.5) = 16.62$ N.

Topic 9
Friction & Inclined Planes
static · kinetic · coefficient · incline decomposition

Friction Force

Friction opposes relative motion (or attempted motion) between surfaces in contact.

Static Friction (object not moving)

$$f_s \leq \mu_s N$$

$f_s$ adjusts to prevent motion, up to the maximum $\mu_s N$. If applied force exceeds $\mu_s N$, the object starts to move.

Kinetic Friction (object sliding)

$$f_k = \mu_k N$$

Constant magnitude, always opposes the direction of motion. Typically $\mu_k < \mu_s$ (easier to keep sliding than to start).

Direction: Friction always opposes motion (or impending motion). Draw it opposing the velocity (kinetic) or opposing the applied force (static).

Inclined Plane Decomposition

For a block on an incline at angle $\theta$:

  • Parallel to incline: $mg\sin\theta$ (down the slope)
  • Perpendicular to incline: $mg\cos\theta$ (into the surface)
  • Normal force: $N = mg\cos\theta$ (if no other vertical forces)
  • Friction: $f \leq \mu N = \mu mg\cos\theta$ (up the slope if moving/sliding down)

Critical Angle (just begins to slide)

At the verge of sliding (static friction at maximum):

$$\tan\theta_{\text{crit}} = \mu_s$$
Worked Example 9.1 — Static friction limit

A 15 kg box rests on a horizontal surface with $\mu_s = 0.4$, $\mu_k = 0.3$. A horizontal force F is applied. Find: (a) max F before it starts moving; (b) acceleration if F = 70 N.

(a) Max static friction

$$N = mg = 15(9.81) = 147.15 \text{ N}$$

$$f_{s,\text{max}} = \mu_s N = 0.4(147.15) = 58.86 \text{ N}$$

Box starts moving when $F > 58.86$ N.

(b) If F = 70 N (sliding, so kinetic friction applies)

$$f_k = \mu_k N = 0.3(147.15) = 44.15 \text{ N}$$

$$\sum F_x = ma \implies 70 - 44.15 = 15a \implies a = 1.72 \text{ m/s}^2$$

Worked Example 9.2 — Block on incline (no friction)

A 5 kg block rests on a frictionless incline at 25°. Find the acceleration down the slope.

FBD: N perpendicular, mg sin 25° down the slope

$$\sum F_{\parallel} = ma \implies mg\sin 25° = ma$$

$$a = g\sin 25° = 9.81(0.423) = 4.15 \text{ m/s}^2$$

Worked Example 9.3 — Block on incline with friction

A 10 kg block on a 30° incline has $\mu_k = 0.2$. Find the acceleration.

Perpendicular: N = mg cos 30°

$$N = 10(9.81)(0.866) = 84.95 \text{ N}$$

Parallel (taking down as positive)

$$mg\sin 30° - f_k = ma$$

$$10(9.81)(0.5) - 0.2(84.95) = 10a$$

$$49.05 - 16.99 = 10a \implies a = 3.21 \text{ m/s}^2$$

Practice Problems
  1. A 8 kg block on a horizontal surface has $\mu_s = 0.5$, $\mu_k = 0.35$. A horizontal force of 50 N is applied. Find: (a) Does it move? (b) If yes, what is the acceleration?
  2. A 12 kg block is on a 20° incline with $\mu_k = 0.15$. Find: (a) the friction force; (b) acceleration down the slope.
  3. Find the minimum coefficient of static friction needed to prevent a block from sliding down a 35° incline.
▶ Show Answers

1a: $N = 8(9.81) = 78.48$ N. $f_{s,\text{max}} = 0.5(78.48) = 39.24$ N. Since $50 > 39.24$, it moves.

1b: $f_k = 0.35(78.48) = 27.47$ N. $50 - 27.47 = 8a \implies a = 2.82$ m/s².

2a: $N = 12(9.81)\cos 20° = 110.6$ N. $f_k = 0.15(110.6) = 16.59$ N.

2b: $12(9.81)\sin 20° - 16.59 = 12a \implies 40.22 - 16.59 = 12a \implies a = 1.97$ m/s².

Topic 10
Connected Bodies & Pulleys
constraint equations · Atwood machines · systems with multiple forces

Strategy for Connected Bodies

  1. Draw separate FBDs for each object
  2. Identify constraint — how are the motions related? (e.g., rope connects them → same |a|)
  3. Choose direction for each body (usually align with their motion)
  4. Write $\sum F = ma$ for each body
  5. Solve the system of equations
Tension is the same throughout a massless, inextensible rope. Tension pulls equally on both ends. If a rope passes over a massless, frictionless pulley, tension is the same on both sides.

Atwood Machine

Two masses connected by a rope over a pulley. Let $m_1 > m_2$. $m_1$ accelerates down, $m_2$ accelerates up.

$$a = \frac{(m_1 - m_2)g}{m_1 + m_2} \qquad T = \frac{2m_1 m_2 g}{m_1 + m_2}$$

Common Constraint: Rope Length

If two blocks are connected by a rope of fixed length, the sum of their displacements along the rope is constant. This often means $a_1 = a_2$ (same magnitude, possibly opposite directions).

Worked Example 10.1 — Atwood machine

Masses $m_1 = 7$ kg and $m_2 = 3$ kg hang from a massless rope over a frictionless pulley. Find: (a) acceleration; (b) tension.

FBD for m_1 (taking down as positive)

$$m_1 g - T = m_1 a$$

FBD for m_2 (taking up as positive)

$$T - m_2 g = m_2 a$$

Add equations to eliminate T

$$m_1 g - m_2 g = (m_1 + m_2)a$$

$$a = \frac{(7-3)(9.81)}{10} = 3.92 \text{ m/s}^2$$

Find T from either equation

$$T = m_2(g + a) = 3(9.81 + 3.92) = 41.19 \text{ N}$$

Worked Example 10.2 — Two blocks, one on table

Block A (5 kg) on a frictionless table is connected by a rope over a pulley to hanging block B (3 kg). Find: (a) acceleration; (b) tension.

FBD for A (horizontal)

$$T = m_A a$$

FBD for B (vertical, down positive)

$$m_B g - T = m_B a$$

Substitute T from first into second

$$m_B g - m_A a = m_B a$$

$$m_B g = (m_A + m_B)a \implies a = \frac{3(9.81)}{8} = 3.68 \text{ m/s}^2$$

Tension

$$T = 5(3.68) = 18.4 \text{ N}$$

Worked Example 10.3 — Incline + hanging mass

Block A (4 kg) on a frictionless 30° incline is connected by a rope to hanging block B (6 kg). Find the acceleration and tension.

FBD for A (parallel to incline, up positive)

$$T - m_A g\sin 30° = m_A a$$

FBD for B (down positive)

$$m_B g - T = m_B a$$

Add equations

$$m_B g - m_A g\sin 30° = (m_A + m_B)a$$

$$6(9.81) - 4(9.81)(0.5) = 10a$$

$$58.86 - 19.62 = 10a \implies a = 3.92 \text{ m/s}^2$$

Tension

$$T = m_A(a + g\sin 30°) = 4(3.92 + 4.905) = 35.3 \text{ N}$$

Practice Problems
  1. Atwood machine with $m_1 = 10$ kg, $m_2 = 6$ kg. Find: (a) acceleration; (b) tension; (c) time for $m_1$ to descend 2 m from rest.
  2. Block A (8 kg) on a table connected to hanging block B (4 kg). Table has friction $\mu_k = 0.25$. Find: (a) acceleration; (b) tension.
  3. Two blocks on a frictionless incline (20°) connected by a rope: $m_1 = 5$ kg higher up, $m_2 = 3$ kg lower. Find the acceleration (which way does the system move?).
▶ Show Answers

1a: $a = \frac{(10-6)(9.81)}{16} = 2.45$ m/s².

1b: $T = 6(9.81 + 2.45) = 73.56$ N.

1c: $s = \frac{1}{2}at^2 \implies 2 = 0.5(2.45)t^2 \implies t = 1.28$ s.

2a: $N = 8(9.81) = 78.48$ N, $f_k = 0.25(78.48) = 19.62$ N. $m_B g - T = m_B a$, $T - f_k = m_A a$. Add: $4(9.81) - 19.62 = 12a \implies a = 1.63$ m/s².

2b: $T = 8(1.63) + 19.62 = 32.66$ N.

3: Both experience $mg\sin 20°$ down the slope. Since $m_1 > m_2$, $m_1$ slides down. Net force = $(m_1 - m_2)g\sin 20° = 2(9.81)(0.342) = 6.71$ N. $a = 6.71/8 = 0.84$ m/s² down the slope.
