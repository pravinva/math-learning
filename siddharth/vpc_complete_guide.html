Subtopic 1
Polynomial Foundations
From factor structure to multiplicity-driven graph behaviour
Most later mistakes in Vieta and complex sections are not "hard math" mistakes; they start with weak structure reading. This section trains interpretation before heavy algebra.
ElucidationWhy multiplicity controls touching vs crossing▶

Near a root \(x=r\), a factor \((x-r)^m\) dominates local behavior. If \(m\) is even, \((x-r)^m\ge 0\) on both sides of \(r\), so sign does not flip and the graph touches then turns. If \(m\) is odd, \((x-r)^m\) changes sign across \(r\), so the graph must cross.

Think process: before graphing, ask "does sign flip at this root?" That single question gives crossing behavior instantly.
DefinitionStructure vocabulary that drives all later methods▶

For \(P(x)=a_nx^n+\cdots+a_0\), you need to identify degree, leading coefficient, root multiplicity, and real versus complex roots.

ObjectWhat it controlsFast interpretation
Degree \(n\)Maximum number of rootsTotal roots counting multiplicity is exactly \(n\)
Leading coefficientEnd directionCombined with degree parity gives tail directions
MultiplicityTouch/cross behaviourOdd crosses, even touches/bounces
Real rootx-interceptVisible on real graph
Complex rootNo real interceptExists algebraically, not on x-axis
PatternDecode a factored polynomial in 20 seconds▶
1
Count multiplicities for degree.
2
Multiply highest-power parts for leading term sign/parity.
3
List roots with multiplicity tags (simple/double/triple).
4
Predict touch/cross at each real root.
5
Estimate intervals of sign with one-point testing if needed.
ExampleThree deep worked reads▶
Example 1 - Read \(f(x)=-2(x+3)^2(x-1)^3\)
1
Degree \(=2+3=5\). Leading term \(-2x^5\).
2
Odd degree + negative lead: left tail up, right tail down.
3
Root \(x=-3\) multiplicity 2 (touch), root \(x=1\) multiplicity 3 (cross with flattening).
KEY Structural reading predicts graph before expansion.
Example 2 - Build monic polynomial with roots \(2,-1,-1,4\)
\[P(x)=(x-2)(x+1)^2(x-4)\]
1
Duplicate root means squared factor.
2
Total multiplicity \(1+2+1=4\), so quartic as required.
NoteHigh-frequency structural mistakes▶
Mistake 1: forgetting multiplicity when counting degree and roots.
Mistake 2: assuming every algebraic root appears as x-intercept (false for complex roots).
Subtopic 2
Factor + Rational Root Strategy
Move from random guessing to deterministic root search
Rational root theorem does not solve by itself; it narrows the search. Factor theorem confirms. This pair is your entry point to cubic and quartic solving.
ElucidationWhy \(\pm p/q\) are the only rational candidates▶

Suppose \(\frac{p}{q}\) (in lowest terms) is a root of \(a_nx^n+\cdots+a_0=0\). Substituting and clearing denominators shows \(p\) must divide \(a_0\), and \(q\) must divide \(a_n\). So the candidate set is finite and forced by coefficient divisibility.

Method choice rule: use rational-root theorem only when coefficients are integers and you are solving for exact roots.
DefinitionRemainder theorem, factor theorem, candidate roots▶

When dividing by \((x-a)\), remainder equals \(P(a)\). Hence:

\[(x-a)\text{ is a factor } \Longleftrightarrow P(a)=0\]

For integer-coefficient polynomial \(a_nx^n+\cdots+a_0\), rational roots are among \(\pm\frac{p}{q}\), where \(p\mid a_0\), \(q\mid a_n\).

PatternEfficient candidate testing order▶
1
List candidate set from rational theorem.
2
Try small integers first: \(\pm1,\pm2,\pm3\).
3
Stop immediately at first root; deflate before further testing.
ExampleRoot-testing with discussion▶
Example - Solve \(2x^3-3x^2-8x+12=0\)
1
Candidates \(\pm1,\pm2,\pm3,\pm4,\pm\frac12,\pm\frac32,\pm6,\pm12\).
2
Try \(x=2\): \(16-12-16+12=0\). Root found quickly.
3
Deflate (next subtopic) to get quadratic \(2x^2+x-6\), then roots \(\frac32,-2\).
ANSWER \(x=2,\frac32,-2\)
Subtopic 3
Deflation and Complete Solving Flow
From one known root to all roots with minimal overhead
Exam speed comes from reducing degree fast. Deflation is the bridge between root detection and full solution.
ElucidationWhy deflation is the optimal next step▶

Once one root is confirmed, continuing to test more candidates on the original polynomial wastes effort. Deflation removes one solved factor and lowers degree, reducing arithmetic and exposing easier structure (often quadratic).

1
Confirm root exactly (\(P(r)=0\)).
2
Deflate immediately to quotient \(Q(x)\).
3
Solve \(Q(x)=0\) with the simplest method available.
DefinitionDeflation by synthetic division▶

If \(r\) is root of \(P(x)\), then \(P(x)=(x-r)Q(x)\). Finding \(Q\) is deflation.

ExampleComplete cubic solve with verification▶
Example - \(x^3-6x^2+11x-6=0\)
1
Test \(x=1\): root.
2
Deflate by \(x-1\) to \(x^2-5x+6\).
3
Factor quadratic: \((x-2)(x-3)\).
4
Back-check all roots in original polynomial.
ANSWER \(x=1,2,3\)
Subtopic 4
Vieta Deep Dive
Forward use, reverse construction, and sanity checks
Vieta is not just "sum and product"; it is a full coefficient-root consistency system. Mastering it reduces solving workload dramatically.
ElucidationWhere Vieta formulas come from (not magic)▶

Start from factor form \(a(x-r_1)(x-r_2)\cdots(x-r_n)\). Expanding produces coefficient terms that are symmetric sums of roots. Comparing this expansion with standard coefficient form gives Vieta directly.

Sign memory anchor: coefficients alternate signs in expansion, so Vieta signs alternate too.
DefinitionQuadratic, cubic, and quartic relations▶
PolynomialRoot relation
\(ax^2+bx+c\)\(r_1+r_2=-\frac ba,\; r_1r_2=\frac ca\)
\(ax^3+bx^2+cx+d\)\(r_1+r_2+r_3=-\frac ba,\; \sum r_ir_j=\frac ca,\; r_1r_2r_3=-\frac da\)
Monic quartic \(x^4+Ax^3+Bx^2+Cx+D\)\(\sum r_i=-A,\; \sum r_ir_j=B,\; \sum r_ir_jr_k=-C,\; \prod r_i=D\)
PatternReverse-build from root summaries▶
1
Write monic target first unless scale specified.
2
Place alternating signs carefully; this is the most common error source.
3
Run a quick sanity check with one synthetic relation (for example, product).
ExampleCubic and quartic reverse construction▶
Example 1 - Cubic with \(\sum r_i=2,\sum r_ir_j=-5,\prod r_i=6\)
\[x^3-2x^2-5x-6=0\]
CHECK Product term \(-(-6)=6\) matches target.
Example 2 - Quartic roots \(1,2,-1,3\)

Symmetric sums give coefficients \(-5,5,5,-6\).

\[x^4-5x^3+5x^2+5x-6\]
Subtopic 5
Sign Charts and Graph Behaviour
Convert factors into interval signs and sketch logic
This section supports inequality questions and checks whether your root set is physically consistent with a graph description.
ElucidationWhy one test point per interval is enough▶

Between consecutive real roots, polynomial factors never pass through zero, so each factor keeps fixed sign on that interval. Product sign is therefore constant on the entire interval. One sample point determines the whole interval sign.

ExampleFactor-interval method▶
Solve \((x-3)(x+1)^2 \le 0\)
1
Critical points: \(-1\) (double), \(3\) (simple).
2
\((x+1)^2\) non-negative, so sign follows \((x-3)\) except zero at \(-1\).
3
Expression is \(\le0\) on \((-\infty,3]\).
ANSWER \(x\le3\)
Subtopic 6
Complex Number Algebra
Foundations in \(a+bi\), conjugates, and equation systems
If arithmetic is shaky here, polar and De Moivre become noise. Build clean habits now: standard form, conjugate normalization, and real/imag separation.
ElucidationWhy conjugates eliminate imaginary denominators▶

\((a+bi)(a-bi)=a^2+b^2\) because cross imaginary terms cancel: \(abi-abi=0\). That cancellation is exactly why multiplying by conjugate rationalizes the denominator.

Self-check: final denominator after rationalization must be real. If not, you missed a sign.
DefinitionCore operations and conjugates▶

\((a+bi)(a-bi)=a^2+b^2\) is the key identity for rationalizing complex denominators.

ExampleDivision and system solve▶
Example 1 - \(\frac{2+3i}{1-2i}\)
\[\frac{2+3i}{1-2i}\cdot\frac{1+2i}{1+2i}=\frac{-4+7i}{5}=-\frac45+\frac75i\]
Example 2 - Solve \((x+yi)(1+i)=3+5i\)
1
\((x-y)+(x+y)i=3+5i\)
2
Real: \(x-y=3\), Imag: \(x+y=5\)
3
Solve: \(x=4,y=1\)
Subtopic 7
Modulus, Argument, Argand Geometry
Geometric language for complex numbers
This is where many students lose marks by using inverse tangent without quadrant correction. You need a deterministic argument procedure.
ElucidationWhy inverse tangent alone is insufficient▶

\(\tan\theta\) has period \(\pi\), so the same tangent value occurs in opposite quadrants. \(\tan^{-1}(b/a)\) only gives a reference family, not the unique geometric direction. Signs of \(a\) and \(b\) must select the correct quadrant.

PatternQuadrant-safe principal argument workflow▶
1
Compute reference angle \(\theta_r=\tan^{-1}\left|\frac ba\right|\).
2
Use sign of \((a,b)\) to place in Q1/Q2/Q3/Q4.
3
Convert to principal interval required by your course.
ExampleArgument correction example▶
For \(z=-2-2i\): find \(|z|\) and principal argument
1
\(|z|=\sqrt{(-2)^2+(-2)^2}=2\sqrt2\)
2
Reference angle \(=\pi/4\), point in Q3.
3
Principal argument in \((-\pi,\pi]\): \(-3\pi/4\).
Subtopic 8
Polar Form and De Moivre
Powering and multiplying complex numbers with angle arithmetic
Polar form compresses repeated multiplications into one line. This section is the bridge to roots of unity and hard argument questions.
ElucidationWhy multiplication becomes add-angles, multiply-moduli▶

Polar form rewrites complex multiplication using trigonometric angle-addition identities. The geometry view: multiplying by \(r\text{cis}\theta\) scales lengths by \(r\) and rotates by \(\theta\). Repeating this \(n\) times yields De Moivre naturally.

DefinitionPolar representation and De Moivre identity▶
\[z=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)=r\,\text{cis}\,\theta,\qquad (r\,\text{cis}\,\theta)^n=r^n\text{cis}(n\theta)\]
ExamplePowering and angle reduction▶
\((1+i)^8\)
1
\(1+i=\sqrt2\,\text{cis}(\pi/4)\)
2
\((\sqrt2)^8\text{cis}(2\pi)=16\)
ANSWER \(16\)
Subtopic 9
n-th Roots and Roots of Unity
All roots, evenly spaced geometry, and periodicity
This subtopic often appears as a high-yield concept question. The key is indexing roots correctly with \(k=0,\dots,n-1\).
ElucidationWhy roots are equally spaced on a circle▶

Adding \(2\pi\) to an argument leaves the same complex number. Dividing \(\theta+2k\pi\) by \(n\) creates angles separated by \(2\pi/n\), so the roots are vertices of a regular \(n\)-gon on radius \(r^{1/n}\).

DefinitionGeneral n-th root formula▶
\[z_k=r^{1/n}\text{cis}\left(\frac{\theta+2k\pi}{n}\right),\quad k=0,\dots,n-1\]

For unity roots (\(r=1,\theta=0\)), points are equally spaced every \(2\pi/n\).

ExampleSolve \(z^4=-16\)▶
1
\(-16=16\,\text{cis}(\pi+2m\pi)\)
2
Modulus roots: \(16^{1/4}=2\)
3
Angles: \((\pi+2k\pi)/4=\pi/4+k\pi/2\), \(k=0,1,2,3\)
ROOTS \(2\,\text{cis}(\pi/4),2\,\text{cis}(3\pi/4),2\,\text{cis}(5\pi/4),2\,\text{cis}(7\pi/4)\)
Subtopic 10
Conjugate Roots and Complex Locus
Bridge between polynomial roots and Argand geometry constraints
This is where polynomial and complex geometry merge: conjugate pairs in real-coefficient polynomials, and shape interpretation of modulus/argument equations.
ElucidationWhy conjugate pairs must occur for real coefficients▶

If \(P(x)\) has real coefficients, then conjugating \(P(a+bi)=0\) gives \(P(a-bi)=0\) because conjugation passes through sums/products and leaves real coefficients unchanged. So non-real roots arrive in pairs.

DefinitionConjugate root theorem + basic loci▶

If coefficients are real and \(a+bi\) is a root, then \(a-bi\) is also a root.

Complex conditionGeometric object
\(|z-z_0|=r\)Circle center \(z_0\), radius \(r\)
\(\arg(z-z_0)=\alpha\)Ray from \(z_0\) at angle \(\alpha\)
ExampleRecover polynomial from one complex root▶
Monic cubic with roots \(3\) and \(1-2i\)
1
Conjugate root \(1+2i\) must also be present.
2
Complex pair gives factor \(x^2-2x+5\).
3
Multiply by \((x-3)\): \(x^3-5x^2+11x-15\).
