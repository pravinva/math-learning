Week 6A · Topic 1
Solving Exponential Equations
Learn how to solve equations where the unknown is the index by taking logarithms of both sides.
DEFINITION Exponential Equations

To solve equations where the unknown is the index, take the log of both sides.

If \( a^x = c \) then \( \log_a a^x = \log_a c \), so \( x = \log_a c \)
If \( e^x = c \) then \( \ln e^x = \ln c \), so \( x = \ln c \)

Remember: \( \log_e \) is the same as \( \ln \) (natural logarithm)

PATTERN Solution Steps

Step 1: Ensure the only term on the LHS is the exponential (isolate \( e^x \) or \( a^x \))

Step 2: Take the log to base \( e \) (or appropriate base) of both sides

Step 3: Simplify using \( \log_e e^x = x \) or \( \ln e^x = x \)

Step 4: Solve for \( x \)

EXAMPLE Solve \( 1 + e^x = 9 \)

Step 1: Isolate the exponential

\[ e^x = 8 \]

Step 2: Take ln of both sides

\[ \ln e^x = \ln 8 \]

Step 3: Simplify

\[ x = \ln 8 \]

This is the exact form. For a decimal approximation: \( x \approx 2.079 \)

EXAMPLE Solve \( 3e^{2x} = 48 \) in exact form

Step 1: Ensure coefficient of exponential is 1

\[ e^{2x} = 16 \]

Step 2: Take ln of both sides

\[ \ln(e^{2x}) = \ln 16 \]

Step 3: Simplify

\[ 2x = \ln 16 \]

Step 4: Solve for x

\[ x = \frac{\ln 16}{2} = \frac{\ln 2^4}{2} = \frac{4\ln 2}{2} = 2\ln 2 \]

NOTE Exact vs Approximate Forms

Exact form: Leave answer as a logarithm (e.g., \( x = \ln 8 \) or \( x = 2\ln 2 \))

Approximate form: Calculate decimal value (e.g., \( x \approx 2.079 \))

Always provide the form requested in the question!

Week 6A · Topic 2
Graphs of Exponential Functions
Understand exponential functions \( y = e^x \) and \( y = a^x \), their properties, transformations, and how to sketch them.
DEFINITION Exponential Functions

An exponential function is of the form \( f(x) = a^x \) where the base \( a \) is a positive real number other than 1, and the variable is the power/exponent.

Examples: \( f(x) = 2^x \), \( f(x) = 3^x \), \( f(x) = 10^x \), \( f(x) = e^x \)

Note: \( e \approx 2.71828 \) is a special irrational number called Euler's number.

Power function vs Exponential function:

  • Power function: \( f(x) = x^3 \) (base is variable)
  • Exponential function: \( f(x) = 3^x \) (exponent is variable)
PATTERN Properties of \( y = a^x \) (when \( a > 1 \))
  • The graph passes through the point \( (0, 1) \) since \( a^0 = 1 \)
  • As \( x \to \infty \), \( y \to \infty \) (increases rapidly)
  • As \( x \to -\infty \), \( y \to 0 \) (approaches but never reaches zero)
  • Horizontal asymptote: \( y = 0 \)
  • Domain: \( x \in \mathbb{R} \) (all real numbers)
  • Range: \( y > 0 \) or \( (0, \infty) \)
  • The graph is always positive (never negative or zero)
  • Concave up shape
EXAMPLE Graph of \( y = e^x \)

The graph of \( y = e^x \) is very similar to all exponential graphs of the form \( y = a^x \) where \( a > 1 \).

Key points:

  • Passes through \( (0, 1) \)
  • When \( x = 1 \), \( y = e \approx 2.72 \)
  • When \( x = 2 \), \( y = e^2 \approx 7.39 \)
  • When \( x = -1 \), \( y = e^{-1} \approx 0.368 \)
  • When \( x = -2 \), \( y = e^{-2} \approx 0.135 \)
PATTERN Vertical Translations: \( y = e^x + c \)

Adding a constant \( c \) shifts the graph vertically:

  • \( y = e^x + 2 \): shift UP by 2 units
  • \( y = e^x - 4 \): shift DOWN by 4 units

Effect on properties:

  • Horizontal asymptote becomes \( y = c \)
  • Domain: \( x \in \mathbb{R} \) (unchanged)
  • Range: \( y > c \)

The vertical translation affects the range but NOT the domain.

PATTERN Horizontal Translations: \( y = e^{x+d} \)

Adding a constant to the exponent shifts the graph horizontally (opposite direction to the sign):

  • \( y = e^{x+3} \): shift LEFT by 3 units
  • \( y = e^{x-1} \): shift RIGHT by 1 unit

Effect on properties:

  • Horizontal asymptote: \( y = 0 \) (unchanged)
  • Domain: \( x \in \mathbb{R} \) (unchanged)
  • Range: \( y > 0 \) (unchanged)

The horizontal translation does NOT affect the domain or range.

PATTERN Reflections

Reflection in y-axis: \( y = e^{-x} \)

  • Flips the graph horizontally
  • Now decreasing instead of increasing
  • Still passes through \( (0, 1) \)

Reflection in x-axis: \( y = -e^x \)

  • Flips the graph vertically
  • Graph is now below x-axis (always negative)
  • Horizontal asymptote becomes \( y = 0 \) (from below)
  • Range becomes \( y < 0 \)
NOTE Sketching Exponential Graphs

To sketch an exponential graph, identify:

  1. Domain and range
  2. Horizontal asymptote (usually \( y = c \) for vertical shifts)
  3. y-intercept (let \( x = 0 \))
  4. x-intercept if it exists (let \( y = 0 \) and solve)
  5. General shape (increasing/decreasing, concave up/down)
  6. Check a point to verify your sketch

Week 6A · Topic 3
Graphs of Logarithmic Functions
Explore logarithmic functions \( y = \log_e(x) \) and their transformations. Understand the inverse relationship with exponential functions.
DEFINITION Logarithmic Functions

The logarithmic function is the inverse of the exponential function.

A logarithmic function is of the form \( f(x) = \log_b x \) where the base \( b \) is a positive real number other than 1.

Examples: \( f(x) = \log_2 x \), \( f(x) = \log_{10} x \), \( f(x) = \log_e x \) (or \( \ln x \))

Remember: A logarithm is a power!

\( \log_b x = y \) means \( b^y = x \)

PATTERN Properties of \( y = \log_b x \)
  • The graph passes through the point \( (1, 0) \) since \( \log_b 1 = 0 \)
  • x-intercept = 1
  • Domain: \( x > 0 \) (cannot take log of negative numbers or zero!)
  • Range: \( y \in \mathbb{R} \) (all real numbers)
  • Vertical asymptote: \( x = 0 \) (the y-axis)
  • Concave down shape
  • As \( x \to 0^+ \), \( y \to -\infty \)
  • As \( x \to \infty \), \( y \to \infty \) (but slowly)
NOTE Important Reminder

A logarithm (which is a power) may be positive, negative, or zero.

We CANNOT find the logarithm of negative numbers or zero!

Examples:

  • \( \log_e 10 > 0 \) (positive)
  • \( \log_e 1 = 0 \) (zero)
  • \( \log_e 0.5 < 0 \) (negative)
  • \( \log_e(-5) \) is UNDEFINED
PATTERN Inverse Relationship: \( y = e^x \) and \( y = \log_e x \)

The graphs of \( y = e^x \) and \( y = \log_e x \) are reflections of each other through the line \( y = x \).

Comparison:

Property \( y = e^x \) \( y = \log_e x \)
Domain \( x \in \mathbb{R} \) \( x > 0 \)
Range \( y > 0 \) \( y \in \mathbb{R} \)
Asymptote Horizontal: \( y = 0 \) Vertical: \( x = 0 \)
Intercept y-intercept at (0,1) x-intercept at (1,0)
PATTERN Vertical Translations: \( y = \log_e(x) + c \)

Adding a constant \( c \) shifts the graph vertically:

  • \( y = \log_e(x) + 2 \): shift UP by 2 units
  • \( y = \log_e(x) - 3 \): shift DOWN by 3 units

Effect on properties:

  • Vertical asymptote: \( x = 0 \) (unchanged)
  • Domain: \( x > 0 \) (unchanged)
  • Range: \( y \in \mathbb{R} \) (unchanged)

The vertical translation does NOT affect the domain or range of logarithmic functions.

PATTERN Horizontal Translations: \( y = \log_e(x + d) \)

Adding a constant inside the logarithm shifts the graph horizontally (opposite direction to the sign):

  • \( y = \log_e(x + 3) \): shift LEFT by 3 units
  • \( y = \log_e(x - 5) \): shift RIGHT by 5 units

Effect on properties:

  • Vertical asymptote shifts to \( x = -d \)
  • Domain changes to \( x > -d \) (or \( x + d > 0 \))
  • Range: \( y \in \mathbb{R} \) (unchanged)

The horizontal translation affects the domain and asymptote but NOT the range.

Examples:

  • \( y = \log_e(x - 1) \): Domain is \( x > 1 \), asymptote at \( x = 1 \)
  • \( y = \log_e(x + 3) \): Domain is \( x > -3 \), asymptote at \( x = -3 \)
PATTERN Reflections

Reflection in y-axis: \( y = \log_e(-x) \)

  • Domain becomes \( x < 0 \)
  • Vertical asymptote at \( x = 0 \) (from left)
  • x-intercept at \( (-1, 0) \)

Reflection in x-axis: \( y = -\log_e(x) \)

  • Flips the graph vertically
  • x-intercept still at \( (1, 0) \)
  • Domain and vertical asymptote unchanged
NOTE Sketching Logarithmic Graphs

To sketch a logarithmic graph, identify:

  1. Domain (solve \( x + d > 0 \) for horizontal shifts)
  2. Range (usually all real numbers)
  3. Vertical asymptote (from domain restriction)
  4. x-intercept (let \( y = 0 \) and solve)
  5. General shape (concave down, increasing slowly)
  6. Check a point to verify your sketch
