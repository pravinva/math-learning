TOPIC 4.1
Line Forms and Conversions
Master every line form, when to use each, and how to convert quickly between them in exam conditions.
DEFINITION Core Linear Forms

Useful forms of a line:

  • Slope-intercept: \(y=mx+c\)
  • Point-slope: \(y-y_1=m(x-x_1)\)
  • Two-point: \(y-y_1=\frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}(x-x_1)\)
  • General: \(Ax+By+C=0\)
Same line, different form. Choose the one that matches the given data.
PATTERN Conversion Workflow

To get slope: isolate \(y\) and read coefficient of \(x\).

To get intercepts from general form: set \(y=0\) for x-intercept, set \(x=0\) for y-intercept.

To avoid algebra slips: calculate slope once, then substitute point.

EXAMPLE Convert \(2x-3y+9=0\) into slope-intercept form

\(2x-3y+9=0\Rightarrow -3y=-2x-9\Rightarrow y=\frac{2}{3}x+3\).

So gradient \(m=\frac{2}{3}\), y-intercept \(c=3\).

TOPIC 4.2
Parallel and Perpendicular Slopes
Use slope rules to classify line relationships and construct required equations from geometric constraints.
PATTERNKey slope rules
  • Parallel lines: \(m_1=m_2\).
  • Perpendicular lines: \(m_1m_2=-1\) (negative reciprocals).
  • Horizontal line slope \(0\), vertical line slope undefined.
EXAMPLEPerpendicular through point

Given \(y=2x+1\), perpendicular slope is \(-\frac12\).

Through \((4,-3)\): \(y+3=-\frac12(x-4)\Rightarrow y=-\frac12x-1\).

Second: \(y=-\frac12x+\frac52\). Product \(=\frac12(-\frac12)=-\frac14\neq-1\). Not perpendicular.

Line through \((1,2)\) is perpendicular to line through \((0,0)\), \((4,1)\). Find equation.

Both are same line: \(y=7\).

TOPIC 4.3
Distance, Midpoint and Section Formula
Measurement tools of coordinate geometry: length, center point, and ratio division on a line segment.
PATTERNWhat are we finding? What technique to use?

1) Distance

What are we finding? The straight-line length between two points.

Technique: subtract x-values, subtract y-values, square both, add, then square root.

\(\displaystyle d=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}\)

2) Midpoint

What are we finding? The exact center point of the segment joining two points.

Technique: average the x-values, and average the y-values.

\(\displaystyle M\left(\frac{x_1+x_2}{2},\frac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)\)

3) Section (internal ratio \(m:n\))

What are we finding? A point that splits the segment in a given ratio.

Technique: use weighted average of coordinates using the given ratio \(m:n\).

\(\displaystyle P\left(\frac{mx_2+nx_1}{m+n},\frac{my_2+ny_1}{m+n}\right)\)
NOTEInternal ratio \(m:n\) — what the question means

Problem in words: You know two endpoints \(A\) and \(B\). You need the coordinates of a point \(P\) between them.

The ratio \(m:n\) means \(AP\) has \(m\) parts and \(PB\) has \(n\) parts.

So if ratio is \(1:4\), point \(P\) is much closer to \(A\) than to \(B\).

Use this once: \[ P\left(\frac{mx_2+nx_1}{m+n},\frac{my_2+ny_1}{m+n}\right) \]

How to avoid confusion: coefficient of \(B\)-coordinates is \(m\), coefficient of \(A\)-coordinates is \(n\). This is the standard internal-division setup.

  • If ratio is \(1:1\), answer must be midpoint.
  • If first number is smaller (like \(1:4\)), answer should be closer to first point \(A\).
EXAMPLEOne clear solved example (step-by-step)

Question: \(A(2,3)\), \(B(8,15)\). Point \(P\) divides \(AB\) in ratio \(1:2\). Find \(P\).

Step 1 (identify values): \(x_1=2,\ y_1=3,\ x_2=8,\ y_2=15,\ m=1,\ n=2\).

Step 2 (substitute):

\[ P=\left(\frac{1\cdot8+2\cdot2}{1+2},\frac{1\cdot15+2\cdot3}{1+2}\right) \]

Step 3 (simplify): \(P=(4,7)\).

Quick sense check: ratio is \(1:2\), so \(P\) should be closer to \(A(2,3)\). \((4,7)\) is indeed closer to \(A\) than to \(B\).

TOPIC 4.4
General Equation, Intercepts, and Geometry Meaning
Understand how coefficients in \(Ax+By+C=0\) control slope, intercepts, and line orientation.
DEFINITIONGeneral form interpretation

For \(Ax+By+C=0\) (\(B\neq0\)), slope \(m=-A/B\), y-intercept \(-C/B\), x-intercept \(-C/A\) (\(A\neq0\)).

\(\displaystyle y=-\frac{A}{B}x-\frac{C}{B}\)
NOTESpecial cases
  • \(B=0\Rightarrow x=\text{constant}\) (vertical line).
  • \(A=0\Rightarrow y=\text{constant}\) (horizontal line).
EXAMPLEWorked exam-style set: Q21 to Q23 (from your sheet)

Q21. Line through \((1,2)\), \((-1,3)\):

\[ m=\frac{3-2}{-1-1}=-\frac12. \]

Use point-slope with \((1,2)\):

\[ y-2=-\frac12(x-1)\Rightarrow 2y-4=-x+1\Rightarrow x+2y-5=0. \]

Intercepts: x-intercept (\(y=0\)) gives \(x=5\Rightarrow (5,0)\). y-intercept (\(x=0\)) gives \(2y=5\Rightarrow (0,\frac52)\).

Q22(a). Parallel to \(3x-7y=0\), through \((2,3)\):

\[ 3x-7y=0\Rightarrow y=\frac37x\Rightarrow m=\frac37. \]

\[ y-3=\frac37(x-2)\Rightarrow 7y-21=3x-6\Rightarrow 3x-7y+15=0. \]

Q22(b). Perpendicular to \(4x+y+9=0\), through \((1,-4)\):

\[ 4x+y+9=0\Rightarrow y=-4x-9\Rightarrow m_1=-4. \]

Perpendicular slope \(m_2=\frac14\). Then

\[ y+4=\frac14(x-1)\Rightarrow 4y+16=x-1\Rightarrow x-4y-17=0. \]

Q22(c). Prove perpendicular:

Line through \((1,2),(-5,6)\): \[ m_a=\frac{6-2}{-5-1}=\frac4{-6}=-\frac23. \]

Line through \((-2,3),(2,9)\): \[ m_b=\frac{9-3}{2-(-2)}=\frac64=\frac32. \]

\[ m_a m_b = -\frac23\cdot\frac32=-1 \] so the lines are perpendicular.

Q23(a). Midpoint of \((4,5)\), \((-2,11)\):

\[ M=\left(\frac{4+(-2)}{2},\frac{5+11}{2}\right)=(1,8). \]

Q23(b). Point dividing segment from \((1,3)\) to \((9,-7)\) in ratio \(3:2\) (internally):

\[ P=\left(\frac{3(9)+2(1)}{3+2},\frac{3(-7)+2(3)}{3+2}\right) =\left(\frac{29}{5},-3\right). \]

TOPIC 4.7
Intersection and Simultaneous Equations
Move from line geometry into system solving: same intersection idea, exact algebraic methods.
DEFINITIONWhat a simultaneous solution means

A simultaneous solution is a coordinate pair that satisfies both equations at once. Graphically, this is an intersection point.

PATTERNMethod chooser
  • Substitution: easiest when one equation already gives \(y=\cdots\) or \(x=\cdots\).
  • Elimination: easiest when a variable can be cancelled quickly by adding/subtracting equations.
  • Linear + non-linear: usually use substitution, then solve resulting quadratic.
EXAMPLELinear-linear system

Solve \(y=2x-5\), \(2x+3y=-3\).

Substitute: \(2x+3(2x-5)=-3\Rightarrow 8x-15=-3\Rightarrow x=\frac{3}{2}\).

Then \(y=2(\frac{3}{2})-5=-2\). Intersection is \((1.5,-2)\).

EXAMPLELinear-nonlinear system

Solve \(x^2+y^2=16\) and \(x-y=4\).

From line: \(y=x-4\). Substitute: \(x^2+(x-4)^2=16\Rightarrow 2x^2-8x=0\Rightarrow 2x(x-4)=0\).

So \(x=0\) or \(x=4\). Hence points \((0,-4)\) and \((4,0)\).

TOPIC 4.8
Testing for a Function
A relation is a function only when each input has exactly one output. Learn fast tests and common traps.
DEFINITIONFunction vs relation

A relation is a set of input-output pairs. It is a function only if every x-value maps to one and only one y-value.

PATTERNVertical line test

If any vertical line cuts a graph at more than one point, that graph is not a function.

EXAMPLE\(y=x^2\) vs \(y^2=x\)

\(y=x^2\): each \(x\) gives one \(y\), so function.

\(y^2=x\): for \(x=4\), \(y=2\) and \(y=-2\), so not a function.

Practice Problems
1
Is \(y=3x-1\) a function? Explain.
HINT: One y for each x?
SOLUTION

Yes. Linear rules always assign one unique y to each x.

2
Is \(y^2=x\) a function of \(x\)?
HINT: Test \(x=9\).
SOLUTION

No. \(x=9\) gives \(y=3\) and \(-3\).

3
Is \(x^2+y^2=9\) a function of \(x\)?
HINT: Circle check at \(x=0\).
SOLUTION

No. At \(x=0\), \(y=3\) or \(-3\).

4
Is \(x=4\) a function of \(x\)?
HINT: Think vertical line graph.
SOLUTION

No. A vertical line fails the vertical line test (same x with many y-values).

5
Is \(y=\sqrt{x-2}\) a function? State domain too.
HINT: Root gives principal nonnegative y.
SOLUTION

Yes, function. Domain \(x\ge2\).

6
Given pairs \((1,2),(2,5),(1,4)\), is this a function?
HINT: Repeated input test.
SOLUTION

No. Input \(1\) has outputs \(2\) and \(4\).

7
Given pairs \((1,2),(2,2),(3,2)\), is this a function?
HINT: Many-to-one is allowed.
SOLUTION

Yes. Different inputs sharing same output is still a function.

8
Is \(y=\pm\sqrt{x}\) a function?
HINT: Plus/minus produces two outputs.
SOLUTION

No. For \(x=9\), outputs are \(3\) and \(-3\).

TOPIC 4.5
Angle of Inclination
Explore the relationship between the gradient of a line and the angle it makes with the positive x-axis.
DEFINITION Angle of Inclination

The angle of inclination of a line is the angle \( \theta \) measured anti-clockwise from the positive x-axis to the line.

The angle of inclination is always in the range \( 0° \leq \theta \leq 180° \).

\( 0° \leq \theta \leq 180° \)
PATTERN Gradient and Angle Relationship

The gradient \( m \) of a line is related to its angle of inclination \( \theta \) by the formula:

\( m = \tan \theta \)

This means:

  • If \( m > 0 \), then \( 0° < \theta < 90° \) (acute angle)
  • If \( m < 0 \), then \( 90° < \theta < 180° \) (obtuse angle)
  • If \( m = 0 \), then \( \theta = 0° \) (horizontal line)
  • If the line is vertical, then \( \theta = 90° \) (gradient undefined)
EXAMPLE Finding the Angle of Inclination

Question: Find the angle of inclination of the line passing through the points \( A(1, 2) \) and \( B(4, 8) \).

Solution:

Step 1: Find the gradient using \( m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \)

\( m = \frac{8 - 2}{4 - 1} = \frac{6}{3} = 2 \)

Step 2: Use \( m = \tan \theta \) to find \( \theta \)

\( 2 = \tan \theta \)

\( \theta = \tan^{-1}(2) \)

\( \theta \approx 63.4° \)

Answer: The angle of inclination is approximately \( 63.4° \).

NOTE Working with Obtuse Angles

When the gradient is negative, the angle of inclination is obtuse (between 90° and 180°).

For example, if \( m = -1 \), then:

\( \tan \theta = -1 \)

Your calculator will give \( \theta = -45° \), but we need \( 0° \leq \theta \leq 180° \).

So we add 180°: \( \theta = -45° + 180° = 135° \)

General rule: If your calculator gives a negative angle, add 180° to get the angle of inclination.

TOPIC 4.6
Collinear Points
Learn how to determine whether three or more points lie on the same straight line.
DEFINITION Collinear Points

Three or more points are said to be collinear if they all lie on the same straight line.

To test if points are collinear, we check whether the gradients between consecutive pairs of points are equal.

PATTERN Testing for Collinearity

For three points \( A \), \( B \), and \( C \) to be collinear:

\( m_{AB} = m_{BC} = m_{AC} \)

You only need to show that two of these gradients are equal.

For example, if \( m_{AB} = m_{BC} \), then \( A \), \( B \), and \( C \) are collinear.

Remember: Use the gradient formula \( m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \)

EXAMPLE Testing if Points are Collinear

Question: Determine whether the points \( A(1, 2) \), \( B(3, 6) \), and \( C(5, 10) \) are collinear.

Solution:

Step 1: Find the gradient from A to B

\( m_{AB} = \frac{6 - 2}{3 - 1} = \frac{4}{2} = 2 \)

Step 2: Find the gradient from B to C

\( m_{BC} = \frac{10 - 6}{5 - 3} = \frac{4}{2} = 2 \)

Step 3: Compare the gradients

Since \( m_{AB} = m_{BC} = 2 \), the points are collinear.

NOTE Finding Unknown Coordinates

You can use collinearity to find unknown coordinates.

Example: If \( A(2, 3) \), \( B(4, 7) \), and \( C(6, k) \) are collinear, find \( k \).

Solution:

Since the points are collinear: \( m_{AB} = m_{BC} \)

\( \frac{7 - 3}{4 - 2} = \frac{k - 7}{6 - 4} \)

\( \frac{4}{2} = \frac{k - 7}{2} \)

\( 2 = \frac{k - 7}{2} \)

\( 4 = k - 7 \)

\( k = 11 \)
