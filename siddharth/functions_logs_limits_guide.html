Topic 1
Graph Transformations
Moving, flipping, and stretching any graph using one framework
Once you know what one function looks like, you can work out what any transformed version of it looks like without recalculating. The entire subject reduces to four operations applied to either the inside or outside of f(x). Get the inside/outside distinction right and you will never confuse yourself again.
DefinitionThe four transformations▶

Starting from a base graph \(y = f(x)\):

Vertical translation
\(y = f(x) + a\)
shift up by a
Horizontal translation
\(y = f(x - a)\)
shift right by a
Vertical stretch/reflect
\(y = af(x)\)
stretch by factor a; if a < 0, reflect in x-axis
Horizontal stretch/reflect
\(y = f(ax)\)
compress by factor a; if a < 0, reflect in y-axis
The inside/outside rule: changes outside f(...) affect y directly — they do what they say. Changes inside f(...) affect x — they do the opposite of what they say. So f(x − 3) shifts right (not left), and f(2x) compresses (not stretches).
PatternInside vs outside — the one distinction that matters▶
TransformationWhere it actsEffect on graphIntuition
\(f(x) + a\)Outside (on y)Shift up a unitsy increases by a
\(f(x) - a\)Outside (on y)Shift down a unitsy decreases by a
\(f(x - a)\)Inside (on x)Shift right a unitsNeed bigger x to get same y
\(f(x + a)\)Inside (on x)Shift left a unitsNeed smaller x to get same y
\(af(x),\; a>1\)OutsideStretch vertically by ay-values multiplied by a
\(-f(x)\)OutsideReflect in x-axisy becomes −y
\(f(ax),\; a>1\)InsideCompress horizontally by aReaches same y sooner
\(f(-x)\)InsideReflect in y-axisx becomes −x
The most confusing one: \(f(x+3)\) shifts LEFT by 3, not right. Why? Because to get the same output as \(f(0)\), you now need \(x = -3\). The graph moves left to compensate for the +3 inside.
ExampleFour worked examples — applying transformations to y = x²▶
Example 1 — Describe the transformation: \(y = (x-3)^2 + 1\)
1
Start from \(y = x^2\).
2
\((x-3)^2\): inside the function, −3. Shifts right by 3.
3
\(+1\): outside the function. Shifts up by 1.
ANSWER Shift right 3, up 1. Vertex moves from (0,0) to (3,1).
Example 2 — Describe: \(y = -(x+2)^2\)
1
\((x+2)^2\): inside, +2. Shifts left by 2.
2
Negative outside: \(-f(x)\). Reflects in x-axis. Parabola opens downward.
ANSWER Shift left 2, reflect in x-axis. Vertex at (−2, 0), opens down.
Example 3 — Find the equation after shifting \(y = \sqrt{x}\) left 4 and down 2
1
Left 4: change inside → \(\sqrt{x+4}\)
2
Down 2: change outside → \(\sqrt{x+4} - 2\)
ANSWER \(y = \sqrt{x+4} - 2\)
Example 4 — The point (2, 5) lies on \(y = f(x)\). Where does it go under \(y = 2f(x-1) + 3\)?
1
Inside: \(x - 1\) shifts right by 1. x-coordinate: \(2 + 1 = 3\)
2
Outside: \(2f(\cdot) + 3\). y-coordinate: \(2 \times 5 + 3 = 13\)
ANSWER Point moves to (3, 13)
NoteOrder of transformations and common traps▶

When multiple transformations are combined, apply them in this order: horizontal stretch/compress → horizontal shift → vertical stretch → vertical shift.

Watch out: \(f(x+3)\) is a shift LEFT by 3. Students instinctively say "right". Think: which x-value gives \(f(0)\)? The answer is \(x = -3\), so the graph is three units to the LEFT of where \(f(0)\) used to be.
Watch out: \(f(2x)\) compresses horizontally by a factor of 2 (not stretches). The graph becomes narrower — it reaches its values twice as fast.
Key features (vertex, x-intercepts, asymptotes) transform the same way as any other point. Track what happens to them to sketch the transformed graph quickly.
Topic 2
Inverse Functions
Undoing a function — swap the inputs and outputs
If a function takes x and gives you y, the inverse takes y and gives you back x. It is the mathematical equivalent of an undo button. Understanding inverse functions unlocks the relationship between exponentials and logarithms — they are inverses of each other, which is why they appear together.
DefinitionWhat an inverse function is▶

The inverse of \(f(x)\), written \(f^{-1}(x)\), is the function that undoes f. Formally:

\[ f^{-1}(f(x)) = x \qquad \text{and} \qquad f(f^{-1}(x)) = x \]

Applying f then its inverse returns you to where you started — and vice versa.

The domain and range swap:

\[ \text{Domain of } f^{-1} = \text{Range of } f \qquad \text{Range of } f^{-1} = \text{Domain of } f \]
Graphically: the graph of \(f^{-1}\) is the graph of \(f\) reflected in the line \(y = x\). This is because the x and y coordinates are swapped.
Important: not every function has an inverse. A function only has an inverse if it is one-to-one (each output comes from exactly one input). Test: a horizontal line crosses the graph at most once.
PatternHow to find an inverse — three steps▶
1
Write \(y = f(x)\).
2
Swap x and y: write \(x = f(y)\).
3
Rearrange to make y the subject. That is \(f^{-1}(x)\).

This works because the inverse literally swaps the roles of input and output.

Key link: \(\log_a(x)\) and \(a^x\) are inverses of each other. Swapping gives: \(\log_a(a^x) = x\) and \(a^{\log_a x} = x\). This is why taking log of both sides solves exponential equations.
ExampleFour fully worked examples▶
Example 1 — Find the inverse of \(f(x) = 2x + 5\)
1
Write \(y = 2x + 5\)
2
Swap: \(x = 2y + 5\)
3
Rearrange: \(x - 5 = 2y \Rightarrow y = \dfrac{x-5}{2}\)
ANSWER \(f^{-1}(x) = \dfrac{x-5}{2}\)

Check: \(f(f^{-1}(x)) = 2 \cdot \dfrac{x-5}{2} + 5 = (x-5)+5 = x\) ✓

Example 2 — Find the inverse of \(f(x) = x^3 - 4\)
1
Swap: \(x = y^3 - 4\)
2
Rearrange: \(y^3 = x+4 \Rightarrow y = \sqrt[3]{x+4}\)
ANSWER \(f^{-1}(x) = \sqrt[3]{x+4}\)
Example 3 — Show that \(f(x) = e^x\) and \(g(x) = \ln x\) are inverses
1
\(f(g(x)) = e^{\ln x} = x\) ✓ (by definition of ln)
2
\(g(f(x)) = \ln(e^x) = x\) ✓ (by definition of ln)
SHOWN \(e^x\) and \(\ln x\) are inverses — their graphs are reflections in \(y = x\)
Example 4 — Why does \(f(x) = x^2\) not have an inverse (over all reals)?

The function \(f(x) = x^2\) gives the same output for both \(x = 3\) and \(x = -3\): both give 9. So the "inverse" would need to send 9 to both 3 and −3 simultaneously — that is not a function.

Fix: restrict the domain to \(x \geq 0\). Then \(f^{-1}(x) = \sqrt{x}\).

KEY POINT Inverse only exists when the function is one-to-one. Restrict domain if needed.
NoteCommon mistakes with inverses▶
Watch out: \(f^{-1}(x) \neq \dfrac{1}{f(x)}\). The notation \(f^{-1}\) means the inverse function, not the reciprocal. For example, \(\sin^{-1}(x)\) means arcsin(x), not \(\dfrac{1}{\sin x}\).
Watch out: always check that your found inverse actually works by verifying \(f(f^{-1}(x)) = x\).
If the graph of \(f\) passes the horizontal line test (no horizontal line crosses it twice), the function has an inverse over its full domain.
Topic 3
Exponential & Logarithmic Equations
The log laws and how to solve equations where the unknown is in an exponent
When the unknown is in an exponent — like \(3^x = 20\) — you cannot solve it by ordinary algebra. The tool you need is the logarithm: it is specifically designed to bring the exponent down. Taking log of both sides of an exponential equation is the single most important technique here.
DefinitionLogarithm definition and the key relationship▶

\(\log_a(x) = y\) means exactly the same as \(a^y = x\). Logarithm and exponentiation are two ways of writing the same relationship:

\[ \log_a(x) = y \;\iff\; a^y = x \]

Special bases used constantly:

NotationBaseMeaning
\(\log x\) or \(\log_{10} x\)10Common logarithm
\(\ln x\)\(e \approx 2.718\)Natural logarithm

The log laws — these are the tools for solving equations:

\[ \log(ab) = \log a + \log b \]
\[ \log\!\left(\frac{a}{b}\right) = \log a - \log b \]
\[ \log(a^n) = n\log a \qquad \leftarrow \text{ this one brings the exponent down} \]
\[ \log_a a = 1 \qquad \log_a 1 = 0 \]
PatternThree equation types and how to attack each▶
Equation typeStrategy
\(a^x = b\)Take \(\log\) or \(\ln\) of both sides. Use \(\log(a^x) = x\log a\) to bring x down.
\(\log_a(x) = b\)Rewrite as \(x = a^b\) using the definition.
\(\log(f(x)) = \log(g(x))\)Logs equal ⟹ arguments equal: \(f(x) = g(x)\). Check domain after solving.

Change of base formula — useful when the base is not 10 or e:

\[ \log_a b = \frac{\ln b}{\ln a} = \frac{\log b}{\log a} \]
ExampleFive fully worked examples▶
Example 1 — Solve \(3^x = 20\)
1
Take ln of both sides: \(\ln(3^x) = \ln 20\)
2
Use power law: \(x \ln 3 = \ln 20\)
3
Divide: \(x = \dfrac{\ln 20}{\ln 3} \approx \dfrac{2.996}{1.099} \approx 2.727\)
ANSWER \(x = \dfrac{\ln 20}{\ln 3} \approx 2.73\)
Example 2 — Solve \(5e^{2x} = 80\)
1
Isolate the exponential: \(e^{2x} = 16\)
2
Take ln: \(2x = \ln 16\)
3
Divide: \(x = \dfrac{\ln 16}{2} = \ln 4 \approx 1.386\)
ANSWER \(x = \ln 4 \approx 1.39\)
Example 3 — Solve \(\log_2(x+3) = 5\)
1
Convert: \(x + 3 = 2^5 = 32\)
2
Solve: \(x = 29\)
ANSWER \(x = 29\)
Example 4 — Solve \(\ln(x) + \ln(x-2) = \ln 3\)
1
Use product law: \(\ln(x(x-2)) = \ln 3\)
2
Logs equal ⟹ arguments equal: \(x(x-2) = 3\)
3
Expand: \(x^2 - 2x - 3 = 0 \Rightarrow (x-3)(x+1) = 0\)
4
Check domain: \(x > 0\) and \(x > 2\) needed. So \(x = 3\) only (\(x = -1\) rejected).
ANSWER \(x = 3\)
Always check domain — log equations often produce extraneous solutions that make the argument of a log negative.
Example 5 — Simplify \(\log_3 81 - \log_3 9\)
1
Use quotient law: \(\log_3\!\left(\dfrac{81}{9}\right) = \log_3 9\)
2
\(\log_3 9 = \log_3 3^2 = 2\)
ANSWER \(2\)
NoteLog law mistakes and domain traps▶
Watch out: \(\log(a+b) \neq \log a + \log b\). The product law applies to \(\log(ab)\), not \(\log(a+b)\). There is no simplification for \(\log(a+b)\).
Watch out: \(\log(a^n) = n\log a\), not \((\log a)^n\). The n comes down as a multiplier, it does not stay as a power on the log.
Domain: \(\log(x)\) is only defined for \(x > 0\). When solving log equations, write down the domain constraints first, then check solutions against them at the end.
Topic 4
Growth, Decay & Compound Interest
Real-world exponential models
Exponential functions are not just an algebraic exercise — they describe how populations grow, how radioactive substances decay, how bank accounts compound, and how viruses spread. All of these are the same equation wearing different clothes. Once you recognise the template, you can solve all of them.
DefinitionThe three model formulas▶

Exponential growth/decay:

\[ N(t) = N_0 \, e^{kt} \]

\(N_0\) = initial amount, \(k > 0\) = growth, \(k < 0\) = decay, \(t\) = time.

Compound interest (n times per year):

\[ A = P\!\left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt} \]

\(P\) = principal, \(r\) = annual rate (as decimal), \(n\) = compoundings per year, \(t\) = years.

Continuous compounding:

\[ A = Pe^{rt} \]

This is the limit as n → ∞ of the compound interest formula.

ScenarioFormulaFind t by...
Growth/decay\(N = N_0 e^{kt}\)Taking ln of both sides
Half-lifeSet \(N = \frac{N_0}{2}\), solve for tSame — take ln
Doubling timeSet \(N = 2N_0\), solve for tSame — take ln
Compound interest\(A = P(1+r/n)^{nt}\)Take log, use power law
PatternIdentifying and setting up the model▶

Every growth/decay problem follows the same steps:

1
Identify the initial value \(N_0\) (value at \(t=0\)).
2
Find k using a second known data point — substitute and take ln.
3
Write the full model with \(N_0\) and k known.
4
Answer the question — substitute the target value and solve for t, or substitute t and solve for N.
The key insight: solving for t always requires taking a logarithm. Solving for N (given t) never does — just substitute and compute.
ExampleFour fully worked examples▶
Example 1 — Population growth

A population starts at 500 and grows to 800 in 4 years. When does it reach 2000?

1
\(N_0 = 500\). Model: \(N = 500e^{kt}\).
2
Find k using \(N(4) = 800\): \(800 = 500e^{4k} \Rightarrow e^{4k} = 1.6 \Rightarrow 4k = \ln 1.6 \Rightarrow k = \dfrac{\ln 1.6}{4} \approx 0.1175\)
3
Solve for t when \(N = 2000\): \(2000 = 500e^{0.1175t} \Rightarrow e^{0.1175t} = 4 \Rightarrow t = \dfrac{\ln 4}{0.1175} \approx 11.8\) years
ANSWER The population reaches 2000 after approximately 11.8 years.
Example 2 — Radioactive decay / half-life

A substance decays to half its mass in 30 years (half-life = 30 years). How much remains after 100 years if you start with 200g?

1
Find k: \(\frac{N_0}{2} = N_0 e^{30k} \Rightarrow \frac{1}{2} = e^{30k} \Rightarrow 30k = \ln\frac{1}{2} = -\ln 2 \Rightarrow k = \dfrac{-\ln 2}{30}\)
2
Model: \(N = 200\,e^{\frac{-\ln 2}{30}t}\)
3
At \(t = 100\): \(N = 200\,e^{\frac{-\ln 2}{30} \times 100} = 200 \times 2^{-100/30} \approx 200 \times 0.0992 \approx 19.8\text{g}\)
ANSWER Approximately 19.8 g remains after 100 years.
Example 3 — Compound interest

\$5000 is invested at 4% per annum, compounded monthly. What is it worth after 10 years?

1
\(P = 5000,\; r = 0.04,\; n = 12,\; t = 10\)
2
\(A = 5000\!\left(1 + \dfrac{0.04}{12}\right)^{120} = 5000 \times (1.003\overline{3})^{120}\)
3
\(\approx 5000 \times 1.4908 \approx \$7{,}454\)
ANSWER Approximately \$7,454 after 10 years.
Example 4 — How long to double? (Rule of 72)

How long does it take \$1000 to double at 6% compounded continuously?

1
Model: \(A = 1000e^{0.06t}\). Set \(A = 2000\).
2
\(2 = e^{0.06t} \Rightarrow \ln 2 = 0.06t \Rightarrow t = \dfrac{\ln 2}{0.06} \approx 11.55\) years
ANSWER Approximately 11.6 years to double.

Rule of 72: divide 72 by the interest rate % to estimate doubling time. 72/6 = 12 years — very close.

NoteModel setup mistakes and unit traps▶
Watch out: interest rates must be in decimal form in the formula. 4% = 0.04. Using 4 instead of 0.04 gives a wildly wrong answer.
Watch out: k is negative for decay. If you find a positive k for a decay problem, something is wrong. Double-check by substituting back — after time passes, you should have less than the starting amount.
Doubling time and half-life depend only on k, not on \(N_0\). So a substance with a given half-life behaves the same regardless of how much you start with.
Topic 5
Rate of Change, Limits & Continuity
What calculus is actually about, built from the ground up
Calculus begins with one question: how fast is something changing at this exact moment? The average rate of change over an interval is easy — it is just slope. The challenge is finding the rate at a single point. This requires the concept of a limit: what value does something approach as we get closer and closer? Understanding this bridge is what makes calculus feel inevitable rather than magical.
DefinitionAverage rate of change and instantaneous rate of change▶

Average rate of change of \(f(x)\) from \(x=a\) to \(x=b\):

\[ \text{AROC} = \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a} = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} \]

This is the slope of the secant line joining the two points \((a, f(a))\) and \((b, f(b))\).

Instantaneous rate of change at \(x = a\):

\[ \text{IROC at } a = \lim_{b \to a} \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a} \]

This is the slope of the tangent line at \(x = a\) — found by letting the two points of the secant get infinitely close together.

What is a limit?

\[ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \]

This reads: "as x gets arbitrarily close to a (but never equals a), f(x) gets arbitrarily close to L". The limit is about the approach, not the value at the point.

PatternHow to evaluate limits and what continuity means▶

Evaluating limits — try these strategies in order:

1
Direct substitution: try plugging in \(x = a\). If the result is a real number, that is the limit.
2
If you get 0/0: factorise the numerator/denominator, cancel the common factor, then substitute.
3
If you still can't simplify: use a table of values — approach from left and right and see what value you approach.

Continuity — a function is continuous at \(x = a\) if three things hold:

1
\(f(a)\) is defined.
2
\(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) exists.
3
\(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)\). The limit equals the actual value.
A function is continuous at a point if you can draw through it without lifting your pen. The limit tells you where the graph is heading; continuity requires it to actually arrive there.
ExampleFive worked examples▶
Example 1 — Average rate of change of \(f(x) = x^2\) from x=1 to x=4
\[ \text{AROC} = \frac{f(4)-f(1)}{4-1} = \frac{16-1}{3} = \frac{15}{3} = 5 \]
ANSWER Average rate of change = 5

This is the slope of the line joining (1,1) and (4,16).

Example 2 — Direct substitution: \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 3}(2x^2 - 5)\)
\[ 2(3)^2 - 5 = 18 - 5 = 13 \]
ANSWER \(13\)
Example 3 — \(0/0\) form: \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 2}\frac{x^2-4}{x-2}\)
1
Direct substitution gives \(\frac{0}{0}\) — indeterminate. Factorise.
2
\(\dfrac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2}\). Cancel \((x-2)\) (valid since \(x \neq 2\) in a limit).
3
Substitute: \(\lim_{x\to 2}(x+2) = 4\)
ANSWER \(4\)
Example 4 — Limit from a table: \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0}\frac{\sin x}{x}\)

Direct substitution gives 0/0. Build a table:

x0.10.010.001→ 0
\(\sin x / x\)0.99830.999980.9999998→ 1
ANSWER \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin x}{x} = 1\)
Example 5 — Is \(f(x) = \dfrac{x^2-1}{x-1}\) continuous at \(x=1\)?
1
\(f(1)\) is undefined (division by zero). Condition 1 fails.
2
However, \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 1}\frac{x^2-1}{x-1} = \lim_{x\to 1}(x+1) = 2\). The limit exists.
ANSWER Not continuous at x=1 — the function has a hole (removable discontinuity) at x=1. The limit is 2 but f(1) is undefined.
NoteWhat limits are not and types of discontinuity▶
The limit is about approach, not arrival. \(\lim_{x \to 2} f(x)\) asks what f(x) approaches as x gets close to 2 — not what f(2) equals. The function may not even be defined at x=2.

Topic 6
The Difference Quotient
The formula that becomes the derivative — the bridge to calculus
The difference quotient is the average rate of change of f(x) over a tiny interval of width h. When we let h shrink to zero — take the limit — the average rate becomes the instantaneous rate of change, which is the derivative. Every derivative rule in calculus was derived exactly this way. Understanding this formula means understanding where calculus comes from.
DefinitionThe difference quotient and its limit▶

The difference quotient of \(f(x)\) is:

\[ \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \]

This is the slope of the secant line joining \((x,\,f(x))\) and \((x+h,\,f(x+h))\).

As \(h \to 0\), the two points merge into one and the secant becomes the tangent. The limit gives the derivative:

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} \]
This is the definition of the derivative — every derivative rule you will learn in calculus (power rule, product rule, chain rule) is derived from exactly this limit. The rules are just convenient shortcuts for this formula.
PatternHow to compute the difference quotient — four steps▶
1
Find \(f(x+h)\): replace every x in \(f(x)\) with \((x+h)\). Expand carefully — do not skip brackets.
2
Subtract \(f(x)\): compute \(f(x+h) - f(x)\). Most terms will cancel — the result should not contain any terms without an h.
3
Divide by h: the expression \(\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\). Factor h from the numerator and cancel — you must always be able to cancel the h.
4
Take the limit as \(h \to 0\): substitute h = 0 into the simplified expression. This is \(f'(x)\).
Watch out at step 1: \(f(x+h) \neq f(x) + h\) and \((x+h)^2 \neq x^2 + h^2\). You must expand fully. Skipping the expansion is the single most common error.
ExampleFour fully worked examples — building up in complexity▶
Example 1 — Difference quotient for \(f(x) = x^2\)
1
\(f(x+h) = (x+h)^2 = x^2 + 2xh + h^2\)
2
\(f(x+h) - f(x) = x^2 + 2xh + h^2 - x^2 = 2xh + h^2\)
3
\(\dfrac{2xh+h^2}{h} = \dfrac{h(2x+h)}{h} = 2x + h\)
4
Limit: \(\displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}(2x+h) = 2x\)
DERIVATIVE \(f'(x) = 2x\)

This confirms the power rule: for \(x^2\), the derivative is \(2x\). The difference quotient derived it from scratch.

Example 2 — Difference quotient for \(f(x) = 3x^2 - 2x + 1\)
1
\(f(x+h) = 3(x+h)^2 - 2(x+h) + 1 = 3x^2+6xh+3h^2-2x-2h+1\)
2
\(f(x+h)-f(x) = 6xh+3h^2-2h\)
3
\(\dfrac{h(6x+3h-2)}{h} = 6x+3h-2\)
4
Limit: \(6x - 2\)
DERIVATIVE \(f'(x) = 6x - 2\)
Example 3 — Evaluate the difference quotient numerically at a specific point

For \(f(x) = x^2\), compute the difference quotient at \(x=3\) for \(h=0.1\) and \(h=0.01\). What does this approach?

1
\(h=0.1\): \(\dfrac{(3.1)^2 - 9}{0.1} = \dfrac{9.61-9}{0.1} = \dfrac{0.61}{0.1} = 6.1\)
2
\(h=0.01\): \(\dfrac{(3.01)^2-9}{0.01} = \dfrac{0.0601}{0.01} = 6.01\)
3
As \(h\to 0\), this approaches \(6 = 2(3) = f'(3)\). ✓
ANSWER The difference quotient approaches 6, the slope of the tangent at x=3.
Example 4 — Difference quotient for \(f(x) = \dfrac{1}{x}\)
1
\(f(x+h) = \dfrac{1}{x+h}\)
2
\(f(x+h)-f(x) = \dfrac{1}{x+h} - \dfrac{1}{x} = \dfrac{x-(x+h)}{x(x+h)} = \dfrac{-h}{x(x+h)}\)
3
Divide by h: \(\dfrac{-h}{h \cdot x(x+h)} = \dfrac{-1}{x(x+h)}\)
4
Limit: \(\displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{-1}{x(x+h)} = \frac{-1}{x^2}\)
DERIVATIVE \(f'(x) = -\dfrac{1}{x^2}\)
NoteThe big picture — where you are heading▶

The difference quotient is the bridge. Here is the complete chain:

1
Average rate of change = slope of secant = \(\dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\)
2
Let the interval shrink: write \(b = x+h\), \(a = x\). Secant slope = \(\dfrac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\)
3
Take the limit as \(h \to 0\): secant becomes tangent. Result = \(f'(x)\) = derivative.
Every derivative rule you will encounter in calculus — power rule, product rule, chain rule — is just a precomputed version of this limit. The rules exist so you do not have to re-derive it every time. But the difference quotient is the reason those rules are true.
The h must cancel. If you cannot cancel h from the denominator before taking the limit, you have made an algebraic error somewhere. Step 3 (divide by h) only works after h has been factored out of the numerator.

TOPIC 1
Graph Transformations
Inside vs outside. Track key points.
1
The graph of \(y=f(x)\) contains the point \((4,\,3)\). State where this point maps to under \(y = -f(2x) + 1\).
Apply inside transformation to x-coord, outside transformations to y-coord. Order matters.
▶
Identify each transformation. Apply the x-change to the x-coordinate and the y-changes to the y-coordinate.
FULL SOLUTION
1
Inside: \(f(2x)\). Horizontal compression by 2. New x: \(4 \div 2 = 2\)
2
Outside: \(-f(\cdot)\). Reflection in x-axis. New y: \(-3\)
3
Outside: \(+1\). Shift up 1. Final y: \(-3+1 = -2\)
ANSWER Point maps to \((2,\,-2)\)
2
Write the equation of \(y = \sqrt{x}\) after: reflect in the y-axis, then shift right 3 and up 2.
Build up the equation one transformation at a time.
▶
FULL SOLUTION
1
Reflect in y-axis: \(f(-x) \Rightarrow y = \sqrt{-x}\)
2
Shift right 3 (inside, subtract 3): \(y = \sqrt{-(x-3)} = \sqrt{3-x}\)
3
Shift up 2 (outside, add 2): \(y = \sqrt{3-x}+2\)
ANSWER \(y = \sqrt{3-x}+2\)
TOPIC 2
Inverse Functions
Swap x and y. Rearrange. Check.
3
Find the inverse of \(f(x) = \dfrac{3x+1}{x-2}\) and state its domain.
Swap x and y, then rearrange to make y the subject — you will need to collect y terms.
▶
Start: \(x = \frac{3y+1}{y-2}\). Multiply both sides by \((y-2)\). Expand. Collect all y terms on one side. Factor out y. Divide.
FULL SOLUTION
1
Swap: \(x = \dfrac{3y+1}{y-2}\)
2
Multiply: \(x(y-2) = 3y+1 \Rightarrow xy-2x = 3y+1\)
3
Collect y: \(xy-3y = 2x+1 \Rightarrow y(x-3) = 2x+1\)
4
Divide: \(y = \dfrac{2x+1}{x-3}\)
5
Domain of \(f^{-1}\): \(x \neq 3\) (denominator = 0 excluded).
ANSWER \(f^{-1}(x) = \dfrac{2x+1}{x-3},\quad x \neq 3\)
TOPIC 3
Exponential & Log Equations
Isolate the exponential. Take log. Use power law.
4
Solve \(2^{x+1} = 5^x\).
Take ln of both sides. Use the power law on both terms. Collect x terms.
▶
FULL SOLUTION
1
Take ln: \(\ln 2^{x+1} = \ln 5^x\)
2
Power law: \((x+1)\ln 2 = x\ln 5\)
3
Expand: \(x\ln 2 + \ln 2 = x\ln 5\)
4
Collect x: \(\ln 2 = x\ln 5 - x\ln 2 = x(\ln 5 - \ln 2) = x\ln\frac{5}{2}\)
5
\(x = \dfrac{\ln 2}{\ln(5/2)} \approx \dfrac{0.693}{0.916} \approx 0.756\)
ANSWER \(x = \dfrac{\ln 2}{\ln 2.5} \approx 0.756\)
5
Solve \(\log_2(x+5) - \log_2(x-1) = 3\).
Use the quotient law first, then convert to exponential form.
▶
FULL SOLUTION
1
Quotient law: \(\log_2\!\left(\dfrac{x+5}{x-1}\right) = 3\)
2
Convert: \(\dfrac{x+5}{x-1} = 2^3 = 8\)
3
Solve: \(x+5 = 8(x-1) = 8x-8 \Rightarrow 13 = 7x \Rightarrow x = \dfrac{13}{7}\)
4
Check domain: \(x + 5 > 0\) ✓ and \(x - 1 > 0 \Rightarrow x > 1\). \(\frac{13}{7} \approx 1.86 > 1\) ✓
ANSWER \(x = \dfrac{13}{7}\)
TOPIC 4
Growth, Decay & Interest
Set up the model. Find k. Answer the question.
6
A drug in the bloodstream decreases from 300 mg to 200 mg in 4 hours. Find the half-life of the drug.
Find k first using the given data, then set \(N = 150\) and solve for t.
▶
FULL SOLUTION
1
Model: \(N = 300e^{kt}\). At \(t=4\), \(N=200\): \(200 = 300e^{4k} \Rightarrow e^{4k} = \frac{2}{3} \Rightarrow k = \frac{\ln(2/3)}{4} \approx -0.1014\)
2
Half-life: set \(N = 150\): \(150 = 300e^{kt_{1/2}} \Rightarrow \frac{1}{2} = e^{kt_{1/2}} \Rightarrow t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln(1/2)}{k} = \frac{-\ln 2}{k}\)
3
\(t_{1/2} = \dfrac{-\ln 2}{-0.1014} = \dfrac{\ln 2}{0.1014} \approx 6.83\) hours
ANSWER Half-life ≈ 6.8 hours
7
\$8000 is invested at 5% per annum compounded quarterly. How many years until it reaches \$12,000?
Set up \(A = P(1+r/n)^{nt}\), substitute known values, isolate the exponential, take log.
▶
FULL SOLUTION
1
\(P=8000,\; r=0.05,\; n=4,\; A=12000\). Formula: \(12000 = 8000\!\left(1.0125\right)^{4t}\)
2
Divide: \(1.5 = (1.0125)^{4t}\)
3
Take ln: \(\ln 1.5 = 4t\ln 1.0125\)
4
\(t = \dfrac{\ln 1.5}{4\ln 1.0125} \approx \dfrac{0.4055}{4 \times 0.01242} \approx \dfrac{0.4055}{0.04969} \approx 8.16\) years
ANSWER Approximately 8.2 years
TOPIC 5
Rate of Change & Limits
Average rate is slope of secant. Factorise 0/0 forms.
8
Find \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 3} \dfrac{x^2 - x - 6}{x - 3}\).
Direct substitution gives 0/0. Factorise the numerator.
▶
FULL SOLUTION
1
Direct substitution: \(\frac{9-3-6}{0} = \frac{0}{0}\). Indeterminate — factorise.
2
Factorise numerator: \(x^2-x-6 = (x-3)(x+2)\)
3
Cancel: \(\dfrac{(x-3)(x+2)}{x-3} = x+2\) (valid since \(x \neq 3\) in the limit)
4
Substitute: \(\lim_{x\to 3}(x+2) = 5\)
ANSWER \(5\)
9
A car travels so that its distance is \(s(t) = t^2 + 3t\) metres after \(t\) seconds. Find its average speed between \(t=2\) and \(t=5\), and estimate its instantaneous speed at \(t=2\) using \(h=0.001\).
AROC formula for average speed. Difference quotient for instantaneous.
▶
FULL SOLUTION
1
\(s(2)=4+6=10,\quad s(5)=25+15=40\)
2
Average speed: \(\dfrac{40-10}{5-2} = \dfrac{30}{3} = 10\) m/s
3
Instantaneous at \(t=2\), \(h=0.001\): \(s(2.001) = (2.001)^2+3(2.001) = 4.004001+6.003 = 10.007001\)
4
\(\dfrac{10.007001-10}{0.001} = \dfrac{0.007001}{0.001} \approx 7.001 \approx 7\) m/s
ANSWER Average speed = 10 m/s. Instantaneous speed at t=2 ≈ 7 m/s.
TOPIC 6
The Difference Quotient
Find f(x+h), subtract f(x), divide by h, cancel, take limit.
10
Use the difference quotient to find \(f'(x)\) for \(f(x) = x^3\).
Expand \((x+h)^3\) using the binomial expansion. Most terms will cancel with \(x^3\).
▶
Step 1: find \((x+h)^3\). Step 2: subtract \(x^3\). Step 3: divide by h and factor. Step 4: take limit.
FULL SOLUTION
1
\(f(x+h) = (x+h)^3 = x^3 + 3x^2h + 3xh^2 + h^3\)
2
\(f(x+h)-f(x) = 3x^2h + 3xh^2 + h^3\)
3
Divide: \(\dfrac{h(3x^2+3xh+h^2)}{h} = 3x^2+3xh+h^2\)
4
Limit: \(\displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}(3x^2+3xh+h^2) = 3x^2\)
DERIVATIVE \(f'(x) = 3x^2\)
11
Use the difference quotient to find \(f'(x)\) for \(f(x) = \sqrt{x}\).
You will need to rationalise the numerator by multiplying by the conjugate \(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}\).
▶
FULL SOLUTION
1
\(\dfrac{\sqrt{x+h}-\sqrt{x}}{h}\)
2
Multiply top and bottom by \(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}\):
\[ \frac{(\sqrt{x+h}-\sqrt{x})(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x})}{h(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x})} = \frac{(x+h)-x}{h(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x})} = \frac{h}{h(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x})} \]
3
Cancel h: \(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}}\)
4
Limit: \(\displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{1}{\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}\)
DERIVATIVE \(f'(x) = \dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}\)
12
Find the slope of the tangent to \(f(x) = 2x^2 - 3x\) at \(x = 4\) using the difference quotient.
Find f'(x) using the difference quotient, then substitute x=4.
▶
FULL SOLUTION
1
\(f(x+h)=2(x+h)^2-3(x+h)=2x^2+4xh+2h^2-3x-3h\)
2
\(f(x+h)-f(x) = 4xh+2h^2-3h = h(4x+2h-3)\)
3
Divide by h and take limit: \(f'(x) = 4x - 3\)
4
At \(x=4\): \(f'(4) = 4(4)-3 = 13\)
ANSWER Slope of tangent at x=4 is 13
