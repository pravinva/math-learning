MATH282 · Week 2-3
Double Integrals, Order Reversal, Polar Coordinates
Full tutorial from region interpretation to Jacobian-based conversion, with structured setup routines.
Why this matters: When quantities vary over a region (temperature plate, mass sheet, pressure map), one-dimensional integrals are not enough. Double integrals are the correct accumulation tool.
PatternBuild it: from strips to area accumulation▶

Write region \(R\), choose strip direction, then integrate inner strip accumulation and outer sweep.

\[ \iint_R f(x,y)\,dA = \int_a^b\int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)} f(x,y)\,dy\,dx = \int_c^d\int_{h_1(y)}^{h_2(y)} f(x,y)\,dx\,dy \]

For polar regions:

\[ x=r\cos\theta,\quad y=r\sin\theta,\quad dA=r\,dr\,d\theta \]
GuidedGuided lesson routine (use this every problem)▶
  1. Sketch and label boundary curves with intersection points.
  2. Pick integration order that gives simpler inner bounds.
  3. If changing order, rewrite inequalities carefully and re-sketch.
  4. If circular/radial region appears, test polar early.
  5. In polar, convert integrand and include Jacobian \(r\).
ExampleWorked examples with full setup logic▶
Example 1 — rectangle region

\(\int_0^2\int_1^3 (x+2y)\,dy\,dx\).

Inner integral gives \(2x+8\), outer gives \(20\).

Example 2 — reverse order

\(\int_0^1\int_x^1 f(x,y)\,dy\,dx\). Region is \(0\le x\le y\le1\), so reversed form is \(\int_0^1\int_0^y f(x,y)\,dx\,dy\).

Example 3 — area by double integral

Between \(y=2x\) and \(y=x^2\), \(0\le x\le2\):

\[ A=\int_0^2\int_{x^2}^{2x}1\,dy\,dx=\int_0^2(2x-x^2)\,dx=\frac{4}{3} \]
Example 4 — polar disk

\(\iint_{x^2+y^2\le4}(x^2+y^2)\,dA=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2 r^2\cdot r\,dr\,d\theta=8\pi\).

NoteCommon mistakes and recovery checks▶
Recovery 1
If you forget the polar Jacobian \(r\), area scaling is wrong. Write "\(dA\to r\,dr\,d\theta\)" as a dedicated step.
Recovery 2
After reversing order, test a point from the region against your new bounds.
Recovery 3
If inner integral looks impossible but region is circular, switch to polar early.
