1
Why was Pascal's triangle invented in the first place?
▶

The problem that motivated it: how do you expand (a+b)ⁿ for large n without multiplying out every bracket by hand? Doing (a+b)⁵ manually requires four separate multiplications and dozens of terms to track.

Mathematicians noticed the coefficients had a pattern:

(a+b)² → coefficients 1, 2, 1
(a+b)³ → coefficients 1, 3, 3, 1
(a+b)⁴ → coefficients 1, 4, 6, 4, 1
Each row is the sum of the two entries above it in the previous row.

The triangle is a lookup table. To expand (a+b)⁷, go to row 7 and read off 1, 7, 21, 35, 35, 21, 7, 1 as your coefficients. No algebra required.

The deeper insight — that these coefficients must follow this rule — comes from what polynomial multiplication actually is. When you expand (a+b)ⁿ, you're choosing to take either a or b from each of the n brackets. The coefficient of a^(n−r)b^r counts how many ways you can pick b from exactly r brackets. That count obeys Pascal's rule because adding one more bracket (going from n to n+1) either includes or excludes that new bracket — the same binary split that builds the triangle.

The triangle wasn't discovered by accident. The structure of polynomial multiplication forces it. The triangle is the only possible arrangement these coefficients could have.

Pascal didn't invent it — the triangle appeared in India around 200 BC (Pingala, for Sanskrit metre patterns), in China around 1303 AD (Yang Hui), and in Persia around 1000 AD (Al-Karaji). Pascal's 1653 treatise was the first to connect it systematically to probability, combinations, and the binomial theorem — which is why his name stuck in the West.

2
Why does each cell decompose into exactly two parents — never one, never three?
▶

When you single out one item, you ask a yes/no question: is this item in the group or not? A yes/no question has exactly two answers. There is no third option. This is why the split produces exactly two subproblems — it's logically forced, not a choice of method.

Why not one parent?
You can't reduce a choice-of-r-from-n problem to a single smaller problem. The two cases (item in / item out) are genuinely different problems with different r values — they can't be collapsed into one.
Why not three parents?
There's nothing a third parent could represent. You've already covered all possibilities with "in" and "out." Any further split would just subdivide one of those two cases — giving the same answer twice.

The binary split is also why the parents are always one row up. Once you resolve the yes/no question about item n, you're left with n−1 items in the pool — which is exactly what row n−1 tracks. Resolving one item = moving up one row. This is deterministic, not approximate.

This structure is identical to how binary trees work in computer science: each node branches into exactly two children because it encodes one binary decision. Pascal's triangle is a binary decision tree, drawn as a triangle.

The triangle has exactly 2 parents per cell because it encodes one binary question per step. Every counting problem at row n resolves — in one decision — into two problems at row n−1. Every cell is forced by the two above it for the same reason: those two cells represent the only two outcomes of that decision.
3
What do rows and columns each represent — and why are they different questions?
▶

Rows and columns ask two completely different questions about the same numbers.

Row n — fix the pool size
"I have exactly n items. How many ways can I choose 0, 1, 2, … n of them?" The values across row n are C(n,0), C(n,1), …, C(n,n). You're varying how many you select while the pool stays fixed.
Column r — fix the selection size
"I always choose exactly r items. How does the count grow as I add more items to the pool?" Reading down column r: C(r,r), C(r+1,r), C(r+2,r), … You're growing the pool while keeping the selection size fixed.
What each column produces:
Column rSequence (reading down)NameWhy
01, 1, 1, 1, 1 …All onesThere is always exactly 1 way to choose nothing from any pool
11, 2, 3, 4, 5 …Natural numbersChoosing 1 item from n items gives n choices
21, 3, 6, 10, 15 …Triangular numbersC(n,2) = n(n−1)/2 — dots arranged as a growing triangle
31, 4, 10, 20, 35 …Tetrahedral numbersC(n,3) = n(n−1)(n−2)/6 — stacked triangular layers
41, 5, 15, 35 …Pentatope numbersC(n,4) — 4-dimensional analogues of tetrahedra
Each column is the running sum of the previous column. Triangular numbers are the running sums of natural numbers. Tetrahedral numbers are the running sums of triangular numbers. This is the Hockey Stick Identity: C(r,r) + C(r+1,r) + … + C(n,r) = C(n+1, r+1). Every diagonal accumulates the one to its left.
4
The Sierpiński pattern — why do odd numbers form a fractal?
▶

Switch to the Sierpiński mode above (colour odd numbers navy, even numbers white). A triangular fractal appears — the Sierpiński triangle — at every scale. This is not a coincidence.

Why it happens — parity arithmetic

Pascal's rule says C(n,r) = C(n−1,r−1) + C(n−1,r). If you only care about odd or even, the rule becomes:

odd + odd = even
even + even = even
odd + even = odd

This is identical to XOR logic: a cell is odd if and only if exactly one of its two parents is odd. That rule, applied recursively from the edges (which are all 1s = odd), generates the same self-similar pattern at every scale.

The fractal structure

Look at rows 0 to 2ᵏ−1 for any k. That block of rows is an exact copy of rows 0 to 2^(k−1)−1, tiled three times — top, bottom-left, bottom-right — with a triangle of even numbers (whites) in the middle. This three-way self-similarity is the definition of the Sierpiński fractal.

Rows 0–1 (k=1)
Two rows: top 1, then 1 1. All odd. One small triangle.
Rows 0–3 (k=2)
Four rows. Row 2 is 1, 2, 1 — the middle entry (2) is even, white. Three copies of the k=1 triangle appear around a white gap in the centre.
The theorem behind it (Lucas, 1878): C(n, r) is odd if and only if, in binary, every bit of r is also a bit of n. In other words, r's binary representation is a bitwise subset of n's. The white triangles appear exactly where this fails — where r has a 1-bit that n does not.

The diagonals — famous sequences hidden inside the triangle
▶

Switch to Diagonals mode above. The colour bands show diagonals running parallel to the left edge. Each diagonal contains a different famous number sequence — because each diagonal is a fixed column r, and reading down a fixed-r column counts "choose r from a growing pool."

Row sums = powers of 2

Adding all entries across row n always gives 2ⁿ. The reason: every item in your pool is either included or excluded — 2 choices per item, n items, so 2ⁿ total subsets. Summing C(n,0)+C(n,1)+…+C(n,n) counts every possible subset exactly once.

Row 0 → 1 = 2⁰ Row 1 → 2 = 2¹ Row 2 → 4 = 2² Row 3 → 8 = 2³ Row 4 → 16 = 2⁴ Row 8 → 256 = 2⁸
Fibonacci in the shallow diagonals

Draw lines cutting across the triangle at a shallow angle (upper-left to lower-right, not straight down). Sum each such line:

Shallow diagonal 0: C(0,0) = 1
Shallow diagonal 1: C(1,0) = 1
Shallow diagonal 2: C(2,0)+C(1,1) = 1+1 = 2
Shallow diagonal 3: C(3,0)+C(2,1) = 1+2 = 3
Shallow diagonal 4: C(4,0)+C(3,1)+C(2,2) = 1+3+1 = 5
Shallow diagonal 5: 1+4+3 = 8
→ The Fibonacci sequence: 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21 …
Why Fibonacci? The recurrence F(n) = F(n−1) + F(n−2) maps directly onto a combinatorial identity involving Pascal's triangle. Each shallow diagonal sum is forced by the same recursive structure that defines the Fibonacci sequence.
The Key Insight: A problem in row n always decomposes into two problems in row n−1, because singling out one item drops you exactly one row. The triangle is just that decomposition drawn out spatially for every possible n and r at once — which is also why the binomial coefficients live in rows: each row n holds all the ways to choose from n items, and each coefficient is forced by the two choices above it (include or exclude the last item).
