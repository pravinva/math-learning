Week 11 Deep Dive: Composites

Outline mapping: Ch16 §16.1-16.15.

Full scaffold

  1. Specify reinforcement type (particle, short fiber, continuous fiber, laminate).
  2. Align model with load direction (longitudinal vs transverse).
  3. Compute elastic response with rule-of-mixtures bounds.
  4. Check transfer condition with critical fiber length.
  5. Convert into design recommendation and layup direction.

Core equations

Longitudinal modulus: EL = Ef*Vf + Em*Vm
Transverse approximation: 1/ET = Vf/Ef + Vm/Em
Critical fiber length: lc = sigmaf* * d / (2*tauc)

Worked example 1

Ef=230 GPa, Em=3 GPa, Vf=0.55, Vm=0.45:

EL=230*0.55 + 3*0.45 = 127.85 GPa.

Worked example 2

If sigmaf*=2.5 GPa, d=12 um, tauc=35 MPa:

lc=(2.5e9*12e-6)/(2*35e6)=0.000429 m=0.429 mm.

Fibers much shorter than this are inefficient for full reinforcement potential.

Design implication: "same material composition" can perform very differently depending on fiber length and orientation.

Quiz (with solutions)

  1. Why is layup orientation central for composite design under multiaxial loads?
  2. If fibers are shorter than critical length, what happens to expected reinforcement effect?
  3. Why might a composite with excellent EL still fail in service if ET is neglected?
Solution 1

Because reinforcement orientation creates anisotropy: load transfer is strongest when load aligns with stiff fibers.

Solution 2

Load transfer is incomplete, so fiber contribution to stiffness/strength is reduced relative to continuous or sufficiently long fibers.

Solution 3

Off-axis loads can trigger matrix-dominated deformation or delamination if transverse and interlaminar behavior is not considered.
