Week 9 Deep Dive: Electrical Properties

Outline mapping: Ch2 §2.2-2.3; Ch18 §18.1-18.17.

Full scaffold

  1. Identify conduction class (metal/semiconductor/ionic/polymer).
  2. Write sigma = n*q*mu and isolate which term changes with condition.
  3. Convert conductivity to resistivity and component resistance when needed.
  4. Use Hall relation to infer carrier type/density trend.
  5. State design consequence (heating loss, sensing accuracy, contact reliability).

Core equations

sigma = n*q*mu
rho = 1/sigma
R = rho*L/A
Hall (single carrier approx): RH = EH/(J*B) approx -1/(n*q)

Worked example 1

If n doubles and mu halves, sigma stays constant. Therefore conductivity can remain unchanged even after doping/processing changes if mobility is degraded proportionally.

Worked example 2

Given rho=2e-6 ohm-m, L=0.4 m, A=2e-6 m^2:

R = rho*L/A = (2e-6*0.4)/(2e-6)=0.4 ohm.

This directly links materials property to circuit-level behavior.

Common mistake: mixing bulk resistivity and measured resistance without geometry conversion.

Quiz (with solutions)

  1. Why can increased doping fail to improve conductivity as much as expected?
  2. If resistivity decreases from 5e-6 to 4e-6 ohm-m, by what fraction does conductivity change?
  3. What physical quantity does Hall coefficient mainly help estimate in the single-carrier model?
Solution 1

Semiconductors gain carriers with temperature; metals usually lose mobility due to stronger scattering, so their resistivity tends to increase.

Solution 2

sigma is inverse of rho. sigma_old=1/5e-6, sigma_new=1/4e-6, ratio=1.25 so conductivity rises by 25%.

Solution 3

Carrier concentration (and sign/type information depending on convention and measurement details).
