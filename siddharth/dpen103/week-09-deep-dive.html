Week 9 Deep Dive: Electrical Properties

Outline mapping: Ch2 §2.2-2.3; Ch18 §18.1-18.17.

Scaffold

  1. Classify conduction mechanism: metallic, semiconducting, ionic, polymeric.
  2. Use sigma=n*q*mu to reason about trends.
  3. Use resistivity inverse relation and geometry for resistance.
  4. Use Hall effect sign/magnitude to infer dominant carrier behavior.

Core explanation

Conductivity depends on carrier concentration and mobility. Metals usually have high n; semiconductors can vary n strongly by doping and temperature. Hall measurements help estimate carrier type/density.

Worked example

If n doubles and mobility halves, conductivity sigma=n*q*mu remains unchanged. This is why microstructural/defect effects on mobility can offset doping increases.

Quiz (with solutions)

  1. Why can semiconductor conductivity rise with temperature while metal conductivity often drops?
  2. If rho decreases by 20%, what happens to sigma?
  3. What does a strong Hall response imply in simple single-carrier interpretation?
Solution 1

Semiconductors gain carriers with temperature; metals usually lose mobility due to stronger scattering, so their resistivity tends to increase.

Solution 2

Sigma=1/rho, so sigma increases by 25% when rho goes from 1.0 to 0.8 (normalized units).

Solution 3

Large Hall coefficient magnitude suggests lower carrier concentration (for single-carrier approximation).
