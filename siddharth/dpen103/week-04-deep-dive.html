Week 4 Deep Dive: Strengthening + Fracture and Fatigue

Outline mapping: Ch7 §7.8-7.9; Ch8 §8.1-8.8.

Scaffolded approach

  1. Separate static failure from cyclic failure modes.
  2. If cracks are present, move to fracture mechanics (K-based) reasoning.
  3. For cyclic loads, use S-N concepts and crack-initiation factors.

Core explanation

Fracture toughness KIC captures crack-growth resistance. Stress intensity KI depends on applied stress, crack size, and geometry factor Y: KI = Y*sigma*sqrt(pi*a). Failure risk increases sharply with flaw size.

Fatigue is progressive damage under repeated loading. Surface condition, notches, residual stress, and environment strongly affect life.

Worked example

If KIC=40 MPa*sqrt(m), Y=1.1, a=2 mm=0.002 m, then allowable sigma ≈ KIC/(Y*sqrt(pi*a)) ≈ 459 MPa.

If crack grows to 8 mm, allowable stress halves approximately because of sqrt(a) dependence.

Quiz (with full worked solutions)

  1. Why can two parts with same material and same nominal stress fail at different times?
  2. What is the design meaning of KIC?
  3. Why does polishing often improve fatigue life?
Solution 1

Differences in flaw size, notch sharpness, residual stress, microstructure, and surface finish change crack initiation and growth rates.

Solution 2

KIC is the critical stress-intensity threshold in mode-I opening; it quantifies fracture resistance for cracked bodies.

Solution 3

Polishing lowers surface stress concentrators and initiation sites, delaying fatigue crack nucleation.
