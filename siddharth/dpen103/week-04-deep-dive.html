Week 4 Deep Dive: Strengthening + Fracture and Fatigue

Outline mapping: Ch7 §7.8-7.9; Ch8 §8.1-8.8.

Full scaffold

  1. Classify loading: static, impact, or cyclic.
  2. If flaw/crack exists, switch to stress-intensity approach.
  3. Compute allowable stress from KIC criterion.
  4. For cyclic service, evaluate initiation and propagation factors.
  5. Translate result into inspection interval or redesign choice.

Core equations

KI = Y * sigma * sqrt(pi*a)
Fracture risk when KI >= KIC
Allowable stress: sigma_allow = KIC / (Y*sqrt(pi*a))

This makes flaw size central to safe design. Doubling crack size does not just double risk; allowable stress falls with square-root relation.

Worked example

Given KIC=40 MPa*sqrt(m), Y=1.1, a=2 mm=0.002 m:

sigma_allow = 40 / (1.1*sqrt(pi*0.002)) ≈ 459 MPa.

If crack grows to 8 mm (4x), allowable stress drops by factor 1/sqrt(4)=1/2, to about 230 MPa.

Interpretation: small crack growth can force major load reduction.

Fatigue reasoning path

  1. Determine stress range and mean stress.
  2. Use S-N trend for baseline life.
  3. Apply modifiers: notch, surface finish, size, environment, residual stress.
  4. Decide design action: lower stress, improve finish, introduce compressive surface stress, or change material.

Quiz (with full worked solutions)

  1. If crack size doubles, by what factor does allowable stress change (all else fixed)?
  2. Why can a component fail in fatigue at stresses below yield?
  3. Name two practical ways to increase fatigue life without changing base alloy.
Solution 1

Allowable stress scales with 1/sqrt(a), so doubling a reduces allowable stress by 1/sqrt(2) (about 0.707 of original).

Solution 2

Cyclic loading drives crack initiation/propagation over many cycles; local crack-tip conditions can cause damage accumulation even when nominal stress is below yield.

Solution 3

Improve surface finish and introduce compressive residual stress (for example shot peening). Both reduce effective crack initiation/growth tendency.
