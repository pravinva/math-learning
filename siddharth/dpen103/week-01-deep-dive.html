Core explanation

Bonding type is the first predictor of behavior. Metallic bonding supports electron mobility and easier slip, which helps conductivity and ductility. Ionic/covalent bonding generally gives high stiffness and melting temperature, but reduced slip systems and higher brittleness in tension.

Crystal structure (FCC/BCC/HCP) changes packing and slip behavior. For density calculations, use: density = (atoms per cell × atomic weight) / (unit-cell volume × Avogadro number).

Worked example

Given BCC (n=2), a=0.286 nm, A=55.85 g/mol:

Convert a to cm, compute Vc=a^3, then rho=(2*55.85)/(Vc*6.022e23) ≈ 7.86 g/cm^3.

Sanity check: value is close to iron density, so result is physically reasonable.

Quiz (with full worked solutions)

  1. Why can ceramics be stiff but brittle compared with many metals?
  2. If FCC APF is 0.74 and BCC APF is 0.68, what does this suggest about packing efficiency?
  3. A metal has larger lattice parameter but same n and A as another. Which tends to be denser?
Solution 1

Strong directional ionic/covalent bonds resist elastic deformation (high modulus), but dislocation motion is limited and crack-tip plastic blunting is weaker, so fracture can be brittle.

Solution 2

FCC is more closely packed. Higher APF means more atomic volume fraction inside unit cell, often associated with different slip behavior and sometimes higher density for comparable atomic size/mass.

Solution 3

Larger lattice parameter means larger unit-cell volume denominator, so density tends to decrease if atom count and atomic weight stay the same.
