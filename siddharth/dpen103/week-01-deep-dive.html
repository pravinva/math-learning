Week 1 Deep Dive: Introduction, Bonding, Crystals

Outline mapping: Ch1 §1.1-1.6; Ch2 §2.1-2.7; Ch3 §3.1-3.6, §3.13-3.14.

Full scaffold (worked, not just headings)

Below is a complete walk-through using one realistic component. Use this same workflow for any Week 1 material-selection question.

Step 1 — Start with function (what the component must do)

Example component: a small motor mounting bracket used in a humid coastal workshop.

Functional requirements

  • Carry static load of 3 kN without permanent deformation.
  • Keep alignment under load (deflection should stay low).
  • Survive repeated vibration cycles (fatigue relevance).
  • Resist corrosion in humid/salt-prone environment.
  • Stay light enough for easy assembly and transport.
Why this matters: "Strong" alone is not enough. The part must meet stiffness, durability, corrosion, and mass constraints simultaneously.

Step 3 — Link targets to bonding and crystal structure (with reasoning)

Bonding-level prediction

  • Metallic bonding: delocalized electrons -> electrical conductivity, often useful ductility and toughness.
  • Ionic/covalent bonding: strong directional bonds -> high stiffness and temperature capability, but often more brittle in tension.

Crystal-level prediction (metals)

  • FCC metals: usually more slip systems active at room temperature -> often better formability/ductility.
  • BCC metals: packing is less dense; strength/ductility response depends strongly on temperature and alloying.

At this stage you do not have to pick one material yet; you are narrowing by mechanism-based behavior.

Step 4 — Use crystal geometry to compute density (full derivation)

Use the unit-cell density relation:

\[ \rho=\frac{nA}{V_cN_A} \]

where \(n\)=atoms/unit cell, \(A\)=atomic weight (g/mol), \(V_c\)=unit-cell volume (cm\(^3\)), \(N_A=6.022\times10^{23}\).

Worked density calculation (BCC iron-like case)

  1. Given: \(n=2,\ A=55.85\ \text{g/mol},\ a=0.286\ \text{nm}\).
  2. Convert lattice parameter: \(0.286\ \text{nm}=0.286\times10^{-7}\ \text{cm}\).
  3. Cell volume: \(V_c=a^3=(0.286\times10^{-7})^3=2.34\times10^{-23}\ \text{cm}^3\).
  4. Mass per cell: \(m_c=\frac{nA}{N_A}=\frac{2(55.85)}{6.022\times10^{23}}=1.85\times10^{-22}\ \text{g}\).
  5. Density: \(\rho=\frac{m_c}{V_c}=\frac{1.85\times10^{-22}}{2.34\times10^{-23}}\approx7.9\ \text{g/cm}^3\).
Check: value is physically plausible for steel/iron family materials.

What to write in an exam answer

  1. State function and constraints.
  2. State property targets (with at least one quantitative relation).
  3. Link bonding and structure to expected behavior.
  4. Show one crystal-density calculation.
  5. Conclude with justified material shortlist and rejection reason.

Quiz (with full worked solutions)

  1. You need a lightweight bracket in humid conditions. Which two property groups must be evaluated together before choosing between steel and aluminum, and why?
  2. Two BCC alloys have identical \(n\) and \(A\). Alloy B has 2% larger lattice parameter \(a\). Which is denser and why?
  3. Given \(n=4\), \(A=63.5\ \text{g/mol}\), \(a=0.361\ \text{nm}\), calculate density using unit-cell method.
Solution 1

Evaluate (a) mass/stiffness/strength together and (b) corrosion/environment resistance. Aluminum lowers mass but has lower modulus than steel, so section geometry may need change; steel may need coating to satisfy corrosion durability.

Solution 2

Density \(\rho\propto 1/a^3\) when \(n\) and \(A\) are fixed. The alloy with larger \(a\) has larger unit-cell volume, so lower density.

Solution 3

Convert \(a\): \(0.361\ \text{nm}=3.61\times10^{-8}\ \text{cm}\). \(V_c=a^3=4.70\times10^{-23}\ \text{cm}^3\). Mass per cell \(=\frac{4(63.5)}{6.022\times10^{23}}=4.22\times10^{-22}\ \text{g}\). Density \(=4.22\times10^{-22}/4.70\times10^{-23}\approx8.98\ \text{g/cm}^3\).
