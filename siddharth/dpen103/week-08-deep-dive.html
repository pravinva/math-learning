Week 8 Deep Dive: Integrative Materials Selection

Outline mapping: No explicit chapter sections listed (special/guest content week). This page provides synthesis scaffolding.

Scaffold

  1. Define function, constraints, and failure mode risks.
  2. Shortlist material classes by primary property needs.
  3. Evaluate process route compatibility and cost.
  4. Evaluate sustainability/lifecycle and uncertainty risks.

Core explanation

Engineering selection is multi-objective, not single-property optimization. A high-strength material may fail if processing introduces defects; a low-cost choice may fail lifecycle goals. Good solutions explicitly trade off performance, manufacturability, risk, and sustainability.

Worked example

For a lightweight beam: compare aluminum alloy, CFRP, and steel using stiffness-to-weight, fatigue risk, joining complexity, and cost. Final choice may differ by production volume and maintenance strategy.

Quiz (with solutions)

  1. Why is selecting by only tensile strength often poor engineering?
  2. Name two non-mechanical factors that can dominate final choice.
  3. How can process route invalidate a nominal property advantage?
Solution 1

Real parts fail via multiple modes (fatigue, corrosion, creep, fracture toughness limits) and must also satisfy processing, cost, and lifecycle constraints.

Solution 2

Manufacturing processability and lifecycle/environmental cost (also availability/supply-chain risk).

Solution 3

If process induces porosity/residual stress/poor bonding, actual part performance can be far below handbook values.
