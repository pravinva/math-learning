Week 12 Deep Dive: Ceramics

Outline mapping: Ch12 §12.2-12.4, §12.8-12.11.

Full scaffold

  1. Identify ceramic structure and loading mode (tension/compression/thermal shock).
  2. Assume flaw-controlled strength unless proven otherwise.
  3. Estimate allowable stress using fracture relation.
  4. Evaluate process and surface quality impacts on flaw population.
  5. Recommend geometry/process changes to improve reliability.

Core relation

sigma_f approx KIC / (Y*sqrt(pi*a))

Ceramic strength is not a single fixed number; it depends strongly on defect size distribution and stress state.

Worked example 1

If flaw size increases from a to 4a, allowable stress scales by 1/sqrt(4)=1/2. So reliable tensile strength can collapse even when chemistry is unchanged.

Worked example 2 (design action)

  1. Observed edge chipping after machining.
  2. Likely increase in effective surface flaw size.
  3. Use conservative reduced allowable stress until finishing/process correction is applied.
  4. Mitigate with improved finishing, compression layer, and smoother radii.
Key point: for brittle systems, process control is a mechanical-property control lever.

Quiz (with solutions)

  1. Why can two ceramic batches of same composition show different measured strengths?
  2. If surface flaw size doubles, what happens to allowable tensile stress trend?
  3. Give two practical ways to improve ceramic reliability in tension-sensitive service.
Solution 1

Because tensile cracks drive brittle failure; compression tends to close cracks and lowers crack-opening driving force.

Solution 2

Allowable stress scales with 1/sqrt(a), so doubling a reduces allowable stress by 1/sqrt(2) (about 0.707x).

Solution 3

Improve finishing/process quality to reduce flaws, and redesign geometry to reduce stress concentration (larger fillets/smoother transitions).
