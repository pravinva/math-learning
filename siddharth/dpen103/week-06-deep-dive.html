Week 6 Deep Dive: Binary Phase Diagrams and Lever Rule

Outline mapping: Ch9 §9.7-9.14.

Full scaffold

  1. Plot overall composition C0 and temperature T on diagram.
  2. Confirm single-phase or two-phase region.
  3. If two-phase, draw tie-line at same T and read C_alpha, C_beta.
  4. Apply mass balance (lever rule) carefully with consistent units.
  5. Translate fractions into expected microstructure fractions after equilibrium cooling.

Derivation from mass conservation

W_alpha + W_beta = 1
C0 = W_alpha*C_alpha + W_beta*C_beta

Substitute W_beta = 1 - W_alpha and solve:

W_alpha=(C_beta-C0)/(C_beta-C_alpha)
W_beta=(C0-C_alpha)/(C_beta-C_alpha)

Worked example 1

C_alpha=20 wt%, C_beta=80 wt%, C0=35 wt%.

W_alpha=(80-35)/(80-20)=45/60=0.75. W_beta=(35-20)/(80-20)=15/60=0.25.

Interpretation: 75% alpha and 25% beta by mass at that temperature.

Worked example 2 (sanity check)

If C0 moves toward C_beta, denominator fixed, numerator for W_beta increases, so beta fraction rises. Always do this trend check before final answer.

Frequent error: reading tie-line from wrong temperature or swapping C_alpha and C_beta positions.

Quiz (with solutions)

  1. Why is the tie-line temperature critical when using lever rule?
  2. If C_alpha=10, C_beta=70, C0=40 (wt%), compute W_alpha and W_beta.
  3. How can you quickly check if your phase fractions are physically sensible?
Solution 1

Phase compositions are temperature dependent; using another temperature gives wrong C_alpha/C_beta and wrong fractions.

Solution 2

W_alpha=(70-40)/(70-10)=30/60=0.5. W_beta=(40-10)/(70-10)=30/60=0.5.

Solution 3

Fractions must be between 0 and 1 and sum to 1. Also, if C0 is midpoint between endpoints, fractions should be equal.
