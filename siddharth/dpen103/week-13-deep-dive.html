Week 13 Deep Dive: Revision and Synthesis

Outline mapping: Revision week.

Full revision scaffold (execution plan)

  1. Build one synthesis sheet: equations + what each equation is for + unit checks.
  2. Practice classification first: identify topic from wording before touching equations.
  3. Solve mixed sets where chapters are deliberately shuffled.
  4. For every error, write root cause and correction rule.
  5. Re-test weak areas after 2, 7, and 21 days.

Core explanation

Revision week is about transfer, not repetition. You should be able to move from structure-property reasoning to quantitative calculation and back to design decisions in one answer.

Exam strategy: if stuck, start from "what is being asked physically?" then map to equation.

Worked synthesis method

  1. Read prompt and classify: mechanical/thermal/electrical/magnetic/composite/phase.
  2. State assumptions and units.
  3. Compute with one governing relation.
  4. Validate trend against physical intuition.
  5. Finish with a design implication sentence.

This 5-line structure can be reused in almost every short-answer calculation problem.

Integrated quiz (with full worked solutions)

  1. You need low hysteresis loss and good formability. Which class/category direction is likely best and why?
  2. Two designs have equal static stress, but one has sharp notches under cyclic loading. Which fails first and why?
  3. A lightweight requirement conflicts with high-temperature creep resistance. How do you structure your trade-off analysis?
  4. You are given a stress-strain curve and asked for design suitability. What 4 checks do you perform before deciding?
Solution 1

Soft magnetic metallic alloys are typical for low hysteresis losses. Then check manufacturability, cost, and operating environment.

Solution 2

The notched design usually fails first in fatigue due to stress concentration accelerating crack initiation and growth.

Solution 3

Define quantified targets (mass, max temperature, life), shortlist candidates, compare specific properties, creep data, process route, and lifecycle cost, then justify final choice with risks.

Solution 4

(1) Yield margin vs service stress, (2) modulus vs deflection need, (3) toughness/resilience vs loading type, (4) likely failure mode under actual service (fatigue/fracture/corrosion/temperature).
