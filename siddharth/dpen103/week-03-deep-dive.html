Week 3 Deep Dive: Imperfections and Dislocations

Outline mapping: Ch4 §4.1-4.3, §4.5-4.7; Ch7 §7.1-7.7, §7.10-7.13.

Full scaffold

  1. Classify defect type (vacancy/substitutional atom/dislocation/grain boundary/void).
  2. Identify governing motion: atom diffusion or dislocation glide/climb.
  3. Write the mechanism equation.
  4. Infer trend direction before calculating.
  5. State tradeoff: strength gain vs ductility/process effects.

Core equations and meanings

Vacancy concentration: n_v = N * exp(-Qv / kT)
Hall-Petch: sigma_y = sigma0 + ky * d^(-1/2)

First equation explains temperature sensitivity of diffusion-related behavior. Second equation quantifies grain-size strengthening by boundary barriers to dislocation motion.

Worked example 1: Hall-Petch scaling

If alloy B has grain size dB = 4 dA, then dB^(-1/2)=0.5 dA^(-1/2). Therefore grain-size strengthening term in B is half of A. Alloy A is stronger from grain-size effect.

Worked example 2: trend with temperature

From n_v relation, increasing T reduces Qv/kT and raises exp term, so vacancies rise rapidly. That means diffusion-controlled processes (creep, sintering, homogenization) accelerate strongly with temperature.

Exam note: always mention exponential sensitivity, not just "increases a bit".

Quiz (with full worked solutions)

  1. A metal undergoes grain growth from 10 um to 40 um. How does Hall-Petch strengthening term change?
  2. Why can high-temperature processing both help and hurt final strength?
  3. Name one mechanism that increases strength and one that restores ductility after cold work.
Solution 1

d^(-1/2) scales by 1/sqrt(4)=0.5, so Hall-Petch term halves. Yield stress contribution from grain boundaries decreases.

Solution 2

Higher T can reduce defects and improve homogeneity (help), but can also promote grain growth and recovery/recrystallization that reduce dislocation-based strengthening (hurt).

Solution 3

Strength increase: strain hardening or grain refinement. Ductility recovery: annealing/recovery/recrystallization.
