Week 3 Deep Dive: Imperfections and Dislocations

Outline mapping: Ch4 §4.1-4.3, §4.5-4.7; Ch7 §7.1-7.7, §7.10-7.13.

Scaffolded reasoning

  1. Identify which defect class dominates the problem (point, line, interface, volume).
  2. Ask how the defect changes mobility (atoms for diffusion, dislocations for plasticity).
  3. Translate to property trend (strength up/down, ductility up/down, rate effects).

Core explanation

Vacancies are needed for substitutional diffusion. Dislocations enable plastic deformation at practical stresses. Strengthening mechanisms work by obstructing dislocation motion: grain boundaries, solute atoms, dislocation-dislocation interactions, and precipitates.

Hall-Petch: yield stress = sigma0 + ky*d^(-1/2). Smaller grains increase barrier density and raise strength.

Worked example

Two alloys differ only by grain size: d2=4*d1. Hall-Petch term scales as d^-1/2, so term2 = (1/2)*term1. The finer-grain alloy has larger strengthening contribution.

Quiz (with full worked solutions)

  1. Why does raising temperature usually increase vacancy concentration?
  2. Name two strengthening mechanisms and explain how each impedes dislocations.
  3. Why can excessive grain growth reduce yield stress?
Solution 1

Vacancy concentration follows an Arrhenius form exp(-Qv/kT). Higher T reduces Qv/kT, so exponential term rises, increasing equilibrium vacancies.

Solution 2

(a) Grain-size strengthening: boundaries block slip transfer. (b) Solid-solution strengthening: solute strain fields interact with dislocation strain fields and resist movement.

Solution 3

Larger grains reduce boundary density, lowering dislocation barriers and shrinking Hall-Petch strengthening contribution.
