Week 10 Deep Dive: Magnetic Properties

Outline mapping: Ch20 §20.1-20.12.

Full scaffold

  1. Classify magnetic response type first.
  2. Read B-H loop features (Br, Hc, saturation trend).
  3. Estimate hysteresis energy from loop area.
  4. Map application: AC core vs permanent magnet vs sensor element.
  5. Select based on losses, stability, and manufacturability.

Core equations

M = chi*H
B = mu0*(H + M)

Loop area corresponds to energy loss per magnetization cycle per unit volume.

Worked example

Material A and B have similar saturation induction, but A has 35% smaller hysteresis loop area. For transformer core duty, A is preferred due to lower cyclic loss and lower heat generation.

Design note: high-frequency operation amplifies impact of hysteresis and eddy-current losses.

Application mapping

  • Soft magnetic: low coercivity, narrow loop, low loss.
  • Hard magnetic: high coercivity and remanence, stable retained flux.
  • Tradeoff: materials optimized for one role are often poor for the other.

Quiz (with solutions)

  1. Why is loop area a practical thermal design concern in magnetic cores?
  2. A material has very high Br but low Hc. Is it automatically a good permanent magnet?
  3. Why do AC magnetic devices usually avoid very hard magnetic materials?
Solution 1

Low coercivity means less field needed for reversal and lower hysteresis losses in cyclic operation.

Solution 2

Not automatically. Low Hc means it is easier to demagnetize; stable permanent magnets need sufficiently high coercivity.

Solution 3

Hard magnetic materials require larger reversing fields and can produce higher cyclic losses in core-type duties.
