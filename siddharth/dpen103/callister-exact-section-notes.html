How to Think Through Any Materials Problem

Scaffolded reasoning template

  1. Classify the material: metal, ceramic, polymer, composite.
  2. Identify structure level: atomic bonding, crystal/amorphous, microstructure, defects.
  3. Choose governing property equation: mechanical, thermal, electrical, magnetic, or phase-fraction relation.
  4. Map process -> structure -> property: ask what processing changed and which structure variable moved.
  5. Check realism: units, magnitude, and whether trend matches physical intuition.
Use this on every tutorial question. If you skip steps 1-2 and jump to equations, you usually lose marks when the question changes context.

Week 1 Lesson — Ch1, Ch2, Ch3

Design mindsetBondingCrystal geometry

Learning outcomes

  • Explain why the same load case needs different materials in different environments.
  • Predict broad behavior from bonding type (stiffness, melting point, conductivity, brittleness).
  • Compute crystal density and APF from unit-cell geometry.

Intuition build

Bonding sets how strongly atoms resist separation and sliding. Strong directional bonds (ionic/covalent) generally increase stiffness and melting point but reduce easy slip, so brittleness rises. Metallic bonding supports electron mobility and dislocation motion, giving conductivity and ductility.

\[ \rho=\frac{nA}{V_cN_A} \]
Worked derivation: APF for FCC

1) FCC has \(n=4\) atoms/cell. 2) Face diagonal relation gives \(4R=\sqrt{2}a\). 3) So \(a=2\sqrt{2}R\). 4) Atomic volume in cell is \(4(4/3)\pi R^3\). 5) Cell volume is \(a^3\). Therefore:

\[ APF_{FCC}=\frac{4(4/3)\pi R^3}{(2\sqrt{2}R)^3}=\frac{\pi}{3\sqrt2}\approx0.74 \]
Worked example: density from BCC data

Given \(n=2\), \(A=55.85\ \text{g/mol}\), \(a=0.286\ \text{nm}\):

\[ \rho=\frac{2(55.85)}{(0.286\times10^{-7})^3(6.022\times10^{23})}\approx 7.86\ \text{g/cm}^3 \]

Check: result is close to iron density, so order of magnitude is plausible.

Structure to property chain

Practice prompts

  1. Explain why ionic ceramics can have high modulus but low tensile fracture strain.
  2. Given \(a\) and \(A\), compute density for FCC and compare with BCC assumption.

Week 2 Lesson — Ch6 Mechanical Properties of Metals

Stress-strainYieldTrue vs engineering values

Guided problem flow

  1. Plot/inspect stress-strain curve.
  2. Mark elastic region and slope \(E\).
  3. Find yield method used (offset or distinct yield).
  4. Compute toughness/resilience as area-under-curve concepts.
  5. Convert to true stress-strain if large plastic strains are involved.
\[ \sigma=\frac{F}{A_0},\ \varepsilon=\frac{\Delta L}{L_0},\ \sigma_T=\sigma(1+\varepsilon),\ \varepsilon_T=\ln(1+\varepsilon) \]
Derivation: modulus of resilience
\[ U_r=\int_0^{\varepsilon_y}\sigma\,d\varepsilon =\int_0^{\varepsilon_y}E\varepsilon\,d\varepsilon =\frac{1}{2}E\varepsilon_y^2 =\frac{\sigma_y^2}{2E} \]

Interpretation: materials with high \(\sigma_y\) and moderate \(E\) can absorb more elastic energy before yielding.

Worked example: convert and interpret

If engineering strain is \(0.16\) at engineering stress \(360\ \text{MPa}\):

\[ \sigma_T=360(1+0.16)=417.6\ \text{MPa},\quad \varepsilon_T=\ln(1.16)=0.148 \]

True stress is higher because actual area is smaller during plastic extension.

Common pitfall: mixing engineering stress with true strain in one constitutive equation.

Week 3 Lesson — Ch4 and Ch7 (selected)

What defects do to strength

Defects are not just "imperfections"; they are the mechanism by which materials deform and fail. Vacancies control diffusion; dislocations control plastic flow; grain boundaries block dislocation motion.

\[ n_v=N\exp\!\left(-\frac{Q_v}{kT}\right) \]
\[ \sigma_y=\sigma_0+k_yd^{-1/2} \quad\text{(Hall-Petch)} \]
How to reason with Hall-Petch
  1. If grain size \(d\) decreases, \(d^{-1/2}\) increases.
  2. The additive strengthening term increases.
  3. Yield stress rises; ductility may reduce depending on other mechanisms.

Dislocation pile-up at boundary

Practice prompts

  1. Explain why diffusion-controlled processes accelerate strongly with temperature.
  2. Compare two steels with grain sizes \(d_1\) and \(4d_1\). Estimate relative Hall-Petch term.

Week 4 Lesson — Ch7.8-7.9 and Ch8.1-8.8

Fracture mechanics bridge

Classical stress methods fail when cracks exist. Fracture mechanics introduces crack size and toughness into design decisions.

\[ K_I=Y\sigma\sqrt{\pi a},\quad \text{fracture when }K_I\ge K_{IC} \]
Derivation: allowable stress for a known crack
\[ \sigma_{allow}=\frac{K_{IC}}{Y\sqrt{\pi a}} \]

This equation is a design filter: increasing \(K_{IC}\) or reducing \(a\) increases safe load capacity.

Fatigue thinking scaffold
  1. Identify stress amplitude and mean stress.
  2. Use S-N curve for life estimate \(N_f\).
  3. Adjust expectation based on surface finish, residual stress, environment, and notch severity.
  4. If crack exists, switch to crack-growth perspective.
In fatigue, failures occur below yield stress. "No yielding observed" does not mean "safe forever."

Week 5 Lesson — Ch8.9-8.15, Ch9.1-9.6, Ch19.1-19.4

Creep

Primary: strain rate decreases. Secondary: approximately constant rate, often design-limiting. Tertiary: accelerating damage toward rupture.

Use in design

  • Match service temperature to homologous temperature concerns.
  • Choose alloy/microstructure with lower steady-state creep rate.

Phase terminology (foundation before lever rule week)

  • Phase: homogeneous region.
  • Microstructure: arrangement, morphology, and amount of phases.
  • Equilibrium: no net driving force for phase amount/composition change.
\[ \Delta L=\alpha L_0\Delta T,\quad q_x=-kA\frac{dT}{dx} \]

Creep curve components

Practice prompts

  1. A bar is constrained and heated; discuss when thermal stress arises and when it does not.
  2. Explain why two alloys with similar room-temperature strength can have very different creep life.

Week 6 Lesson — Ch9.7-9.14 Binary Phase Diagrams

Lever rule from mass balance (must know derivation)

In a two-phase region (\(\alpha+\beta\)) at fixed \(T\), the tie-line endpoints provide \(C_\alpha\) and \(C_\beta\). Overall composition is \(C_0\). Mass conservation gives the phase fractions.

\[ W_\alpha+W_\beta=1,\quad C_0=W_\alpha C_\alpha+W_\beta C_\beta \]
\[ W_\alpha=\frac{C_\beta-C_0}{C_\beta-C_\alpha},\quad W_\beta=\frac{C_0-C_\alpha}{C_\beta-C_\alpha} \]
Worked example with numbers

If tie-line gives \(C_\alpha=20\%\), \(C_\beta=80\%\), and alloy \(C_0=35\%\):

\[ W_\alpha=\frac{80-35}{80-20}=\frac{45}{60}=0.75,\quad W_\beta=\frac{35-20}{80-20}=0.25 \]

Interpretation: 75% alpha phase, 25% beta phase by mass.

Tie-line and lever arms

Exam trap: reading tie-line endpoints from wrong phase boundary or wrong temperature.

Week 7 Lesson — Ch14 and Ch15 Polymers

Scaffold: from molecule to behavior

  1. Identify chain architecture (linear, branched, crosslinked).
  2. Identify polymer class (thermoplastic, thermoset, elastomer).
  3. Locate operating temperature relative to \(T_g\) and \(T_m\).
  4. Predict viscoelastic and fracture response.
\[ DP_n=\frac{M_n}{m} \]
\[ X_c=\frac{\rho_c(\rho_s-\rho_a)}{\rho_s(\rho_c-\rho_a)} \]
Worked example: estimate degree of polymerization

If \(M_n=120{,}000\ \text{g/mol}\) and repeat-unit mass \(m=100\ \text{g/mol}\), then:

\[ DP_n=120{,}000/100=1200 \]

Longer chains generally raise entanglement and can increase toughness and viscosity during processing.

Practice prompts

  1. Explain why a polymer can be tough at one temperature and brittle at a lower one.
  2. Compare expected behavior of a heavily crosslinked network vs a linear thermoplastic.

Week 9 Lesson — Ch2.2-2.3 + Ch18.1-18.17 Electrical Properties

Core electrical model

Electrical response in solids can be reasoned through carrier concentration \(n\), charge \(q\), and mobility \(\mu\).

\[ \sigma=nq\mu,\quad \rho=\frac{1}{\sigma},\quad J=\sigma E \]
Derivation: conductivity relation

Carrier charge density is \(nq\). Under field \(E\), average drift velocity is \(v_d=\mu E\). Current density is \(J=nqv_d=nq\mu E\). By Ohm form \(J=\sigma E\), so \(\sigma=nq\mu\).

\[ R_H=\frac{E_H}{JB}\approx -\frac{1}{nq} \]
When conductivity drops with temperature for metals but rises for semiconductors, do not panic: mechanisms are different.

Week 11 Lesson — Ch16 Composites

Design logic for composites

Composites are not "strong because mixed"; they are engineered so load transfers from matrix to reinforcement effectively in desired directions.

\[ E_L=E_fV_f+E_mV_m \]
\[ \frac{1}{E_T}=\frac{V_f}{E_f}+\frac{V_m}{E_m} \]
\[ l_c=\frac{\sigma_f^\*d}{2\tau_c} \]
Worked example: longitudinal modulus

Given \(E_f=230\ \text{GPa}\), \(E_m=3\ \text{GPa}\), \(V_f=0.55\), \(V_m=0.45\):

\[ E_L=230(0.55)+3(0.45)=127.85\ \text{GPa} \]
Fiber orientation controls anisotropy. A laminate can be very stiff in one direction and weak in another.

Week 12 Lesson — Ch12 Ceramics (selected sections)

Scaffold from structure to brittle failure

  1. Identify ceramic bonding/structure features.
  2. Recognize limited plasticity mechanisms at room temperature.
  3. Treat flaws as dominant strength-controlling variables.
  4. Apply fracture relation to estimate allowable stress.
\[ \sigma_f\approx\frac{K_{IC}}{Y\sqrt{\pi a}} \]
Worked sizing thought process

If a design review discovers flaw size doubled, allowable tensile stress scales with \(1/\sqrt{a}\), so allowable stress drops by \(1/\sqrt{2}\) (about 29% reduction).

Practice prompts

  1. Explain why ceramic compressive components are common but high-tension ceramic parts are risky.
  2. List two process improvements that can raise ceramic reliability by reducing effective flaw severity.
