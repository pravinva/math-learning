Week 7 Deep Dive: Polymers Structure and Properties

Outline mapping: Ch14 §14.1-14.12; Ch15 §15.1-15.16.

Full scaffold

  1. Identify polymer family and chain architecture.
  2. Locate operating temperature against Tg and Tm.
  3. Estimate chain mobility and expected stress-strain shape.
  4. Account for time effects (creep, stress relaxation).
  5. Connect processing choices to final microstructure/crystallinity.

Core equations

Degree of polymerization: DPn = Mn / m
Crystallinity estimate (density method): Xc = [rho_c*(rho_s-rho_a)]/[rho_s*(rho_c-rho_a)]

These relations connect molecular scale and structure to observed behavior.

Worked example 1

Mn=120000 g/mol, repeat unit m=100 g/mol => DPn=1200.

Interpretation: long chains increase entanglement, often improving toughness but also increasing melt viscosity during processing.

Worked example 2 (service temperature logic)

Polymer A has Tg=80 C and operates at 25 C -> glassy tendency. Polymer B has Tg=-20 C at same service -> rubbery/compliant tendency. This single comparison explains major differences in stiffness and impact response.

Pitfall: do not discuss polymer performance without stating service temperature relative to Tg.

Quiz (with solutions)

  1. Two polymers have identical composition but different crosslink density. Why can their processing and stiffness differ greatly?
  2. What happens to expected brittleness when service temperature moves far below Tg?
  3. Why must designers consider creep for polymer components under constant load?
Solution 1

Because their Tg values differ relative to room temperature; above Tg is more rubbery, below Tg more glassy.

Solution 2

Below Tg, chain mobility drops, so the polymer behaves stiffer and usually more brittle.

Solution 3

Creep can increase strain over time even at constant stress, causing dimensional drift, seal loss, or misalignment in long-service parts.
