Week 2 Deep Dive: Mechanical Properties of Metals

Outline mapping: Ch6 §6.1-6.12.

Scaffolded solution flow

  1. Plot/inspect stress-strain curve and identify elastic region.
  2. Extract modulus from slope.
  3. Identify yield criterion and plastic zone.
  4. Compute resilience/toughness from area-under-curve concepts.
  5. Use true stress/strain when large plastic deformation matters.

Core explanation

Engineering stress uses original area. True stress tracks actual area reduction during deformation, so it is better for large plastic strains. Resilience is recoverable elastic energy density; toughness is total energy to fracture.

Design/safety factors are used because material properties vary with processing, defects, and statistical scatter.

Worked example

Given engineering stress = 360 MPa, engineering strain = 0.16:

True stress = 360*(1+0.16)=417.6 MPa. True strain = ln(1.16)=0.148.

Interpretation: true stress > engineering stress because area has contracted.

Quiz (with full worked solutions)

  1. Why is modulus mostly insensitive to small porosity compared with strength?
  2. A sample has yield stress 450 MPa and E=210 GPa. Compute modulus of resilience.
  3. Why is using only ultimate tensile strength risky for fatigue design?
Solution 1

Elastic modulus is governed by bond stiffness and atomic spacing. Small defect populations can strongly affect crack initiation/strength but often have weaker first-order effect on linear elastic slope.

Solution 2

Resilience Ur = sigma_y^2/(2E) = (450e6)^2 /(2*210e9) ≈ 4.82e5 J/m^3.

Solution 3

Fatigue failure can occur below yield/UTS due to cyclic crack initiation and propagation. Need S-N or crack-growth methods, not static strength alone.
