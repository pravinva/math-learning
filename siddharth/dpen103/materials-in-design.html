Crystal structures · defects · bonding types · atomic packing

Crystal Structures

Most engineering metals are crystalline — atoms arranged in a repeating 3D pattern called a crystal lattice. The smallest repeating unit is the unit cell. Three common metallic crystal structures:

1. Face-Centered Cubic (FCC)

  • Atoms at 8 corners + 6 face centers
  • Atoms per cell: $N = 8 \times \frac{1}{8} + 6 \times \frac{1}{2} = 4$
  • Coordination number (nearest neighbors): 12
  • Atomic packing factor: APF = 0.74 (densest packing)
  • Lattice parameter vs radius: $a = 2\sqrt{2}R$
  • Examples: Al, Cu, Au, Ag, Ni, Pb

2. Body-Centered Cubic (BCC)

  • Atoms at 8 corners + 1 center
  • Atoms per cell: $N = 8 \times \frac{1}{8} + 1 = 2$
  • Coordination number: 8
  • APF = 0.68
  • Lattice parameter: $a = \frac{4R}{\sqrt{3}}$
  • Examples: Fe (α-ferrite), Cr, W, Mo, V

3. Hexagonal Close-Packed (HCP)

  • Two hexagonal layers with intermediate layer offset
  • Atoms per cell: 6
  • Coordination number: 12
  • APF = 0.74 (same as FCC)
  • Ideal c/a ratio = 1.633
  • Examples: Mg, Zn, Ti, Co, Zr
Atomic Packing Factor (APF)
$$\text{APF} = \frac{\text{Volume of atoms in unit cell}}{\text{Volume of unit cell}} = \frac{N \times \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3}{V_{\text{cell}}}$$

Theoretical Density

The density of a crystal can be calculated from the unit cell:

$$\rho = \frac{n \cdot A}{V_c \cdot N_A}$$

where $n$ = atoms per cell, $A$ = atomic mass (g/mol), $V_c$ = cell volume (cm³), $N_A$ = Avogadro's number

Miller Indices and Crystallographic Planes

Miller indices $(hkl)$ describe crystallographic planes and directions using the reciprocal intercepts with the three axes.

Procedure to find Miller indices:

  1. Find intercepts of the plane with x, y, z axes (use ∞ if parallel)
  2. Take reciprocals of intercepts
  3. Clear fractions to get smallest integers
  4. Enclose in parentheses: $(hkl)$

Important families of planes:

  • $(100)$, $(010)$, $(001)$ — cube faces
  • $(110)$, $(101)$, $(011)$ — diagonal planes
  • $(111)$ — octahedral plane (densest in FCC)
Notation: $(hkl)$ = specific plane; $\{hkl\}$ = family of equivalent planes; $[uvw]$ = direction; $\langle uvw \rangle$ = family of equivalent directions

Crystal Defects

Point Defects (0D)

  • Vacancy: Missing atom from lattice site — creates lower density
  • Interstitial: Extra atom squeezed into interstitial site — increases density
  • Substitutional impurity: Foreign atom replaces host atom (size similar)
  • Frenkel defect: Atom displaced from lattice → interstitial (vacancy-interstitial pair)
  • Schottky defect: In ionic crystals — cation + anion vacancy pair (maintains charge neutrality)
Equilibrium Vacancy Concentration
$$N_v = N \exp\left(-\frac{Q_v}{kT}\right)$$

$Q_v$ = activation energy for vacancy formation, $k$ = Boltzmann constant, $T$ = absolute temperature

Line Defects (1D) — Dislocations

Dislocations are line defects where atoms are misaligned. They are the primary mechanism of plastic deformation.

  • Edge dislocation: Extra half-plane of atoms inserted ⊥ to slip plane
  • Screw dislocation: Atoms spiral around dislocation line like a parking garage ramp
  • Burgers vector $\vec{b}$: Magnitude and direction of lattice distortion

Planar Defects (2D)

  • Grain boundaries: Interface between two grains (crystals) with different orientations
  • Twin boundaries: Mirror-image crystal orientations across a plane
  • Stacking faults: Error in stacking sequence of close-packed planes

Bonding Types

1. Metallic Bonding

  • Valence electrons delocalized in "electron sea"
  • Non-directional → permits plastic deformation via slip
  • High electrical & thermal conductivity
  • Examples: Fe, Al, Cu

2. Ionic Bonding

  • Electrostatic attraction between cations (+) and anions (−)
  • Non-directional but brittle (like charges repel during shear)
  • Typically insulators; high melting points
  • Examples: NaCl, MgO, Al₂O₃

3. Covalent Bonding

  • Electron pairs shared between atoms
  • Directional — creates strong, rigid structures
  • Hard, brittle, high melting point
  • Examples: diamond, SiC, Si₃N₄

4. van der Waals Bonding

  • Weak secondary bonds (dipole interactions)
  • Low melting points, soft
  • Examples: polymers, graphite layers
Engineering significance of defects: Vacancies enhance diffusion. Dislocations control strength and ductility. Grain boundaries impede dislocation motion (Hall-Petch strengthening). Interstitials increase resistivity.
Worked Example 1.1 — FCC Atomic Packing Factor

Derive the atomic packing factor for FCC. Show that APF = 0.74.

Step 1 — Atoms per cell

FCC: $N = 8 \times \frac{1}{8} + 6 \times \frac{1}{2} = 4$ atoms

Step 2 — Volume of atoms

$$V_{\text{atoms}} = 4 \times \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3 = \frac{16}{3}\pi R^3$$

Step 3 — Lattice parameter

Atoms touch along face diagonal: $4R = a\sqrt{2} \implies a = 2\sqrt{2}R$

$$V_{\text{cell}} = a^3 = (2\sqrt{2}R)^3 = 16\sqrt{2}R^3$$

Step 4 — APF

$$\text{APF} = \frac{16\pi R^3 / 3}{16\sqrt{2}R^3} = \frac{\pi}{3\sqrt{2}} = 0.7405 \approx 0.74$$

Worked Example 1.2 — Theoretical Density of Copper

Copper (Cu) has FCC structure with $a = 0.3615$ nm, atomic mass $A = 63.5$ g/mol. Calculate theoretical density.

Given

Volume of unit cell

$$V_c = a^3 = (3.615 \times 10^{-8})^3 = 4.724 \times 10^{-23} \text{ cm}^3$$

Density

$$\rho = \frac{4 \times 63.5}{4.724 \times 10^{-23} \times 6.022 \times 10^{23}} = \frac{254}{28.46} = 8.93 \text{ g/cm}^3$$

This matches the experimental density of copper.

Worked Example 1.4 — Vacancy Concentration

Calculate the equilibrium number of vacancies in 1 cm³ of copper at 1000°C. Given: $Q_v = 0.9$ eV, density = $8.4$ g/cm³ at this T, atomic mass = 63.5 g/mol.

Step 1 — Total atoms in 1 cm³

$$N = \frac{\rho \cdot N_A}{A} = \frac{8.4 \times 6.022 \times 10^{23}}{63.5} = 7.97 \times 10^{22}$$

Step 2 — Convert temperature and energy

$T = 1000 + 273 = 1273$ K, $k = 8.617 \times 10^{-5}$ eV/K

$$\frac{Q_v}{kT} = \frac{0.9}{8.617 \times 10^{-5} \times 1273} = 8.21$$

Step 3 — Vacancy concentration

$$N_v = N \exp(-8.21) = 7.97 \times 10^{22} \times 2.73 \times 10^{-4} = 2.18 \times 10^{19}$$

About 1 in 3660 sites is vacant at 1000°C.

Stress and Strain — Fundamental Definitions

Engineering Stress

Force per unit original cross-sectional area:

$$\sigma = \frac{F}{A_0} \quad \text{(units: Pa or MPa)}$$

Tensile stress: positive (pulling). Compressive stress: negative (pushing).

Engineering Strain

Change in length divided by original length (dimensionless):

$$\varepsilon = \frac{\Delta L}{L_0} = \frac{L_i - L_0}{L_0}$$

True Stress and True Strain

At large deformations, engineering stress/strain become inaccurate. True values use instantaneous dimensions:

$$\sigma_T = \frac{F}{A_i} = \sigma(1 + \varepsilon)$$ $$\varepsilon_T = \ln\left(\frac{L_i}{L_0}\right) = \ln(1 + \varepsilon)$$

The Stress-Strain Curve

A tensile test gradually increases load while measuring elongation. Key regions:

1. Elastic Region

  • Linear relationship: $\sigma = E\varepsilon$ (Hooke's Law)
  • Young's modulus $E$ = slope = stiffness (GPa)
  • Deformation is reversible — remove load, material returns to original shape
  • For metals: $E$ ranges 70 GPa (Al) to 400 GPa (W)

2. Yield Point

  • Yield strength $\sigma_y$: stress at which permanent deformation begins
  • Defined by 0.2% offset method (draw line parallel to elastic slope from $\varepsilon = 0.002$)
  • Typical values: 200–600 MPa for steels, 100–500 MPa for Al alloys

3. Plastic Region

  • Material undergoes permanent, irreversible deformation
  • Strain hardening: material becomes stronger as it deforms
  • Ultimate Tensile Strength (UTS): maximum stress on engineering curve
  • After UTS, necking begins (localized thinning)

4. Fracture

  • Material separates into two pieces
  • Ductile fracture: significant necking, cup-and-cone surface
  • Brittle fracture: little/no plastic deformation, flat surface
Hooke's Law
$$\sigma = E\varepsilon \quad \text{(valid only in elastic region)}$$

Elastic Properties

Poisson's Ratio

When a material is stretched in one direction, it contracts in perpendicular directions:

$$\nu = -\frac{\varepsilon_{\text{lateral}}}{\varepsilon_{\text{axial}}}$$

Typical values: $\nu \approx 0.25$ to 0.35 for most metals. Perfectly incompressible: $\nu = 0.5$.

Modulus of Resilience

Energy absorbed per unit volume during elastic deformation (area under elastic portion of curve):

$$U_r = \frac{\sigma_y^2}{2E}$$

Plastic Properties

Ductility

Ability to deform plastically before fracture. Measured by:

$$\%EL = \frac{L_f - L_0}{L_0} \times 100\% \quad \text{(percent elongation)}$$ $$\%RA = \frac{A_0 - A_f}{A_0} \times 100\% \quad \text{(percent reduction in area)}$$

Ductile: %EL > 5%. Brittle: %EL < 5%.

Toughness

Total energy absorbed per unit volume before fracture (total area under stress-strain curve). High toughness requires both strength AND ductility.

Stiffness vs Strength vs Hardness: Stiffness = resistance to elastic deformation (E). Strength = resistance to plastic deformation ($\sigma_y$). Hardness = resistance to localized plastic deformation (indentation).

Hardness Testing

Hardness measures resistance to permanent indentation. Common tests:

Brinell Hardness (HB)

  • 10 mm hardened steel ball pressed into surface
  • HB = Load / Surface area of indentation
  • Good for coarse materials, not surface-sensitive

Vickers Hardness (HV)

  • Diamond pyramid indenter (136° angle)
  • HV = Load / Surface area of pyramidal indentation
  • Can test very thin sections and surface layers

Rockwell Hardness (HR)

  • Measures depth of indentation
  • HRA, HRB, HRC scales for different materials
  • Fast, direct reading, widely used in industry
Approximate Correlation
$$\text{UTS (MPa)} \approx 3.45 \times \text{HB}$$

Temperature and Strain Rate Effects

  • Increasing temperature: decreases $E$, $\sigma_y$, UTS; increases ductility
  • Increasing strain rate: increases $\sigma_y$ and UTS; decreases ductility
  • At high T (> 0.4 $T_m$): creep becomes significant
Worked Example 2.1 — Stress and Strain Calculation

A steel rod with diameter 10 mm and length 2 m is subjected to a tensile load of 20 kN. The rod elongates by 2.6 mm. Calculate: (a) engineering stress; (b) engineering strain; (c) Young's modulus.

(a) Engineering Stress

$$A_0 = \frac{\pi d^2}{4} = \frac{\pi (0.01)^2}{4} = 7.854 \times 10^{-5} \text{ m}^2$$

$$\sigma = \frac{F}{A_0} = \frac{20000}{7.854 \times 10^{-5}} = 254.6 \text{ MPa}$$

(b) Engineering Strain

$$\varepsilon = \frac{\Delta L}{L_0} = \frac{0.0026}{2} = 0.0013 = 0.13\%$$

(c) Young's Modulus

$$E = \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon} = \frac{254.6}{0.0013} = 195.8 \text{ GPa}$$

This is consistent with typical values for steel (200–210 GPa).

Worked Example 2.2 — True Stress and Strain

A specimen with $A_0 = 100$ mm² is stretched to $A_i = 85$ mm² under load $F = 30$ kN. The original length was 50 mm and current length is 58 mm. Calculate: (a) engineering stress and strain; (b) true stress and strain.

(a) Engineering Values

$$\sigma = \frac{30000}{100 \times 10^{-6}} = 300 \text{ MPa}$$

$$\varepsilon = \frac{58 - 50}{50} = 0.16$$

(b) True Values

$$\sigma_T = \frac{30000}{85 \times 10^{-6}} = 353 \text{ MPa}$$

Or: $\sigma_T = \sigma(1 + \varepsilon) = 300(1.16) = 348$ MPa (slight difference due to rounding)

$$\varepsilon_T = \ln\left(\frac{58}{50}\right) = \ln(1.16) = 0.148$$

Worked Example 2.3 — Poisson's Ratio

A cylindrical specimen with diameter 10 mm is loaded in tension. Under load, the diameter decreases to 9.997 mm and length increases from 100 mm to 100.2 mm. Calculate Poisson's ratio.

Axial Strain

$$\varepsilon_{\text{axial}} = \frac{100.2 - 100}{100} = 0.002$$

Lateral Strain

$$\varepsilon_{\text{lateral}} = \frac{9.997 - 10}{10} = -0.0003$$

Poisson's Ratio

$$\nu = -\frac{\varepsilon_{\text{lateral}}}{\varepsilon_{\text{axial}}} = -\frac{-0.0003}{0.002} = 0.15$$

Worked Example 2.4 — Modulus of Resilience

A steel has $E = 207$ GPa and $\sigma_y = 450$ MPa. Calculate the modulus of resilience.

Apply Formula

$$U_r = \frac{\sigma_y^2}{2E} = \frac{(450 \times 10^6)^2}{2 \times 207 \times 10^9}$$

$$= \frac{2.025 \times 10^{17}}{4.14 \times 10^{11}} = 4.89 \times 10^5 \text{ J/m}^3 = 0.49 \text{ MJ/m}^3$$

Worked Example 2.5 — Ductility Calculation

A tensile specimen has $L_0 = 50$ mm, $A_0 = 200$ mm². After fracture: $L_f = 62.5$ mm, $A_f = 135$ mm². Calculate %EL and %RA.

Percent Elongation

$$\%EL = \frac{62.5 - 50}{50} \times 100 = 25\%$$

Percent Reduction in Area

$$\%RA = \frac{200 - 135}{200} \times 100 = 32.5\%$$

This material is ductile (%EL > 5%).

Worked Example 2.6 — Hardness to Tensile Strength

A steel component has Brinell hardness HB = 250. Estimate the ultimate tensile strength.

Use Empirical Correlation

$$\text{UTS} \approx 3.45 \times \text{HB} = 3.45 \times 250 = 862.5 \text{ MPa}$$

Therefore, UTS ≈ 860 MPa.

Failure: Fracture & Fatigue
Fracture mechanics · stress intensity · fatigue life · creep

Fracture Mechanics

Ductile vs Brittle Fracture

Ductile fracture:

  • Significant plastic deformation before fracture
  • Cup-and-cone morphology (necking visible)
  • Fibrous, dull fracture surface
  • Typical of FCC metals at room temperature
  • Slow, stable crack growth

Brittle fracture:

  • Little or no plastic deformation
  • Flat, perpendicular to applied stress
  • Shiny, granular surface with cleavage facets
  • Typical of ceramics, BCC metals at low T
  • Fast, unstable crack propagation
Ductile-to-brittle transition: Many BCC metals (e.g., steel) become brittle below a critical temperature. This caused the Titanic disaster and failures of Liberty ships in WWII.

Stress Concentration

Cracks, holes, and notches create localized stress amplification:

$$\sigma_{\text{max}} = K_t \cdot \sigma_0$$

$K_t$ = stress concentration factor (geometry-dependent), $\sigma_0$ = nominal stress

For an elliptical crack: $K_t = 1 + 2\sqrt{a/\rho_t}$ where $a$ = crack length, $\rho_t$ = radius of curvature at crack tip.

Griffith Theory

Cracks propagate when the energy released exceeds the energy required to create new crack surfaces:

$$\sigma_c = \sqrt{\frac{2E\gamma_s}{\pi a}}$$

$\gamma_s$ = surface energy, $a$ = half crack length, $E$ = Young's modulus

Key insight: Strength decreases with $1/\sqrt{a}$ — longer cracks propagate at lower stress.

Fracture Toughness

Modern approach uses stress intensity factor $K$:

$$K = Y\sigma\sqrt{\pi a}$$

$Y$ = geometry factor (≈1 for through-thickness crack), $\sigma$ = applied stress, $a$ = crack half-length

Fracture toughness $K_{IC}$: Material property — critical stress intensity for unstable crack growth (plane strain conditions).

Design Criterion
$$K < K_{IC} \quad \text{(safe)} \qquad K \geq K_{IC} \quad \text{(catastrophic failure)}$$

Units: MPa√m or ksi√in. Typical values:

Material$K_{IC}$ (MPa√m)
Aluminum alloys20–40
Steels50–150
Ceramics3–5
Polymers0.5–3

Fatigue

Fatigue is failure under cyclic (repeated) loading at stresses below $\sigma_y$. Accounts for ~90% of all mechanical failures in service.

Fatigue Parameters

$$\sigma_{\text{mean}} = \frac{\sigma_{\max} + \sigma_{\min}}{2}$$ $$\sigma_a = \frac{\sigma_{\max} - \sigma_{\min}}{2} \quad \text{(stress amplitude)}$$ $$R = \frac{\sigma_{\min}}{\sigma_{\max}} \quad \text{(stress ratio)}$$

S-N Curve (Wöhler Curve)

Plot of stress amplitude $S$ vs number of cycles to failure $N$ (log scale).

  • Ferrous alloys (steels): exhibit endurance limit — stress below which infinite life is possible
  • Non-ferrous alloys (Al, Cu): no true endurance limit — use fatigue strength at $10^7$ or $10^8$ cycles
Endurance limit for steels: $S_e \approx 0.5 \times \text{UTS}$ (for polished specimens). Surface finish, notches, and corrosion reduce this significantly.

Fatigue Life Prediction — Basquin's Law

$$\sigma_a = \sigma_f' (2N_f)^b$$

$\sigma_f'$ = fatigue strength coefficient, $b$ = fatigue strength exponent (≈−0.1), $N_f$ = cycles to failure

Three Stages of Fatigue Failure

  1. Crack initiation: Surface defect, slip band, or inclusion acts as stress concentrator
  2. Crack propagation: Slow, stable growth — creates beach marks (clamshell pattern)
  3. Final fracture: Sudden, rapid failure when crack reaches critical size

Creep

Time-dependent plastic deformation at elevated temperature (typically $T > 0.4T_m$). Important for turbines, reactors, furnaces.

Three Stages of Creep

  1. Primary creep: Strain rate decreases (strain hardening dominates)
  2. Secondary (steady-state) creep: Constant strain rate (balance of hardening and recovery)
  3. Tertiary creep: Accelerating strain rate → necking and fracture
Steady-State Creep Rate
$$\dot{\varepsilon}_s = A\sigma^n \exp\left(-\frac{Q_c}{RT}\right)$$

$A, n$ = material constants, $Q_c$ = activation energy, $R$ = gas constant, $T$ = absolute temperature

Worked Example 3.1 — Fracture Toughness Calculation

A large steel plate contains a through-thickness crack of length 40 mm. If the plate fractures at 300 MPa and $Y = 1.0$, calculate $K_{IC}$.

Given

$a = 20$ mm = 0.02 m (half-length), $\sigma_c = 300$ MPa, $Y = 1.0$

Apply Formula

$$K_{IC} = Y\sigma_c\sqrt{\pi a} = 1.0 \times 300 \times \sqrt{\pi \times 0.02}$$

$$= 300 \times \sqrt{0.0628} = 300 \times 0.2507 = 75.2 \text{ MPa}\sqrt{\text{m}}$$

Worked Example 3.2 — Maximum Allowable Crack Size

A steel component is subjected to 200 MPa stress. The steel has $K_{IC} = 50$ MPa√m. Using $Y = 1.12$, find the maximum tolerable through-thickness crack length.

Rearrange Formula

$$K_{IC} = Y\sigma\sqrt{\pi a} \implies a = \frac{1}{\pi}\left(\frac{K_{IC}}{Y\sigma}\right)^2$$

Substitute

$$a = \frac{1}{\pi}\left(\frac{50}{1.12 \times 200}\right)^2 = \frac{1}{3.14159}\left(\frac{50}{224}\right)^2$$

$$= \frac{1}{3.14159}(0.2232)^2 = \frac{0.0498}{3.14159} = 0.0158 \text{ m} = 15.8 \text{ mm}$$

Total crack length = $2a = 31.6$ mm.

Worked Example 3.3 — Fatigue Stress Parameters

A component experiences cyclic loading: $\sigma_{\max} = 180$ MPa, $\sigma_{\min} = -60$ MPa (compression). Calculate: (a) mean stress; (b) stress amplitude; (c) stress ratio $R$.

(a) Mean Stress

$$\sigma_m = \frac{180 + (-60)}{2} = \frac{120}{2} = 60 \text{ MPa}$$

(b) Stress Amplitude

$$\sigma_a = \frac{180 - (-60)}{2} = \frac{240}{2} = 120 \text{ MPa}$$

(c) Stress Ratio

$$R = \frac{\sigma_{\min}}{\sigma_{\max}} = \frac{-60}{180} = -0.33$$

This is a reversed loading condition ($R < 0$).

Worked Example 3.4 — Fatigue Life Using S-N Data

An S-N curve for a steel shows: at $N = 10^5$ cycles, $S = 400$ MPa; at $N = 10^6$ cycles, $S = 300$ MPa. Estimate cycles to failure at $S = 350$ MPa (assume log-log linear relationship).

Log-Log Relationship

$$\log S = -b \log N + C$$

Two points: $(10^5, 400)$ and $(10^6, 300)$

$$\log 400 = -b \times 5 + C$$

$$\log 300 = -b \times 6 + C$$

Solve for $b$

$$\log 400 - \log 300 = b$$

$$b = \log(400/300) = \log(1.333) = 0.125$$

Find $C$

$$\log 400 = -0.125 \times 5 + C \implies C = 2.602 + 0.625 = 3.227$$

Solve for $N$ at $S = 350$

$$\log 350 = -0.125 \log N + 3.227$$

$$2.544 = -0.125 \log N + 3.227$$

$$\log N = \frac{3.227 - 2.544}{0.125} = 5.46$$

$$N = 10^{5.46} = 2.9 \times 10^5 \text{ cycles}$$

Worked Example 3.5 — Griffith Crack Length

Calculate the critical crack length for soda-lime glass ($E = 69$ GPa, $\gamma_s = 0.3$ J/m²) under tensile stress of 50 MPa.

Griffith Equation

$$\sigma_c = \sqrt{\frac{2E\gamma_s}{\pi a}} \implies a = \frac{2E\gamma_s}{\pi \sigma_c^2}$$

Substitute

Overview of Strengthening

All strengthening mechanisms work by impeding dislocation motion. Dislocations are the carriers of plastic deformation — if you can stop dislocations from moving, you increase the material's yield strength.

Trade-off: Strengthening typically reduces ductility. A very strong material is often brittle. Engineers must balance strength and toughness for each application.

1. Grain Boundary Strengthening (Hall-Petch)

Grain boundaries act as barriers to dislocation motion. Dislocations cannot easily cross from one grain to another because the crystal orientations differ.

Hall-Petch Equation

$$\sigma_y = \sigma_0 + k_y d^{-1/2}$$

$\sigma_0$ = friction stress (lattice resistance), $k_y$ = Hall-Petch slope (material constant), $d$ = average grain diameter

Key insight: Finer grains ($d$ smaller) → more grain boundaries → higher $\sigma_y$.

Grain refinement methods:

  • Faster cooling rates during solidification
  • Controlled rolling/forging below recrystallization temperature
  • Addition of grain refiners (e.g., Al-Ti-B in aluminum)

2. Solid Solution Strengthening

Dissolved solute atoms create lattice distortions (strain fields) that interact with dislocation stress fields, making movement harder.

Two types:

  • Substitutional: Solute atom replaces host atom (similar size). Example: Cu in Al (brass)
  • Interstitial: Small atom fits into interstitial site. Example: C in Fe (steel)

Effectiveness: Interstitial strengthening is more potent (larger distortion). Carbon in steel is highly effective even at low concentrations.

$$\Delta \sigma_y \propto c^{n}$$

$c$ = solute concentration, $n$ ≈ 0.5–1.0 depending on solute type

3. Strain Hardening (Work Hardening / Cold Working)

Plastic deformation at $T < 0.3T_m$ increases dislocation density. Dislocations become tangled and block each other's motion.

Percent Cold Work

$$\%CW = \frac{A_0 - A_d}{A_0} \times 100\%$$

$A_0$ = original cross-sectional area, $A_d$ = deformed area

Effects of cold work:

  • Increases: $\sigma_y$, UTS, hardness
  • Decreases: ductility (%EL, %RA), electrical conductivity
  • Can lead to residual stresses and distortion

Recovery, Recrystallization, Grain Growth

Annealing at elevated temperature reverses cold work effects:

  1. Recovery (low T): Dislocation rearrangement, some stress relief, minor softening
  2. Recrystallization ($T \approx 0.3$–$0.5 T_m$): New strain-free grains nucleate and grow, replacing deformed structure. Significant softening, ductility restored.
  3. Grain growth (high T): Grains coarsen to reduce boundary area. Decreases strength (Hall-Petch reversed).

4. Precipitation Hardening (Age Hardening)

Coherent precipitate particles obstruct dislocation motion. This produces the highest strength in many Al and Ni alloys.

Three-Stage Process

  1. Solution treatment: Heat to single-phase region (e.g., 500–550°C for Al alloys), dissolve all precipitates → homogeneous solid solution.
  2. Quench: Rapid cool to room T → supersaturated solid solution (SSSS) — thermodynamically unstable.
  3. Ageing: Heat to intermediate T (e.g., 150–200°C). Solute atoms diffuse and form fine, coherent precipitates (e.g., θ″ or GP zones in Al-Cu).
Over-ageing: If aged too long or at too high T, precipitates coarsen and lose coherency → strength decreases. There is an optimal ageing time for peak strength.

Example systems:

AlloyPrecipitateApplication
Al-Cu (2xxx)θ′ (CuAl₂)Aircraft structures
Al-Mg-Si (6xxx)Mg₂SiExtrusions, automotive
Ni-based superalloysγ′ (Ni₃Al)Turbine blades
Combining Mechanisms

Strengthening effects are approximately additive:

$$\sigma_y \approx \sigma_0 + \Delta\sigma_{GB} + \Delta\sigma_{SS} + \Delta\sigma_{WH} + \Delta\sigma_{PPT}$$

Phase Diagrams — The Essentials

A phase diagram is a map showing which phases are stable at given temperature and composition (at constant pressure). Critical for understanding alloy behavior, heat treatment, and microstructure evolution.

Gibbs Phase Rule

$$F = C - P + 2$$

$F$ = degrees of freedom, $C$ = number of components, $P$ = number of phases

For isobaric (constant pressure) binary systems: $F = C - P + 1 = 2 - P + 1 = 3 - P$

Isomorphous Systems (Complete Solid Solubility)

Example: Cu-Ni. Two phases: liquid ($L$) and solid solution ($\alpha$).

  • Liquidus line: Above this, alloy is 100% liquid
  • Solidus line: Below this, alloy is 100% solid
  • Two-phase region: Between liquidus and solidus, $L + \alpha$ coexist

The Lever Rule

In a two-phase region, the lever rule calculates the weight fraction of each phase.

Lever Rule for Two Phases α and β
$$W_\alpha = \frac{C_\beta - C_0}{C_\beta - C_\alpha} \qquad W_\beta = \frac{C_0 - C_\alpha}{C_\beta - C_\alpha}$$

$C_0$ = overall composition, $C_\alpha, C_\beta$ = compositions of α and β at the given temperature (read from phase boundaries)

Lever analogy: Think of $C_0$ as the fulcrum of a lever. The fraction of each phase is inversely proportional to its distance from $C_0$.

Check: $W_\alpha + W_\beta = 1$ always. If not, you made an error.

Eutectic Systems

Example: Pb-Sn solder. Three solid phases: $\alpha$ (Pb-rich), $\beta$ (Sn-rich), liquid.

Eutectic Reaction

At eutectic composition and temperature:

$$L \xrightarrow{\text{cool}} \alpha + \beta \quad \text{(eutectic microstructure: fine lamellar mixture)}$$

Key features:

  • Eutectic composition: lowest melting point in the system
  • Eutectic microstructure: alternating layers of α and β (looks like fingerprint under microscope)
  • Hypoeutectic (left of eutectic): primary $\alpha$ + eutectic
  • Hypereutectic (right of eutectic): primary $\beta$ + eutectic

Fe-Fe₃C Phase Diagram (Iron-Carbon)

The most important phase diagram in materials science. Basis for understanding steel and cast iron.

Key Phases

Eutectoid Reaction (727°C, 0.76 wt% C)

$$\gamma_{(0.76\%C)} \xrightarrow{\text{cool}} \alpha_{(0.022\%C)} + \text{Fe}_3\text{C}_{(6.7\%C)} \quad \text{(product = pearlite)}$$

Pearlite: Lamellar structure of alternating ferrite and cementite layers. Named for its pearl-like luster under optical microscope.

Steel Classifications

  • Hypoeutectoid steel (C < 0.76%): Microstructure = proeutectoid ferrite + pearlite. Example: 0.4% C → soft, ductile
  • Eutectoid steel (C = 0.76%): 100% pearlite. Good balance of strength and toughness.
  • Hypereutectoid steel (0.76% < C < 2.11%): Proeutectoid cementite + pearlite. Example: 1.0% C → harder, less ductile
  • Cast iron (C > 2.11%): Contains graphite or cementite; brittle in tension

Heat Treatment of Steel

1. Annealing (Full Anneal)

  • Heat above A₃ (for hypoeutectoid) or A₁ (for hypereutectoid) → austenitize
  • Slow furnace cool → coarse pearlite
  • Result: Soft, ductile, low strength, easy to machine

2. Normalizing

  • Heat above A₃ → austenitize
  • Air cool (faster than anneal) → finer pearlite
  • Result: Refined grain structure, better mechanical properties than annealed

3. Quench & Temper

  • Quench: Rapid cool from austenite → martensite (body-centered tetragonal, super-saturated with C)
  • Martensite: Extremely hard (~65 HRC) but brittle
  • Temper: Reheat to 150–600°C → precipitate fine carbides → reduce hardness but increase toughness
  • Final product: Strong, tough, and ductile (balance depends on tempering T)
Martensite formation is diffusionless! Carbon atoms are trapped in interstitial sites, distorting the BCC lattice into BCT. This distortion causes extreme hardness and brittleness.

TTT Diagrams (Time-Temperature-Transformation)

TTT diagrams predict microstructure based on cooling rate. Key regions:

  • Nose: Minimum time for pearlite formation
  • Above nose: Coarse pearlite (slow cool)
  • Below nose but above M_s: Fine pearlite or bainite
  • Below M_s (martensite start): Martensite forms (time-independent)

Electrical Conductivity Fundamentals

Ohm's Law (Material Form)

$$J = \sigma E \qquad \text{or} \qquad E = \rho J$$

$J$ = current density (A/m²), $\sigma$ = conductivity (S/m), $\rho$ = resistivity (Ω·m), $E$ = electric field (V/m)

Circuit form: $V = IR$ where $R = \rho L / A$

Conductivity vs Resistivity

$$\sigma = \frac{1}{\rho}$$

Typical values:

Material$\sigma$ (S/m)$\rho$ (Ω·m)
Copper$6 \times 10^7$$1.7 \times 10^{-8}$
Silicon (intrinsic)$4 \times 10^{-4}$$2.3 \times 10^3$
Glass$10^{-11}$$10^{11}$

Band Theory

In solids, atomic orbitals overlap to form energy bands.

Conductors (Metals)

  • Valence band partially filled OR valence and conduction bands overlap
  • Electrons can move freely under applied field
  • $\sigma \sim 10^6$–$10^8$ S/m
  • $\sigma$ decreases with temperature (more phonon scattering)

Insulators

  • Wide band gap $E_g > 4$ eV
  • Valence band full, conduction band empty
  • Negligible thermal excitation at room T
  • $\sigma < 10^{-10}$ S/m

Semiconductors

  • Narrow band gap: Si = 1.12 eV, Ge = 0.67 eV, GaAs = 1.42 eV
  • At 0 K: insulator. At room T: some electrons thermally excited to conduction band
  • $\sigma \sim 10^{-6}$–$10^4$ S/m (tunable by doping)
  • $\sigma$ increases with temperature (more carriers)

Matthiessen's Rule

Total resistivity is the sum of contributions:

$$\rho_{\text{total}} = \rho_{\text{thermal}} + \rho_{\text{impurity}} + \rho_{\text{deformation}}$$
  • $\rho_{\text{thermal}}$: phonon scattering (increases with T)
  • $\rho_{\text{impurity}}$: impurity/solute atoms (independent of T)
  • $\rho_{\text{deformation}}$: dislocations from cold work

Intrinsic Semiconductors

Pure Si or Ge. Thermal excitation creates electron-hole pairs:

$$n = p = n_i$$

$n$ = electron concentration, $p$ = hole concentration, $n_i$ = intrinsic carrier concentration

$$n_i^2 = N_c N_v \exp\left(-\frac{E_g}{kT}\right)$$

$N_c, N_v$ = effective densities of states, $E_g$ = band gap

Conductivity:

$$\sigma = n|e|\mu_e + p|e|\mu_h = n_i |e|(\mu_e + \mu_h)$$

$|e| = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C, $\mu_e, \mu_h$ = electron and hole mobilities (m²/V·s)

Extrinsic Semiconductors (Doped)

n-type (Donor Doping)

  • Add Group V atoms (P, As, Sb) to Si
  • Extra electron weakly bound → easily ionized at room T
  • Majority carriers: electrons ($n \gg p$)
  • $n \approx N_d$ (donor concentration)

p-type (Acceptor Doping)

  • Add Group III atoms (B, Al, Ga) to Si
  • Creates hole (missing electron) → accepts electron from valence band
  • Majority carriers: holes ($p \gg n$)
  • $p \approx N_a$ (acceptor concentration)
Law of mass action: $np = n_i^2$ always holds (equilibrium condition). In n-type: $n \uparrow \implies p \downarrow$ to keep $np$ constant.

p-n Junction Diode

Junction between p-type and n-type creates a depletion region (no mobile carriers) and built-in potential.

Forward Bias (p +, n −)

  • Reduces depletion width
  • Lowers barrier → current flows exponentially with voltage
  • $I = I_0 [\exp(eV/kT) - 1]$

Reverse Bias (p −, n +)

  • Widens depletion region
  • Increases barrier → tiny reverse saturation current $I_0$
  • Used in rectifiers, photodiodes, LEDs

Fundamental Relationships

$$B = \mu_0(H + M)$$

$B$ = magnetic flux density (T), $H$ = magnetic field (A/m), $M$ = magnetization (A/m), $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}$ T·m/A

$$\chi_m = \frac{M}{H} \qquad \mu_r = 1 + \chi_m \qquad B = \mu_0 \mu_r H$$

$\chi_m$ = magnetic susceptibility, $\mu_r$ = relative permeability

Ferromagnetism and Domains

Below the Curie temperature $T_C$, ferromagnetic materials spontaneously develop magnetic domains — regions (~0.01–1 mm) where all atomic magnetic moments are aligned parallel.

Why domains form:

  • Minimizes magnetostatic energy (reduces external field)
  • Domains separated by domain walls (Bloch walls) ~100 nm thick

Magnetization process:

  1. Low field: Domain wall motion — favorably aligned domains grow at expense of others (reversible)
  2. Moderate field: Domain rotation — domains rotate toward field direction (irreversible)
  3. High field: Saturation — all domains aligned with field, $M = M_s$

Hysteresis Loop

Plot of $B$ vs $H$ (or $M$ vs $H$) during cyclic loading. Key parameters:

  • Saturation magnetization $M_s$: Maximum M when all domains aligned
  • Remanence (retentivity) $M_r$: M remaining when $H = 0$ (after saturation)
  • Coercivity $H_c$: Reverse field needed to reduce $M$ to zero
Area of hysteresis loop = energy dissipated per cycle (as heat). Wide loop = high energy loss.

Soft vs Hard Magnetic Materials

Soft Magnets (Low $H_c$, Narrow Loop)

  • Easy to magnetize and demagnetize
  • Low hysteresis loss → transformers, motors, inductors
  • Examples: Pure Fe, Fe-Si (electrical steel), Ni-Fe (permalloy), ferrites (MnZn, NiZn)
  • Typical $H_c < 1000$ A/m

Hard Magnets (High $H_c$, Wide Loop)

  • Difficult to demagnetize once magnetized
  • High remanence and coercivity → permanent magnets
  • Applications: Motors, generators, speakers, MRI, magnetic storage
  • Examples: Alnico (Al-Ni-Co), SmCo₅, Nd₂Fe₁₄B (strongest known permanent magnet)
  • Typical $H_c > 10^5$ A/m

Worked Example 7.1 — Magnetic Susceptibility

A material has relative permeability $\mu_r = 1200$. Calculate magnetic susceptibility $\chi_m$.

Relationship

$$\mu_r = 1 + \chi_m \implies \chi_m = \mu_r - 1 = 1200 - 1 = 1199$$

Large positive $\chi_m$ → ferromagnetic or ferrimagnetic material.

Worked Example 7.2 — Flux Density in a Magnetic Material

A magnetic field $H = 1000$ A/m is applied to a material with $\mu_r = 500$. Calculate $B$.

Apply Formula

$$B = \mu_0 \mu_r H = 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \times 500 \times 1000$$

$$= 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \times 5 \times 10^5 = 2\pi \times 10^{-1} = 0.628 \text{ T}$$

Worked Example 7.3 — Magnetization from B and H

In a ferromagnet, $B = 1.5$ T and $H = 200$ A/m. Calculate magnetization $M$.

Rearrange Fundamental Equation

$$B = \mu_0(H + M) \implies M = \frac{B}{\mu_0} - H$$

Substitute

$$M = \frac{1.5}{4\pi \times 10^{-7}} - 200 = \frac{1.5}{1.257 \times 10^{-6}} - 200$$

$$= 1.193 \times 10^6 - 200 \approx 1.193 \times 10^6 \text{ A/m}$$

M is ~6000× larger than H — typical for ferromagnets!

Worked Example 7.4 — Energy Loss per Cycle

A transformer core (volume 0.01 m³) has hysteresis loop area = 500 J/m³. Operating at 60 Hz, calculate power loss.

Energy per cycle

$$E = 500 \text{ J/m}^3 \times 0.01 \text{ m}^3 = 5 \text{ J/cycle}$$

Power (energy per second)

$$P = E \times f = 5 \times 60 = 300 \text{ W}$$

Significant! This is why soft magnets with narrow loops are essential for AC applications.

Metals

Most engineering structures rely on metals for strength, ductility, and formability.

Ferrous Alloys (Fe-based)

  • Plain-carbon steels: 0.05–2% C. Low C → ductile (construction). High C → hard (tools).
  • Alloy steels: Add Cr, Ni, Mo, V → enhance hardenability, corrosion resistance, high-T strength. Example: 4340 steel (Ni-Cr-Mo).
  • Stainless steels: >11% Cr → passive Cr₂O₃ layer. Austenitic (304, 316), ferritic, martensitic types.
  • Cast irons: 2.5–4% C + Si. Gray (graphite flakes), ductile (nodular graphite), white (cementite).

Non-Ferrous Alloys

  • Aluminum alloys: Low density (2.7 g/cm³), good corrosion resistance. 2xxx (Al-Cu), 6xxx (Al-Mg-Si), 7xxx (Al-Zn).
  • Titanium alloys: High strength-to-weight, biocompatible. Aerospace, biomedical. Ti-6Al-4V most common.
  • Copper alloys: Brass (Cu-Zn), bronze (Cu-Sn), cupronickel (Cu-Ni). High conductivity.

Ceramics

Ionic/covalent bonding → high hardness, high melting point, brittle.

Traditional Ceramics

  • Clay products (bricks, pottery, porcelain)
  • Cement and concrete
  • Glass (soda-lime, borosilicate)

Advanced (Engineering) Ceramics

  • Oxides: Al₂O₃ (alumina), ZrO₂ (zirconia) — wear-resistant, biocompatible
  • Carbides: SiC, WC — cutting tools, armor
  • Nitrides: Si₃N₄ — gas turbine blades, high-T bearings
Brittle failure: Ceramics have no plastic deformation. Fracture is catastrophic and occurs at stress concentrations (surface flaws). Weibull statistics describe strength variability.

Polymers

Long-chain molecules (macromolecules) based on C-C backbone.

Thermoplastics

  • Linear or branched chains held by weak vdW forces
  • Can be melted and reshaped repeatedly
  • Examples: PE (polyethylene), PP (polypropylene), PVC, PS (polystyrene), PET, Nylon, PMMA

Thermosets

  • Cross-linked 3D network (covalent bonds)
  • Cannot be remelted — decompose on heating
  • Examples: Epoxy, phenolic (Bakelite), polyurethane, vulcanized rubber

Glass Transition Temperature $T_g$

  • Below $T_g$: Glassy (hard, brittle)
  • Above $T_g$: Rubbery (flexible, elastic)
  • Polymers used above $T_g$ for flexibility (rubber), below for rigidity (plastic parts)

Degree of Polymerization

$$DP = \frac{\overline{M}_n}{m_r}$$

$\overline{M}_n$ = number-average molecular weight, $m_r$ = repeat unit molecular weight

Composite Materials

Combination of two or more materials to achieve properties unattainable by individual constituents.

Components

  • Matrix: Continuous phase — transfers load, protects fibers. Can be polymer, metal, or ceramic.
  • Reinforcement: Discontinuous phase — carries load. Fibers (glass, carbon, aramid), particles (SiC, Al₂O₃).

Types of Composites

  • Particle-reinforced: Isotropic. Example: concrete (cement + aggregate), WC-Co (cemented carbide).
  • Fiber-reinforced: Anisotropic. Continuous fibers (unidirectional, woven) or short fibers (random mat).
  • Structural: Laminates (plywood, CFRP layups), sandwich panels (foam core + face sheets).

Rule of Mixtures (Continuous Fiber Composites)

Longitudinal Loading (Parallel to Fibers) — Isostrain

Fibers and matrix experience same strain: $\varepsilon_c = \varepsilon_f = \varepsilon_m$

$$E_c(\text{long}) = E_f V_f + E_m V_m$$

Upper bound (Voigt model). $V_f + V_m = 1$.

Transverse Loading (Perpendicular to Fibers) — Isostress

Fibers and matrix experience same stress: $\sigma_c = \sigma_f = \sigma_m$

$$\frac{1}{E_c(\text{trans})} = \frac{V_f}{E_f} + \frac{V_m}{E_m}$$

Lower bound (Reuss model).

Density and Strength

$$\rho_c = \rho_f V_f + \rho_m V_m$$ $$\sigma_{c(\text{long})} = \sigma_f V_f + \sigma_m V_m \quad \text{(tensile strength, approx.)}$$
Specific properties: $\sigma/\rho$ (specific strength), $E/\rho$ (specific modulus). Composites excel here — e.g., CFRP (carbon fiber reinforced polymer) has specific strength 5× steel!
Worked Example 8.1 — Longitudinal Modulus of Composite

Glass fiber-epoxy composite: $V_f = 0.6$, $E_f = 72$ GPa (glass), $E_m = 3.5$ GPa (epoxy). Calculate $E_c$ (longitudinal).

Apply Rule of Mixtures

$$V_m = 1 - 0.6 = 0.4$$

$$E_c = E_f V_f + E_m V_m = 72 \times 0.6 + 3.5 \times 0.4$$

$$= 43.2 + 1.4 = 44.6 \text{ GPa}$$

Much stiffer than pure epoxy (3.5 GPa)!

Worked Example 8.2 — Transverse Modulus

Same composite as Ex. 8.1. Calculate $E_c$ (transverse).

Isostress (Series) Model

$$\frac{1}{E_c} = \frac{V_f}{E_f} + \frac{V_m}{E_m} = \frac{0.6}{72} + \frac{0.4}{3.5}$$

$$= 0.00833 + 0.1143 = 0.1226 \text{ GPa}^{-1}$$

$$E_c = \frac{1}{0.1226} = 8.16 \text{ GPa}$$

Much lower than longitudinal (44.6 GPa) → strong anisotropy!

Worked Example 8.3 — Composite Density

Carbon fiber-epoxy: $V_f = 0.5$, $\rho_f = 1.8$ g/cm³, $\rho_m = 1.2$ g/cm³. Calculate composite density.

Apply Rule of Mixtures

$$\rho_c = \rho_f V_f + \rho_m V_m = 1.8 \times 0.5 + 1.2 \times 0.5$$

$$= 0.9 + 0.6 = 1.5 \text{ g/cm}^3$$

Very lightweight! Compare to aluminum (2.7 g/cm³) or steel (7.8 g/cm³).

Worked Example 8.4 — Volume Fraction from Weight Fraction

Composite with 70 wt% Al₂O₃ particles ($\rho = 3.9$ g/cm³) in Al matrix ($\rho = 2.7$ g/cm³). Find volume fraction of Al₂O₃.

Weight to Volume Conversion

Let total mass = 100 g. $W_{\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3} = 70$ g, $W_{\text{Al}} = 30$ g.

$$V_{\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3} = \frac{70}{3.9} = 17.95 \text{ cm}^3$$

$$V_{\text{Al}} = \frac{30}{2.7} = 11.11 \text{ cm}^3$$

$$V_{\text{total}} = 17.95 + 11.11 = 29.06 \text{ cm}^3$$

Volume Fraction

$$V_f = \frac{17.95}{29.06} = 0.618 = 61.8\%$$

Worked Example 8.5 — Specific Modulus Comparison

Steel: $E = 200$ GPa, $\rho = 7.8$ g/cm³. CFRP: $E = 150$ GPa, $\rho = 1.6$ g/cm³. Compare specific moduli.

Specific Modulus = $E/\rho$

$$\text{Steel: } \frac{200}{7.8} = 25.6 \text{ GPa·cm}^3/\text{g}$$

$$\text{CFRP: } \frac{150}{1.6} = 93.8 \text{ GPa·cm}^3/\text{g}$$

CFRP is 3.7× better on a weight basis → ideal for aerospace!
