Faraday's Law, Transformers & Electromagnetic Waves

Changing magnetic fields induce EMF (Faraday's law) — the principle behind transformers and, ultimately, electromagnetic waves themselves. This week ties together induction, transformers, the EM spectrum, modulation/bandwidth in communication, and a first look at how metals, semiconductors and insulators differ. Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Magnetic flux: $\Phi_B=\displaystyle\int\vec B\cdot d\vec A$; for uniform $B$ over flat area $A$: $\Phi_B=BA\cos\theta$.
  • Faraday's law: $\varepsilon=-N\dfrac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$ — a changing flux through a loop (or coil of $N$ turns) induces an EMF.
  • Lenz's law: the induced current opposes the change in flux that created it (energy conservation / minus sign in Faraday's law).
  • Ideal transformer: $\dfrac{V_s}{V_p}=\dfrac{N_s}{N_p}$ and (power conserved) $I_pV_p=I_sV_s$, so $\dfrac{I_s}{I_p}=\dfrac{N_p}{N_s}$.
  • EM waves: travel at $c=3.00\times10^8$ m/s in vacuum; $c=f\lambda$. $\vec E\perp\vec B\perp$ direction of travel.
  • Modulation: a lower-frequency signal is encoded onto a higher-frequency carrier wave (AM: amplitude varies; FM: frequency varies). Bandwidth is the range of frequencies a signal/channel occupies or can carry.
  • Conductors vs. semiconductors vs. insulators: distinguished by the size of the energy gap between filled and empty electron energy bands — no gap (metals), small gap (semiconductors, thermally/optically bridgeable), large gap (insulators).

0 Faraday's Law & Induced EMF

So far, magnetic fields have been created by steady currents (Biot-Savart) and have exerted forces on moving charges. Faraday's law reveals the other half of the electromagnetic story: a changing magnetic flux through a loop induces an EMF in that loop — even if there's no battery anywhere in sight. This is how generators, transformers, and (eventually) electromagnetic waves themselves work.

Key idea

$$\Phi_B=\int\vec B\cdot d\vec A, \qquad \varepsilon=-N\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$$

Flux can change because $B$ changes, the loop's area changes, or the angle between $\vec B$ and the loop's normal changes — Faraday's law doesn't care which. Lenz's law (the minus sign) says the induced current always flows in the direction that opposes the change in flux — if it didn't, you could build a perpetual motion machine, so the sign is really energy conservation in disguise.

Worked example

A flat coil of 200 turns, area $0.05\ \text{m}^2$, sits with its plane perpendicular to a magnetic field that increases uniformly from 0.10 T to 0.40 T in 0.50 s. Find the induced EMF.

$\Delta\Phi_B = A\,\Delta B = (0.05)(0.40-0.10)=0.015\ \text{Wb}$ (per turn).

$\varepsilon=N\dfrac{\Delta\Phi_B}{\Delta t}=200\times\dfrac{0.015}{0.50}=6.0\ \text{V}$ (magnitude; Lenz's law gives the direction, opposing the increase in $B$).

Practice 1

A single loop of area $0.02\ \text{m}^2$ has a uniform field of 0.5 T perpendicular to it. Find the flux through the loop.

Solution

$\Phi_B=BA=0.5\times0.02=0.01\ \text{Wb}$.

Practice 2

A coil of 50 turns and area $0.01\ \text{m}^2$ experiences a field that drops from 0.8 T to 0.2 T in 0.2 s (field perpendicular to the coil throughout). Find the induced EMF.

Solution

$\varepsilon=N\dfrac{|\Delta\Phi_B|}{\Delta t}=50\times\dfrac{(0.01)(0.8-0.2)}{0.2}=50\times\dfrac{0.006}{0.2}=1.5\ \text{V}$.

Practice 3

A rectangular loop is rotated in a uniform magnetic field so that the angle between $\vec B$ and the loop's normal goes from $0^\circ$ to $90^\circ$. Explain, using $\Phi_B=BA\cos\theta$, what happens to the flux and hence roughly when the induced EMF is largest.

Solution

As $\theta$ goes from $0^\circ$ to $90^\circ$, $\cos\theta$ falls smoothly from 1 to 0, so $\Phi_B$ decreases smoothly to zero. The induced EMF depends on the rate of change of flux, $d\Phi_B/dt=-BA\sin\theta\,\dfrac{d\theta}{dt}$, which is largest in magnitude when $\sin\theta$ is largest, i.e. near $\theta=90^\circ$ (where the flux itself is momentarily near zero but changing fastest) — not when the flux is largest. This is the basis of AC generators: EMF and flux are $90^\circ$ out of phase.

Practice 4

A bar magnet is pushed north-pole-first into a coil, then held stationary inside it. Describe (using Lenz's law, no calculation) what the induced current does during each phase, and why.

Solution

While the magnet is moving in, flux through the coil is increasing, so by Lenz's law the induced current flows in whichever direction creates a magnetic field that opposes that increase (the coil effectively presents a like pole toward the approaching magnet, repelling it) — this is why pushing a magnet into a coil takes work. Once the magnet is held stationary, flux stops changing, $d\Phi_B/dt=0$, and the induced current drops to zero, regardless of how strong the field is at that fixed position.

1 The Transformer

A transformer uses Faraday's law directly: an AC current in a primary coil creates a changing flux, which (via a shared iron core) threads through a secondary coil and induces an EMF there. Because the same changing flux passes through both coils, the induced EMF per turn is the same in each — giving a simple ratio between primary and secondary voltages set purely by the turns ratio.

Key idea

$$\frac{V_s}{V_p}=\frac{N_s}{N_p}$$

For an ideal (lossless) transformer, power in equals power out: $I_pV_p=I_sV_s$, which rearranges to $\dfrac{I_s}{I_p}=\dfrac{N_p}{N_s}$ — step up the voltage and you necessarily step down the current by the same factor, and vice versa. This is exactly why long-distance power transmission uses very high voltages: for a given power, higher $V$ means lower $I$, and resistive losses go as $I^2R$.

Worked example

A transformer has 500 turns on the primary and 100 turns on the secondary. The primary is connected to 240 V AC and draws 2.0 A. Find the secondary voltage, the secondary current, and confirm power is conserved.

$V_s=V_p\dfrac{N_s}{N_p}=240\times\dfrac{100}{500}=48\ \text{V}$ (a step-down transformer).

$I_s=I_p\dfrac{N_p}{N_s}=2.0\times\dfrac{500}{100}=10\ \text{A}$.

Check: $P_p=I_pV_p=2.0\times240=480\ \text{W}$; $P_s=I_sV_s=10\times48=480\ \text{W}$. Equal, as required for an ideal transformer.

Practice 5

A step-up transformer has $N_p=100$ and $N_s=1000$. If $V_p=12$ V, find $V_s$.

Solution

$V_s=V_p\dfrac{N_s}{N_p}=12\times\dfrac{1000}{100}=120\ \text{V}$.

Practice 6

A transformer steps 230 V down to 9.2 V. If the primary has 2000 turns, how many turns does the secondary have?

Solution

$N_s=N_p\dfrac{V_s}{V_p}=2000\times\dfrac{9.2}{230}=80\ \text{turns}$.

Practice 7

A transformer's secondary delivers 5.0 A at 100 V. If the primary voltage is 500 V, find the primary current, assuming an ideal (lossless) transformer.

Solution

Power conserved: $I_p=\dfrac{I_sV_s}{V_p}=\dfrac{5.0\times100}{500}=1.0\ \text{A}$.

Practice 8

Explain why power is transmitted over long distances at very high voltage (hundreds of kV) rather than at the voltage actually used by household appliances, using $P_{loss}=I^2R_{line}$.

Solution

For a fixed amount of power $P=IV$ to be delivered, increasing $V$ means $I$ can be proportionally smaller. Since resistive losses in the transmission line scale as $I^2R_{line}$ (with $R_{line}$ fixed by the wire itself), halving $I$ cuts losses to a quarter. Transformers step voltage up for transmission (minimising $I$ and hence losses) and step it back down near consumers to safe, usable levels.

2 Electromagnetic Waves & the EM Spectrum

Faraday's law says a changing $B$ field creates an $E$ field; it turns out (from the full set of Maxwell's equations, beyond what we derive here) that a changing $E$ field likewise creates a $B$ field. Coupled together, oscillating $E$ and $B$ fields can sustain each other and propagate through space as a self-supporting wave — light, radio, X-rays and everything in between are all the same phenomenon at different frequencies.

Key idea

$$c=f\lambda, \qquad c=3.00\times10^{8}\ \text{m/s (in vacuum)}$$

In an EM wave, $\vec E$ and $\vec B$ oscillate in phase, perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation. The EM spectrum (increasing frequency / decreasing wavelength): radio → microwave → infrared → visible → ultraviolet → X-ray → gamma ray. All travel at the same speed $c$ in vacuum — only $f$ and $\lambda$ differ (and hence photon energy, $E=hf$).

Worked example

Find the wavelength of an FM radio wave broadcasting at $f=100$ MHz, and compare it with the wavelength of green light ($f\approx5.5\times10^{14}$ Hz).

$\lambda_{radio}=\dfrac{c}{f}=\dfrac{3.00\times10^8}{1.00\times10^8}=3.00\ \text{m}$.

$\lambda_{light}=\dfrac{c}{f}=\dfrac{3.00\times10^8}{5.5\times10^{14}}=5.45\times10^{-7}\ \text{m}=545\ \text{nm}$ (green light).

Same speed, but the radio wavelength is about $5.5$ million times longer — which is why radio antennas are metres long while visible light interacts with objects on the scale of hundreds of nanometres.

Practice 9

A microwave oven operates at $f=2.45$ GHz. Find its wavelength.

Solution

$\lambda=c/f=\dfrac{3.00\times10^8}{2.45\times10^9}=0.122\ \text{m}=12.2\ \text{cm}$.

Practice 10

X-rays used in medical imaging have wavelength around $1.0\times10^{-10}$ m. Find their frequency.

Solution

$f=c/\lambda=\dfrac{3.00\times10^8}{1.0\times10^{-10}}=3.0\times10^{18}\ \text{Hz}$.

Practice 11

AM radio broadcasts around 1000 kHz, FM around 100 MHz. Which has the longer wavelength, and by roughly what factor?

Solution

$\lambda_{AM}=c/f=\dfrac{3.00\times10^8}{1.0\times10^6}=300\ \text{m}$; $\lambda_{FM}=\dfrac{3.00\times10^8}{1.0\times10^8}=3\ \text{m}$.

AM has the longer wavelength, by a factor of about 100 — consistent with AM using much lower frequencies.

Practice 12

Explain, in terms of what $\vec E$ and $\vec B$ are doing, why an EM wave can travel through a perfect vacuum, unlike a sound wave which needs a medium.

Solution

A changing $E$ field generates a $B$ field, and a changing $B$ field generates an $E$ field (the two mutually regenerate each other as the wave propagates). This mutual regeneration doesn't require any material medium to "carry" a mechanical disturbance the way sound needs air molecules to bump into each other — the fields themselves are the entity that's oscillating and propagating, so EM waves travel perfectly well through empty space.

3 Modulation, Bandwidth & Conductors/Semiconductors/Insulators

To send information (voice, data) over a radio wave, the signal is encoded onto a much higher-frequency carrier wave — a process called modulation. The range of frequencies a modulated signal occupies is its bandwidth; wider bandwidth generally allows faster data transmission but takes up more of the spectrum.

Separately: whether a material conducts, partially conducts, or insulates comes down to how easily its electrons can move between energy bands — a first taste of the band picture you'll build on in later subjects.

Key idea

Amplitude modulation (AM): the carrier's amplitude is varied in step with the signal; frequency stays fixed. Frequency modulation (FM): the carrier's frequency is varied in step with the signal; amplitude stays fixed.

Bandwidth: range of frequencies occupied by (or available to) a signal, $\Delta f = f_{max}-f_{min}$. More bandwidth generally supports higher information/data rates.

Conductors: outer (valence) electrons are free to move with essentially no energy gap to climb — low resistivity. Insulators: a large energy gap separates filled states from the next available ones, so electrons are effectively stuck — very high resistivity. Semiconductors: a small gap that thermal energy (or light, or an added impurity/"dopant") can bridge, giving resistivity that is strongly temperature- and impurity-dependent, and which decreases with temperature (opposite of a metal).

Worked example

A voice signal ranges from about 300 Hz to 3.4 kHz. It's used to amplitude-modulate a carrier at $f_c=1.0$ MHz. AM produces sidebands at $f_c\pm f_{signal}$. Find the total bandwidth occupied by the transmitted AM signal.

The signal's own bandwidth is $3400-300=3100\ \text{Hz}$.

AM produces both an upper sideband ($f_c$ to $f_c+3.4$ kHz) and a lower sideband ($f_c-3.4$ kHz to $f_c$), so the total transmitted bandwidth is twice the signal bandwidth: $2\times3.4\ \text{kHz}=6.8\ \text{kHz}$ (roughly — using the upper frequency limit as the dominant term).

This is why standard AM radio channels are spaced about 9–10 kHz apart — enough room for the full double-sideband signal without overlapping neighbouring stations.

Practice 13

A signal occupies frequencies from 88.0 MHz to 88.2 MHz. Find its bandwidth.

Solution

$\Delta f = 88.2-88.0=0.2\ \text{MHz}=200\ \text{kHz}$.

Practice 14

State whether AM or FM varies the carrier's amplitude and which varies its frequency, and give one practical reason FM is generally less susceptible to noise than AM (conceptual, brief).

Solution

AM varies amplitude (frequency fixed); FM varies frequency (amplitude fixed).

Most electrical noise (lightning, motors, interference) adds unwanted amplitude fluctuations to a received signal. Since an FM receiver extracts information from frequency changes rather than amplitude, it can largely ignore amplitude noise, making FM inherently more noise-resistant than AM.

Practice 15

Explain, in terms of the energy-band picture, why a semiconductor's resistivity decreases as temperature increases, in contrast to a metal's resistivity (which increases).

Solution

In a semiconductor, raising the temperature gives more electrons enough thermal energy to jump the (small) energy gap into the conduction band, creating more charge carriers — more carriers means lower resistivity, even though each carrier also scatters a bit more (as in a metal). The carrier-density effect dominates, so overall resistivity falls with temperature.

In a metal, essentially all possible carriers are already free to conduct even at low temperature (no gap to cross), so raising the temperature only adds more lattice vibrations to scatter off — carrier density barely changes, but scattering increases, so resistivity rises.

Practice 16

Silicon (a semiconductor) is often "doped" with small amounts of other elements to control its conductivity. Based on the band-gap picture above, explain in one or two sentences why doping is a more practical way to control conductivity than simply changing temperature.

Solution

Doping adds impurity atoms that contribute extra carriers (or extra available states) at energies close to the existing bands, letting you set the carrier density — and hence conductivity — precisely and permanently, by choice of dopant and concentration. Relying on temperature to control conductivity would make a device's behaviour drift with its operating environment, which is far less practical for reliable electronics.

Practice 17

Faraday induction: A coil has N=130 turns and area A=0.010 m^2. Field magnitude changes from 0.20 T to 0.58 T in dt=0.035 s. Estimate average induced emf magnitude.

Solution

Per-turn flux change (normal incidence assumed): $Delta Phi = A*Delta B = 0.010*|0.58-0.20|=0.003800$ Wb.

Average emf magnitude: $|emf|=N*|Delta Phi|/Delta t=130*0.003800/0.035=14.114$ V.

Polarity is found by Lenz law; requested value is the magnitude above.
