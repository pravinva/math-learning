Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Coulomb's law: $F = k\dfrac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}$, with $k = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} = 8.99\times10^{9}\ \text{N·m}^2/\text{C}^2$ and $\varepsilon_0 = 8.85\times10^{-12}\ \text{C}^2/(\text{N·m}^2)$.
  • Electric field of a point charge: $\vec E = \dfrac{kq}{r^2}\hat r$ — points away from $+q$, toward $-q$.
  • Force from a field: $\vec F = q_0\vec E$ (field defined via a small positive test charge $q_0$).
  • Superposition: fields (and forces) from several source charges add as vectors: $\vec E_{net}=\sum_i \vec E_i$.
  • Charge densities: $\lambda=dq/dl$ (line), $\sigma=dq/dA$ (surface), $\rho=dq/dV$ (volume); a continuous distribution is built by integrating $d\vec E = \dfrac{k\,dq}{r^2}\hat r$.

  • On-axis field of a ring (radius $R$, charge $Q$, distance $z$ along the axis): $E_z = \dfrac{kQz}{(R^2+z^2)^{3/2}}$.

0 Electric Charge & Coulomb's Law

Charge is a property of matter, just like mass, but it comes in two flavours: positive and negative. Like charges repel, unlike charges attract. Charge is quantised (every charge is a whole-number multiple of $e=1.6\times10^{-19}$ C) and conserved (it isn't created or destroyed, only transferred).

Coulomb's law gives the force between two point charges. It looks just like Newton's law of gravitation in structure — an inverse-square law — but is enormously stronger, and can be attractive or repulsive.

Key idea

$$F = k\frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}, \qquad k=\frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}=8.99\times10^9\ \text{N·m}^2/\text{C}^2$$

The force acts along the line joining the two charges. Work out the magnitude from the formula, then decide the direction separately using "like repel, unlike attract" — don't try to carry signs of $q_1,q_2$ through the formula itself.

Worked example

A charge $q_1=+3\,\mu\text{C}$ sits at the origin and $q_2=-5\,\mu\text{C}$ sits at $x=0.2$ m. Find the force on $q_2$.

Magnitude: $F=k\dfrac{|q_1q_2|}{r^2}=\dfrac{(8.99\times10^9)(3\times10^{-6})(5\times10^{-6})}{(0.2)^2}=\dfrac{0.13485}{0.04}=3.37\ \text{N}$.

Direction: opposite charges attract, so the force on $q_2$ points back toward $q_1$, i.e. in the $-x$ direction. (By Newton's third law, $q_1$ feels an equal 3.37 N pull in the $+x$ direction.)

Practice 1

Two point charges $+2\,\mu$C and $+6\,\mu$C are 30 cm apart. Find the magnitude and nature (attractive/repulsive) of the force between them.

Solution

$F=k\dfrac{q_1q_2}{r^2}=\dfrac{(8.99\times10^9)(2\times10^{-6})(6\times10^{-6})}{(0.3)^2}=\dfrac{0.10788}{0.09}=1.20\ \text{N}$, repulsive (both charges are positive).

Practice 2

A charge $q=+5\,\mu$C sits at the origin. A $+2\,\mu$C charge is at $(0.1,0)$ m and a $-3\,\mu$C charge is at $(0,0.1)$ m. Find the net force on $q$ (magnitude and direction).

Solution

From the $+2\,\mu$C charge (repulsion, pushes $q$ in $-x$): $F_1=k\dfrac{(5\times10^{-6})(2\times10^{-6})}{(0.1)^2}=8.99\ \text{N}$ in $-x$.

From the $-3\,\mu$C charge (attraction, pulls $q$ in $+y$): $F_2=k\dfrac{(5\times10^{-6})(3\times10^{-6})}{(0.1)^2}=13.49\ \text{N}$ in $+y$.

Net: $F_x=-8.99$ N, $F_y=+13.49$ N, so $|F|=\sqrt{8.99^2+13.49^2}=16.2\ \text{N}$, at $\theta=\tan^{-1}(13.49/8.99)=56.3^\circ$ above the $-x$ axis.

Practice 3

A charged object has $-1.6$ nC of charge. How many excess electrons does it carry?

Solution

$n=\dfrac{|Q|}{e}=\dfrac{1.6\times10^{-9}}{1.6\times10^{-19}}=1.0\times10^{10}$ electrons.

Practice 4

Show that the electric repulsion between two protons is vastly stronger than their gravitational attraction, by computing $F_e/F_g$. ($G=6.67\times10^{-11}$, $m_p=1.67\times10^{-27}$ kg.)

Solution

$\dfrac{F_e}{F_g}=\dfrac{ke^2/r^2}{Gm_p^2/r^2}=\dfrac{ke^2}{Gm_p^2}=\dfrac{(8.99\times10^9)(1.6\times10^{-19})^2}{(6.67\times10^{-11})(1.67\times10^{-27})^2}\approx 1.24\times10^{36}$.

The distance $r$ cancels — the ratio is the same at any separation. Gravity is utterly negligible between charged particles.

Practice 5

Two identical small conducting spheres carry $+6\,\mu$C and $-2\,\mu$C. They are touched together (so charge redistributes equally) and then separated to 0.5 m. Find the force between them afterwards.

Solution

Total charge $=6-2=4\,\mu$C, split equally: $2\,\mu$C each.

$F=k\dfrac{(2\times10^{-6})^2}{(0.5)^2}=\dfrac{8.99\times10^9\times4\times10^{-12}}{0.25}=0.144\ \text{N}$, repulsive (both spheres now carry the same sign of charge).

1 The Electric Field of Point Charges

Rather than recalculating force from scratch each time, we define a field in space.

Point charge means "treat all of a charge as concentrated at one point" (an idealization used when object size is tiny compared with distances in the problem).

Suppose a source charge $q_s$ creates a field. At some field point, place a very small positive test charge $q_0$. Measure force $\vec F$ on that test charge, then define:

$$\vec E \equiv \frac{\vec F}{q_0}.$$

So $\vec E$ describes the source's influence at that location, independent of which test charge you used.

Once $\vec E$ is known, force on any charge placed there is $$\vec F = q_{\text{test}}\vec E.$$

Key idea

$$\vec E \equiv \frac{\vec F}{q_0}, \qquad \vec E_{\text{from one point source}} = \frac{kq_s}{r^2}\hat r$$

Why is there only one $q$ in the field formula?
The field formula is for the field created by the source only, so it uses only the source charge $q_s$. The test charge was already divided out in $\vec E=\vec F/q_0$, which is why $q_0$ does not appear in $kq_s/r^2$.

$\hat r$ points from the source charge toward the field point. For $q_s>0$, $\vec E$ points radially outward; for $q_s<0$, radially inward. For several source charges, add the field vectors: $\vec E_{net}=\sum_i \vec E_i$ (superposition).

Worked example

Find the electric field 0.3 m from a $+6\,\mu$C point charge.

Step 1: Identify source charge and distance: $q_s=+6\times10^{-6}\,$C, $r=0.3\,$m.

Step 2: Use point-source field magnitude formula: $E=k\dfrac{|q_s|}{r^2}$.

Step 3: Substitute: $E=\dfrac{(8.99\times10^9)(6\times10^{-6})}{(0.3)^2} =\dfrac{53940}{0.09}=5.99\times10^{5}\ \text{N/C}$.

Direction: radially away from the charge (it's positive).

Practice 6

Find the field 0.2 m from a $-4\,\mu$C point charge (magnitude and direction).

Solution

$E=k\dfrac{4\times10^{-6}}{(0.2)^2}=\dfrac{35960}{0.04}=8.99\times10^{5}\ \text{N/C}$, directed toward the charge (it's negative).

Practice 7

Charges $+3\,\mu$C and $+3\,\mu$C sit at $(-0.1,0)$ m and $(0.1,0)$ m. Find $\vec E$ at the origin.

Solution

By symmetry, the two fields at the origin have equal magnitude but point in opposite directions (each points away from its own source, straight along the $x$-axis, but the sources are on opposite sides) — they cancel exactly. $\vec E=0$ at the origin.

Practice 8

A dipole: $+4\,\mu$C at $(0,0)$ and $-4\,\mu$C at $(0.2,0)$ m. Find $\vec E$ at the midpoint $(0.1,0)$.

Solution

Distance from each charge to the midpoint is 0.1 m. Field from $+4\,\mu$C points away from it, i.e. in $+x$ at the midpoint: $E_1=k\dfrac{4\times10^{-6}}{(0.1)^2}=3.60\times10^{6}\ \text{N/C}$.

Field from $-4\,\mu$C points toward it (attraction direction), also $+x$ at the midpoint: $E_2=3.60\times10^{6}\ \text{N/C}$.

They add: $E_{net}=7.19\times10^{6}\ \text{N/C}$ in $+x$, i.e. pointing from the $+$ charge to the $-$ charge — the standard dipole-midpoint result.

Practice 9

A $+2$ nC test charge experiences a force of $6\times10^{-4}$ N. What is the field magnitude at that point?

Solution

$E=F/q_0=\dfrac{6\times10^{-4}}{2\times10^{-9}}=3\times10^{5}\ \text{N/C}$.

Practice 10

An electron sits in a uniform field of 500 N/C. Find the force on it and its acceleration ($m_e=9.11\times10^{-31}$ kg), including direction relative to $\vec E$.

Solution

$F=eE=(1.6\times10^{-19})(500)=8.0\times10^{-17}\ \text{N}$.

$a=F/m_e=\dfrac{8.0\times10^{-17}}{9.11\times10^{-31}}=8.78\times10^{13}\ \text{m/s}^2$.

Direction: opposite to $\vec E$, since the electron's charge is negative.

2 Continuous Charge Distributions: Setting Up the Integral

Real charged objects — wires, plates, rings — aren't point charges. We handle them by chopping the object into infinitesimal pieces of charge $dq$, treating each as a point charge, and adding (integrating) the field contributions $d\vec E$ from every piece.

The trick that makes these integrals tractable is symmetry: in a symmetric geometry, components of $d\vec E$ perpendicular to some axis cancel in pairs, leaving only the component along the axis to integrate.

Symmetry cancellation diagrams

Pick two mirror elements $dq$ and $dq'$ first. Their $d\vec E$ vectors at the field point have equal magnitude. Perpendicular components cancel; only the symmetry-axis component survives and is integrated.

Ring on axis

Opposite ring elements give equal transverse components in opposite directions, so they cancel pairwise. Only $dE_z$ remains.

Finite line (midpoint axis)

Mirror points left and right of the midpoint give opposite sideways components. The component along the perpendicular bisector survives.

Infinite sheet (normal axis)

For each pair mirrored in the sheet plane, sideways components cancel. Net field is purely normal to the sheet.

Key idea

$$dq=\lambda\,dl\ \text{(line)}, \quad dq=\sigma\,dA\ \text{(surface)}, \quad dq=\rho\,dV\ \text{(volume)}$$ $$d\vec E = \frac{k\,dq}{r^2}\hat r \quad\Rightarrow\quad \vec E = \int d\vec E$$

Standard method: (1) sketch $dq$ and its field $d\vec E$ at the field point; (2) identify which component of $d\vec E$ survives by symmetry; (3) express everything ($r$, the surviving component, $dq$) in terms of one variable; (4) integrate over the whole object.

Worked example

Derive the on-axis field of a uniformly charged ring (radius $R$, total charge $Q$) at a point a distance $z$ along its axis.

Each element $dq=\lambda\,dl$ is the same distance $r=\sqrt{R^2+z^2}$ from the field point, so $|d\vec E|=\dfrac{k\,dq}{R^2+z^2}$ is the same for every element.

By symmetry, the components of $d\vec E$ perpendicular to the axis (radially, around the ring) cancel in pairs; only the axial component $dE_z = dE\cos\theta$ survives, where $\cos\theta = z/r = z/\sqrt{R^2+z^2}$.

$dE_z = \dfrac{k\,dq\,z}{(R^2+z^2)^{3/2}}$. Since $z$ and $r$ are the same for every $dq$, they come out of the integral: $E_z=\dfrac{kz}{(R^2+z^2)^{3/2}}\displaystyle\int dq = \dfrac{kQz}{(R^2+z^2)^{3/2}}$.

3D ring geometry: why every $dq$ has the same $r$

Even though $dq_1$ and $dq_2$ are at different places around the ring, each forms the same right-triangle geometry with legs $R$ (in-plane radius) and $z$ (axis height), so each has the same hypotenuse $r=\sqrt{R^2+z^2}$. Their transverse components cancel by symmetry, while axial components add.

Practice 11

A ring of radius 5 cm carries uniform charge $+8$ nC. Find $\vec E$ on the axis at $z=12$ cm.

Solution

$R^2+z^2=0.05^2+0.12^2=0.0169$; $(0.0169)^{3/2}=0.0169\times0.13=2.197\times10^{-3}$.

$E_z=\dfrac{(8.99\times10^9)(8\times10^{-9})(0.12)}{2.197\times10^{-3}}=\dfrac{8.63}{2.197\times10^{-3}}=3.93\times10^{3}\ \text{N/C}$, directed away from the ring along the axis (charge is positive).

Practice 12

For the same ring, at what value of $z$ is the on-axis field a maximum? (Hint: differentiate $E_z(z)$ with respect to $z$ and set the derivative to zero.)

Solution

Writing $E_z=kQz(R^2+z^2)^{-3/2}$ and differentiating using the product rule, setting $dE_z/dz=0$ gives $R^2+z^2-3z^2=0 \Rightarrow z=R/\sqrt2$.

For $R=0.05$ m, this is $z=0.0354$ m. Close to the ring ($z\to0$) and far away ($z\to\infty$) the field is small; it peaks in between.

Practice 13

Explain, without calculating, why the perpendicular (radial) components of $d\vec E$ cancel for a full ring but would not fully cancel for a charged arc spanning only a quarter of the circle.

Solution

For a full ring, every element has a mirror-image element diametrically opposite whose radial component points the exact opposite way, so pairs cancel completely, leaving only the axial component. A quarter-arc has no such partner for most elements — the radial components from one side of the arc are not balanced by anything on the other side, so the net field has both an axial and a nonzero transverse component.

Practice 14

A uniformly charged straight rod of length $L$ lies along the $x$-axis from $x=0$ to $x=L$, carrying total charge $Q$ (so $\lambda=Q/L$). Set up (but do not evaluate) the integral for $E_x$ at a point on the axis a distance $d$ beyond the end of the rod (i.e. at $x=L+d$).

Solution

Element $dq=\lambda\,dx'$ at position $x'$ is a distance $r=(L+d)-x'$ from the field point, and since the point lies on the same line as the rod, the whole field is axial: $dE_x=\dfrac{k\lambda\,dx'}{[(L+d)-x']^2}$.

$$E_x=\int_0^{L}\frac{k\lambda\,dx'}{[(L+d)-x']^2}$$

(You aren't asked to evaluate it here — recognising the correct integrand and limits is the point.)

3 Field Calculations for Symmetric Distributions

Two geometries come up so often that it's worth knowing their results by heart: the infinite line of charge and the infinite sheet of charge. Both can be derived the "long way" with the integral method above, or (faster) with Gauss's law later in the course — for now, treat them as standard results you can apply directly.

Key idea

Infinite line charge (linear density $\lambda$), field at perpendicular distance $r$: $$E=\frac{2k\lambda}{r}=\frac{\lambda}{2\pi\varepsilon_0 r}$$ directed radially away from the line (toward it, if $\lambda<0$). Note the $1/r$ falloff — slower than the $1/r^2$ of a point charge.

Infinite sheet of charge (surface density $\sigma$), field on either side: $$E=\frac{\sigma}{2\varepsilon_0}$$ uniform — the same magnitude at any distance from the sheet, pointing directly away from it (toward it, if $\sigma<0$).

Worked example

Find $\vec E$ at 5 cm from an infinitely long line with $\lambda=+3$ nC/m.

$E=\dfrac{2k\lambda}{r}=\dfrac{2(8.99\times10^9)(3\times10^{-9})}{0.05}=\dfrac{53.94}{0.05}=1.08\times10^{3}\ \text{N/C}$.

Direction: radially outward from the line (it's positively charged).

Practice 15

An infinite sheet carries $\sigma=2\times10^{-6}\ \text{C/m}^2$. Find the field near the sheet.

Solution

$E=\dfrac{\sigma}{2\varepsilon_0}=\dfrac{2\times10^{-6}}{2(8.85\times10^{-12})}=1.13\times10^{5}\ \text{N/C}$, uniform, pointing directly away from the sheet on each side.

Practice 16

Two large parallel sheets carry equal and opposite surface charge densities $+\sigma$ and $-\sigma$. Find $\vec E$ (a) in the region between the sheets, and (b) outside the pair, and explain why.

Solution

Between the sheets: the fields from $+\sigma$ (pointing away, into the gap) and $-\sigma$ (pointing toward it, also into the gap) add: $E=\dfrac{\sigma}{2\varepsilon_0}+\dfrac{\sigma}{2\varepsilon_0}=\dfrac{\sigma}{\varepsilon_0}$, directed from the $+$ sheet to the $-$ sheet.

Outside the pair (beyond either sheet): the two contributions point in opposite directions and are equal in magnitude, so they cancel — $E=0$. This is the idealised parallel-plate capacitor field you'll use again in Week 2.

Practice 17

A long charged wire has $\lambda=-5$ nC/m. Find the field at $r=10$ cm (magnitude and direction).

Solution

$E=\dfrac{2k|\lambda|}{r}=\dfrac{2(8.99\times10^9)(5\times10^{-9})}{0.1}=\dfrac{89.9}{0.1}=899\ \text{N/C}$, directed radially toward the wire (charge is negative).

Practice 18

Dry air breaks down (sparks) at roughly $E\approx3\times10^{6}$ N/C. What surface charge density on an isolated large sheet would produce this field right at its surface?

Solution

$\sigma=2\varepsilon_0 E=2(8.85\times10^{-12})(3\times10^{6})=5.31\times10^{-5}\ \text{C/m}^2$.

Practice 19

Explain physically why the field of an infinite sheet doesn't fall off with distance, while the field of a point charge falls off as $1/r^2$.

Solution

For a point charge, field lines spread out over the surface of an ever-larger sphere as you move away, so the same total "flux" is diluted over more area — hence $1/r^2$. An infinite sheet has no edges: as you move away, the same infinite area is still "in view" contributing field lines from every direction across the whole plane, so the geometry never dilutes — the field stays constant with distance (at least in the idealised infinite case).

Practice 20

Square electrostatics: Let field point be at A(0,0) of a square with side $a=0.17$ m. Place charges at B(a,0): $+3$ muC, C(0,a): -4 muC, and D(a,a): $+5$ muC. Find net field $E$ at A by components.

Solution

Distances: $AB=a$, $AC=a$, $AD=a*sqrt(2)$. Use $E=kq/r^2$ with signs carried by direction.

From B: $E_{Bx}=-k q_B/a^2=-9.332*10^5$ N/C, $E_{By}=0$.

From C: $E_{Cy}=-k q_C/a^2=1.244*10^6$ N/C, $E_{Cx}=0$.

From D: magnitude $E_D=k q_D/(2a^2)$ and each component is $E_{Dx}=E_{Dy}=-k q_D/(2*sqrt(2)*a^2)=-5.499*10^5$ N/C.

So $E_x=E_{Bx}+E_{Dx}=-1.483*10^6$ N/C and $E_y=E_{Cy}+E_{Dy}=6.944*10^5$ N/C.

Net field: $|E|=sqrt(E_x^2+E_y^2)=1.638*10^6$ N/C, direction $theta = atan2(E_y,E_x)=154.91$ degrees from +x.
