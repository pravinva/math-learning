Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Harmonic wave: $y(x,t)=A\sin(kx-\omega t)$, with wave number $k=2\pi/\lambda$, angular frequency $\omega=2\pi f$, and wave speed $v=f\lambda=\omega/k$.
  • Wave speed on a string: $v=\sqrt{T/\mu}$ ($T$ = tension, $\mu$ = mass per unit length).
  • Standing waves on a string fixed at both ends (length $L$): $\lambda_n=\dfrac{2L}{n}$, $\ f_n=\dfrac{nv}{2L}=nf_1$, $\ n=1,2,3,\dots$
  • Reflection: a wave reflecting off a fixed end inverts (phase flips by $\pi$); off a free end it does not.
  • Beats: two waves of close frequencies $f_1,f_2$ produce a beat frequency $f_{beat}=|f_1-f_2|$.
  • Classical (sound) Doppler effect: $$f'=f\,\frac{v_{sound}\pm v_{observer}}{v_{sound}\mp v_{source}}$$ (top signs when observer/source motion is toward the other; bottom signs when away).
  • Phase vs. group velocity: $v_p=\omega/k$ (speed of a single frequency component); $v_g=d\omega/dk$ (speed of a wave packet's envelope). Equal in a non-dispersive medium; different when the medium is dispersive.

0 Harmonic Waves & Wave Speed

A travelling harmonic wave is the building block for almost everything else this week: a repeating sinusoidal disturbance that moves through space at a fixed speed. Once you can read off amplitude, wavelength and frequency from a wave's equation (or vice versa), the standing-wave, beat and Doppler formulas that follow are just applications of the same basic description.

Key idea

$$y(x,t)=A\sin(kx-\omega t), \qquad k=\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}, \quad \omega=2\pi f, \quad v=f\lambda=\frac{\omega}{k}$$

For a wave on a stretched string: $$v=\sqrt{\frac{T}{\mu}}$$ — higher tension or a lighter string both increase wave speed (this is literally why tightening a guitar string raises its pitch).

Worked example

A wave has amplitude 0.020 m, wavelength 0.50 m, and frequency 4.0 Hz, travelling in the $+x$ direction. Write $y(x,t)$ and find its speed.

$v=f\lambda=4.0\times0.50=2.0\ \text{m/s}$.

$k=\dfrac{2\pi}{\lambda}=\dfrac{2\pi}{0.50}=4\pi\ \text{rad/m}$; $\ \omega=2\pi f=2\pi(4.0)=8\pi\ \text{rad/s}$.

$y(x,t)=0.020\sin(4\pi x-8\pi t)\ \text{m}$.

Practice 1

A sound wave has speed 340 m/s and frequency 500 Hz. Find its wavelength.

Solution

$\lambda=v/f=340/500=0.68\ \text{m}$.

Practice 2

A string under 90 N tension has mass per unit length $\mu=0.0050$ kg/m. Find the wave speed.

Solution

$v=\sqrt{T/\mu}=\sqrt{90/0.0050}=\sqrt{18000}=134\ \text{m/s}$.

Practice 3

A wave is described by $y(x,t)=0.10\sin(3x-6t)$ (SI units). Find its amplitude, wave number, angular frequency, wavelength, frequency, and speed.

Solution

$A=0.10\ \text{m}$, $k=3\ \text{rad/m}$, $\omega=6\ \text{rad/s}$.

$\lambda=2\pi/k=2\pi/3=2.09\ \text{m}$; $\ f=\omega/2\pi=6/2\pi=0.955\ \text{Hz}$; $\ v=\omega/k=6/3=2.0\ \text{m/s}$.

Practice 4

Explain the difference between the wave speed $v$ (how fast the wave pattern moves) and the transverse speed of a single particle in the medium (how fast a point on the string moves up and down), and how you'd compute the latter from $y(x,t)$.

Solution

The wave speed $v=\omega/k$ describes how fast the overall pattern (crests and troughs) propagates through the medium — it's a property of the wave as a whole. An individual particle of the medium doesn't travel along with the wave at all; it simply oscillates up and down (transversely) about a fixed position. Its instantaneous transverse velocity is the partial derivative $\partial y/\partial t$ (holding $x$ fixed), which is generally a different value from $v$, and in fact varies sinusoidally in time rather than being constant.

1 Superposition & Standing Waves

When two or more waves overlap in the same medium, they simply add (the superposition principle) — no collision, no bouncing off each other. A particularly important case is two identical waves travelling in opposite directions (e.g. a wave and its own reflection): they combine into a standing wave, a pattern that oscillates in place rather than travelling, with fixed points (nodes) that never move and points of maximum motion (antinodes) in between.

Key idea

For a string of length $L$ fixed at both ends, only certain wavelengths "fit" (both ends must be nodes): $$\lambda_n=\frac{2L}{n}, \qquad f_n=\frac{nv}{2L}=nf_1, \qquad n=1,2,3,\dots$$

A wave reflecting off a fixed end inverts (undergoes a $\pi$ phase shift, since the end can't move — the reflected wave must always cancel the incoming displacement there). A wave reflecting off a free end does not invert.

Worked example

A string of length 1.2 m, fixed at both ends, supports waves at speed 40 m/s. Find the first three harmonic (resonant) frequencies.

$f_1=\dfrac{v}{2L}=\dfrac{40}{2.4}=16.7\ \text{Hz}$.

$f_2=2f_1=33.3\ \text{Hz}$, $\quad f_3=3f_1=50.0\ \text{Hz}$.

Practice 5

Find the fundamental (longest) wavelength for a 0.80 m string fixed at both ends.

Solution

$\lambda_1=2L=2(0.80)=1.60\ \text{m}$.

Practice 6

A string's third harmonic has frequency 150 Hz. Find its fundamental frequency.

Solution

$f_3=3f_1 \Rightarrow f_1=150/3=50\ \text{Hz}$.

Practice 7

Explain why a wave reflecting off a fixed end (e.g. a string tied to a wall) inverts, while a wave reflecting off a free end does not.

Solution

At a fixed end, the string cannot move at all — its displacement must be zero at every instant. The only way the incoming and reflected waves can sum to exactly zero displacement there at all times is if the reflected wave is an inverted (upside-down) copy of the incoming one, so they cancel. At a free end, there's no such constraint forcing zero displacement; instead, the end is free to move to its maximum possible amplitude, and the reflected wave returns without inversion.

Practice 8

Two waves of equal amplitude and frequency travel in opposite directions along a string, forming a standing wave. Using the superposition principle, explain why some points (nodes) never move at all, while others (antinodes) oscillate with twice the individual wave's amplitude.

Solution

At a node, the two counter-travelling waves are always exactly out of phase (crest meeting trough) at that location, for all time — their displacements are equal and opposite, so by superposition they add to zero at every instant; that point simply never moves. At an antinode, the two waves are always exactly in phase there (crest meeting crest, trough meeting trough), so their displacements add constructively at every instant, reaching twice the amplitude of either individual wave. Every other point on the string is somewhere in between, oscillating with an amplitude that depends on its position along the string.

2 Beats & the Doppler Effect

Two effects that both come from combining waves of slightly different frequency, or from relative motion between source and observer. Beats arise when two nearby frequencies are heard simultaneously; the classical Doppler effect arises when a source and/or observer of sound are moving relative to each other (and to the medium, air).

Key idea

Beats: $$f_{beat}=|f_1-f_2|$$ Classical (sound) Doppler effect: $$f'=f\,\frac{v_{sound}\pm v_{observer}}{v_{sound}\mp v_{source}}$$ Use $+v_{observer}$ if the observer moves toward the source, $-v_{observer}$ if away; use $-v_{source}$ in the denominator if the source moves toward the observer (this raises $f'$), $+v_{source}$ if away.

Worked example

Two tuning forks, at 440 Hz and 444 Hz, are struck together. Find the beat frequency you'd hear.

$f_{beat}=|444-440|=4\ \text{Hz}$ — you'd hear a tone that pulses in loudness 4 times per second.

Practice 9

A car horn emits 500 Hz. The car approaches a stationary listener at 20 m/s ($v_{sound}=340$ m/s). Find the frequency heard.

Solution

Source approaching, so use $-v_{source}$ in the denominator: $f'=f\dfrac{v}{v-v_s}=500\times\dfrac{340}{340-20}=500\times\dfrac{340}{320}=531\ \text{Hz}$.

Practice 10

An ambulance moving away from a stationary listener at 25 m/s emits a siren at 700 Hz. Find the frequency heard ($v_{sound}=343$ m/s).

Solution

Source receding, so use $+v_{source}$: $f'=700\times\dfrac{343}{343+25}=700\times\dfrac{343}{368}=652\ \text{Hz}$.

Practice 11

Two guitar strings are tuned close together; plucked together, you hear 3 beats per second. If one string is known to be exactly at 220 Hz, what are the two possible frequencies of the other string?

Solution

$f_{beat}=|f_1-f_2|=3 \Rightarrow f_2=220\pm3$, so the other string is at either 217 Hz or 223 Hz — the beat frequency alone can't tell you which side it's on without further information (e.g. detuning one string slightly and seeing whether the beat rate increases or decreases).

3 Harmonic Analysis, Wave Packets & Dispersion

Real-world waveforms (a violin note, a voice, a radar pulse) are rarely single pure sine waves — but remarkably, any periodic waveform can be built up by adding together simple harmonic waves of different frequencies, amplitudes and phases (Fourier's insight). A short pulse, similarly, can be thought of as a "wave packet": a superposition of many different frequency components that happen to add constructively only in a small region.

Whether that packet keeps its shape as it travels depends on whether the medium is dispersive — whether wave speed depends on frequency.

Key idea

Phase velocity (speed of a single frequency component): $v_p=\omega/k$. Group velocity (speed of the overall envelope/packet, and generally the speed at which information/energy travels): $v_g=d\omega/dk$.

In a non-dispersive medium, $v_p=v_g$ for all frequencies, so a pulse (built from many frequencies) keeps its shape as it travels. In a dispersive medium, different frequency components travel at different speeds, so a pulse gradually spreads out and changes shape as it propagates.

Worked example

A medium has dispersion relation $\omega(k)=ck$ ($c$ = constant). Show that this medium is non-dispersive, i.e. $v_p=v_g$.

$v_p=\dfrac{\omega}{k}=\dfrac{ck}{k}=c$.

$v_g=\dfrac{d\omega}{dk}=c$ (since $\omega=ck$ is linear in $k$).

Both equal $c$, independent of $k$ — every frequency component travels at the same speed, so a wave packet built from many components keeps its shape as it propagates. (This is, in fact, the dispersion relation for light in vacuum.)

Practice 12

A medium has dispersion relation $\omega(k)=ak^2$ ($a$ = constant). Find $v_p$ and $v_g$ in terms of $k$, and state which is larger.

Solution

$v_p=\omega/k=ak^2/k=ak$.

$v_g=d\omega/dk=2ak$.

$v_g=2v_p$ — the group velocity is twice the phase velocity in this (dispersive) medium, meaning the energy/information carried by a wave packet travels faster than any single crest within it appears to move — a real feature of, e.g., deep-water surface waves.

Practice 13

Explain, in plain language, why a sharp pulse (like a hand clap, or a short radar pulse) spreads out and "smears" in time as it travels through a dispersive medium, while the same pulse keeps its shape in a non-dispersive medium.

Solution

A short pulse is really a superposition of many different frequency components. In a non-dispersive medium, all of these components travel at exactly the same speed, so they stay lined up with each other and the pulse's shape is preserved as it moves. In a dispersive medium, different frequency components travel at different speeds — some outrun others — so the components that started perfectly aligned (to build the sharp pulse) gradually drift out of alignment as they travel, causing the pulse to broaden and lose its sharp shape over distance.

Practice 14

Explain the basic idea of harmonic (Fourier) analysis: how a complicated periodic waveform, like a violin note, can be represented as a sum of simple sine waves, and how the relative strengths of these components relate to the note's "timbre" (tone colour).

Solution

Any periodic waveform, however complex its shape, can be expressed as a sum of a fundamental sine wave (at the note's basic pitch/frequency) plus a series of harmonics (integer multiples of that fundamental frequency), each with its own amplitude and phase. Two instruments playing the exact same pitch (same fundamental frequency) can still sound completely different, because they excite different relative amplitudes of the higher harmonics — that particular mixture of harmonic strengths is what gives an instrument its characteristic timbre, even though the fundamental (perceived pitch) is identical.

Wave equation diagnostics: for y(x,t)=A sin(kx-omega t), compute displacement, transverse velocity, and acceleration at specified x,t.

Solution

Evaluate phase first.

Use y = A sin(phase), vy = partial y/partial t = -A omega cos(phase).

Acceleration ay = partial^2 y/partial t^2 = -omega^2 y.

Practice 18

Dispersion relation analysis: derive phase and group velocities from omega(k), and infer pulse spreading behavior.

Solution

Phase velocity vp = omega/k.

Group velocity vg = domega/dk.

If vp depends on k and differs from vg, medium is dispersive and pulses spread.
