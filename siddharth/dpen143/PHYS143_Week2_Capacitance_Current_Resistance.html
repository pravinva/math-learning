Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Capacitance: $C=\dfrac{Q}{V}$ (farads, F). Parallel-plate: $C=\dfrac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d}$ (or $\dfrac{\kappa\varepsilon_0 A}{d}$ with a dielectric of constant $\kappa$).
  • Capacitors in parallel (same $V$): $C_{eq}=C_1+C_2+\cdots$. In series (same $Q$): $\dfrac{1}{C_{eq}}=\dfrac{1}{C_1}+\dfrac{1}{C_2}+\cdots$
  • Energy stored in a capacitor: $U=\dfrac12 CV^2=\dfrac{Q^2}{2C}=\dfrac12 QV$.
  • Current: $I=\dfrac{dq}{dt}$ (amperes, A). Ohm's law: $V=IR$.
  • Resistance from geometry: $R=\rho\dfrac{L}{A}$, with $\rho$ = resistivity; $\rho$ increases with temperature: $\rho(T)=\rho_0[1+\alpha(T-T_0)]$ ($\alpha$ = temperature coefficient of resistance).
  • Resistors in series (same $I$): $R_{eq}=R_1+R_2+\cdots$. In parallel (same $V$): $\dfrac{1}{R_{eq}}=\dfrac{1}{R_1}+\dfrac{1}{R_2}+\cdots$
  • Power: $P=IV=I^2R=\dfrac{V^2}{R}$.

0 Capacitance & the Parallel-Plate Capacitor

A capacitor is a pair of conductors that store equal and opposite charge $\pm Q$ when a potential difference $V$ is applied between them. The ratio $Q/V$ is fixed by the capacitor's geometry alone — it doesn't depend on $Q$ or $V$ individually.

The commonest example is the parallel-plate capacitor: two flat plates of area $A$ separated by a small gap $d$. From last week you already know the field between two oppositely charged sheets is uniform, $E=\sigma/\varepsilon_0$ — that's exactly the setup here.

Key idea

$$C=\frac{Q}{V}\ \ [\text{F} = \text{C/V}], \qquad C_{\text{parallel-plate}}=\frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d}$$

For a parallel-plate capacitor, $V=Ed=\dfrac{\sigma d}{\varepsilon_0}=\dfrac{Qd}{\varepsilon_0 A}$, so $C=Q/V=\varepsilon_0 A/d$ follows directly. More area or a smaller gap both increase capacitance.

Worked example

A parallel-plate capacitor has plates of area $2.0\times10^{-2}\ \text{m}^2$ separated by $0.5$ mm. Find its capacitance, and the charge stored at $V=12$ V.

$C=\dfrac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d}=\dfrac{(8.85\times10^{-12})(2.0\times10^{-2})}{5\times10^{-4}}=3.54\times10^{-10}\ \text{F}=354\ \text{pF}$.

$Q=CV=(3.54\times10^{-10})(12)=4.25\times10^{-9}\ \text{C}=4.25\ \text{nC}$.

Practice 1

A capacitor stores $8\,\mu$C when charged to 200 V. Find its capacitance.

Solution

$C=Q/V=\dfrac{8\times10^{-6}}{200}=4\times10^{-8}\ \text{F}=40\ \text{nF}$.

Practice 2

A parallel-plate capacitor has $C=100$ pF with plate separation 1 mm. If the separation is doubled (nothing else changed), what is the new capacitance?

Solution

$C\propto 1/d$, so doubling $d$ halves $C$: new $C=50$ pF.

Practice 3

Find the plate area needed for a parallel-plate capacitor with $C=1.0\ \mu$F and plate separation $d=1.0$ mm (no dielectric).

Solution

$A=\dfrac{Cd}{\varepsilon_0}=\dfrac{(1.0\times10^{-6})(1.0\times10^{-3})}{8.85\times10^{-12}}=113\ \text{m}^2$.

That's enormous — a good reminder of why real 1 $\mu$F capacitors use dielectrics and rolled/layered geometries rather than plain air-gap plates.

Practice 4

A 250 pF capacitor is charged to 6.0 V, then disconnected from the battery. If the plate separation is then doubled (charge stays fixed since it's isolated), find the new capacitance and new voltage.

Solution

$C$ halves: $C_{new}=125$ pF. Charge is conserved: $Q=C_{old}V_{old}=(250\times10^{-12})(6.0)=1.5\times10^{-9}$ C.

$V_{new}=Q/C_{new}=\dfrac{1.5\times10^{-9}}{125\times10^{-12}}=12\ \text{V}$ — the voltage doubles.

1 Dielectrics & Energy Storage

Slipping an insulating material (a dielectric) between the plates increases the capacitance by a factor $\kappa$ (the dielectric constant, $\kappa>1$), because the dielectric partially cancels the field between the plates, so less voltage is needed for the same charge.

Charging a capacitor takes work, because as charge builds up, each new bit of charge has to be pushed against the field of the charge already there. That work ends up stored as electrical potential energy in the field between the plates.

Key idea

$$C_{\text{with dielectric}}=\kappa C_{\text{vacuum}}=\frac{\kappa\varepsilon_0 A}{d}, \qquad U=\frac12 CV^2=\frac{Q^2}{2C}=\frac12 QV$$

All three energy formulas are equivalent — pick whichever variables you're given. Energy density in the field itself: $u=\tfrac12\varepsilon_0 E^2$ (vacuum) or $\tfrac12\kappa\varepsilon_0 E^2$ (with dielectric).

Worked example

A parallel-plate capacitor with $C_0=200$ pF (air gap) is charged to 50 V, then a dielectric with $\kappa=4$ is inserted (fully filling the gap) while the capacitor stays connected to the 50 V supply. Find the new capacitance, new charge, and change in stored energy.

$C=\kappa C_0=4\times200=800\ \text{pF}$. Voltage is unchanged (still connected to 50 V): $V=50$ V.

$Q_{new}=CV=(800\times10^{-12})(50)=4.0\times10^{-8}\ \text{C}$ (compared with $Q_0=C_0V=1.0\times10^{-8}$ C before).

$U_{new}=\tfrac12 CV^2=\tfrac12(800\times10^{-12})(50)^2=1.0\times10^{-6}\ \text{J}$; $U_0=\tfrac12(200\times10^{-12})(50)^2=2.5\times10^{-7}\ \text{J}$. Energy increased fourfold, matching $\kappa=4$ (the battery supplied the extra energy).

Practice 5

A 40 pF air-gap capacitor is filled with a dielectric of $\kappa=2.5$. Find the new capacitance.

Solution

$C=\kappa C_0=2.5\times40=100\ \text{pF}$.

Practice 6

A 5.0 $\mu$F capacitor is charged to 120 V. How much energy is stored?

Solution

$U=\tfrac12 CV^2=\tfrac12(5.0\times10^{-6})(120)^2=3.6\times10^{-2}\ \text{J}=36\ \text{mJ}$.

Practice 7

A capacitor stores 200 $\mu$J of energy while holding 40 $\mu$C of charge. Find its capacitance and the voltage across it.

Solution

Using $U=Q^2/2C$: $C=\dfrac{Q^2}{2U}=\dfrac{(40\times10^{-6})^2}{2(200\times10^{-6})}=4.0\times10^{-6}\ \text{F}=4.0\ \mu\text{F}$.

$V=Q/C=\dfrac{40\times10^{-6}}{4.0\times10^{-6}}=10\ \text{V}$.

Practice 8

A capacitor is charged to voltage $V_0$ and then disconnected from the battery before a dielectric $\kappa$ is inserted. Explain qualitatively (no calculation needed) what happens to $Q$, $C$, $V$, and $U$, and why $U$ changes even though no battery is doing work anymore.

Solution

$Q$ stays fixed (isolated, nowhere for charge to go). $C$ increases by factor $\kappa$. Since $V=Q/C$, $V$ decreases by factor $\kappa$. Energy $U=Q^2/2C$ therefore decreases by factor $\kappa$ (unlike the constant-voltage case, where energy increased). The "missing" energy goes into the mechanical work of pulling the dielectric in — the field does work on the dielectric as it's inserted.

2 Electric Current, Resistance & Ohm's Law

Current is the rate of flow of charge past a point: $I=dq/dt$. In a conductor, applying a potential difference sets up an electric field that drives a steady drift of charge carriers. For many materials ("ohmic" materials), the current is directly proportional to the applied voltage — this is Ohm's law.

Resistance depends on the material (resistivity $\rho$) and geometry: long thin wires resist more than short fat ones.

Key idea

$$I=\frac{dq}{dt}\ [\text{A}=\text{C/s}], \qquad V=IR, \qquad R=\rho\frac{L}{A}$$ $$\rho(T)=\rho_0\left[1+\alpha(T-T_0)\right]$$

$\alpha>0$ for ordinary metals (resistivity rises with temperature). Superconductors are the extreme opposite case: below a critical temperature $T_c$, $\rho\to0$ exactly.

Worked example

A copper wire ($\rho=1.68\times10^{-8}\ \Omega\cdot\text{m}$) is 2.0 m long with cross-sectional area $1.0\ \text{mm}^2$. Find its resistance, and the current through it when 3.0 V is applied.

$R=\rho\dfrac{L}{A}=\dfrac{(1.68\times10^{-8})(2.0)}{1.0\times10^{-6}}=3.36\times10^{-2}\ \Omega$.

$I=V/R=\dfrac{3.0}{3.36\times10^{-2}}=89.3\ \text{A}$.

Practice 9

A steady current of 2.5 A flows for 4 minutes. How much charge passes?

Solution

$q=I t=2.5\times(4\times60)=600\ \text{C}$.

Practice 10

A resistor carries 0.4 A when 12 V is applied. Find its resistance.

Solution

$R=V/I=12/0.4=30\ \Omega$.

Practice 11

A nichrome wire ($\rho=1.10\times10^{-6}\ \Omega\cdot\text{m}$) has resistance $R=5.0\ \Omega$ and length 0.80 m. Find its cross-sectional area and radius.

Solution

$A=\rho L/R=\dfrac{(1.10\times10^{-6})(0.80)}{5.0}=1.76\times10^{-7}\ \text{m}^2$.

$A=\pi r^2 \Rightarrow r=\sqrt{A/\pi}=\sqrt{1.76\times10^{-7}/\pi}=2.37\times10^{-4}\ \text{m}=0.237\ \text{mm}$.

Practice 12

A copper wire has resistance 2.0 $\Omega$ at $20^\circ$C. Its temperature coefficient of resistance is $\alpha=0.0039\ /^\circ\text{C}$. Find its resistance at $80^\circ$C.

Solution

$R(T)=R_0[1+\alpha(T-T_0)]=2.0[1+0.0039(80-20)]=2.0[1+0.234]=2.47\ \Omega$.

Practice 13

Explain why a superconductor's resistance dropping to exactly zero below $T_c$ means a current, once started in a closed superconducting loop, can in principle persist forever with no battery.

Solution

With $R=0$, Ohm's law gives $V=IR=0$ for any current — no voltage (no energy dissipation) is needed to sustain the flow. Since there's no resistive power loss ($P=I^2R=0$), there's nothing draining the current's energy away, so it keeps circulating indefinitely once established.

3 Resistors in Series & Parallel; Power

Just like capacitors, resistors combine differently depending on whether they share the same current (series) or the same voltage (parallel). Notice the pattern is the opposite of capacitors: resistors in series simply add, while resistors in parallel add as reciprocals.

Power is the rate at which electrical energy is converted (usually to heat, in a resistor).

Key idea

Series (same $I$ through each): $$R_{eq}=R_1+R_2+\cdots$$ Parallel (same $V$ across each): $$\frac{1}{R_{eq}}=\frac{1}{R_1}+\frac{1}{R_2}+\cdots$$ Power dissipated in a resistor: $$P=IV=I^2R=\frac{V^2}{R}$$

Worked example

Resistors $R_1=6\ \Omega$ and $R_2=3\ \Omega$ are connected (a) in series and (b) in parallel to a 12 V battery. Find the equivalent resistance, total current, and total power delivered in each case.

(a) Series: $R_{eq}=6+3=9\ \Omega$. $I=V/R_{eq}=12/9=1.33\ \text{A}$. $P=IV=1.33\times12=16.0\ \text{W}$.

(b) Parallel: $\dfrac{1}{R_{eq}}=\dfrac16+\dfrac13=\dfrac12 \Rightarrow R_{eq}=2\ \Omega$. $I=12/2=6.0\ \text{A}$. $P=IV=6.0\times12=72.0\ \text{W}$.

Parallel draws far more current and power from the same battery — each resistor "sees" the full 12 V rather than sharing it.

Practice 14

Three resistors, 4 $\Omega$, 6 $\Omega$ and 10 $\Omega$, are connected in series. Find the equivalent resistance.

Solution

$R_{eq}=4+6+10=20\ \Omega$.

Practice 15

Two resistors of 8 $\Omega$ and 8 $\Omega$ are connected in parallel. Find the equivalent resistance, and explain the general rule for $N$ identical resistors $R$ in parallel.

Solution

$\dfrac{1}{R_{eq}}=\dfrac18+\dfrac18=\dfrac14 \Rightarrow R_{eq}=4\ \Omega$ (half of a single resistor, as expected for two equal ones).

General rule: $N$ identical resistors $R$ in parallel give $R_{eq}=R/N$.

Practice 16

A 100 $\Omega$ resistor is rated to safely dissipate 0.5 W. What is the maximum voltage that can be applied across it?

Solution

$P=V^2/R \Rightarrow V=\sqrt{PR}=\sqrt{0.5\times100}=7.07\ \text{V}$.

Practice 17

A 12 $\Omega$ resistor and a 4 $\Omega$ resistor are in series, connected to a 16 V battery. Find the current, the voltage across each resistor, and the power dissipated in each.

Solution

$R_{eq}=16\ \Omega \Rightarrow I=V/R_{eq}=16/16=1.0\ \text{A}$ (same current through both, since they're in series).

$V_{12\Omega}=IR=1.0\times12=12\ \text{V}$; $\ V_{4\Omega}=1.0\times4=4\ \text{V}$ (they add to 16 V, checks out).

$P_{12\Omega}=I^2R=1.0^2\times12=12\ \text{W}$; $\ P_{4\Omega}=1.0^2\times4=4\ \text{W}$.

Practice 18

A hair dryer is rated 1500 W at 240 V. Find the current it draws and its effective resistance.

Solution

$I=P/V=1500/240=6.25\ \text{A}$.

$R=V/I=240/6.25=38.4\ \Omega$ (equivalently $R=V^2/P=240^2/1500=38.4\ \Omega$).
