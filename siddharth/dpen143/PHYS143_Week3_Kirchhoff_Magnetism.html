Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Kirchhoff's junction (current) rule: $\sum I_{in}=\sum I_{out}$ at any node (charge conservation).
  • Kirchhoff's loop (voltage) rule: $\sum \Delta V = 0$ around any closed loop (energy conservation). Drops of $IR$ across resistors; rises/drops of $\varepsilon$ across EMF sources depending on traversal direction.
  • Magnetic force on a moving charge: $\vec F = q\vec v\times\vec B$, magnitude $F=qvB\sin\theta$, direction from the right-hand rule; $\vec F\perp\vec v$ always, so magnetic forces do no work and only change direction, not speed.
  • Circular motion in a uniform $\vec B$: $r=\dfrac{mv}{qB}$, period $T=\dfrac{2\pi m}{qB}$ (independent of speed).
  • Biot-Savart law: $d\vec B = \dfrac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\dfrac{I\,d\vec l\times\hat r}{r^2}$, with $\mu_0=4\pi\times10^{-7}\ \text{T·m/A}$.
  • Standard results: infinite straight wire, $B=\dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}$; centre of a circular loop of radius $R$, $B=\dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2R}$.
  • Drude-Lorentz model: current carriers drift with $v_d=\dfrac{I}{nqA}$; resistivity emerges from collisions, $\rho=\dfrac{m}{nq^2\tau}$ ($\tau$ = mean time between collisions).

0 Kirchhoff's Laws & Circuit Analysis

Series/parallel reduction breaks down as soon as a circuit has more than one EMF source or a genuinely non-reducible network (e.g. a bridge). Kirchhoff's two rules handle any circuit, however tangled, because they come directly from the two things that must always be conserved: charge and energy.

Key idea

Junction rule (charge conservation): the total current flowing into a node equals the total current flowing out.

Loop rule (energy conservation): going around any closed loop and adding up all the potential changes, you return to your starting potential — the sum is zero. Convention: crossing a resistor in the direction of assumed current gives a drop $-IR$; crossing against it gives $+IR$. Crossing a battery from $-$ to $+$ gives $+\varepsilon$; from $+$ to $-$ gives $-\varepsilon$.

Method: (1) label a current and direction in every branch; (2) apply the junction rule at enough nodes; (3) apply the loop rule around enough independent loops to get as many equations as unknowns; (4) solve simultaneously. Negative answers just mean the true current runs opposite to your guessed direction — that's fine.

Worked example

A circuit has a 10 V battery in series with a 2 $\Omega$ resistor, which then splits into two parallel branches: a 6 $\Omega$ resistor and a 3 $\Omega$ resistor, recombining back to the battery. Find the current through each resistor.

The two parallel resistors share the same voltage $V_p$. Their combined resistance: $\dfrac{1}{R_p}=\dfrac16+\dfrac13=\dfrac12 \Rightarrow R_p=2\ \Omega$.

Total circuit resistance $=2+2=4\ \Omega$ (series). Total current from the battery: $I=\varepsilon/R_{total}=10/4=2.5\ \text{A}$ — this is the current through the 2 $\Omega$ resistor.

Voltage across the parallel section: $V_p=IR_p=2.5\times2=5\ \text{V}$. Then $I_{6\Omega}=V_p/6=5/6=0.83\ \text{A}$ and $I_{3\Omega}=V_p/3=5/3=1.67\ \text{A}$. Check (junction rule): $0.83+1.67=2.5\ \text{A}$. ✓

Practice 1

At a junction, three wires meet. Current of 5 A and 3 A flow in, and one wire carries current out. Find the current in that outgoing wire.

Solution

By the junction rule, current in = current out: $I_{out}=5+3=8\ \text{A}$.

Practice 2

A single loop circuit has a 9 V battery and two resistors, 4 $\Omega$ and 5 $\Omega$, in series. Using the loop rule, write the loop equation and solve for the current.

Solution

Going around the loop: $\varepsilon - IR_1 - IR_2 = 0 \Rightarrow 9-4I-5I=0 \Rightarrow 9=9I \Rightarrow I=1.0\ \text{A}$.

Practice 3

A circuit has two batteries and one resistor, all in a single loop: a 12 V battery and a 4 V battery (oriented so they oppose each other) in series with a 2 $\Omega$ resistor. Find the current.

Solution

Net EMF driving the loop $=12-4=8\ \text{V}$ (they oppose, so subtract). Loop rule: $8-I(2)=0 \Rightarrow I=4.0\ \text{A}$.

Practice 4

Two parallel branches connect the same pair of nodes: branch A has EMF 6 V and resistance 2 $\Omega$; branch B has EMF 3 V and resistance 1 $\Omega$ (both batteries oriented to drive current the same way around the loop formed by the two branches). Set up the loop and junction equations you would need to find the branch currents. (You don't need to solve fully — just set it up.)

Solution

Let $I_A$ flow through branch A and $I_B$ through branch B, both defined flowing from node 1 to node 2 (say). Since the two branches connect the same two nodes with nothing else attached, by the junction rule the current returning must equal $I_A+I_B$ flowing back through some external return path (or, if it's just these two branches forming the whole loop, $I_A=-I_B$, i.e. one battery drives current backward through the other branch).

Loop rule around the single loop formed by A and B: $6-I_A(2) - [-(3-I_B(1))] = 0$, i.e. $6-2I_A = 3-1\cdot I_B$ with $I_B=-I_A$ (same wire, opposite reference direction) — this is exactly the kind of algebra Kirchhoff's rules are built for; the key step examiners look for is correctly defining consistent current directions and writing one loop equation plus the junction relation between $I_A$ and $I_B$.

1 Magnetic Force on a Moving Charge

Unlike the electric force, the magnetic force acts only on moving charge, and it's perpendicular to both the velocity and the field — a fundamentally different kind of force from anything in electrostatics or mechanics so far.

Because $\vec F\perp\vec v$ always, the magnetic force can never speed up or slow down a charge — it only steers it. A charged particle moving perpendicular to a uniform $\vec B$ travels in a circle.

Key idea

$$\vec F = q\vec v\times\vec B, \qquad F=qvB\sin\theta$$

Direction: right-hand rule — point fingers along $\vec v$, curl toward $\vec B$, thumb gives $\vec v\times\vec B$ (then flip for negative $q$). For circular motion, the magnetic force supplies the centripetal force: $qvB=\dfrac{mv^2}{r} \Rightarrow r=\dfrac{mv}{qB}$, and $T=\dfrac{2\pi r}{v}=\dfrac{2\pi m}{qB}$.

Worked example

A proton moves at $2.0\times10^6$ m/s perpendicular to a field $B=0.50$ T. Find the magnetic force on it and the radius of its circular path. ($m_p=1.67\times10^{-27}$ kg, $q=1.6\times10^{-19}$ C.)

$F=qvB\sin90^\circ=(1.6\times10^{-19})(2.0\times10^6)(0.50)=1.6\times10^{-13}\ \text{N}$.

$r=\dfrac{mv}{qB}=\dfrac{(1.67\times10^{-27})(2.0\times10^6)}{(1.6\times10^{-19})(0.50)}=4.18\times10^{-2}\ \text{m}=4.18\ \text{cm}$.

Practice 5

An electron moves at $1.0\times10^7$ m/s at right angles to a 0.20 T field. Find the magnitude of the magnetic force on it.

Solution

$F=qvB=(1.6\times10^{-19})(1.0\times10^7)(0.20)=3.2\times10^{-13}\ \text{N}$.

Practice 6

A charge $+3\,\mu$C moves at $500$ m/s at an angle of $30^\circ$ to a field of 0.8 T. Find the force magnitude.

Solution

$F=qvB\sin\theta=(3\times10^{-6})(500)(0.8)\sin30^\circ=(1.2\times10^{-3})(0.5)=6.0\times10^{-4}\ \text{N}$.

Practice 7

Explain why a charged particle moving parallel to a magnetic field feels no magnetic force, using the vector formula.

Solution

$F=qvB\sin\theta$ with $\theta$ the angle between $\vec v$ and $\vec B$. If the particle moves parallel to $\vec B$, $\theta=0^\circ$, so $\sin\theta=0$ and $F=0$. Equivalently, $\vec v\times\vec B=0$ whenever $\vec v$ and $\vec B$ are parallel (or antiparallel), since the cross product of parallel vectors vanishes.

Practice 8

A proton in a cyclotron moves in a circle of radius 0.30 m under a field of 1.2 T. Find its speed and the period of its orbit.

Solution

From $r=mv/(qB)$: $v=\dfrac{qBr}{m}=\dfrac{(1.6\times10^{-19})(1.2)(0.30)}{1.67\times10^{-27}}=3.45\times10^7\ \text{m/s}$.

$T=\dfrac{2\pi m}{qB}=\dfrac{2\pi(1.67\times10^{-27})}{(1.6\times10^{-19})(1.2)}=5.46\times10^{-8}\ \text{s}$ (note: independent of $v$ or $r$ individually — you could also get this straight from the formula without knowing $v$).

Practice 9

Why do we say the magnetic force does no work on a moving charge, and what does that imply about the particle's kinetic energy and speed as it moves through a purely magnetic field region?

Solution

Work is $dW=\vec F\cdot d\vec s = \vec F\cdot\vec v\,dt$. Since $\vec F=q\vec v\times\vec B$ is always perpendicular to $\vec v$, $\vec F\cdot\vec v=0$ at every instant, so $dW=0$. The magnetic force can never change the particle's kinetic energy, hence never changes its speed — only its direction of motion.

2 Source of the Magnetic Field: the Biot-Savart Law

Just as electric charges create electric fields, moving charges (currents) create magnetic fields. The Biot-Savart law is the magnetic analogue of Coulomb's law for continuous distributions — it tells you the tiny field $d\vec B$ contributed by a small current element $I\,d\vec l$, which you then integrate along the whole current path.

Key idea

$$d\vec B = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\frac{I\,d\vec l\times\hat r}{r^2}, \qquad \mu_0=4\pi\times10^{-7}\ \text{T·m/A}$$

Two standard integrated results worth memorising: infinite straight wire, field at perpendicular distance $r$: $B=\dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}$ (circling the wire, right-hand rule: thumb along $I$, fingers curl in direction of $\vec B$). Centre of a circular loop radius $R$: $B=\dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2R}$.

Worked example

Find the magnetic field 4.0 cm from a long straight wire carrying 15 A, and the field at the centre of a circular loop of radius 4.0 cm carrying the same current.

Straight wire: $B=\dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}=\dfrac{(4\pi\times10^{-7})(15)}{2\pi(0.04)}=\dfrac{(2\times10^{-7})(15)}{0.04}=7.5\times10^{-5}\ \text{T}$.

Loop centre: $B=\dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2R}=\dfrac{(4\pi\times10^{-7})(15)}{2(0.04)}=\dfrac{1.885\times10^{-5}\times15}{0.08}$... more directly: $\mu_0 I=1.885\times10^{-5}$, divide by $2R=0.08$: $B=2.36\times10^{-4}\ \text{T}$ — about three times larger than the straight-wire result at the same distance, because every element of the loop contributes constructively at the centre.

Practice 10

A long straight wire carries 8.0 A. Find the field at 10 cm from the wire.

Solution

$B=\dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}=\dfrac{(4\pi\times10^{-7})(8.0)}{2\pi(0.10)}=\dfrac{(2\times10^{-7})(8.0)}{0.10}=1.6\times10^{-5}\ \text{T}$.

Practice 11

A circular loop of radius 3.0 cm carries a current such that the field at its centre is $2.0\times10^{-4}$ T. Find the current.

Solution

$B=\dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2R}\Rightarrow I=\dfrac{2RB}{\mu_0}=\dfrac{2(0.03)(2.0\times10^{-4})}{4\pi\times10^{-7}}=9.55\ \text{A}$.

Practice 12

Two long straight parallel wires, 5.0 cm apart, each carry 10 A in the same direction. Find the field at the midpoint between them, and explain qualitatively whether they attract or repel.

Solution

At the midpoint (2.5 cm from each wire), each wire produces $B=\dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}=\dfrac{(4\pi\times10^{-7})(10)}{2\pi(0.025)}=8.0\times10^{-5}\ \text{T}$.

By the right-hand rule, at the midpoint the two fields point in opposite directions (each wire's field circles around it, and at the midpoint the circulation senses oppose), so they cancel: $B_{net}=0$.

The wires themselves attract: parallel currents in the same direction always attract (each wire sits in the other's field, and $\vec F=I\vec L\times\vec B$ works out to be directed toward the other wire).

Practice 13

Explain why the Biot-Savart law needs a cross product ($d\vec l\times\hat r$) rather than a simple product like Coulomb's law, in terms of what determines the direction of $d\vec B$.

Solution

Coulomb's law describes a field from a stationary point charge, where the only special direction is the line from source to field point — a scalar magnitude times $\hat r$ suffices. A current element also has an intrinsic direction (the direction of current flow, $d\vec l$), and the resulting $d\vec B$ is perpendicular to both $d\vec l$ and $\hat r$ — exactly what a cross product produces. Without it, there'd be no way to encode the fact that the field circles around the current direction rather than pointing radially.

3 The Drude-Lorentz Model of Conduction

Ohm's law ($V=IR$) is an empirical, macroscopic statement. The Drude-Lorentz model gives a microscopic picture of why it holds: conduction electrons are treated as a "gas" of free particles that accelerate under the applied field but repeatedly collide with the lattice, losing their drift velocity each time. The result is a steady average drift velocity rather than ever-increasing speed.

Key idea

Drift velocity from current: $$I=nqv_dA \ \Rightarrow\ v_d=\frac{I}{nqA}$$ where $n$ is charge-carrier density (carriers per m$^3$), $q$ the carrier charge, $A$ the cross-sectional area.

Microscopic origin of resistivity: between collisions (average time $\tau$), a carrier accelerates as $a=qE/m$, reaching average drift speed $v_d=\tfrac12 a\tau$ (or $a\tau$ depending on convention) before scattering resets it. Combining with $J=nqv_d=\sigma E$ gives $$\rho=\frac{1}{\sigma}=\frac{m}{nq^2\tau}$$ — resistivity is set by carrier density, effective mass, and how often carriers scatter (short $\tau$ = more collisions = higher resistivity), which is exactly why resistivity rises with temperature (more thermal vibration, more scattering, smaller $\tau$).

Worked example

A copper wire of cross-sectional area $2.0\ \text{mm}^2$ carries 5.0 A. Copper has a free-electron density of $n=8.5\times10^{28}\ \text{m}^{-3}$. Find the electron drift velocity.

$v_d=\dfrac{I}{nqA}=\dfrac{5.0}{(8.5\times10^{28})(1.6\times10^{-19})(2.0\times10^{-6})}$.

Denominator: $(8.5\times10^{28})(1.6\times10^{-19})=1.36\times10^{10}$; times $2.0\times10^{-6}$ gives $2.72\times10^{4}$.

$v_d=\dfrac{5.0}{2.72\times10^{4}}=1.84\times10^{-4}\ \text{m/s}$ — a fraction of a millimetre per second. This is why signals travel down a wire almost instantly (the field propagates near light speed) even though individual electrons drift extremely slowly.

Practice 14

A wire of cross-sectional area 1.0 mm$^2$ carries 2.0 A, with carrier density $n=8.5\times10^{28}\ \text{m}^{-3}$ (copper) and carrier charge $e$. Find the drift velocity.

Solution

$v_d=\dfrac{I}{neA}=\dfrac{2.0}{(8.5\times10^{28})(1.6\times10^{-19})(1.0\times10^{-6})}=\dfrac{2.0}{1.36\times10^4}=1.47\times10^{-4}\ \text{m/s}$.

Practice 15

If the cross-sectional area of a wire is halved but the same current is maintained, what happens to the drift velocity? Explain using the formula.

Solution

$v_d=I/(nqA)$, so with $I$, $n$, $q$ fixed, $v_d\propto1/A$. Halving $A$ doubles $v_d$ — the same number of carriers per second must now squeeze through half the cross-section, so each must move faster on average.

Practice 16

Using $\rho=\dfrac{m}{nq^2\tau}$, explain qualitatively why increasing temperature increases a metal's resistivity, in terms of $\tau$.

Solution

Higher temperature means the lattice ions vibrate more vigorously, so conduction electrons scatter off them more frequently — the average time between collisions $\tau$ decreases. Since $\rho\propto1/\tau$, a smaller $\tau$ means a larger $\rho$: resistivity increases with temperature, consistent with the empirical $\rho(T)=\rho_0[1+\alpha(T-T_0)]$ relation from Week 2.

Practice 17

Two wires of the same material and length carry the same current, but wire B has twice the cross-sectional area of wire A. Compare their drift velocities and their resistances.

Solution

Drift velocity: $v_d\propto1/A$, so wire A (smaller area) has twice the drift velocity of wire B.

Resistance: $R=\rho L/A$, so wire A (smaller area) has twice the resistance of wire B.
