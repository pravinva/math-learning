Special Relativity: Postulates, Time Dilation & the Doppler Effect

Modern Physics starts here. Newtonian mechanics assumes velocities add simply and time is absolute — both break down near the speed of light. Einstein's two postulates rebuild mechanics from scratch, giving time dilation, length contraction, the Lorentz transformation, and a Doppler effect that works differently from the sound-wave version you already know. Lay of the land — this week's toolkit

0 Einstein's Postulates & the Breakdown of Newtonian Relativity

In Newtonian (Galilean) relativity, velocities simply add: if you throw a ball forward at 5 m/s from a train moving at 20 m/s, a trackside observer sees the ball at 25 m/s. This seems obviously true — and works perfectly for everyday speeds. But experiments (most famously Michelson-Morley) found that light's speed does not follow this rule: no matter how fast the source or observer moves, light in vacuum is always measured at the same speed, $c$.

Einstein took this seriously as a fundamental law of nature rather than an experimental oddity, and built special relativity on two postulates.

Key idea

Postulate 1 (principle of relativity): the laws of physics take the same form in every inertial (non-accelerating) reference frame — no experiment performed entirely inside a closed, smoothly-moving lab can reveal its own velocity.

Postulate 2 (constancy of $c$): the speed of light in vacuum is $c$ for every inertial observer, regardless of the motion of the light's source.

Worked example

A spaceship moves at $0.5c$ relative to Earth and fires a laser pulse forward. What speed does a stationary Earth observer measure for the laser light?

Naive (Galilean) reasoning would suggest $c+0.5c=1.5c$.

Postulate 2 says this is wrong: every inertial observer measures light in vacuum at exactly $c$, independent of the source's motion. The Earth observer measures the light at $c=3.00\times10^8\ \text{m/s}$ — not a bit more, regardless of how fast the ship was moving when it fired.

Practice 1

Which of Einstein's two postulates explains why an astronaut in a windowless spaceship moving at constant velocity cannot perform any onboard experiment to detect that they are moving (as opposed to sitting still)?

Solution

Postulate 1 (the principle of relativity): the laws of physics are identical in every inertial frame, so no internal experiment can distinguish "moving at constant velocity" from "at rest" — there is no privileged, detectable state of absolute rest.

Practice 2

A star emits light while moving directly away from Earth at high speed. According to Newtonian intuition (like sound from a receding source), you might expect the emitted light's speed (as measured on Earth) to be slower than $c$. What does Einstein's second postulate say instead?

Solution

The second postulate says the speed of light is $c$ for every observer, regardless of the source's motion. The star's motion affects the light's observed frequency (Doppler shift, covered later this week) but never its speed — an Earth observer still measures exactly $c$.

Practice 3

Explain in one or two sentences why Newtonian velocity addition, $u'=u-v$, is inconsistent with postulate 2 when $u=c$.

Solution

If light travels at $c$ in one frame, Galilean addition would predict a different speed, $c-v$, in a frame moving at $v$ relative to the first — but postulate 2 insists every inertial observer measures exactly $c$. Since these two predictions contradict each other whenever $v\neq0$, ordinary Galilean addition cannot be exactly correct; it must be replaced by a rule (the relativistic velocity addition formula, covered below) that always returns $c$ for light.

Practice 4

Historically, physicists initially proposed the existence of a medium (the "luminiferous ether") through which light waves would travel, with light's speed being $c$ only relative to that medium. Explain briefly why the Michelson-Morley experiment's null result (no ether drift detected) was consistent with, and effectively led to, Einstein's second postulate.

Solution

If light needed an ether medium, Earth's motion through that ether should have produced a measurable difference in light's speed depending on direction (like a swimmer's speed relative to the shore depending on whether they swim with or against a current). No such difference was ever detected, however precisely measured. Einstein's second postulate simply accepts this result at face value: light's speed is $c$ for all observers, full stop, with no need for an ether at all.

1 Time Dilation & Length Contraction

If the speed of light really is the same for everyone, something else has to give — and it turns out to be our assumptions about time and space themselves. Moving clocks run slow (as measured by a stationary observer), and moving objects appear shortened along their direction of motion.

Both effects are described by the same factor, $\gamma$, which is essentially 1 at everyday speeds (so we never notice) but grows rapidly as $v\to c$.

Key idea

$$\gamma=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2}}, \qquad \Delta t = \gamma\,\Delta t_0, \qquad L=\frac{L_0}{\gamma}$$

$\Delta t_0$ (proper time) is measured by a clock present at both events, in its own rest frame — every other observer measures a longer interval. $L_0$ (proper length) is the length measured in the object's own rest frame — every other observer measures a shorter length, only along the direction of relative motion.

Worked example

A muon has a proper lifetime of $\tau_0=2.2\ \mu$s. It's created high in the atmosphere moving at $v=0.98c$ toward the ground. Find its dilated lifetime as measured on Earth, and how far it travels (on average) before decaying.

$\gamma=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.98^2}}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.9604}}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{0.0396}}=\dfrac{1}{0.199}=5.03$.

$\Delta t=\gamma\tau_0=5.03\times2.2\ \mu\text{s}=11.06\ \mu\text{s}$.

Distance travelled: $d=v\Delta t=(0.98)(3.00\times10^8)(11.06\times10^{-6})=3.25\times10^{3}\ \text{m}\approx3.25\ \text{km}$. Without time dilation, a $2.2\ \mu$s lifetime at nearly $c$ would only cover about 650 m — time dilation is why muons created in the upper atmosphere are routinely detected at ground level.

Practice 5

Find $\gamma$ for a particle travelling at $v=0.6c$.

Solution

$\gamma=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.6^2}}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.36}}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{0.64}}=\dfrac{1}{0.8}=1.25$.

Practice 6

A spaceship has a proper length of 100 m and travels at $0.8c$ relative to Earth. Find its length as measured by an Earth observer.

Solution

$\gamma=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.64}}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{0.36}}=\dfrac{1}{0.6}=1.667$.

$L=L_0/\gamma=100/1.667=60.0\ \text{m}$.

Practice 7

A clock aboard a spacecraft moving at $0.9c$ relative to Earth ticks off exactly 1.0 hour (its own proper time). How much time elapses on Earth for the same interval?

Solution

$\gamma=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.81}}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{0.19}}=\dfrac{1}{0.4359}=2.294$.

$\Delta t=\gamma\Delta t_0=2.294\times1.0\ \text{hr}=2.29\ \text{hr}$.

Practice 8

In the "twin paradox," one twin stays on Earth while the other travels to a distant star at relativistic speed and returns. Both twins might argue "from my point of view, the other twin was moving, so their clock should be slow." Explain briefly why this apparent symmetry is broken, so that the travelling twin really does return younger.

Solution

Time dilation as derived above strictly applies to inertial (constant-velocity, non-accelerating) frames. The Earth-bound twin remains in a single inertial frame throughout. The travelling twin, however, must accelerate to turn around and come back — they occupy at least two different inertial frames over the course of the trip. This breaks the symmetry: only the Earth twin's aging can be consistently computed from one unchanging inertial frame, and a careful analysis (accounting for the turnaround) shows the travelling twin genuinely accumulates less proper time, i.e. ages less.

2 The Lorentz Transformation Equations

Time dilation and length contraction are special cases of a more general result: the Lorentz transformation, which relates the position and time coordinates of the same event as measured in two different inertial frames moving at relative velocity $v$. It replaces the (now known to be only approximate) Galilean transformation $x'=x-vt$, $t'=t$.

Key idea

$$x'=\gamma(x-vt), \qquad t'=\gamma\left(t-\frac{vx}{c^2}\right)$$

Notice $t'$ depends on both $t$ and $x$ — this is the mathematical root of the relativity of simultaneity: two events simultaneous in one frame ($\Delta t=0$) are generally not simultaneous in another frame, if they occur at different locations ($\Delta x\neq0$).

Relativistic velocity addition follows from the same transformation: if an object moves at speed $u$ in frame $S$, and frame $S'$ moves at $v$ relative to $S$, then its speed in $S'$ is $$u'=\frac{u-v}{1-uv/c^2}$$

Worked example

Two events occur at the same location in the Earth frame ($\Delta x=0$), separated by $\Delta t=1.0\ \mu$s. Find the spatial and time separation of these same two events as measured in a frame moving at $v=0.6c$ relative to Earth.

$\gamma=1.25$ (computed above for $v=0.6c$).

$\Delta t' = \gamma\left(\Delta t - \dfrac{v\,\Delta x}{c^2}\right)=\gamma\,\Delta t$ (since $\Delta x=0$) $=1.25\times1.0\ \mu\text{s}=1.25\ \mu\text{s}$ — this is just time dilation, as expected.

$\Delta x' = \gamma(\Delta x - v\,\Delta t) = -\gamma v\,\Delta t = -(1.25)(0.6\times3.00\times10^8)(1.0\times10^{-6}) = -225\ \text{m}$.

Although the two events happen at the same place in Earth's frame, the moving frame sees them separated by 225 m — because that frame is sliding past Earth between the two events.

Practice 9

A spaceship moving at $0.5c$ relative to Earth fires a probe forward at $0.5c$ relative to itself. Use relativistic velocity addition to find the probe's speed relative to Earth. (Use $u'=\dfrac{u+v}{1+uv/c^2}$ for velocities in the same direction.)

Solution

$u'=\dfrac{0.5c+0.5c}{1+(0.5)(0.5)}=\dfrac{c}{1.25}=0.8c$.

Note this is less than the naive Galilean sum of $1.0c$ — the relativistic formula automatically prevents speeds from ever reaching or exceeding $c$ by combining two sub-light speeds.

Practice 10

Show algebraically that if $u=c$ (light) in one frame, the relativistic velocity addition formula gives $u'=c$ in any frame moving at speed $v$ relative to it — confirming consistency with postulate 2.

Solution

$u'=\dfrac{u+v}{1+uv/c^2}=\dfrac{c+v}{1+vc/c^2}=\dfrac{c+v}{1+v/c}=\dfrac{c+v}{(c+v)/c}=c$.

The $(c+v)$ terms cancel, leaving exactly $c$ regardless of $v$ — exactly what postulate 2 requires.

Practice 11

Two events are simultaneous in the Earth frame ($\Delta t=0$) and separated by $\Delta x=300$ m. Find the time separation $\Delta t'$ of these events as measured in a frame moving at $v=0.6c$ relative to Earth, and comment on what this shows.

Solution

$\gamma=1.25$. $\Delta t'=\gamma\left(\Delta t-\dfrac{v\Delta x}{c^2}\right)=1.25\left(0-\dfrac{(0.6\times3.00\times10^8)(300)}{(3.00\times10^8)^2}\right)$.

$=1.25\times\left(-\dfrac{0.6\times300}{3.00\times10^8}\right)=1.25\times(-6.0\times10^{-7})=-7.5\times10^{-7}\ \text{s}=-0.75\ \mu\text{s}$.

This demonstrates the relativity of simultaneity: events that are simultaneous in one inertial frame are generally not simultaneous in another, whenever they're separated in space.

3 Applications of the Doppler Effect

Just as with sound, relative motion between a source and observer of light shifts the observed frequency — but the relativistic version depends only on the relative velocity between source and observer (there's no separate "medium" to be moving relative to, unlike sound in air), and it includes a built-in time-dilation correction that the classical sound-wave formula doesn't have.

Key idea

Approaching (source and observer getting closer): $$f_{obs}=f_{source}\sqrt{\frac{1+\beta}{1-\beta}} \quad (\text{blueshift, higher }f\text{, shorter }\lambda)$$ Receding (getting farther apart): $$f_{obs}=f_{source}\sqrt{\frac{1-\beta}{1+\beta}} \quad (\text{redshift, lower }f\text{, longer }\lambda)$$ with $\beta=v/c$.

Worked example

A star recedes from Earth at $v=0.10c$, emitting green light at $f_0=5.00\times10^{14}$ Hz. Find the observed frequency.

$f_{obs}=f_0\sqrt{\dfrac{1-0.10}{1+0.10}}=f_0\sqrt{\dfrac{0.90}{1.10}}=f_0\sqrt{0.8182}=f_0\times0.9045$.

$f_{obs}=(5.00\times10^{14})(0.9045)=4.52\times10^{14}\ \text{Hz}$ — a lower frequency (redshifted), consistent with the star receding.

Practice 12

A spaceship approaches Earth at $v=0.20c$, transmitting a radio signal at $f_0=100$ MHz. Find the frequency Earth receives.

Solution

$f_{obs}=f_0\sqrt{\dfrac{1+0.20}{1-0.20}}=f_0\sqrt{\dfrac{1.20}{0.80}}=f_0\sqrt{1.5}=f_0\times1.2247$.

$f_{obs}=100\times1.2247=122.5\ \text{MHz}$.

Practice 13

Explain briefly why the relativistic Doppler formula depends only on the relative velocity between source and observer, unlike the classical (sound) Doppler effect, which gives different formulas depending on whether the source or the observer is the one moving.

Solution

Classical sound Doppler treats the air (medium) as a preferred reference frame — "source moving through still air" and "observer moving through still air with a stationary source" are physically different situations relative to that medium, giving different formulas. Light has no medium (postulate 1 says there's no preferred frame at all), so only the relative velocity between source and observer can matter — there's nothing else for either party's motion to be measured against.

Practice 14

A distant galaxy's spectral line is observed at a wavelength 1.20 times its known emitted (rest-frame) wavelength (redshifted). Using $f\propto1/\lambda$, estimate the galaxy's recession velocity as a fraction of $c$.

Solution

$\dfrac{f_{obs}}{f_0}=\dfrac{\lambda_0}{\lambda_{obs}}=\dfrac{1}{1.20}=0.8333=\sqrt{\dfrac{1-\beta}{1+\beta}}$.

Squaring: $0.6944=\dfrac{1-\beta}{1+\beta} \Rightarrow 0.6944(1+\beta)=1-\beta \Rightarrow 0.6944+0.6944\beta=1-\beta$.

$1.6944\beta=0.3056 \Rightarrow \beta=0.180$, so $v\approx0.18c$.
