Topic 1 · Build intuition first
Vectors and Engineering Direction Thinking
Physics becomes easy when you stop seeing numbers as scalars only and start seeing direction + magnitude together.

Why vectors matter in DPEN143

Engineering systems rarely happen in one dimension. A force on a crane cable, an electric field from several charges, or a magnetic force on a current-carrying wire all require direction-aware reasoning. Vectors are the language that keeps your physics correct.

Core Vector Form
$$\vec{A} = A_x \hat{i} + A_y \hat{j} + A_z \hat{k},\quad |\vec{A}| = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2 + A_z^2}$$

Concept build-up

  • Start with components: break angled vectors into x/y parts.
  • Add components, not magnitudes, when combining vectors.
  • Recover resultant magnitude and direction at the end.
Engineer mindset: never trust intuition alone for direction problems. Resolve, combine, reconstruct.

Solved examples

Worked Example 1

Given: $\vec{A}=8$ N at $40^\circ$, $\vec{B}=6$ N at $140^\circ$.

Step 1: $A_x=8\cos40^\circ=6.13$, $A_y=8\sin40^\circ=5.14$

Step 2: $B_x=6\cos140^\circ=-4.60$, $B_y=6\sin140^\circ=3.86$

Step 3: $R_x=1.53$, $R_y=9.00$

Step 4: $|\vec{R}|=\sqrt{1.53^2+9^2}=9.13$ N, $\theta=\tan^{-1}(9/1.53)=80.3^\circ$

Worked Example 2

A drone has velocity components $v_x=12$ m/s and $v_y=-5$ m/s. Magnitude is $13$ m/s; direction is $22.6^\circ$ below +x. Negative component immediately tells you direction without guessing.

Worked Example 3

For $\vec{F}=3\hat{i}+4\hat{j}$ and displacement $\vec{s}=2\hat{i}+1\hat{j}$, work is dot product:

$W=\vec{F}\cdot\vec{s}=3(2)+4(1)=10\ \text{J}$.

Practice set (Topic 1)
  1. Two forces act at a pin joint: $F_1=18$ N at $35^\circ$ and $F_2=12$ N at $150^\circ$. Find resultant components, magnitude, and direction.
    Answer
    $F_{1x}=14.74$, $F_{1y}=10.33$ N; $F_{2x}=-10.39$, $F_{2y}=6.00$ N. So $R_x=4.35$ N, $R_y=16.33$ N, $|\vec R|=16.90$ N, $\theta=\tan^{-1}(16.33/4.35)=75.1^\circ$.
  2. A force $\vec{P}$ balances $\vec{A}=6\hat{i}+8\hat{j}$ N and $\vec{B}=-2\hat{i}+3\hat{j}$ N. Find $\vec{P}$.
    Answer
    For equilibrium: $\vec A+\vec B+\vec P=0$. So $\vec P=-(\vec A+\vec B)=-(4\hat{i}+11\hat{j})=-4\hat{i}-11\hat{j}$ N. Magnitude $=11.70$ N.
  3. A 20 N force acts at $40^\circ$ to the horizontal while displacement is 5 m along +x. Calculate work done.
    Answer
    $W=Fs\cos\theta=20\times5\times\cos40^\circ=76.6$ J.
  4. Given $\vec{u}=3\hat{i}-4\hat{j}$, find the unit vector $\hat{u}$ and projection of $\vec{v}=8\hat{i}+6\hat{j}$ onto $\vec{u}$.
    Answer
    $|\vec u|=5$, so $\hat u=(3/5)\hat i-(4/5)\hat j$. Scalar projection: $\vec v\cdot\hat u=8(3/5)+6(-4/5)=0$. So $\vec v$ has zero projection on $\vec u$ (perpendicular).
  5. For $\vec{a}=5\hat{i}+2\hat{j}$ and $\vec{b}=x\hat{i}-5\hat{j}$, find $x$ such that vectors are perpendicular.
    Answer
    Perpendicular $\Rightarrow \vec a\cdot\vec b=0$: $5x+2(-5)=0\Rightarrow 5x-10=0\Rightarrow x=2$.
Topic 2 · Electrostatics start
Electric Charge and Coulomb's Law
From force vectors in mechanics to force vectors between charges: same vector language, new physical source.

Concept ladder

Charge creates electric interaction. Positive and negative signs determine attraction/repulsion. Physics gives a quantitative law, but engineering care is in units, signs, and geometry.

Coulomb's Law
$$F = k\frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}, \quad k=8.99\times10^9\ \text{N m}^2/\text{C}^2$$
Direction rule: unlike charges attract, like charges repel. Put this in words before equations.

Common mistakes

  • Forgetting to convert $\mu C$ to C.
  • Using distance instead of squared distance.
  • Ignoring vector direction in multi-charge systems.

Solved examples

Worked Example 1

$q_1=2\mu C$, $q_2=-3\mu C$, separated by 0.20 m.

$F=8.99\times10^9\cdot \frac{(2\times10^{-6})(3\times10^{-6})}{0.2^2}=1.35\ \text{N}$, attractive.

Worked Example 2

Force doubles if separation changes by factor?

Since $F\propto 1/r^2$, to double force, $r$ must become $r/\sqrt{2}$.

Worked Example 3

Net force on charge from two equal opposite-side charges of equal magnitude can cancel if geometry symmetric; always resolve as vectors and check.

Practice set (Topic 2)
  1. Charges $q_1=+5\,\mu$C and $q_2=-2\,\mu$C are 0.30 m apart. Find force magnitude and state attraction/repulsion.
    Answer
    $F=k|q_1q_2|/r^2=8.99\times10^9(10\times10^{-12})/0.09=0.999\approx1.00$ N, attractive.
  2. Two equal charges of $3\,\mu$C exert 2.0 N on each other. What separation is required?
    Answer
    $r=\sqrt{kq^2/F}=\sqrt{(8.99\times10^9)(9\times10^{-12})/2}=0.201$ m.
  3. On the x-axis, $q_A=+4\,\mu$C at $x=0$, $q_B=+1\,\mu$C at $x=0.20$ m, and test charge $q_T=+2\,\mu$C at $x=0.10$ m. Find net force on $q_T$.
    Answer
    Forces are opposite directions. $F_{AT}=k(4\times2)\times10^{-12}/0.1^2=7.19$ N (to +x). $F_{BT}=k(1\times2)\times10^{-12}/0.1^2=1.80$ N (to -x). Net $=5.39$ N to +x.
  4. Find electric field magnitude at 0.50 m from a point charge of $-6\,\mu$C.
    Answer
    $E=k|q|/r^2=8.99\times10^9(6\times10^{-6})/0.25=2.16\times10^5$ N/C, directed toward the negative charge.
  5. Concept + math: if both charges are doubled and separation is halved, by what factor does Coulomb force change?
    Answer
    Charge product factor $=2\times2=4$. Distance factor in denominator $(1/2)^2=1/4$, so force gains another factor 4. Total factor $=16$ increase.
Topic 3 · Energy view
Electric Field, Potential, Capacitance and Dielectrics
Force tells what happens now; potential tells stored energy and what can happen next.

Build the big picture

Electric field is force per unit charge. Electric potential is energy per unit charge. Capacitance describes how much charge can be stored for a given voltage. Dielectrics increase capacitance by reducing internal effective field.

Key Relations
$$E=\frac{F}{q},\quad V=\frac{W}{q},\quad C=\frac{Q}{V},\quad C_\text{parallel plate}=\epsilon_r\epsilon_0\frac{A}{d}$$
QuantityMeaningUnit
Electric field EForce intensity in spaceN/C (or V/m)
Potential VEnergy per chargeVolt
Capacitance CCharge storage per voltFarad
Relative permittivity εrDielectric enhancement factorDimensionless

Solved examples

Worked Example 1

A 5 pC test charge feels 1 mN force. Field: $E=F/q=10^{-3}/(5\times10^{-12})=2\times10^8$ N/C.

Worked Example 2

Parallel plate capacitor: $A=0.02$ m², $d=1$ mm, $\epsilon_r=4$.

$C=4(8.85\times10^{-12})(0.02/0.001)=7.08\times10^{-10}$ F = 708 pF.

Worked Example 3

A capacitor has 12 V across it and stores 24 µC. $C=Q/V=2$ µF. If voltage doubles and C fixed, charge doubles.

Practice set (Topic 3)
  1. Parallel-plate capacitor has $A=0.015$ m², $d=0.80$ mm, and dielectric $\epsilon_r=3.5$. Find capacitance.
    Answer
    $C=\epsilon_r\epsilon_0A/d=3.5(8.85\times10^{-12})(0.015/8.0\times10^{-4})=5.81\times10^{-10}$ F $=581$ pF.
  2. A $22\,\mu$F capacitor is charged to 18 V. Find stored charge and stored energy.
    Answer
    $Q=CV=396\,\mu$C. $U=\frac12CV^2=0.5(22\times10^{-6})(18^2)=3.56\times10^{-3}$ J.
  3. Field between plates is 4500 V/m and spacing is 2.5 mm. Find potential difference.
    Answer
    $V=Ed=4500(2.5\times10^{-3})=11.25$ V.
  4. Two capacitors 6 µF and 3 µF are connected (a) series, (b) parallel. Find equivalent capacitance in each case.
    Answer
    (a) Series: $C_{eq}=(C_1C_2)/(C_1+C_2)=2$ µF. (b) Parallel: $C_{eq}=C_1+C_2=9$ µF.
  5. Concept + math: a dielectric with $\epsilon_r=4$ is inserted fully into an isolated charged capacitor (constant Q). How do $C$, $V$, and stored energy change?
    Answer
    $C$ increases 4x. Since $V=Q/C$, $V$ becomes $1/4$ of original. Energy $U=Q^2/(2C)$ also becomes $1/4$.
Topic 4 · Move from static to flow
Current, Resistance, Ohm's Law, and Superconductivity
Charge flow links physics to real engineering systems: motors, sensors, communications hardware.

Concept progression

Potential difference drives current. Resistance opposes current due to collisions in material. Ohm's law models linear conductors. At very low temperatures, some materials enter superconducting states with near-zero resistance.

Core Circuit Relations
$$I=\frac{Q}{t},\quad V=IR,\quad P=VI=I^2R=\frac{V^2}{R}$$
Engineering caution: "Ohmic" is a model range, not an eternal truth for every component.

Solved examples

Worked Example 1

12 V source across 6 Ω resistor gives $I=2$ A and $P=24$ W.

Worked Example 2

Battery delivers 300 C in 2 minutes. Current: $I=300/120=2.5$ A.

Worked Example 3

For fixed voltage, halving resistance doubles current and doubles heat dissipation risk in nearby components.

Practice set (Topic 4)
  1. A heater is rated 240 V, 1200 W. Find its resistance and operating current.
    Answer
    $I=P/V=1200/240=5$ A. $R=V/I=48$ Ω (or $V^2/P$).
  2. A resistor has maximum safe power 0.50 W and is connected to 12 V. What minimum resistance avoids overload?
    Answer
    $P=V^2/R \Rightarrow R\ge V^2/P = 144/0.5 = 288$ Ω.
  3. Battery transfers 540 C in 3.0 minutes through a load. Find current and if load is 8 Ω, find voltage.
    Answer
    $I=Q/t=540/180=3.0$ A. Voltage $V=IR=24$ V.
  4. A sensor circuit needs 15 mA from a 9 V source using one resistor. Find resistor value and nearest standard choice among 560 Ω, 620 Ω, 680 Ω.
    Answer
    $R=V/I=9/0.015=600$ Ω. Nearest standard is 620 Ω (safer, slightly lower current).
  5. Concept + math: device A draws 1 A at 12 V, device B draws 2 A at 12 V. Compare power and heat risk.
    Answer
    $P_A=12$ W, $P_B=24$ W. Device B dissipates 2x power, so thermal management requirement is significantly higher.
Topic 5 · Systems analysis
DC Circuits and Kirchhoff's Laws
When circuits become networks, conservation laws are your map: charge conservation and energy conservation.

Core laws

Kirchhoff's Laws
$$\sum I_\text{in}=\sum I_\text{out}\quad(\text{KCL}),\qquad \sum V=0\quad(\text{KVL})$$

Use KCL at junctions and KVL around loops. This transforms messy wiring into solvable linear equations.

Solved examples

Worked Example 1

Series resistors 2 Ω, 3 Ω, 5 Ω with 10 V source. $R_{eq}=10$ Ω, $I=1$ A. Voltage drops: 2 V, 3 V, 5 V. KVL check: $2+3+5=10$ V.

Worked Example 2

At a node, currents 2 A and 1.5 A enter; one branch leaves with 0.8 A. Unknown outgoing current is $2+1.5-0.8=2.7$ A.

Worked Example 3

Parallel branch currents distribute inversely with resistance, but branch voltages remain equal.

Practice set (Topic 5)
  1. A 24 V source feeds $R_1=4\Omega$ in series with a parallel pair $R_2=6\Omega$ and $R_3=3\Omega$. Find total current.
    Answer
    $R_{23}=(6\times3)/(6+3)=2\Omega$. Total $R_{eq}=4+2=6\Omega$. Current $I=24/6=4$ A.
  2. Node equation: 5 A enters a node, branch currents are $I_1=1.2$ A, $I_2=0.9$ A and $I_3$ leaving. Find $I_3$.
    Answer
    KCL: $5=1.2+0.9+I_3 \Rightarrow I_3=2.9$ A.
  3. Single loop with 18 V source and resistors 2 Ω, 4 Ω, 3 Ω in series. Find loop current and each voltage drop. Verify KVL.
    Answer
    $I=18/(2+4+3)=2$ A. Drops: 4 V, 8 V, 6 V. Sum $=18$ V, so KVL satisfied.
  4. In a two-loop network solved by equations, you obtain branch currents $I_a=1.5$ A and $I_b=-0.4$ A (assumed direction opposite). What does negative current mean physically?
    Answer
    Magnitude is 0.4 A, flowing opposite to the assumed direction. This is normal in exam solutions.
  5. Concept + math: for fixed source voltage, if one branch resistance in a parallel circuit is reduced, what happens to total current and source power?
    Answer
    Equivalent resistance decreases, so total current increases: $I_{tot}=V/R_{eq}$. Source power $P=VI_{tot}=V^2/R_{eq}$ increases.
Topic 6 · E to B transition
Magnetic Fields and Magnetic Forces
Moving charges and currents create magnetic effects that power motors, generators, and sensing systems.

Key laws and intuition

Magnetic Force
$$\vec{F}=q\vec{v}\times\vec{B},\qquad F= qvB\sin\theta$$
Force on current-carrying wire
$$F=BIL\sin\theta$$

Magnetic force is perpendicular to both velocity and magnetic field direction (right-hand rule). This is why magnetic forces can bend trajectories without doing work on particle speed magnitude.

Solved examples

Worked Example 1

Electron speed $2\times10^6$ m/s enters $0.01$ T field at 90°. Magnitude: $F=evB=(1.6\times10^{-19})(2\times10^6)(0.01)=3.2\times10^{-15}$ N.

Worked Example 2

Wire: $I=4$ A, $L=0.3$ m, $B=0.5$ T, perpendicular. $F=0.5\cdot4\cdot0.3=0.6$ N.

Worked Example 3

Particle with velocity parallel to B-field has $\theta=0$ so magnetic force zero: a useful diagnostic check.

Practice set (Topic 6)
  1. A proton moves at $3.0\times10^6$ m/s perpendicular to a 0.25 T field. Find magnetic force magnitude.
    Answer
    $F=qvB=(1.6\times10^{-19})(3.0\times10^6)(0.25)=1.20\times10^{-13}$ N.
  2. An electron enters a uniform 0.02 T field with speed $1.5\times10^7$ m/s perpendicular to B. Find radius of circular path.
    Answer
    $r=\frac{mv}{qB}=\frac{(9.11\times10^{-31})(1.5\times10^7)}{(1.6\times10^{-19})(0.02)}=4.27\times10^{-3}$ m.
  3. A wire of length 0.40 m carries 6 A in a 0.30 T field at $30^\circ$. Find force on wire.
    Answer
    $F=BIL\sin\theta=0.30\times6\times0.40\times\sin30^\circ=0.36$ N.
  4. A charge moving east in an upward magnetic field experiences force toward south. Is the charge positive or negative?
    Answer
    For positive charge, $\vec v\times\vec B$ would point north. Observed south means charge is negative.
  5. Concept + math: if $B$ doubles and velocity angle changes from $90^\circ$ to $30^\circ$, how does magnetic force change?
    Answer
    $F\propto B\sin\theta$. New/old ratio $=(2\sin30^\circ)/(\sin90^\circ)=2(0.5)/1=1$. Force is unchanged.
Topic 7 · From fields to waves
Electromagnetic Waves and the EM Spectrum
Oscillating electric and magnetic fields propagate energy through space and enable modern communication.

Minimal equations, maximal insight

Wave Relation
$$c=f\lambda,\quad c\approx 3.0\times10^8\ \text{m/s}$$

As frequency increases, wavelength decreases. This one inverse relation helps classify radio, microwave, infrared, visible, UV, X-ray, and gamma regimes.

JavaScript Simulator · EM Spectrum Explorer
Frequency (log10 Hz) 1e14 Hz

Solved examples

Worked Example 1

For $f=100$ MHz, wavelength $=3\times10^8/(10^8)=3$ m.

Worked Example 2

Green light around 550 nm has frequency $f=c/\lambda\approx 5.45\times10^{14}$ Hz.

Worked Example 3

If communication system bandwidth rises, potential data capacity rises, but noise, filtering, and spectrum allocation constraints appear.

Practice set (Topic 7)
  1. Find wavelength for a 900 MHz mobile communication signal.
    Answer
    $\lambda=c/f=3\times10^8/(9\times10^8)=0.333$ m.
  2. An EM wave has wavelength 450 nm. Find frequency and identify approximate spectrum region.
    Answer
    $f=c/\lambda=3\times10^8/(450\times10^{-9})=6.67\times10^{14}$ Hz, visible (blue region).
  3. A radar pulse travels to a target and returns in 40 µs. Estimate target distance.
    Answer
    Round trip distance $=ct=3\times10^8(40\times10^{-6})=12000$ m. One-way distance $=6000$ m.
  4. Find frequency for 3.0 mm wavelength and classify region.
    Answer
    $f=3\times10^8/(3\times10^{-3})=1.0\times10^{11}$ Hz, microwave band.
  5. Concept + math: compare wavelengths of 100 MHz and 1 GHz signals and infer which diffracts more around buildings.
    Answer
    $100$ MHz: $\lambda=3$ m. $1$ GHz: $\lambda=0.3$ m. Longer wavelength (100 MHz) diffracts more effectively around obstacles.
Topic 8 · Communication bridge
Carrier Waves, Modulation and Bandwidth
Raw information is low-frequency; long-distance transmission needs high-frequency carriers.

Concept build-up

Modulation lets a message signal ride on a carrier wave. In amplitude modulation (AM), carrier amplitude follows message shape. Bandwidth is the frequency span occupied by a signal and drives channel requirements.

AM signal model
$$s(t)=A_c\left[1+m\cos(2\pi f_m t)\right]\cos(2\pi f_c t)$$

Solved examples

Worked Example 1

AM with message max frequency 5 kHz has bandwidth $B=2f_m=10$ kHz.

Worked Example 2

If modulation index exceeds 1 in AM, overmodulation causes envelope distortion and receiver errors.

Worked Example 3

Carrier at 1 MHz with message 2 kHz creates sidebands at 998 kHz and 1002 kHz.

Practice set (Topic 8)
  1. For AM transmission with maximum message frequency 8 kHz, calculate required transmission bandwidth.
    Answer
    $B_{AM}=2f_m=16$ kHz.
  2. Carrier frequency is 1.2 MHz and message frequency is 4 kHz. Find upper and lower sideband frequencies.
    Answer
    $f_{USB}=1.204$ MHz, $f_{LSB}=1.196$ MHz.
  3. Given AM envelope peaks $E_{max}=9$ V and $E_{min}=3$ V, find modulation index.
    Answer
    $m=\frac{E_{max}-E_{min}}{E_{max}+E_{min}}=\frac{9-3}{9+3}=0.5$.
  4. An AM signal has modulation index $m=1.2$. Is this acceptable? State the likely waveform issue.
    Answer
    Not acceptable for standard AM; $m>1$ means overmodulation and envelope distortion at receiver.
  5. A communication channel width is 120 kHz. If each AM station needs 10 kHz plus 2 kHz guard on each side (total 14 kHz), how many stations fit?
    Answer
    Maximum stations $=\lfloor120/14\rfloor=8$ stations.
Topic 9 · Wave optics toolkit
Reflection, Refraction, Interference, Diffraction, Polarization
Optics is where geometry and wave behavior meet engineering devices: lenses, cameras, fiber links, and sensors.

Core formulas

Key optics equations
$$\theta_i=\theta_r,\qquad n_1\sin\theta_1=n_2\sin\theta_2,\qquad d\sin\theta=m\lambda$$

Build intuition

  • Reflection keeps angle symmetry around surface normal.
  • Refraction changes direction because speed changes by medium.
  • Interference creates bright/dark patterns via phase differences.
  • Diffraction spreads waves around apertures/edges.
  • Polarization selects oscillation orientation.

Solved examples

Worked Example 1

Light from air ($n=1$) to glass ($n=1.5$), incidence $30^\circ$.

$\sin\theta_2=\sin30^\circ/1.5=0.333\Rightarrow\theta_2=19.47^\circ$.

Worked Example 2

Double slit spacing 0.4 mm, wavelength 600 nm, first-order bright fringe angle:

$\theta\approx \sin^{-1}(\lambda/d)=\sin^{-1}(0.0015)\approx0.086^\circ$.

Worked Example 3

Two ideal polarizers at 60°: transmitted intensity $I=I_0\cos^2 60^\circ=0.25I_0$.

Practice set (Topic 9)
  1. Light travels from air to water ($n=1.33$) at incidence $50^\circ$. Find refracted angle.
    Answer
    $n_1\sin\theta_1=n_2\sin\theta_2 \Rightarrow \sin\theta_2=\sin50^\circ/1.33=0.576$. So $\theta_2=35.2^\circ$.
  2. Find critical angle for glass-air interface ($n_{glass}=1.50$).
    Answer
    $\sin\theta_c=n_2/n_1=1/1.5=0.667$. $\theta_c=41.8^\circ$.
  3. Double slit: $d=0.25$ mm, screen distance $L=1.8$ m, wavelength $650$ nm. Find spacing between adjacent bright fringes.
    Answer
    Fringe spacing $\Delta y=\lambda L/d=(650\times10^{-9}\times1.8)/(2.5\times10^{-4})=4.68\times10^{-3}$ m = 4.68 mm.
  4. Unpolarized light of intensity 80 W/m² passes through two polarizers at $30^\circ$ relative angle. Find transmitted intensity.
    Answer
    After first polarizer: $I_1=I_0/2=40$. After second: $I_2=I_1\cos^2 30^\circ=40(0.75)=30$ W/m².
  5. Concept + math: a lens forms an image at 30 cm when object is 60 cm away. Find focal length and lens type.
    Answer
    Using $1/f=1/u+1/v=1/60+1/30=1/20$, so $f=20$ cm. Positive focal length indicates converging lens.
Topic 10 · Modern model mindset
Quantum Ideas, Wave-Particle Duality, Atomic Physics, Bohr Model
Classical models are powerful, but incomplete. DPEN143 ends by showing when quantization becomes necessary.

Big transition

In modern physics, energy and matter show quantized behavior. Light can act as photons, particles can show wave-like behavior, and atomic spectra require discrete energy levels.

Foundational quantum relations
$$E=hf,\qquad \lambda=\frac{h}{p},\qquad E_n=-\frac{13.6}{n^2}\ \text{eV (Bohr hydrogen)}$$
Learning goal: You do not need to master full quantum mechanics here. You need to know why classical physics alone fails at atomic scale.

Solved examples

Worked Example 1

Photon with $f=6\times10^{14}$ Hz has energy $E=hf=(6.626\times10^{-34})(6\times10^{14})=3.98\times10^{-19}$ J.

Worked Example 2

Hydrogen transition $n=3\to2$: $\Delta E=E_2-E_3=-3.4-(-1.51)=-1.89$ eV. Emitted photon energy magnitude is 1.89 eV.

Worked Example 3

de Broglie wavelength of electron with momentum $2\times10^{-24}$ kg m/s: $\lambda=h/p=3.31\times10^{-10}$ m (atomic scale).

Practice set (Topic 10)
  1. Find photon energy for wavelength 500 nm (in J and eV).
    Answer
    $E=hc/\lambda=(6.626\times10^{-34})(3\times10^8)/(500\times10^{-9})=3.98\times10^{-19}$ J $\approx 2.48$ eV.
  2. Electron momentum is $4.0\times10^{-24}$ kg m/s. Find de Broglie wavelength.
    Answer
    $\lambda=h/p=6.626\times10^{-34}/(4.0\times10^{-24})=1.66\times10^{-10}$ m.
  3. In hydrogen, compute emitted photon energy for transition $n=4\to n=2$.
    Answer
    $E_n=-13.6/n^2$ eV. $E_4=-0.85$ eV, $E_2=-3.40$ eV. Emitted energy $=|E_2-E_4|=2.55$ eV.
  4. Find ionization energy from hydrogen level $n=3$.
    Answer
    Ionization from level $n$ requires $|E_n|=13.6/n^2$. For $n=3$: $13.6/9=1.51$ eV.
  5. Concept + math: can a 1.9 eV photon excite hydrogen from $n=2$ to $n=3$? (Use Bohr levels.)
    Answer
    Needed energy $E_3-E_2=(-1.51)-(-3.40)=1.89$ eV. A 1.9 eV photon is sufficient (approximately resonant).
