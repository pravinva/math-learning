Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Week 1 — Electrostatics: $F=k\dfrac{|q_1q_2|}{r^2}$; $\vec E=\dfrac{kq}{r^2}\hat r$, $\vec F=q\vec E$; superposition; $E_{line}=\dfrac{2k\lambda}{r}$; $E_{sheet}=\dfrac{\sigma}{2\varepsilon_0}$; ring on-axis $E_z=\dfrac{kQz}{(R^2+z^2)^{3/2}}$.
  • Week 2 — Capacitance & Current: $C=Q/V=\varepsilon_0A/d$ (or $\kappa\varepsilon_0A/d$ with dielectric); series/parallel $C$ combine opposite to $R$; $U=\tfrac12CV^2=\tfrac{Q^2}{2C}$; $I=dq/dt$; $V=IR$; $R=\rho L/A$; $\rho(T)=\rho_0[1+\alpha(T-T_0)]$; $P=IV=I^2R=V^2/R$.
  • Week 3 — Kirchhoff & Magnetism: junction rule ($\sum I_{in}=\sum I_{out}$); loop rule ($\sum\Delta V=0$); $\vec F=q\vec v\times\vec B$, $F=qvB\sin\theta$; circular motion $r=mv/(qB)$, $T=2\pi m/(qB)$; Biot-Savart, straight wire $B=\mu_0I/(2\pi r)$, loop centre $B=\mu_0I/(2R)$; Drude drift velocity $v_d=I/(nqA)$.
  • Week 4 — Faraday, Transformers & EM Waves: $\Phi_B=BA\cos\theta$; $\varepsilon=-N\,d\Phi_B/dt$ (Lenz's law for direction); transformer $V_s/V_p=N_s/N_p$, $I_pV_p=I_sV_s$; $c=f\lambda=3.00\times10^8$ m/s; bandwidth $\Delta f=f_{max}-f_{min}$.
  • Exam strategy: identify which topic a question is testing from its keywords (charge/field → Week 1; capacitor/resistor/power → Week 2; multi-loop/moving charge/wire field → Week 3; induction/transformer/wave → Week 4), write down the relevant formula before substituting numbers, and sanity-check units and order of magnitude at the end.

0 Week 1 Recap: Coulomb's Law & the Electric Field

The recurring exam pattern here: get a force or field magnitude from the formula, then reason about direction separately using attraction/repulsion or "away from positive, toward negative." For continuous distributions, you're expected to recognise and apply the standard results (line, sheet, ring) rather than re-derive them under time pressure — save derivations for when a question explicitly asks you to "show that."

Key idea

$$F=k\frac{|q_1q_2|}{r^2}, \quad \vec E=\frac{kq}{r^2}\hat r, \quad \vec F=q\vec E, \quad E_{line}=\frac{2k\lambda}{r}, \quad E_{sheet}=\frac{\sigma}{2\varepsilon_0}$$

Worked example

Exam-style: A $+4\,\mu$C charge sits at the origin. Find the electric field at $(0.3, 0)$ m, then find the force on a $-2\,\mu$C charge placed at that point.

$E=k\dfrac{q}{r^2}=\dfrac{(8.99\times10^9)(4\times10^{-6})}{(0.3)^2}=3.996\times10^5\ \text{N/C}$, directed in $+x$ (away from the positive source).

$F=|q_2|E=(2\times10^{-6})(3.996\times10^5)=0.799\ \text{N}$, directed in $-x$ (attraction pulls the negative charge back toward the source, opposite to $\vec E$ at that point).

Practice 1

Two charges, $+5\,\mu$C and $-5\,\mu$C, are 40 cm apart. Find the force between them, and state whether it's attractive or repulsive.

Solution

$F=k\dfrac{|q_1q_2|}{r^2}=\dfrac{(8.99\times10^9)(5\times10^{-6})(5\times10^{-6})}{(0.4)^2}=\dfrac{0.22475}{0.16}=1.40\ \text{N}$, attractive (opposite signs).

Practice 2

An infinite sheet has $\sigma=-4\times10^{-6}\ \text{C/m}^2$. Find $\vec E$ near the sheet (magnitude and direction).

Solution

$E=\dfrac{\sigma}{2\varepsilon_0}=\dfrac{4\times10^{-6}}{2(8.85\times10^{-12})}=2.26\times10^5\ \text{N/C}$, directed toward the sheet (negative charge).

Practice 3

A charge $+2\,\mu$C at $x=0$ and $+8\,\mu$C at $x=0.6$ m. Find the point on the $x$-axis (between them) where $\vec E=0$.

Solution

Let the point be a distance $x$ from the $+2\,\mu$C charge, so $0.6-x$ from the $+8\,\mu$C charge. Setting the magnitudes equal: $\dfrac{2}{x^2}=\dfrac{8}{(0.6-x)^2}$ (the $k\times10^{-6}$ factors cancel).

$(0.6-x)^2=4x^2 \Rightarrow 0.6-x=2x$ (taking the positive root, since the point must lie between them) $\Rightarrow x=0.2\ \text{m}$.

The field is zero 0.2 m from the $2\,\mu$C charge (0.4 m from the $8\,\mu$C charge) — closer to the weaker charge, as expected.

1 Week 2 Recap: Capacitance, Current & Resistance

The classic exam trap: capacitor combination rules are the opposite way round from resistor combination rules. Before you combine anything, ask "do these share the same voltage (parallel) or the same charge/current (series)?" — that determines which formula applies, for both capacitors and resistors.

Key idea

$$C_{parallel}=\sum C_i, \quad \frac1{C_{series}}=\sum\frac1{C_i}, \qquad R_{series}=\sum R_i, \quad \frac1{R_{parallel}}=\sum\frac1{R_i}$$ $$U=\tfrac12CV^2, \qquad P=IV=I^2R=V^2/R$$

Worked example

Exam-style: Two capacitors, 4 $\mu$F and 12 $\mu$F, are connected in series to a 30 V battery. Find the equivalent capacitance, total charge, and the voltage across the 4 $\mu$F capacitor.

$\dfrac1{C_{eq}}=\dfrac14+\dfrac1{12}=\dfrac{3+1}{12}=\dfrac{4}{12} \Rightarrow C_{eq}=3\ \mu\text{F}$.

In series, both capacitors carry the same charge as the combination: $Q=C_{eq}V=3\times30=90\ \mu\text{C}$.

$V_{4\mu F}=Q/C=90/4=22.5\ \text{V}$ (and $V_{12\mu F}=90/12=7.5\ \text{V}$; together they add to 30 V — check).

Practice 4

Two capacitors, 6 $\mu$F and 3 $\mu$F, are connected in parallel across a 9 V battery. Find the total charge stored.

Solution

$C_{eq}=6+3=9\ \mu\text{F}$ (parallel). $Q=C_{eq}V=9\times9=81\ \mu\text{C}$.

Practice 5

A 60 W light bulb and a 100 W light bulb are each designed for 240 V mains. Which has the higher resistance, and by what factor?

Solution

$R=V^2/P$, so resistance is inversely proportional to power rating (at the same rated voltage). The 60 W bulb has the higher resistance: $R_{60}/R_{100}=100/60=1.67$, so the 60 W bulb's resistance is 1.67 times that of the 100 W bulb.

Practice 6

A 10 $\mu$F capacitor is charged to 200 V then connected (via a switch) to an identical uncharged 10 $\mu$F capacitor, so the charge redistributes equally between them. Find the final voltage on each and the total energy before and after connecting — and comment on the difference.

Solution

Initial charge: $Q_0=CV_0=(10\times10^{-6})(200)=2\times10^{-3}\ \text{C}$. This charge splits equally: $1\times10^{-3}$ C on each capacitor.

Final voltage on each: $V_f=Q/C=\dfrac{1\times10^{-3}}{10\times10^{-6}}=100\ \text{V}$.

Energy before: $U_0=\tfrac12CV_0^2=\tfrac12(10\times10^{-6})(200)^2=0.20\ \text{J}$.

Energy after: $U_f=2\times\tfrac12CV_f^2=2\times\tfrac12(10\times10^{-6})(100)^2=0.10\ \text{J}$.

Half the energy is lost, dissipated as heat in the connecting wires (and possibly a tiny bit of radiation) during the brief redistribution current — a classic and slightly counterintuitive result.

2 Week 3 Recap: Kirchhoff's Laws & Magnetism

For Kirchhoff problems, the exam-critical skill is setting up consistent current directions and enough independent equations — the algebra afterward is routine. For magnetism, always separate the magnitude calculation ($F=qvB\sin\theta$ or the Biot-Savart results) from the direction (right-hand rule), just as with electric fields and forces.

Key idea

$$\sum I_{in}=\sum I_{out}, \qquad \sum\Delta V=0 \text{ (around any loop)}$$ $$\vec F=q\vec v\times\vec B, \quad r=\frac{mv}{qB}, \quad B_{wire}=\frac{\mu_0I}{2\pi r}, \quad B_{loop\ centre}=\frac{\mu_0I}{2R}$$

Worked example

Exam-style: A proton travels at $3.0\times10^6$ m/s directly toward a long straight wire carrying 20 A, arriving at a point 5.0 cm from the wire, moving parallel to the wire's current. Find the magnetic field at that point and the force on the proton there.

$B=\dfrac{\mu_0I}{2\pi r}=\dfrac{(4\pi\times10^{-7})(20)}{2\pi(0.05)}=\dfrac{(2\times10^{-7})(20)}{0.05}=8.0\times10^{-5}\ \text{T}$.

The proton moves parallel to the wire, and $\vec B$ from the wire circles the wire — at the proton's location $\vec B$ is perpendicular to the proton's velocity, so $\sin\theta=1$: $F=qvB=(1.6\times10^{-19})(3.0\times10^6)(8.0\times10^{-5})=3.84\times10^{-17}\ \text{N}$.

Direction: use the right-hand rule twice — once to find $\vec B$'s direction from the wire's current, once more with $\vec v\times\vec B$ to find the force direction (toward or away from the wire, depending on the proton's direction of travel relative to the current).

Practice 7

In a circuit, current flows into a junction from two branches: 3.5 A and 1.5 A. Three branches carry current away, two of which carry 2.0 A each. Find the current in the third outgoing branch.

Solution

Total in: $3.5+1.5=5.0\ \text{A}$. Two outgoing branches carry $2.0+2.0=4.0\ \text{A}$. Third branch: $5.0-4.0=1.0\ \text{A}$.

Practice 8

An electron moving at $4.0\times10^6$ m/s enters a uniform 0.30 T field perpendicular to its velocity. Find the radius of its circular path.

Solution

$r=\dfrac{m_ev}{eB}=\dfrac{(9.11\times10^{-31})(4.0\times10^6)}{(1.6\times10^{-19})(0.30)}=7.59\times10^{-5}\ \text{m}$.

Practice 9

A single-loop circuit has a 6 V battery, a 2 $\Omega$ resistor, and a 3 $\Omega$ resistor, all in series, plus a second 4 V battery oriented to aid the first (same polarity around the loop). Find the current.

Solution

Net EMF $=6+4=10\ \text{V}$ (aiding, so they add). Total resistance $=2+3=5\ \Omega$. $I=10/5=2.0\ \text{A}$.

3 Week 4 Recap: Faraday's Law, Transformers & EM Waves

Faraday's law questions almost always reduce to: find $\Delta\Phi_B$ (or its rate), multiply by $N$, done — the only subtlety is correctly identifying why flux is changing (changing $B$, changing $A$, or changing angle). Transformer and wave questions are typically direct formula substitution, but watch your units (MHz vs Hz, mm$^2$ vs m$^2$, etc.) — this is where most exam marks are lost.

Key idea

$$\varepsilon=-N\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}, \qquad \frac{V_s}{V_p}=\frac{N_s}{N_p}, \qquad I_pV_p=I_sV_s, \qquad c=f\lambda$$

Worked example

Exam-style: A square coil of side 8.0 cm, 40 turns, is pulled out of a 0.60 T field (initially perpendicular to the coil) in 0.15 s, ending up entirely outside the field. Find the average induced EMF, then find what secondary voltage a transformer with $N_p=40$, $N_s=10$ would produce if this EMF were applied to its primary.

Initial flux: $\Phi_i=BA=0.60\times(0.08)^2=3.84\times10^{-3}\ \text{Wb}$. Final flux: $\Phi_f=0$ (coil fully outside the field).

$\varepsilon=N\dfrac{|\Delta\Phi_B|}{\Delta t}=40\times\dfrac{3.84\times10^{-3}}{0.15}=1.024\ \text{V}$.

Transformer: $V_s=V_p\dfrac{N_s}{N_p}=1.024\times\dfrac{10}{40}=0.256\ \text{V}$.

Practice 10

A coil of 100 turns and area $0.02\ \text{m}^2$ sits in a field that changes from 0.3 T to 0.1 T in 0.4 s. Find the induced EMF.

Solution

$\varepsilon=N\dfrac{|\Delta\Phi_B|}{\Delta t}=100\times\dfrac{(0.02)(0.3-0.1)}{0.4}=100\times\dfrac{0.004}{0.4}=1.0\ \text{V}$.

Practice 11

A transformer's primary has 300 turns and draws 0.5 A from a 120 V supply. The secondary delivers current to a load at 20 V. Find the number of secondary turns and the secondary current.

Solution

$N_s=N_p\dfrac{V_s}{V_p}=300\times\dfrac{20}{120}=50\ \text{turns}$.

Power conserved: $I_s=\dfrac{I_pV_p}{V_s}=\dfrac{0.5\times120}{20}=3.0\ \text{A}$.

Practice 12

A radio station broadcasts at $f=95.5$ MHz. Find the wavelength, and state roughly what part of the EM spectrum (radio/microwave/visible/etc.) this falls into.

Solution

$\lambda=c/f=\dfrac{3.00\times10^8}{95.5\times10^6}=3.14\ \text{m}$ — this is in the radio wave region of the spectrum (FM broadcast band).

4 Mixed Exam-Style Problems (Weeks 1–4)

Real exam questions often blend two or more weeks' ideas in a single multi-part problem. These are deliberately mixed — identify which formula(s) each part needs before diving into arithmetic.

Key idea

No single formula here — the skill being tested is recognising which tool applies. Read each part fully before starting, since later parts often build on earlier results.

Worked example

Exam-style, multi-part: A parallel-plate capacitor ($A=0.01\ \text{m}^2$, $d=1.0$ mm, no dielectric) is connected to a 9 V battery. (a) Find its capacitance and stored charge. (b) The charged capacitor is then disconnected and discharged through a $50\ \Omega$ resistor; if the average discharge current over the first instant is $I=Q/(RC)$ at $t=0$, estimate this initial current.

(a) $C=\dfrac{\varepsilon_0A}{d}=\dfrac{(8.85\times10^{-12})(0.01)}{1.0\times10^{-3}}=8.85\times10^{-11}\ \text{F}$. $Q=CV=(8.85\times10^{-11})(9)=7.965\times10^{-10}\ \text{C}$.

(b) Initial voltage across the (still fully charged) capacitor is still 9 V at $t=0$, so by Ohm's law the initial current is simply $I_0=V/R=9/50=0.18\ \text{A}$ — consistent with $I=Q/(RC)=\dfrac{7.965\times10^{-10}}{50\times8.85\times10^{-11}}=0.18\ \text{A}$.

Practice 13

A ring of radius 4.0 cm carries charge $+6$ nC and produces a field of magnitude $E$ at a point 3.0 cm along its axis. (a) Find $E$. (b) If a $-2\,\mu$C charge is placed at that point, find the force on it and state its direction relative to the ring's axis.

Solution

(a) $R^2+z^2=0.04^2+0.03^2=0.0025$; $(0.0025)^{3/2}=0.0025\times0.05=1.25\times10^{-4}$.

$E=\dfrac{kQz}{(R^2+z^2)^{3/2}}=\dfrac{(8.99\times10^9)(6\times10^{-9})(0.03)}{1.25\times10^{-4}}=\dfrac{1.618}{1.25\times10^{-4}}=1.29\times10^4\ \text{N/C}$, directed away from the ring along the axis (ring is positive).

(b) $F=|q|E=(2\times10^{-6})(1.29\times10^4)=2.59\times10^{-2}\ \text{N}$, directed toward the ring (attraction, since the test charge is negative — opposite to $\vec E$'s direction).

Practice 14

A long straight wire carries a current $I$ that produces $B=6.0\times10^{-5}$ T at a distance of 10 cm. (a) Find $I$. (b) A proton moving at $2.0\times10^5$ m/s parallel to the wire passes through this point. Find the force on it.

Solution

(a) $I=\dfrac{2\pi rB}{\mu_0}=\dfrac{2\pi(0.10)(6.0\times10^{-5})}{4\pi\times10^{-7}}=\dfrac{(0.10)(6.0\times10^{-5})}{2\times10^{-7}}=30\ \text{A}$.

(b) $\vec B$ at that point is perpendicular to the proton's velocity (it circles the wire while the proton moves parallel to the wire), so $F=qvB=(1.6\times10^{-19})(2.0\times10^5)(6.0\times10^{-5})=1.92\times10^{-18}\ \text{N}$.

Practice 15

A coil (50 turns, area $0.015\ \text{m}^2$) is connected across a $20\ \Omega$ resistor. It sits in a field that increases uniformly from 0 to 0.80 T in 0.25 s. (a) Find the induced EMF. (b) Find the current through the resistor. (c) Find the power dissipated in the resistor.

Solution

(a) $\varepsilon=N\dfrac{\Delta\Phi_B}{\Delta t}=50\times\dfrac{(0.015)(0.80)}{0.25}=50\times\dfrac{0.012}{0.25}=2.4\ \text{V}$.

(b) $I=\varepsilon/R=2.4/20=0.12\ \text{A}$.

(c) $P=I^2R=(0.12)^2\times20=0.288\ \text{W}$ (equivalently $P=\varepsilon^2/R=2.4^2/20=0.288\ \text{W}$).

Practice 16

A parallel-plate capacitor with plate area $0.02\ \text{m}^2$ and gap 2.0 mm is charged to 100 V, storing energy $U$. (a) Find $C$ and $U$. (b) A dielectric with $\kappa=3$ is now inserted while the capacitor stays connected to the 100 V source. Find the new energy stored, and how much extra energy the source had to supply.

Solution

(a) $C=\dfrac{\varepsilon_0A}{d}=\dfrac{(8.85\times10^{-12})(0.02)}{2.0\times10^{-3}}=8.85\times10^{-11}\ \text{F}$. $U=\tfrac12CV^2=\tfrac12(8.85\times10^{-11})(100)^2=4.425\times10^{-7}\ \text{J}$.

(b) With the dielectric, $C_{new}=\kappa C=3\times8.85\times10^{-11}=2.655\times10^{-10}\ \text{F}$. Since the source keeps $V=100$ V fixed: $U_{new}=\tfrac12C_{new}V^2=\tfrac12(2.655\times10^{-10})(100)^2=1.3275\times10^{-6}\ \text{J}$.

Extra energy supplied: $\Delta U=U_{new}-U=1.3275\times10^{-6}-4.425\times10^{-7}=8.85\times10^{-7}\ \text{J}$ (three times the original energy, consistent with $U\propto C\propto\kappa$ at fixed $V$).
