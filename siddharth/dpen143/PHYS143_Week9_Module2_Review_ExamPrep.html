0 Week 6 Recap: Special Relativity

The exam-critical step is almost always computing $\gamma$ first, correctly, before doing anything else — get $\beta=v/c$ right, square it, subtract from 1, square-root, and invert. Once you have $\gamma$, time dilation and length contraction are single-line substitutions. Watch for which quantity is the proper one (measured in the object's own rest frame) — that's the one you multiply or divide by $\gamma$, never the other way round.

Key idea

$$\gamma=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2}}, \quad \Delta t=\gamma\Delta t_0, \quad L=\frac{L_0}{\gamma}, \quad f_{obs}=f_0\sqrt{\frac{1\pm\beta}{1\mp\beta}}$$

Worked example

Exam-style: A spacecraft has a proper length of 60 m and travels at $v=0.8c$ relative to Earth. (a) Find its length as measured from Earth. (b) A clock onboard ticks off 10 s of proper time during the trip; how much time elapses on Earth for the same interval?

$\gamma=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.64}}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{0.36}}=\dfrac{1}{0.6}=1.667$.

(a) $L=L_0/\gamma=60/1.667=36.0\ \text{m}$.

(b) $\Delta t=\gamma\Delta t_0=1.667\times10=16.7\ \text{s}$.

Practice 1

A particle moves at $v=0.9c$. Find $\gamma$.

Solution

$\gamma=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.81}}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{0.19}}=\dfrac{1}{0.4359}=2.294$.

Practice 2

A rod has proper length 2.0 m. An observer measures it to be 1.2 m long as it flies past. Find the speed of the rod relative to that observer.

Solution

$L=L_0/\gamma \Rightarrow \gamma=L_0/L=2.0/1.2=1.667$.

$\gamma^2=1/(1-\beta^2) \Rightarrow \beta^2=1-1/\gamma^2=1-1/2.778=1-0.36=0.64 \Rightarrow \beta=0.8$, so $v=0.8c$.

Practice 3

A spaceship recedes from Earth at $v=0.15c$, transmitting at $f_0=200$ MHz. Find the received frequency on Earth.

Solution

$f_{obs}=f_0\sqrt{\dfrac{1-0.15}{1+0.15}}=f_0\sqrt{\dfrac{0.85}{1.15}}=f_0\sqrt{0.7391}=f_0\times0.8597$.

$f_{obs}=200\times0.8597=172.0\ \text{MHz}$.

1 Week 7 Recap: Photons, X-rays & the Bohr Atom

These questions almost always start with a photon energy calculation, $E=hc/\lambda$ — get comfortable doing this in eV using $hc\approx1240$ eV·nm, since work functions and Bohr energy levels are conventionally given in eV too. For hydrogen transitions, always double check which level is higher (more negative $E_n$ means lower energy, i.e. more tightly bound) before deciding whether a photon is absorbed or emitted.

Key idea

$$E=\frac{hc}{\lambda}, \quad KE_{max}=hf-\phi, \quad \lambda_{min}=\frac{hc}{eV}, \quad E_n=-\frac{13.6\ \text{eV}}{n^2}$$

Worked example

Exam-style: A metal has $\phi=1.9$ eV. Light of wavelength 450 nm strikes it. (a) Find the maximum kinetic energy of ejected electrons. (b) A hydrogen atom, in a separate part of the same apparatus, has an electron drop from $n=4$ to $n=2$; find the wavelength of that emitted photon, and state whether it could itself eject photoelectrons from the same metal.

(a) $E_{photon}=1240/450=2.756\ \text{eV}$. $KE_{max}=2.756-1.9=0.856\ \text{eV}$.

(b) $E_4=-13.6/16=-0.850\ \text{eV}$, $E_2=-13.6/4=-3.4\ \text{eV}$. $\Delta E=E_4-E_2=-0.850-(-3.4)=2.55\ \text{eV}$ (emitted, since the electron drops from higher $n$ to lower $n$).

$\lambda=1240/2.55=486.3\ \text{nm}$ (this is the Balmer H$\beta$ line). Since $2.55\ \text{eV}>\phi=1.9\ \text{eV}$, yes — this photon carries enough energy to eject a photoelectron from the metal, with $KE_{max}=2.55-1.9=0.65\ \text{eV}$.

Practice 4

Find the threshold wavelength (longest wavelength that can still eject photoelectrons) for a metal with $\phi=2.0$ eV.

Solution

At threshold, $E_{photon}=\phi$ exactly: $\lambda_0=\dfrac{1240}{\phi}=\dfrac{1240}{2.0}=620\ \text{nm}$.

Practice 5

An X-ray tube produces $\lambda_{min}=0.062$ nm. Find its accelerating voltage.

Solution

$V=\dfrac{1240}{\lambda_{min}[\text{nm}]}=\dfrac{1240}{0.062}=2.0\times10^4\ \text{V}=20\ \text{kV}$.

Practice 6

Find the energy and wavelength of the photon absorbed when a hydrogen electron is excited from $n=2$ to $n=4$.

Solution

$E_2=-3.4\ \text{eV}$, $E_4=-0.850\ \text{eV}$. $\Delta E=E_4-E_2=-0.850-(-3.4)=2.55\ \text{eV}$ (absorbed, moving to higher $n$).

$\lambda=1240/2.55=486.3\ \text{nm}$ (same transition as the worked example, just in reverse — absorption instead of emission).

2 Week 8 Recap: Matter Waves, Uncertainty & Nuclear Physics

De Broglie problems are a momentum calculation followed by $\lambda=h/p$ — for accelerated charges, get momentum from $KE=eV=p^2/2m$ first. Particle-in-a-box and uncertainty questions both hinge on the same physical idea (confinement forces a spread in momentum/energy) but use different formulas — know which one a question is asking for. Nuclear questions are usually either a clean half-life/decay-constant conversion or a direct $E=\Delta mc^2$ substitution.

Key idea

$$\lambda=\frac{h}{p}, \quad E_n=\frac{n^2h^2}{8mL^2}, \quad \Delta x\,\Delta p\geq\frac{\hbar}{2}, \quad N=N_0e^{-\lambda t}, \quad T_{1/2}=\frac{0.693}{\lambda}$$

Worked example

Exam-style: An electron is confined to a 1D box of width 0.50 nm. (a) Find its ground-state energy. (b) Use the uncertainty principle with $\Delta x\approx L$ to estimate the minimum kinetic energy, and compare with (a).

(a) $E_1=\dfrac{h^2}{8m_eL^2}=\dfrac{(6.626\times10^{-34})^2}{8(9.11\times10^{-31})(0.50\times10^{-9})^2}=\dfrac{4.39\times10^{-67}}{1.822\times10^{-48}}=2.41\times10^{-19}\ \text{J}=1.51\ \text{eV}$.

(b) Taking $\Delta x\approx L=0.50$ nm: $\Delta p\geq\dfrac{\hbar}{2\Delta x}=\dfrac{1.055\times10^{-34}}{2(0.50\times10^{-9})}=1.055\times10^{-25}\ \text{kg·m/s}$.

$KE\approx\dfrac{(\Delta p)^2}{2m_e}=\dfrac{(1.055\times10^{-25})^2}{2(9.11\times10^{-31})}=\dfrac{1.113\times10^{-50}}{1.822\times10^{-30}}=6.11\times10^{-21}\ \text{J}=0.038\ \text{eV}$ — the two methods give the same order of physics (both say confinement forces nonzero minimum energy), though the numerical values differ because the uncertainty principle only gives a lower-bound estimate, not the exact quantized result from solving the particle-in-a-box problem properly.

Practice 7

A proton (mass $1.67\times10^{-27}$ kg) moves at $5.0\times10^5$ m/s. Find its de Broglie wavelength.

Solution

$p=mv=(1.67\times10^{-27})(5.0\times10^5)=8.35\times10^{-22}\ \text{kg·m/s}$.

$\lambda=\dfrac{6.626\times10^{-34}}{8.35\times10^{-22}}=7.94\times10^{-13}\ \text{m}$.

Practice 8

A radioactive isotope has a decay constant $\lambda=0.0231\ \text{day}^{-1}$. Find its half-life, and the fraction of a sample remaining after 60 days.

Solution

$T_{1/2}=\dfrac{0.693}{0.0231}=30.0\ \text{days}$.

60 days $=2$ half-lives, so fraction remaining $=(1/2)^2=0.25=25\%$.

Practice 9

A nucleus has $Z=6$ protons and $N=6$ neutrons (carbon-12), with a measured mass defect of $\Delta m=0.0990$ u. Find its total binding energy, and the binding energy per nucleon.

Solution

$E_b=\Delta m\times931.5=0.0990\times931.5=92.2\ \text{MeV}$.

Per nucleon (12 nucleons total): $92.2/12=7.68\ \text{MeV/nucleon}$.

3 Mixed Exam-Style Problems (Weeks 6–8)

As with the Module 1 review, real exam questions often combine ideas from more than one week. Read each multi-part problem fully before starting — later parts frequently reuse an earlier result.

Key idea

No single formula — the skill here is recognising which week's tool each part of a question needs, and correctly carrying results from one part into the next.

Worked example

Exam-style, multi-part: A muon (rest lifetime $2.2\ \mu$s) is created moving at $v=0.995c$. (a) Find $\gamma$ and the dilated lifetime measured on Earth. (b) The muon subsequently decays, releasing a photon-equivalent energy of 5.0 MeV; if instead this energy came from a nuclear mass defect, find the corresponding mass defect in atomic mass units ($1\ \text{u}=931.5$ MeV).

(a) $\gamma=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.995^2}}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.990025}}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{0.009975}}=\dfrac{1}{0.09987}=10.01$.

$\Delta t=\gamma\Delta t_0=10.01\times2.2\ \mu\text{s}=22.0\ \mu\text{s}$.

(b) $\Delta m = E/c^2$; in the convenient units given, $\Delta m[\text{u}]=E[\text{MeV}]/931.5=5.0/931.5=5.37\times10^{-3}\ \text{u}$ — illustrating that $E=mc^2$ (Week 8's nuclear energy formula) is really the same mass-energy relation underlying relativity from Week 6, just applied in a different context.

Practice 10

An electron is accelerated through a potential difference $V$ such that its de Broglie wavelength is 0.20 nm. (a) Find its momentum. (b) Find the accelerating voltage $V$ used (non-relativistic).

Solution

(a) $p=h/\lambda=\dfrac{6.626\times10^{-34}}{0.20\times10^{-9}}=3.313\times10^{-24}\ \text{kg·m/s}$.

(b) $KE=\dfrac{p^2}{2m_e}=\dfrac{(3.313\times10^{-24})^2}{2(9.11\times10^{-31})}=\dfrac{1.098\times10^{-47}}{1.822\times10^{-30}}=6.026\times10^{-18}\ \text{J}=37.7\ \text{eV}$.

Since $KE=eV$, the accelerating voltage is numerically $V=37.7\ \text{V}$.

Practice 11

A spacecraft moves at $v=0.6c$ relative to Earth carrying a laser that emits at $\lambda_0=500$ nm (in the ship's own frame), pointed back toward Earth (receding). (a) Find the observed wavelength on Earth (hint: use $f_{obs}=f_0\sqrt{(1-\beta)/(1+\beta)}$ and $\lambda\propto1/f$). (b) Find the observed photon energy on Earth and compare with the emitted photon energy.

Solution

(a) $\lambda_{obs}=\lambda_0\sqrt{\dfrac{1+\beta}{1-\beta}}=500\sqrt{\dfrac{1.6}{0.4}}=500\sqrt{4}=500\times2=1000\ \text{nm}$ (redshifted to double the wavelength, since receding lowers frequency and raises wavelength).

(b) $E_{emitted}=1240/500=2.48\ \text{eV}$; $E_{observed}=1240/1000=1.24\ \text{eV}$ — exactly half, since the observed photon energy scales the same way frequency does, and here $f_{obs}=f_0/2$.

Practice 12

A sample of a radioactive isotope has activity such that its mass defect per decay releases 4.8 MeV, and it has a half-life of 12 hours. Starting from $N_0=2.0\times10^{12}$ nuclei, find (a) the total energy released after exactly 2 half-lives (i.e. all nuclei that have decayed by then), and (b) how many nuclei remain undecayed at that point.

Solution

After 2 half-lives, the fraction remaining is $(1/2)^2=0.25$, so the fraction decayed is $1-0.25=0.75$.

(a) Nuclei decayed $=0.75\times2.0\times10^{12}=1.5\times10^{12}$. Total energy released $=1.5\times10^{12}\times4.8\ \text{MeV}=7.2\times10^{12}\ \text{MeV}$.

(b) Nuclei remaining $=0.25\times2.0\times10^{12}=5.0\times10^{11}$.
