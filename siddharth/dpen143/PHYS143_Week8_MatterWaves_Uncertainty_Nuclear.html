0 Wave-Particle Duality & de Broglie Matter Waves

The photoelectric effect showed light — normally thought of as a wave — behaving like discrete particles. De Broglie proposed the reverse should also be true: matter — normally thought of as particles — should exhibit wave behaviour, with a wavelength set by its momentum. This isn't just a philosophical symmetry; electron diffraction experiments confirmed it directly.

Key idea

$$\lambda=\frac{h}{p}=\frac{h}{mv}$$

For non-relativistic particles accelerated from rest through a potential difference $V$: $KE=eV=\tfrac12mv^2 \Rightarrow p=\sqrt{2m\cdot KE}$, which you then substitute into $\lambda=h/p$.

Worked example

An electron is accelerated from rest through 100 V. Find its de Broglie wavelength.

$KE=eV=100\ \text{eV}=100\times1.6\times10^{-19}=1.6\times10^{-17}\ \text{J}$.

$p=\sqrt{2m_eKE}=\sqrt{2(9.11\times10^{-31})(1.6\times10^{-17})}=\sqrt{2.915\times10^{-47}}=5.40\times10^{-24}\ \text{kg·m/s}$.

$\lambda=\dfrac{h}{p}=\dfrac{6.626\times10^{-34}}{5.40\times10^{-24}}=1.23\times10^{-10}\ \text{m}=0.123\ \text{nm}$ — comparable to atomic spacings, which is exactly why electron beams can diffract off crystals.

Practice 1

Find the de Broglie wavelength of a 1.0 kg ball moving at 10 m/s, and comment on whether its wave nature would ever be noticeable.

Solution

$\lambda=\dfrac{h}{mv}=\dfrac{6.626\times10^{-34}}{(1.0)(10)}=6.63\times10^{-35}\ \text{m}$.

This is roughly $10^{20}$ times smaller than a proton — utterly unobservable. Macroscopic objects always have negligible de Broglie wavelengths, which is why we never notice everyday objects diffracting or interfering.

Practice 2

Find the de Broglie wavelength of an electron moving at $2.0\times10^6$ m/s.

Solution

$p=m_ev=(9.11\times10^{-31})(2.0\times10^6)=1.822\times10^{-24}\ \text{kg·m/s}$.

$\lambda=\dfrac{6.626\times10^{-34}}{1.822\times10^{-24}}=3.64\times10^{-10}\ \text{m}=0.364\ \text{nm}$.

Practice 3

An electron is accelerated through 400 V. Find its de Broglie wavelength.

Solution

$KE=eV=400\times1.6\times10^{-19}=6.4\times10^{-17}\ \text{J}$.

$p=\sqrt{2m_eKE}=\sqrt{2(9.11\times10^{-31})(6.4\times10^{-17})}=\sqrt{1.166\times10^{-46}}=1.080\times10^{-23}\ \text{kg·m/s}$.

$\lambda=\dfrac{6.626\times10^{-34}}{1.080\times10^{-23}}=6.13\times10^{-11}\ \text{m}=0.0613\ \text{nm}$.

1 Wave Functions & Probability

If a particle has wave-like character, what exactly is "waving"? The wave function $\psi(x,t)$ itself is not directly measurable (it can even be complex-valued), but Max Born showed that its squared magnitude gives something very physical: the probability of finding the particle at a given location.

Key idea

$$|\psi(x)|^2\,dx = \text{probability of finding the particle in } [x, x+dx]$$ $$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|\psi(x)|^2\,dx = 1 \quad \text{(normalization: the particle must be found somewhere)}$$

Where $|\psi|^2$ is large, the particle is likely to be found; where it's zero, the particle is never found there. This inherently probabilistic description (not a lack of measurement precision, but a fundamental feature of quantum systems) is one of the biggest conceptual shifts from classical to quantum physics.

Worked example

A particle confined to a 1D box of length $L$ has ground-state wave function $\psi(x)=\sqrt{2/L}\sin(\pi x/L)$ for $0\leq x\leq L$. Verify that it's correctly normalized.

$\displaystyle\int_0^L|\psi(x)|^2\,dx = \int_0^L \frac{2}{L}\sin^2\!\left(\frac{\pi x}{L}\right)dx$.

Using the standard identity $\displaystyle\int_0^L\sin^2(\pi x/L)\,dx = L/2$: the integral becomes $\dfrac{2}{L}\times\dfrac{L}{2}=1$.

The normalization condition is satisfied — exactly as required, since the particle must certainly be found somewhere inside the box.

Practice 6

Explain, using the Born interpretation, why $|\psi|^2$ (rather than $\psi$ itself) is the physically meaningful quantity, given that $\psi$ can be negative or even complex-valued.

Solution

A probability must be a non-negative real number, but $\psi$ itself can take negative or complex values — it can't directly represent a probability. Squaring the magnitude, $|\psi|^2=\psi^*\psi$, always produces a non-negative real number, which can be sensibly interpreted as (and normalized to give) a probability density. The wave function's sign or phase still matters physically — it's essential for interference effects when wave functions from different sources overlap and add — but the directly observable probability of finding the particle somewhere always comes from $|\psi|^2$.

2 Particle in a Box & the Uncertainty Principle

Confining a matter wave (like an electron) to a finite region forces it into standing-wave patterns, just like a guitar string fixed at both ends can only vibrate at certain resonant frequencies. This directly produces quantised energy levels — the simplest fully solvable example in quantum mechanics.

Separately (but related in spirit), Heisenberg showed there's a fundamental trade-off between how precisely you can simultaneously know a particle's position and its momentum — not a limitation of instruments, but a basic feature of how waves work.

Key idea

Particle in a 1D box (width $L$, infinitely high walls): $$E_n=\frac{n^2h^2}{8mL^2}, \quad n=1,2,3,\dots$$ Note $E_n\propto n^2$ (levels spread apart, not evenly spaced) and $E_n\propto1/L^2$ (a smaller box forces higher minimum energy — you can never have $E=0$, since $n\geq1$).

Heisenberg uncertainty principle: $$\Delta x\,\Delta p\geq\frac{\hbar}{2}, \qquad \hbar=\frac{h}{2\pi}=1.055\times10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$$ The more precisely you pin down position ($\Delta x$ small), the less precisely momentum can be known ($\Delta p$ must grow), and vice versa.

Worked example

An electron is confined to a 1D box of width $L=1.0$ nm. Find its ground-state ($n=1$) energy in eV.

$E_1=\dfrac{h^2}{8m_eL^2}=\dfrac{(6.626\times10^{-34})^2}{8(9.11\times10^{-31})(1.0\times10^{-9})^2}$.

Numerator: $(6.626\times10^{-34})^2=4.39\times10^{-67}$. Denominator: $8\times9.11\times10^{-31}\times1.0\times10^{-18}=7.29\times10^{-48}$.

$E_1=\dfrac{4.39\times10^{-67}}{7.29\times10^{-48}}=6.02\times10^{-20}\ \text{J}=\dfrac{6.02\times10^{-20}}{1.6\times10^{-19}}=0.377\ \text{eV}$.

Practice 7

For the same electron-in-a-box ($L=1.0$ nm), find the $n=2$ energy level.

Solution

$E_n=n^2E_1$, so $E_2=4\times0.377=1.51\ \text{eV}$.

Practice 8

Find the energy (and wavelength) of the photon emitted if the electron drops from $n=2$ to $n=1$ in this box.

Solution

$\Delta E=E_2-E_1=1.51-0.377=1.13\ \text{eV}$.

$\lambda=\dfrac{1240}{1.13}=1097\ \text{nm}$ — in the near infrared.

Practice 9

An electron's position is measured to a precision of $\Delta x=0.10$ nm (roughly the size of an atom). Estimate the minimum resulting uncertainty in its momentum, and the corresponding uncertainty in its velocity.

Solution

$\Delta p \geq \dfrac{\hbar}{2\Delta x}=\dfrac{1.055\times10^{-34}}{2(1.0\times10^{-10})}=5.28\times10^{-25}\ \text{kg·m/s}$.

$\Delta v=\Delta p/m_e=\dfrac{5.28\times10^{-25}}{9.11\times10^{-31}}=5.79\times10^{5}\ \text{m/s}$ — a substantial fraction of the electron's typical atomic-scale speed, showing why electrons in atoms can't be thought of as sitting still at a definite point.

Practice 10

Explain qualitatively, using the uncertainty principle, why confining a particle to a smaller box forces it to have a higher minimum kinetic energy (consistent with $E_n\propto1/L^2$ in the particle-in-a-box formula).

Solution

Confining a particle to a smaller region means $\Delta x$ is smaller. By $\Delta x\,\Delta p\geq\hbar/2$, a smaller $\Delta x$ forces $\Delta p$ (and hence the particle's minimum possible momentum, and thus minimum kinetic energy $KE=p^2/2m$) to be larger. This is exactly the qualitative content of $E_n\propto1/L^2$: shrink the box, and the particle is forced to have more kinetic energy even in its lowest ($n=1$) state — it simply cannot be brought to rest inside a confined region.

3 Nuclear Physics

Closing out Module 2: the nucleus itself. Protons and neutrons bound together in a nucleus have slightly less total mass than the same particles separated — that "missing" mass was converted to the binding energy holding the nucleus together, via $E=mc^2$. Unstable nuclei decay randomly but predictably on average, following an exponential decay law.

Key idea

$$E_b=\Delta m\,c^2, \qquad \Delta m = Zm_p+Nm_n-M_{nucleus}, \qquad 1\ \text{u}=931.5\ \text{MeV}/c^2$$ $$N(t)=N_0e^{-\lambda t}, \qquad T_{1/2}=\frac{\ln2}{\lambda}=\frac{0.693}{\lambda}$$

After each half-life, exactly half of the remaining nuclei have decayed; after $n$ half-lives, a fraction $(1/2)^n$ of the original sample remains.

Worked example

Iodine-131 has a half-life of 8.0 days. Find its decay constant, and the fraction of a sample remaining after 24 days.

$\lambda=\dfrac{0.693}{T_{1/2}}=\dfrac{0.693}{8.0}=0.0866\ \text{day}^{-1}$.

24 days $=3\times8.0$ days $=3$ half-lives, so the fraction remaining is $(1/2)^3=1/8=12.5\%$.

Cross-check with the exponential form: $N/N_0=e^{-\lambda t}=e^{-(0.0866)(24)}=e^{-2.079}=0.125$. ✓

Practice 11

A radioactive sample has decay constant $\lambda=0.10\ \text{yr}^{-1}$. Find its half-life.

Solution

$T_{1/2}=\dfrac{0.693}{0.10}=6.93\ \text{years}$.

Practice 12

A nucleus has a mass defect of $\Delta m=0.030$ u. Find its binding energy in MeV.

Solution

$E_b=\Delta m\times931.5\ \text{MeV/u}=0.030\times931.5=27.9\ \text{MeV}$.

Practice 13

A sample starts with $N_0=8.0\times10^{10}$ nuclei and has a half-life of 5.0 hours. Find the number of nuclei remaining after 15 hours.

Solution

15 hours $=3$ half-lives, so $N=N_0(1/2)^3=\dfrac{8.0\times10^{10}}{8}=1.0\times10^{10}$ nuclei.

Practice 14

The binding energy per nucleon peaks around iron (mass number $\approx56$) and is lower for both much lighter and much heavier nuclei. Explain briefly why this means fusion of light nuclei and fission of heavy nuclei can both release energy.

Solution

Any nuclear process that moves nuclei toward the peak (iron) in binding energy per nucleon ends up with more total binding energy than it started with, and that extra binding energy is released (as kinetic energy of the products, gamma rays, etc.), by mass-energy conservation. Light nuclei (far below the peak) release energy when they fuse into medium-mass nuclei closer to the peak; heavy nuclei (also below the peak, on the other side) release energy when they split (fission) into medium-mass fragments closer to the peak. Either direction, moving toward iron increases binding energy per nucleon and releases energy in the process.
