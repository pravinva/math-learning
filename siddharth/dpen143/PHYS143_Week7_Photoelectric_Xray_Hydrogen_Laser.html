The Photoelectric Effect, X-rays, the Hydrogen Atom & Lasers

Four phenomena this week, all showing that light and matter exchange energy in discrete chunks rather than continuously: the photoelectric effect (light knocking electrons out of a metal), X-ray production (the reverse process), the hydrogen atom's discrete energy levels, and lasers, which exploit those discrete levels to produce coherent light. A handy constant throughout: $hc\approx1240\ \text{eV·nm}$. Solutions stay hidden until you click.

Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Photon energy: $E=hf=\dfrac{hc}{\lambda}$, with $h=6.626\times10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}=4.136\times10^{-15}\ \text{eV·s}$; handy shortcut $hc\approx1240\ \text{eV·nm}$.
  • Photoelectric effect: $KE_{max}=hf-\phi$ ($\phi$ = work function); threshold frequency $f_0=\phi/h$; stopping potential $eV_{stop}=KE_{max}$.
  • X-ray production (Duane-Hunt law): minimum wavelength $\lambda_{min}=\dfrac{hc}{eV}$, where $V$ is the tube's accelerating voltage; equivalently $V[\text{volts}]=1240/\lambda_{min}[\text{nm}]$.
  • Bohr hydrogen atom: $E_n=-\dfrac{13.6\ \text{eV}}{n^2}$; photon energy for a transition $=|E_{final}-E_{initial}|$; Rydberg formula $\dfrac1\lambda=R\!\left(\dfrac1{n_f^2}-\dfrac1{n_i^2}\right)$, $R=1.097\times10^7\ \text{m}^{-1}$.
  • Lasers: built on stimulated emission (an incoming photon triggers an excited atom to emit an identical, in-phase photon) plus population inversion (more atoms excited than in the ground state), producing coherent, monochromatic, directional light.

0 The Photoelectric Effect

Shine light on a metal surface and, above a certain frequency, electrons are ejected instantly — but below that frequency, no electrons come out no matter how bright the light. This was completely inexplicable using classical wave theory (where energy should build up gradually with intensity, at any frequency) and was one of the key pieces of evidence that light itself is quantised into photons, each carrying energy $E=hf$.

Key idea

$$KE_{max}=hf-\phi, \qquad f_0=\frac{\phi}{h}, \qquad eV_{stop}=KE_{max}$$

$\phi$ (the work function) is the minimum energy needed to free an electron from the metal, usually quoted in eV. Below $f_0$, every photon simply doesn't carry enough energy to free even one electron — adding more photons (more intensity) doesn't help, since photons don't combine their energy on a single electron.

Worked example

A metal has work function $\phi=2.3$ eV. Light of wavelength 400 nm strikes it. Find the maximum kinetic energy of the ejected electrons and the stopping potential.

Photon energy: $E=\dfrac{hc}{\lambda}=\dfrac{1240\ \text{eV·nm}}{400\ \text{nm}}=3.10\ \text{eV}$.

$KE_{max}=E-\phi=3.10-2.30=0.80\ \text{eV}$ (or $0.80\times1.6\times10^{-19}=1.28\times10^{-19}\ \text{J}$).

Stopping potential: $V_{stop}=KE_{max}/e=0.80\ \text{V}$ (numerically equal in volts, since $KE_{max}$ is already in eV).

Practice 1

Find the threshold frequency for a metal with work function $\phi=2.3$ eV.

Solution

Convert $\phi$ to joules: $2.3\times1.6\times10^{-19}=3.68\times10^{-19}\ \text{J}$.

$f_0=\phi/h=\dfrac{3.68\times10^{-19}}{6.626\times10^{-34}}=5.55\times10^{14}\ \text{Hz}$.

Practice 2

Light of wavelength 250 nm shines on platinum ($\phi=4.5$ eV). Determine whether photoelectrons are emitted, and if so, find their maximum kinetic energy.

Solution

$E_{photon}=\dfrac{1240}{250}=4.96\ \text{eV}$, which exceeds $\phi=4.5$ eV, so yes, photoelectrons are emitted.

$KE_{max}=4.96-4.5=0.46\ \text{eV}$.

Practice 3

A photocell's stopping potential is measured as 1.2 V when illuminated with 300 nm light. Find the work function of the metal.

Solution

$E_{photon}=\dfrac{1240}{300}=4.133\ \text{eV}$.

$KE_{max}=eV_{stop}=1.2\ \text{eV}$.

$\phi=E_{photon}-KE_{max}=4.133-1.2=2.93\ \text{eV}$.

Practice 4

Explain why increasing the intensity of light below the threshold frequency produces no photoelectrons no matter how bright the light is, and why this was a problem for the classical (wave) picture of light.

Solution

Each photon carries a fixed energy $hf$, set only by frequency. If $hf<\phi$, no single photon carries enough energy to free an electron, and (unlike waves, which classically deposit energy continuously and could in principle be left to "accumulate" on an electron given enough time and intensity) photons interact with electrons one at a time — a weak photon can't team up with another to free an electron. More intensity just means more (still individually too-weak) photons per second, so nothing is emitted regardless of brightness. This flatly contradicted the classical wave prediction that sufficiently intense light at any frequency should eventually eject electrons, and was strong evidence for light's particle (photon) nature.

1 X-ray Production

X-ray tubes run the photoelectric idea in reverse: high-speed electrons, accelerated through a large voltage, slam into a metal target and lose their kinetic energy, radiating it away as photons (Bremsstrahlung, "braking radiation"). Most electrons lose their energy gradually over several collisions (producing a spread of X-ray wavelengths), but the shortest possible wavelength is set by the rare electron that loses all its kinetic energy in a single collision, in one photon.

Key idea

$$\lambda_{min}=\frac{hc}{eV}, \qquad \text{equivalently } V[\text{volts}]=\frac{1240}{\lambda_{min}[\text{nm}]}$$

$V$ is the accelerating voltage of the X-ray tube. This minimum-wavelength cutoff (unlike the continuous Bremsstrahlung spectrum around it) is a direct, clean consequence of energy conservation for a single electron-to-single-photon conversion, and is a reliable way to determine $h$ experimentally (the Duane-Hunt law).

Worked example

An X-ray tube operates at an accelerating voltage of 50 kV. Find the minimum wavelength of X-rays produced.

$\lambda_{min}=\dfrac{hc}{eV}=\dfrac{1240\ \text{eV·nm}}{50000\ \text{eV}}=0.0248\ \text{nm}=24.8\ \text{pm}$.

Practice 5

An X-ray tube operates at 80 kV. Find $\lambda_{min}$.

Solution

$\lambda_{min}=\dfrac{1240}{80000}=0.0155\ \text{nm}=15.5\ \text{pm}$.

Practice 6

What accelerating voltage is needed to produce X-rays with $\lambda_{min}=0.10$ nm?

Solution

$V=\dfrac{1240}{\lambda_{min}[\text{nm}]}=\dfrac{1240}{0.10}=12400\ \text{V}=12.4\ \text{kV}$.

Practice 7

Explain physically why the X-ray spectrum has a sharp minimum wavelength cutoff $\lambda_{min}$, but no correspondingly sharp maximum wavelength.

Solution

Each electron arrives with a fixed maximum kinetic energy $KE=eV$ set by the tube voltage. If an electron loses all of this energy in a single collision, it emits one photon carrying the maximum possible energy, which corresponds to the minimum possible wavelength ($E=hc/\lambda$, so max $E$ = min $\lambda$) — no photon can exceed this energy, since no electron has more energy to give. On the other hand, an electron can lose an arbitrarily small amount of energy in a glancing collision (or across many small collisions), producing arbitrarily low-energy (long-wavelength) photons, so there's no corresponding sharp cutoff at the long-wavelength end.

2 The Hydrogen Atom

The Bohr model explains hydrogen's discrete spectral lines by proposing that electrons only occupy specific, quantised orbits (energy levels), and that light is absorbed or emitted only when an electron jumps between these levels — the emitted or absorbed photon carries exactly the energy difference between the two levels.

Key idea

$$E_n=-\frac{13.6\ \text{eV}}{n^2}, \qquad n=1,2,3,\dots$$

A transition from level $n_i$ to level $n_f$ involves a photon of energy $\Delta E=|E_{n_i}-E_{n_f}|$ (absorbed if the electron moves up, emitted if it moves down), and correspondingly $\lambda=hc/\Delta E$. Equivalently, the Rydberg formula gives $1/\lambda$ directly: $$\frac1\lambda=R\left(\frac1{n_f^2}-\frac1{n_i^2}\right), \qquad R=1.097\times10^7\ \text{m}^{-1}$$ (Lyman series: transitions down to $n_f=1$, in the UV; Balmer series: down to $n_f=2$, partly visible.)

Worked example

Find the energy and wavelength of the photon emitted when a hydrogen electron drops from $n=3$ to $n=2$ (the Balmer-series H$\alpha$ line).

$E_3=-\dfrac{13.6}{9}=-1.511\ \text{eV}$, $\quad E_2=-\dfrac{13.6}{4}=-3.4\ \text{eV}$.

Photon energy $=E_2-E_3$... more carefully, energy is released as the electron drops from the higher level ($n=3$) to the lower one ($n=2$): $\Delta E=E_3-E_2=-1.511-(-3.4)=1.889\ \text{eV}$.

$\lambda=\dfrac{hc}{\Delta E}=\dfrac{1240}{1.889}=656.4\ \text{nm}$ — this is the famous red H$\alpha$ line, confirming the model against a real observed spectral line.

Practice 8

Find the ionization energy of hydrogen from the ground state ($n=1\to n=\infty$).

Solution

$E_{ionize}=E_\infty-E_1=0-(-13.6)=13.6\ \text{eV}$.

Practice 9

Find the wavelength of the photon emitted for the $n=2\to n=1$ transition (the Lyman-$\alpha$ line).

Solution

$E_2=-3.4\ \text{eV}$, $E_1=-13.6\ \text{eV}$. $\Delta E=E_2-E_1=-3.4-(-13.6)=10.2\ \text{eV}$.

$\lambda=1240/10.2=121.6\ \text{nm}$ — in the ultraviolet.

Practice 10

An electron in the ground state ($n=1$) absorbs a photon and jumps to $n=3$. Find the photon energy required and its wavelength.

Solution

$E_1=-13.6\ \text{eV}$, $E_3=-1.511\ \text{eV}$. $\Delta E=E_3-E_1=-1.511-(-13.6)=12.09\ \text{eV}$ (absorbed).

$\lambda=1240/12.09=102.6\ \text{nm}$.

Practice 11

Use the Rydberg formula directly to find the wavelength of the $n=3\to n=2$ transition, and check it against the Bohr-energy result from the worked example above.

Solution

$\dfrac1\lambda=R\left(\dfrac1{2^2}-\dfrac1{3^2}\right)=1.097\times10^7(0.25-0.1111)=1.097\times10^7\times0.1389=1.524\times10^6\ \text{m}^{-1}$.

$\lambda=1/(1.524\times10^6)=6.56\times10^{-7}\ \text{m}=656\ \text{nm}$ — matches the 656.4 nm found earlier from the energy-level differences, confirming the two methods agree.

3 Lasers

"Laser" stands for Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation. An excited atom can decay to a lower energy state in two ways: spontaneously (randomly, in a random direction, with random phase), or when triggered by a passing photon of just the right energy (stimulated emission) — in which case the emitted photon is a perfect copy of the triggering one: same energy, same phase, same direction. Build up enough of this stimulated (rather than spontaneous) emission, and you get an intense, coherent beam.

Key idea

For net light amplification (rather than net absorption), you need a population inversion: more atoms in the excited state than in the lower state, so a photon is more likely to trigger stimulated emission than to simply be absorbed. This requires actively "pumping" energy in (optically, electrically, etc.) to maintain the inversion. The result is coherent light: monochromatic (single wavelength), in-phase, and highly directional — very different from an ordinary lamp's spontaneous, incoherent emission.

Worked example

A He-Ne laser emits red light at $\lambda=632.8$ nm. Find the photon energy in eV and in joules.

$E=\dfrac{hc}{\lambda}=\dfrac{1240}{632.8}=1.960\ \text{eV}$.

$E=1.960\times1.6\times10^{-19}=3.14\times10^{-19}\ \text{J}$.

Practice 12

Explain the difference between spontaneous emission and stimulated emission, and why stimulated emission produces coherent light while spontaneous emission does not.

Solution

Spontaneous emission happens randomly: an excited atom decays on its own after some random delay, emitting a photon in a random direction with a random phase, uncorrelated with any other emitted photon. Stimulated emission is triggered by an incoming photon of matching energy, and the emitted photon exactly matches the triggering photon's energy, phase, and direction. Many stimulated-emission events triggered by (and matching) the same original photon(s) build up a beam of photons that are all in step with each other — that shared phase and direction is precisely what "coherent" means.

Practice 13

A laser produces 5.0 mW of power at $\lambda=532$ nm (green). Find the number of photons it emits per second.

Solution

$E_{photon}=\dfrac{1240}{532}=2.331\ \text{eV}=2.331\times1.6\times10^{-19}=3.73\times10^{-19}\ \text{J}$.

$N=\dfrac{P}{E_{photon}}=\dfrac{5.0\times10^{-3}}{3.73\times10^{-19}}=1.34\times10^{16}\ \text{photons/s}$.

Practice 14

Explain what "population inversion" means and why it's necessary for a laser to amplify light rather than simply absorb it.

Solution

Population inversion means more atoms occupy an excited (higher-energy) state than occupy the lower state involved in the laser transition — the opposite of the normal thermal-equilibrium situation, where lower states are always more populated. With more atoms excited than not, an incoming photon is statistically more likely to trigger stimulated emission (adding a matching photon to the beam) than to be absorbed by one of the (relatively few) atoms still in the lower state. Without inversion, absorption would dominate over stimulated emission, and the light would be attenuated rather than amplified as it passed through.
