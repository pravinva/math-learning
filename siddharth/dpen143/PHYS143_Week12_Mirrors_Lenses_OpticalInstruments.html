Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Mirror/lens equation (same form for both): $$\frac{1}{d_o}+\frac{1}{d_i}=\frac{1}{f}$$
  • Magnification: $m=-\dfrac{d_i}{d_o}=\dfrac{h_i}{h_o}$. Negative $m$ = inverted image; $|m|>1$ = enlarged; $|m|<1$ = reduced.
  • Mirrors: $f=R/2$; concave (converging) mirror has $f>0$; convex (diverging) mirror has $f<0$.
  • Lenses: converging (convex) lens has $f>0$; diverging (concave) lens has $f<0$. Sign convention: $d_i>0$ for a real image (opposite side from the object, for lenses); $d_i<0$ for a virtual image (same side as the object).
  • Lens combinations: the image formed by the first lens becomes the object for the next lens in the system.
  • Simple magnifier: angular magnification $M=\dfrac{25\ \text{cm}}{f}$ (image at infinity, relaxed eye) or $M=1+\dfrac{25\ \text{cm}}{f}$ (image at the near point).
  • Compound microscope: $M\approx\left(\dfrac{L}{f_o}\right)\!\left(\dfrac{25\ \text{cm}}{f_e}\right)$ ($L$ = tube length). Telescope: $M=\dfrac{f_o}{f_e}$ (relaxed viewing).

0 Plane & Spherical Mirrors

A plane mirror always produces a virtual, upright, same-size image as far behind the mirror as the object is in front. Curved (spherical) mirrors are more interesting: depending on where the object sits relative to the focal point, a concave mirror can produce a real, inverted image or a virtual, upright, magnified one — while a convex mirror always produces a virtual, reduced image, which is exactly why they're used for wide-angle viewing (security mirrors, car side mirrors).

Key idea

$$\frac{1}{d_o}+\frac{1}{d_i}=\frac{1}{f}, \qquad m=-\frac{d_i}{d_o}, \qquad f=\frac{R}{2}$$

Concave mirror: $f>0$ (real focal point in front of the mirror). Convex mirror: $f<0$ (virtual focal point behind the mirror). A positive $d_i$ means a real image in front of the mirror; negative $d_i$ means a virtual image behind it.

Worked example

A concave mirror has $f=15$ cm. An object sits at $d_o=40$ cm. Find the image position and magnification.

$\dfrac{1}{d_i}=\dfrac1f-\dfrac1{d_o}=\dfrac1{15}-\dfrac1{40}=\dfrac{8-3}{120}=\dfrac{5}{120}=\dfrac1{24}$.

$d_i=24\ \text{cm}$ (positive $\Rightarrow$ real image, same side as the object).

$m=-d_i/d_o=-24/40=-0.60$ (negative $\Rightarrow$ inverted; $|m|<1\Rightarrow$ reduced).

Practice 1

An object is placed 10 cm from a concave mirror with $f=15$ cm (inside the focal length). Find the image position and describe the image.

Solution

$\dfrac1{d_i}=\dfrac1{15}-\dfrac1{10}=\dfrac{2-3}{30}=-\dfrac1{30} \Rightarrow d_i=-30\ \text{cm}$ (negative $\Rightarrow$ virtual, behind the mirror).

$m=-d_i/d_o=-(-30)/10=+3.0$ (upright, magnified) — this is the classic "shaving/makeup mirror" case.

Practice 2

A convex mirror has $R=40$ cm (so $f=-20$ cm). An object sits at $d_o=30$ cm. Find the image position and magnification.

Solution

$\dfrac1{d_i}=\dfrac1f-\dfrac1{d_o}=\dfrac1{-20}-\dfrac1{30}=-\dfrac1{20}-\dfrac1{30}=\dfrac{-3-2}{60}=-\dfrac1{12} \Rightarrow d_i=-12\ \text{cm}$ (virtual, behind the mirror).

$m=-d_i/d_o=-(-12)/30=+0.40$ (upright, reduced) — typical convex-mirror behaviour.

Practice 3

Explain why a convex mirror always produces a virtual, upright, reduced image, for any object distance, using the fact that its focal length is negative.

Solution

With $f<0$ for a convex mirror, $\dfrac1{d_i}=\dfrac1f-\dfrac1{d_o}$ is always the sum of two negative quantities (since $d_o>0$ always for a real object), so $\dfrac1{d_i}$ is always negative, meaning $d_i$ is always negative — the image is always virtual, regardless of how close or far the object is. Working through $m=-d_i/d_o$ with $d_i$ negative always gives a positive $m$ (upright), and it can be shown that $|m|<1$ always as well (reduced) for a convex mirror — which is exactly why they're used where a wide field of view matters more than image size, like car side mirrors and shop security mirrors.

1 Thin Lenses

Lenses use the same governing equation as mirrors, but light passes through a lens rather than bouncing off it, which changes the geometric convention: for a lens, a real image forms on the opposite side from the object (where the light actually converges after passing through), while a virtual image forms on the same side as the object.

Key idea

$$\frac{1}{d_o}+\frac{1}{d_i}=\frac{1}{f}, \qquad m=-\frac{d_i}{d_o}$$

Converging (convex) lens: $f>0$. Diverging (concave) lens: $f<0$. For a system of two or more lenses, solve one lens at a time: the image from lens 1 becomes the object for lens 2, with its object distance measured from lens 2 (accounting for the physical separation between the lenses).

Worked example

A converging lens has $f=20$ cm. An object sits at $d_o=50$ cm. Find the image.

$\dfrac1{d_i}=\dfrac1{20}-\dfrac1{50}=\dfrac{5-2}{100}=\dfrac3{100} \Rightarrow d_i=33.3\ \text{cm}$.

$m=-d_i/d_o=-33.3/50=-0.667$ (real, inverted, reduced).

Practice 4

A diverging lens has $f=-25$ cm. An object sits at $d_o=40$ cm. Find the image.

Solution

$\dfrac1{d_i}=\dfrac1{-25}-\dfrac1{40}=-\dfrac1{25}-\dfrac1{40}=\dfrac{-8-5}{200}=-\dfrac{13}{200} \Rightarrow d_i=-15.4\ \text{cm}$ (virtual, same side as the object).

$m=-d_i/d_o=-(-15.4)/40=+0.385$ (upright, reduced) — diverging lenses always give this kind of image, regardless of object distance, for the same reason convex mirrors do.

Practice 5

Two thin lenses, $f_1=10$ cm and $f_2=15$ cm, sit 30 cm apart. An object is 20 cm in front of lens 1. Find where lens 1's image forms, then use it to find the final image position from lens 2.

Solution

Lens 1: $\dfrac1{d_{i1}}=\dfrac1{10}-\dfrac1{20}=\dfrac{2-1}{20}=\dfrac1{20} \Rightarrow d_{i1}=20\ \text{cm}$ (real image, 20 cm behind lens 1).

This image becomes the object for lens 2. Since lens 2 is 30 cm from lens 1, the object distance for lens 2 is $30-20=10\ \text{cm}$.

Lens 2: $\dfrac1{d_{i2}}=\dfrac1{15}-\dfrac1{10}=\dfrac{2-3}{30}=-\dfrac1{30} \Rightarrow d_{i2}=-30\ \text{cm}$ (virtual final image, 30 cm in front of lens 2, i.e. back toward lens 1).

Practice 6

In the two-lens problem above, if lens 1's image had formed beyond lens 2's position (i.e. more than 30 cm from lens 1), the object distance for lens 2 would come out negative. Explain briefly what this situation physically represents.

Solution

A negative object distance for the second lens represents a virtual object: the light converging toward where lens 1's image "would have formed" is intercepted by lens 2 before it actually gets there, so lens 2 receives converging (rather than diverging) light. The lens equation still applies formally with $d_o<0$ in this case — it's a standard, if slightly less intuitive, situation that arises naturally in multi-lens systems and eyepiece designs.

2 The Eye & the Simple Magnifier

The human eye's near point — the closest distance at which it can comfortably focus — is conventionally taken as 25 cm. A magnifying glass works by letting you bring an object much closer than 25 cm while still forming a sharp (virtual) image the eye can focus on, which increases the angle the object subtends at your eye — and it's angular size, not physical size, that determines how large something appears.

Key idea

Angular magnification of a simple magnifier: $$M=\frac{25\ \text{cm}}{f} \quad (\text{relaxed eye, image at infinity})$$ $$M=1+\frac{25\ \text{cm}}{f} \quad (\text{image formed at the near point instead})$$

Worked example

A magnifying glass has $f=5.0$ cm. Find its angular magnification with the image formed at infinity.

$M=\dfrac{25}{f}=\dfrac{25}{5.0}=5.0\times$.

Practice 7

Find the magnification of a magnifier with $f=2.5$ cm, image at infinity.

Solution

$M=25/2.5=10\times$.

Practice 8

For the same $f=2.5$ cm magnifier, find $M$ if the image is instead formed at the near point (25 cm).

Solution

$M=1+25/f=1+25/2.5=1+10=11\times$ — slightly higher than the relaxed-eye (infinity) case.

Practice 9

Explain, in terms of angular size, why a magnifying glass makes small objects appear larger, even though it doesn't change the object's actual physical size.

Solution

What determines how large an object appears to your eye is the angle it subtends at your retina, not its actual physical size. Without a lens, you can only bring an object as close as your near point (25 cm) before it blurs, capping the angle it can subtend. A magnifying glass forms a clear virtual image of an object held much closer than 25 cm, letting that closer (and therefore more angularly spread out) view still be in focus — increasing the subtended angle, and hence the apparent size, well beyond what unaided viewing allows.

3 Compound Microscope & Telescope

Both instruments use two lenses, but for different purposes and geometries. A microscope's objective lens forms a highly magnified real image of a very close, tiny object; the eyepiece then acts as a simple magnifier on that intermediate image. A telescope's objective instead forms a real image of a very distant object at (or near) its focal point; the eyepiece again magnifies that image, but the emphasis is on angular magnification of already-large objects, not on magnifying tiny ones.

Key idea

Compound microscope: $$M\approx\left(\frac{L}{f_o}\right)\!\left(\frac{25\ \text{cm}}{f_e}\right)$$ where $L$ is the tube length (roughly the distance between the lenses), $f_o$ the objective's focal length (short), $f_e$ the eyepiece's focal length.

Telescope (relaxed viewing, image at infinity): $$M=\frac{f_o}{f_e}$$ where $f_o$ (objective, long focal length) and $f_e$ (eyepiece, short focal length).

Worked example

A telescope has an objective focal length of 100 cm and an eyepiece focal length of 2.0 cm. Find its angular magnification.

$M=\dfrac{f_o}{f_e}=\dfrac{100}{2.0}=50\times$.

Practice 10

A compound microscope has $f_o=0.80$ cm, $f_e=2.5$ cm, and tube length $L=16$ cm. Estimate its overall magnification.

Solution

$M\approx\left(\dfrac{L}{f_o}\right)\left(\dfrac{25}{f_e}\right)=\left(\dfrac{16}{0.80}\right)\left(\dfrac{25}{2.5}\right)=(20)(10)=200\times$.

Practice 11

A telescope's eyepiece is swapped from $f_e=2.0$ cm to $f_e=1.0$ cm (objective unchanged, $f_o=80$ cm). Find the magnification before and after, and explain qualitatively why a shorter-focal-length eyepiece gives higher magnification.

Solution

Before: $M=80/2.0=40\times$. After: $M=80/1.0=80\times$ — magnification doubles.

A shorter focal length means the eyepiece bends (converges) light more strongly. Since the eyepiece is acting as a simple magnifier on the intermediate image formed by the objective, a shorter $f_e$ increases the angular magnification of that intermediate image by the same logic as the simple-magnifier formula $M=25/f$ — smaller $f$, bigger $M$.

Practice 12

Explain the basic two-stage magnification process in a compound microscope: what role the objective lens plays, and what role the eyepiece plays, tying the two subtopics of this section together.

Solution

The objective lens (very short focal length, placed very close to the tiny specimen) forms a real, substantially enlarged intermediate image inside the microscope tube — this is standard image formation by a converging lens with the object placed just outside its focal point. The eyepiece then takes over exactly like a simple magnifier, viewing this already-enlarged intermediate image from close range and further magnifying its angular size for the observer's eye. The overall magnification is the product of these two stages, which is exactly what the formula $M\approx(L/f_o)(25/f_e)$ captures: the objective's magnification factor times the eyepiece's angular magnification factor.

Practice 13

Lens imaging: A thin converging lens has focal length f=14 cm and object distance u=25 cm. Find image distance, magnification, and image type.

Solution

Lens equation: 1/f = 1/u + 1/v, so 1/v = 1/14 - 1/25 = 0.031429 cm^-1.

Image distance: v = 31.8182 cm.

Magnification: m = -v/u = -31.8182/25 = -1.2727. Therefore image is real and inverted.
