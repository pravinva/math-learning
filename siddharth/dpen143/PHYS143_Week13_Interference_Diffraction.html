Interference & Diffraction

The final teaching week ties light's wave nature together: coherence and thin-film interference (why soap bubbles show colour), the Michelson interferometer and Young's double slit, single-slit diffraction and the resolving power it limits, and diffraction gratings. All of it comes from the same core idea — path difference determines whether waves add constructively or destructively. Solutions stay hidden until you click.

Lay of the land — this week's toolkit
  • Path & phase difference: $\Delta\varphi=\dfrac{2\pi}{\lambda}\Delta(\text{path})$; constructive when path difference $=m\lambda$, destructive when $=(m+\tfrac12)\lambda$.
  • Coherence: a stable interference pattern requires sources with a fixed, constant phase relationship — typically achieved by splitting light from a single source into two paths.
  • Thin-film interference (single relative phase shift, e.g. a film in air): bright (constructive) reflection when $2nt=(m+\tfrac12)\lambda$; dark when $2nt=m\lambda$ ($n$ = film index, $t$ = thickness).
  • Michelson interferometer: moving a mirror by $\Delta d$ shifts $N=2\Delta d/\lambda$ fringes (factor of 2 from the round trip).
  • Young's double slit: bright fringes at $d\sin\theta=m\lambda$; fringe spacing on a distant screen $y_m\approx\dfrac{m\lambda L}{d}$.
  • Single-slit diffraction: dark fringes at $a\sin\theta=m\lambda$, $m=1,2,3,\dots$ ($m=0$ is the bright central maximum, not dark).
  • Rayleigh criterion (resolution): $\theta_{min}=1.22\dfrac{\lambda}{D}$ ($D$ = aperture diameter).
  • Diffraction grating: bright maxima at $d\sin\theta=m\lambda$, with $d$ = spacing between adjacent slits (grating spacing).

0 Phase Difference, Coherence & Thin-Film Interference

Interference is what happens when two (or more) coherent waves overlap: depending on their path-length difference, they can reinforce (constructive) or cancel (destructive). "Coherent" means the sources maintain a fixed phase relationship — two separate light bulbs never produce visible interference, because their phases fluctuate randomly and independently, washing out any pattern. Splitting light from a single source into two paths (as in a thin film, or the setups later this week) guarantees coherence.

Key idea

$$\Delta\varphi=\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\Delta(\text{path}), \qquad \text{constructive: path diff.}=m\lambda, \quad \text{destructive: path diff.}=\left(m+\tfrac12\right)\lambda$$

Thin-film interference (film of index $n$, thickness $t$, with a phase shift at only one of the two reflecting surfaces — the common case for a film like soap or oil surrounded by lower-index media on both sides): $$\text{bright (constructive) reflection: } 2nt=\left(m+\tfrac12\right)\lambda, \qquad \text{dark (destructive): } 2nt=m\lambda$$ (If both surfaces produce a phase shift, or neither does, the two conditions swap.)

Worked example

Find the minimum thickness of a soap film ($n=1.33$) in air that produces constructive interference (appears bright) for reflected light of $\lambda=480$ nm at normal incidence.

Minimum thickness corresponds to the smallest $m$, i.e. $m=0$: $2nt=(0+\tfrac12)\lambda=0.5\lambda$.

$t=\dfrac{\lambda}{4n}=\dfrac{480}{4(1.33)}=\dfrac{480}{5.32}=90.2\ \text{nm}$.

Practice 1

For the same soap film ($n=1.33$, air on both sides), find the minimum nonzero thickness that produces destructive interference (appears dark) for $\lambda=480$ nm.

Solution

Destructive: $2nt=m\lambda$. $m=0$ gives the trivial $t=0$, so the minimum nonzero thickness is at $m=1$: $t=\dfrac{\lambda}{2n}=\dfrac{480}{2(1.33)}=180.5\ \text{nm}$.

Practice 2

A MgF$_2$ coating ($n=1.38$) is applied to glass ($n=1.50$) as an antireflection coating for $\lambda=550$ nm at normal incidence. Because the refractive index increases at both surfaces (air$\to$MgF$_2$, then MgF$_2\to$glass), the two phase shifts cancel, swapping which condition gives destructive interference: here, minimum reflection occurs when $2nt=(m+\tfrac12)\lambda$. Find the minimum coating thickness.

Solution

Minimum thickness: $m=0$, so $2nt=0.5\lambda \Rightarrow t=\dfrac{\lambda}{4n}=\dfrac{550}{4(1.38)}=\dfrac{550}{5.52}=99.6\ \text{nm}$.

Practice 3

Explain why a coherent light source (or a coherent setup, like light from a single source split into two paths) is necessary to observe a stable interference pattern, and why two separate ordinary light bulbs shining on the same screen don't produce visible fringes.

Solution

An interference pattern depends on a fixed phase relationship between the two overlapping waves at every point on the screen — only then does the pattern of constructive and destructive regions stay put long enough to see. Two separate, ordinary light bulbs each emit light with rapidly and randomly fluctuating phase (since they come from vast numbers of independent atomic emission events), and the two bulbs' phases have no fixed relationship to each other at all. Any interference pattern that briefly forms shifts around randomly and averages out to uniform illumination on any practical timescale, so no stable fringes are ever visible. Splitting light from a single source (a thin film's two reflecting surfaces, or the two slits/paths used later this week) guarantees the two waves share a fixed phase relationship, making a stable pattern observable.

1 Michelson Interferometer & Young's Double Slit

Two classic ways of splitting light into two coherent paths and recombining them. The Michelson interferometer splits a beam with a half-silvered mirror, sends the two halves down separate arms to be reflected back, and recombines them — moving one mirror changes that arm's path length and shifts the interference pattern. Young's double slit instead sends light through two narrow, closely-spaced slits, and the light diffracting from each slit overlaps and interferes on a distant screen.

Key idea

Michelson interferometer: moving a mirror a distance $\Delta d$ changes the round-trip path length by $2\Delta d$, shifting $$N=\frac{2\Delta d}{\lambda}$$ fringes past a reference point.

Young's double slit (slit spacing $d$, screen distance $L\gg d$): bright fringes at $$d\sin\theta=m\lambda, \qquad y_m\approx\frac{m\lambda L}{d}$$

Worked example

In a Michelson interferometer, a mirror is moved 0.015 mm and 48 fringes are counted passing a reference point. Find the wavelength of light used.

$\lambda=\dfrac{2\Delta d}{N}=\dfrac{2(0.015\ \text{mm})}{48}=\dfrac{0.030\ \text{mm}}{48}=6.25\times10^{-4}\ \text{mm}=625\ \text{nm}$.

Practice 4

A double-slit experiment uses slits separated by $d=0.20$ mm, a screen at $L=2.0$ m, and light of $\lambda=600$ nm. Find the spacing between adjacent bright fringes on the screen.

Solution

$y=\dfrac{\lambda L}{d}=\dfrac{(600\times10^{-9})(2.0)}{0.20\times10^{-3}}=\dfrac{1.2\times10^{-6}}{2.0\times10^{-4}}=6.0\times10^{-3}\ \text{m}=6.0\ \text{mm}$.

Practice 5

In a double-slit setup with $d=0.15$ mm, the third bright fringe ($m=3$) appears at $\theta=0.86^\circ$ from the centre. Find the wavelength used.

Solution

$\lambda=\dfrac{d\sin\theta}{m}=\dfrac{(0.15\times10^{-3})\sin(0.86^\circ)}{3}=\dfrac{(0.15\times10^{-3})(0.01501)}{3}=\dfrac{2.25\times10^{-6}}{3}=7.51\times10^{-7}\ \text{m}=751\ \text{nm}$ (red light).

Practice 6

Explain why the Michelson fringe-shift formula includes a factor of 2 (i.e. why $N=2\Delta d/\lambda$ rather than simply $\Delta d/\lambda$).

Solution

Light in a Michelson interferometer travels from the beam-splitter to the movable mirror, reflects, and travels back to the beam-splitter — a round trip. Moving the mirror by $\Delta d$ therefore changes the one-way distance by $\Delta d$, but changes the total round-trip path length (which is what actually determines the interference condition at the beam-splitter) by $2\Delta d$. It's this round-trip path change that must be divided by $\lambda$ to count fringes.

2 Single-Slit Diffraction & Resolution

A double slit assumes each slit is essentially a point source, but a real slit has finite width, and light diffracting through it produces its own pattern — a bright central band flanked by progressively dimmer side bands, with dark fringes in between. This isn't just a curiosity: diffraction fundamentally limits how sharply any optical instrument (a telescope, a microscope, even your eye) can resolve two closely-spaced objects, via the Rayleigh criterion.

Key idea

Single-slit dark fringes (slit width $a$): $$a\sin\theta=m\lambda, \qquad m=1,2,3,\dots$$ Note $m=0$ is not a dark fringe — it's the centre of the bright central maximum.

Rayleigh criterion (minimum resolvable angular separation, circular aperture of diameter $D$): $$\theta_{min}=1.22\frac{\lambda}{D}$$ Smaller apertures diffract light more strongly, blurring point sources together and degrading resolution — which is why bigger telescope mirrors resolve finer detail.

Worked example

Light of $\lambda=550$ nm passes through a single slit of width $a=0.040$ mm. Find the angle to the first dark fringe.

$\sin\theta=\dfrac{m\lambda}{a}=\dfrac{(1)(550\times10^{-9})}{0.040\times10^{-3}}=\dfrac{5.5\times10^{-7}}{4.0\times10^{-5}}=0.01375$.

$\theta=\sin^{-1}(0.01375)=0.788^\circ$.

Practice 7

For the same slit ($a=0.040$ mm, $\lambda=550$ nm), find the angle to the second dark fringe ($m=2$).

Solution

$\sin\theta=\dfrac{2(550\times10^{-9})}{0.040\times10^{-3}}=0.0275 \Rightarrow \theta=1.576^\circ$.

Practice 8

A telescope has an objective diameter $D=10$ cm. Using the Rayleigh criterion, find the minimum angular separation it can resolve for light of $\lambda=550$ nm.

Solution

$\theta_{min}=1.22\dfrac{\lambda}{D}=1.22\times\dfrac{550\times10^{-9}}{0.10}=6.71\times10^{-6}\ \text{rad}$.

Practice 9

Two stars are separated by an angle of $3.0\times10^{-6}$ rad as seen from Earth. What minimum telescope aperture diameter is needed to resolve them as distinct points ($\lambda=550$ nm)?

Solution

$D=1.22\dfrac{\lambda}{\theta_{min}}=1.22\times\dfrac{550\times10^{-9}}{3.0\times10^{-6}}=\dfrac{6.71\times10^{-7}}{3.0\times10^{-6}}=0.224\ \text{m}=22.4\ \text{cm}$.

Practice 10

Explain why a larger aperture $D$ gives better resolving power (smaller $\theta_{min}$), connecting this to the general wave behaviour that narrower apertures diffract light more strongly.

Solution

Diffraction spreading is generally more pronounced when an aperture is comparable to (or smaller than) the wavelength passing through it, and less pronounced for apertures much larger than the wavelength — this is exactly what $\theta_{min}\propto1/D$ captures: a larger aperture diffracts light less, keeping the light from a point source concentrated into a tighter central spot on the detector. Two point sources whose diffraction spots overlap less (because each spot is smaller, from the larger aperture) are easier to tell apart as separate images — hence better resolution with bigger apertures.

3 Diffraction Gratings

A diffraction grating is essentially a double slit scaled up to many hundreds or thousands of closely-spaced slits. It obeys the same underlying condition as the double slit, but with many more sources interfering, the bright maxima become far sharper and more intense — making gratings the tool of choice for precisely measuring wavelengths (spectroscopy).

Key idea

$$d\sin\theta=m\lambda, \qquad m=0,\pm1,\pm2,\dots$$ where $d$ is the spacing between adjacent slits (grating spacing), often quoted via the number of lines per unit length: $d=1/N_{\text{lines per length}}$. Because so many slits contribute constructively at exactly the same angle (and destructively everywhere else, through cancellation across the many slits), grating maxima are much narrower and brighter than the broad double-slit fringes covered earlier this week.

Worked example

A diffraction grating has 5000 lines/cm. Find its grating spacing $d$, and the angle of the first-order ($m=1$) maximum for light of $\lambda=600$ nm.

$d=\dfrac{1}{5000\ \text{lines/cm}}=2.0\times10^{-4}\ \text{cm}=2000\ \text{nm}=2.0\times10^{-6}\ \text{m}$.

$\sin\theta=\dfrac{m\lambda}{d}=\dfrac{(1)(600\times10^{-9})}{2.0\times10^{-6}}=0.30 \Rightarrow \theta=17.5^\circ$.

Practice 11

For the same grating ($d=2000$ nm), find the angle for the second-order ($m=2$) maximum of the same 600 nm light.

Solution

$\sin\theta=\dfrac{2(600)}{2000}=0.60 \Rightarrow \theta=36.9^\circ$.

Practice 12

A grating has $d=1667$ nm (600 lines/mm). Find the highest diffraction order $m$ observable for light of $\lambda=650$ nm.

Solution

Since $\sin\theta\leq1$: $m\leq d/\lambda=1667/650=2.565$.

So the highest integer order is $m=2$ ($m=3$ would require $\sin\theta>1$, which is impossible).

Practice 13

Explain briefly why a diffraction grating with many closely-spaced slits produces much sharper and brighter interference maxima than a simple two-slit setup, even though both are governed by the same condition $d\sin\theta=m\lambda$.

Solution

At exactly the angle satisfying $d\sin\theta=m\lambda$, light from every slit in the grating arrives in phase and adds constructively, producing a very bright, intense maximum. At angles even slightly away from this condition, the contributions from hundreds or thousands of slits are spread across a full range of phases and interfere destructively with each other almost completely (unlike a double slit, where only two contributions can partially cancel) — so the grating's bright fringes are both far more intense at their peak and far narrower (sharper) than the broad, gently-varying fringes from just two slits.
