Topic 1
What is i?
Inventing a number that shouldn't exist
You have always been told you cannot take the square root of a negative number. That is true — if you only allow real numbers. Complex numbers exist because mathematicians decided to invent a new number to fill that gap. Once you accept one new rule, everything else follows logically.
DefinitionThe imaginary unit i▶

We define a new number i by the single rule:

\[ i = \sqrt{-1} \qquad \text{equivalently} \qquad i^2 = -1 \]

That is the entire definition. Everything in complex numbers flows from this one fact.

Using this, we can now take square roots of any negative number:

\[ \sqrt{-9} = \sqrt{9 \times (-1)} = \sqrt{9}\,\sqrt{-1} = 3i \]
\[ \sqrt{-5} = \sqrt{5}\,\sqrt{-1} = i\sqrt{5} \]
Convention: write \(i\sqrt{5}\), not \(\sqrt{5}\,i\). Putting i before a surd avoids it being misread as \(\sqrt{5i}\).
PatternPowers of i cycle in fours▶

Because \(i^2 = -1\), higher powers of i just keep cycling through four values:

\(i^1\)
\(i\)
\(i^2\)
\(-1\)
\(i^3\)
\(-i\)
\(i^4\)
\(1\)

Then it repeats: \(i^5 = i\), \(i^6 = -1\), \(i^7 = -i\), \(i^8 = 1\), ...

To find any power of i, divide the exponent by 4 and look at the remainder:

\[ i^n: \quad \text{remainder } 0 \to 1,\quad 1 \to i,\quad 2 \to -1,\quad 3 \to -i \]
Why does it cycle? Because \(i^4 = (i^2)^2 = (-1)^2 = 1\). Multiplying by \(i^4\) is the same as multiplying by 1 — so the pattern resets every 4 steps.

2

ANSWER \(\sqrt{-16} = 4i\)

1

2
Remainder 3 corresponds to \(-i\).
ANSWER \(i^{23} = -i\)
Example 3 — Simplify \(i^{100}\)
1
Divide 100 by 4: \(100 = 4 \times 25 + 0\). Remainder is 0.
2
Remainder 0 corresponds to 1.
ANSWER \(i^{100} = 1\)
Example 4 — Simplify \(i^3 + i^4 + i^5\)
1
\(i^3 = -i,\quad i^4 = 1,\quad i^5 = i\)
2

NoteCommon mistakes with i▶
Watch out: \(\sqrt{-a} \times \sqrt{-b} \neq \sqrt{ab}\) when both a and b are positive. For example \(\sqrt{-4}\times\sqrt{-9} = 2i \times 3i = 6i^2 = -6\), not \(\sqrt{36}=6\). Always convert to i-form first before multiplying.
Watch out: \(i^2 = -1\), not \(+1\). When you see \(i^2\) appear in a calculation, replace it with \(-1\) immediately. Missing this is the single most common error.
i is not a variable — it is a fixed number just like \(\pi\) or \(e\). You cannot "solve for i".
Topic 2
The form a + bi
Real part, imaginary part, and basic arithmetic
Now that we have i, we can write numbers like \(3 + 4i\) or \(2 - 7i\). These are called complex numbers. They have two components — a real part and an imaginary part — and you treat them almost exactly like algebra, with one extra rule: collect like terms (real with real, imaginary with imaginary).
DefinitionStandard form and terminology▶

A complex number is written in the form \(z = a + bi\) where:

\[ z = \underbrace{a}_{\text{real part}} + \underbrace{b}_{\text{imaginary part}}i \]

Both a and b are ordinary real numbers. We write:

\[ \text{Re}(z) = a \qquad \text{Im}(z) = b \]

Note: Im(z) = b, not bi. The imaginary part is just the coefficient of i — it is itself a real number.

Complex numberRe(z)Im(z)Notes

\(2 - 5i\)2−5Imaginary part is −5, not 5
\(7\)70Real numbers are complex numbers with Im = 0

PatternAdding, subtracting, scalar multiplication▶

Treat a + bi exactly like algebra — collect like terms. Real with real, imaginary with imaginary.

Addition:

\[ (a+bi) + (c+di) = (a+c) + (b+d)i \]

Subtraction:

\[ (a+bi) - (c+di) = (a-c) + (b-d)i \]

Scalar multiplication (multiply by an ordinary real number k):

\[ k(a+bi) = ka + kbi \]
Think of it like collecting terms in \(3x + 2y\) — you cannot combine the real and imaginary parts any more than you can combine x and y terms. They are different types.

Example 1 — Add \((3+4i) + (1-2i)\)
1
Group real parts: \(3 + 1 = 4\)
2
Group imaginary parts: \(4i + (-2i) = 2i\)
ANSWER \(4 + 2i\)
Example 2 — Subtract \((5-3i) - (2+7i)\)
1
Distribute the minus: \(5 - 3i - 2 - 7i\)
2
Real parts: \(5 - 2 = 3\)
3
Imaginary parts: \(-3i - 7i = -10i\)
ANSWER \(3 - 10i\)
Example 3 — Find Re(z) and Im(z) for \(z = 4 - 9i\)
ANSWER \(\text{Re}(z) = 4, \quad \text{Im}(z) = -9\)

Im(z) = −9 not 9. The sign belongs to the imaginary part.

Example 4 — Two complex numbers are equal: \(2x + 3yi = 6 - 9i\). Find x and y.

Two complex numbers are equal if and only if their real parts are equal AND their imaginary parts are equal.

1
Real parts equal: \(2x = 6 \Rightarrow x = 3\)
2
Imaginary parts equal: \(3y = -9 \Rightarrow y = -3\)
ANSWER \(x = 3,\quad y = -3\)
NoteEquality and what trips people up▶
Two complex numbers \(a+bi = c+di\) if and only if \(a=c\) AND \(b=d\). This gives you two equations from one complex equation — very useful for solving problems.
Watch out: \(\text{Im}(3-5i) = -5\), not 5. The imaginary part includes its sign.
Watch out: When subtracting, distribute the minus sign across both parts: \((a+bi)-(c+di) = a+bi-c-di\). Forgetting to negate the imaginary part is the most common error here.
Topic 3
Multiplication
Expand the brackets, then use i² = −1
Multiplying complex numbers is just expanding brackets — exactly like you would in algebra. The only extra step is that whenever \(i^2\) appears, you replace it with \(-1\). That one substitution is the whole trick.
DefinitionThe multiplication rule▶

Multiply \((a+bi)(c+di)\) by expanding all four pairs (FOIL), then replace \(i^2\) with \(-1\):

\[ (a+bi)(c+di) = ac + adi + bci + bdi^2 \]
\[ = ac + adi + bci + bd(-1) \]
\[ = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i \]

You do not need to memorise the final formula. Just expand and replace \(i^2 = -1\) every time.

PatternThe two-step process▶
1
Expand — multiply every term in the first bracket by every term in the second (FOIL or grid method). You get four terms.
2
Replace — substitute \(i^2 = -1\) wherever it appears. This turns the imaginary-squared term into a real number.
3
Collect — group real terms together and imaginary terms together to get the result in \(a+bi\) form.
The key insight: \(i^2 = -1\) converts what looks like an imaginary term into a real number. This is what keeps complex multiplication "closed" — the result is always another complex number in \(a+bi\) form.
ExampleFour fully worked examples▶
Example 1 — Multiply \((2+3i)(1+4i)\)
1
Expand: \(2\cdot1 + 2\cdot4i + 3i\cdot1 + 3i\cdot4i\)
2
\(= 2 + 8i + 3i + 12i^2\)
3
Replace \(i^2=-1\): \(= 2 + 8i + 3i + 12(-1)\)
4
Collect: \(= (2-12) + (8+3)i = -10 + 11i\)
ANSWER \(-10 + 11i\)
Example 2 — Multiply \((3-2i)(4-i)\)
1
Expand: \(12 - 3i - 8i + 2i^2\)
2
Replace \(i^2=-1\): \(12 - 3i - 8i + 2(-1)\)
3
Collect: \((12-2) + (-3-8)i = 10 - 11i\)
ANSWER \(10 - 11i\)
Example 3 — Expand \((1+2i)^2\)
1
Write as \((1+2i)(1+2i)\). Expand: \(1 + 2i + 2i + 4i^2\)
2
Replace: \(1 + 4i + 4(-1) = 1 + 4i - 4\)
3
Collect: \(-3 + 4i\)
ANSWER \(-3 + 4i\)
Watch out: \((1+2i)^2 \neq 1 + 4i^2\). You cannot just square each term separately. Expand the brackets fully.
Example 4 — Multiply \((5+3i)(5-3i)\)
1
Expand: \(25 - 15i + 15i - 9i^2\)
2
The \(-15i\) and \(+15i\) cancel. Replace: \(25 - 9(-1) = 25 + 9 = 34\)
ANSWER \(34\) — a real number!

Notice: the imaginary parts cancelled completely. This is not a coincidence — it always happens when you multiply a complex number by its conjugate. Topic 4 explains why.

NoteWhat trips people up in multiplication▶
Watch out: the most common error is forgetting to multiply the last pair (the \(bi \times di = bdi^2\) term), then forgetting to replace \(i^2\) with \(-1\). Do the replacement as soon as you see \(i^2\) — do not leave it to the end.
Watch out: \((a+bi)^2 = a^2 + 2abi + b^2i^2 = a^2 - b^2 + 2abi\). Students often write \(a^2 + b^2i^2\), missing the middle term \(2abi\).
Topic 4
Conjugate & Division
How to divide by a complex number
Division by a complex number feels impossible at first — how do you divide by something like \(3+2i\)? The answer is the conjugate trick: multiply top and bottom by the conjugate of the denominator. The denominator becomes a real number, and then ordinary division works. This is the most tested technique in complex numbers.
DefinitionThe complex conjugate▶

The complex conjugate of \(z = a+bi\) is written \(\bar{z}\) and is defined by flipping the sign of the imaginary part:

\[ z = a + bi \qquad \bar{z} = a - bi \]
z\(\bar{z}\)
\(3 + 4i\)\(3 - 4i\)
\(2 - 7i\)\(2 + 7i\)
\(5\)\(5\)
\(i\)\(-i\)

The critical property: multiplying z by its conjugate always gives a real number:

\[ z\bar{z} = (a+bi)(a-bi) = a^2 + b^2 \]

The imaginary parts cancel and you are left with \(a^2+b^2\), which is always real and positive (as long as z ≠ 0).

PatternHow to divide complex numbers▶

To compute \(\dfrac{a+bi}{c+di}\):

1
Write the division as a fraction.
2
Identify the conjugate of the denominator: \(c-di\).
3
Multiply top and bottom by that conjugate. This is valid because you are multiplying by \(\dfrac{c-di}{c-di} = 1\).
4
Expand the top. The bottom becomes \(c^2+d^2\), a real number.
5
Simplify by dividing through by \(c^2+d^2\). Write in \(a+bi\) form.
Why does this work? Because multiplying top and bottom by the same number does not change the value of the fraction. The conjugate is specifically chosen so the denominator becomes real.
ExampleThree fully worked examples▶
Example 1 — Compute \(\dfrac{3+i}{2-i}\)
1
Conjugate of denominator: \(2+i\)
2
Multiply top and bottom: \(\dfrac{(3+i)(2+i)}{(2-i)(2+i)}\)
3
Denominator: \(4 + 1 = 5\)
4
Numerator: \(6 + 3i + 2i + i^2 = 6 + 5i - 1 = 5 + 5i\)
5
Divide: \(\dfrac{5+5i}{5} = 1 + i\)
ANSWER \(1 + i\)
Example 2 — Compute \(\dfrac{2+3i}{1-2i}\)
1
Conjugate: \(1+2i\). Denominator becomes \(1+4=5\).
2
Numerator: \((2+3i)(1+2i) = 2 + 4i + 3i + 6i^2 = 2 + 7i - 6 = -4 + 7i\)
3
Result: \(\dfrac{-4+7i}{5} = -\dfrac{4}{5} + \dfrac{7}{5}i\)
ANSWER \(-\dfrac{4}{5} + \dfrac{7}{5}i\)
Example 3 — Write \(\dfrac{1}{i}\) in a+bi form
1
Conjugate of i is \(-i\). Multiply: \(\dfrac{1 \times (-i)}{i \times (-i)} = \dfrac{-i}{-i^2} = \dfrac{-i}{1} = -i\)
ANSWER \(-i\)

Check: \(i \times (-i) = -i^2 = -(-1) = 1\). ✓

NoteProperties of conjugates — useful identities▶

These come up in exam questions — worth knowing:

\[ z + \bar{z} = 2a \qquad \text{(always real)} \]
\[ z - \bar{z} = 2bi \qquad \text{(always imaginary)} \]
\[ z\bar{z} = a^2 + b^2 \qquad \text{(always real and } \geq 0\text{)} \]
If a polynomial with real coefficients has \(z = a+bi\) as a root, then \(\bar{z} = a-bi\) is also a root. Complex roots always come in conjugate pairs when coefficients are real.
Watch out: the conjugate of \(2-3i\) is \(2+3i\), not \(-2+3i\). Only the sign of the imaginary part changes.
Topic 5
The Argand Diagram
Complex numbers as points on a plane
Real numbers live on a line. Complex numbers need a plane. The real part is the x-coordinate and the imaginary part is the y-coordinate. This geometric picture — called the Argand diagram — turns abstract algebra into something you can see, and makes modulus and argument immediately obvious.
DefinitionPlotting complex numbers▶

The Argand diagram is a coordinate plane where:

\[ z = a + bi \quad \longleftrightarrow \quad \text{point } (a,\, b) \]

The horizontal axis is the real axis. The vertical axis is the imaginary axis.

Each complex number corresponds to exactly one point. The origin represents \(z = 0\).

PatternModulus as distance — geometric meaning▶

The modulus of z, written \(|z|\), is the distance from the origin to the point z in the Argand diagram:

\[ |z| = |a+bi| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} \]

This is just Pythagoras — the point (a,b) is at distance \(\sqrt{a^2+b^2}\) from the origin.

Modulus is always \(\geq 0\). It equals 0 only when \(z = 0\). Also: \(z\bar{z} = |z|^2\). This gives a quick way to find the denominator in division: \(|c+di|^2 = c^2+d^2\).
ExampleThree worked examples▶
Example 1 — Find \(|3+4i|\)
\[ |3+4i| = \sqrt{3^2+4^2} = \sqrt{9+16} = \sqrt{25} = 5 \]
ANSWER \(|3+4i| = 5\)

This is a 3-4-5 Pythagorean triple — appears frequently in exam questions.

Example 2 — Find \(|2-5i|\)
\[ |2-5i| = \sqrt{4+25} = \sqrt{29} \]
ANSWER \(\sqrt{29}\)
Example 3 — Verify \(z\bar{z} = |z|^2\) for \(z = 3+4i\)
1
\(\bar{z} = 3-4i\)
2
\(z\bar{z} = (3+4i)(3-4i) = 9+16 = 25\)
3
\(|z|^2 = 5^2 = 25\) ✓
VERIFIED \(z\bar{z} = |z|^2 = 25\)

Topic 6
Modulus & Argument
Polar form — describing a complex number by distance and angle
Instead of describing a point by its x and y coordinates, you can describe it by how far it is from the origin (modulus) and what angle it makes with the positive real axis (argument). This polar form is essential for understanding multiplication geometrically, and appears in every complex numbers exam.
MotivationWhy switch from \(a+bi\) to polar form?▶

In Cartesian form \(z=a+bi\), addition and subtraction are easy. But multiplication/division are messy if you stay in \(a+bi\).

Polar form fixes this: once a complex number is written as length + angle, multiplication becomes:

\[ \text{multiply lengths, add angles} \]

That is exactly why this topic matters: it turns complicated algebra into geometric operations (scaling + rotation).

Practical view: Cartesian form is good for plotting and adding vectors. Polar form is good for rotations, powers, roots, and roots of unity.
ComparisonTrig unit circle vs complex unit circle▶
Trig unit circleComplex unit circle
Point at angle \(\theta\) has coordinates \((\cos\theta,\sin\theta)\)Point at angle \(\theta\) is complex number \(z=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta\)
Radius is \(1\)Modulus is \(|z|=1\)
\(\cos\theta\) is x-coordinate\(\Re(z)=\cos\theta\)
\(\sin\theta\) is y-coordinate\(\Im(z)=\sin\theta\)
Angles wrap by \(2\pi\)Arguments wrap by \(2\pi\) as well
So the complex unit circle is not a different circle — it is the same unit circle, interpreted as numbers rather than just points.
DefinitionModulus, argument, and polar form▶

For \(z = a+bi\):

\[ \text{Modulus: } r = |z| = \sqrt{a^2+b^2} \]
\[ \text{Argument: } \theta = \arg(z) = \arctan\!\left(\frac{b}{a}\right) \quad \text{(adjusted for correct quadrant)} \]

The argument is the angle measured anticlockwise from the positive real axis, in the range \((-\pi, \pi]\) (called the principal argument).

Combining these gives polar form:

\[ z = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta) \]

Converting back: \(a = r\cos\theta\), \(b = r\sin\theta\). So:

\[ z = a+bi = r\cos\theta + ir\sin\theta = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta) \]
DerivationHow modulus and \(\arctan\) combine to give \(z=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)\)▶

This is the exact step students ask about: why do these two separate definitions suddenly combine?

1
Start with \(z=a+bi\), so the point is \((a,b)\) in the Argand plane.
2
Define modulus by distance from origin: \[ r=|z|=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}. \]
3
Define argument \(\theta\) as the angle from positive real axis. In the right triangle formed by \((a,b)\): \[ \cos\theta=\frac{a}{r},\qquad \sin\theta=\frac{b}{r}. \] (And \(\tan\theta=\frac{b}{a}\), with quadrant correction.)
4
Rearrange: \[ a=r\cos\theta,\qquad b=r\sin\theta. \]
5
Substitute into \(z=a+bi\): \[ z=r\cos\theta+i(r\sin\theta)=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta). \]
KEY IDEA \(\arctan\) gives the angle, modulus gives the radius, and trig resolves that radius back into real/imaginary components.
Important: \(\theta=\arctan(b/a)\) alone is not enough — you must place \(\theta\) in the correct quadrant before using \(\cos\theta,\sin\theta\).
DefinitionExponential form and Euler's identity▶

Once a number is in polar form, we introduce a shorter notation:

\[ e^{i\theta} \;:=\; \cos\theta+i\sin\theta \]

So polar form can be written as:

\[ z=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)=re^{i\theta} \]

This is called the exponential form of a complex number.

The most famous special case is \(\theta=\pi\):

\[ e^{i\pi}=\cos\pi+i\sin\pi=-1+0i=-1 \]
\[ \boxed{e^{i\pi}+1=0} \]
Euler's identity links the five fundamental constants \(e,\,i,\,\pi,\,1,\,0\) in one equation.
DerivationWhat does \(e^{i\theta}\) actually mean?▶

Students often ask: "How can \(e\) to an imaginary power become trig?" The key idea is that \(e^{i\theta}\) is a unit-circle point at angle \(\theta\).

1
For real exponents, \(e^x\) means repeated continuous growth on the real line.
2
For imaginary exponents, the value leaves the real line and moves into the complex plane.
3
Euler's formula defines that movement: \[ e^{i\theta}=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta. \] So modulus is \(1\), and argument is \(\theta\).
4
Therefore multiplying by \(e^{i\theta}\) rotates any complex number by \(\theta\): \[ z\mapsto ze^{i\theta}. \]

So what is \(e^i\)? Put \(\theta=1\) radian:

\[ e^i=e^{i\cdot1}=\cos(1)+i\sin(1)\approx 0.5403+0.8415i \]

That is the unit-circle point at angle 1 radian (about \(57.3^\circ\)).

Memory line: \(e^{i\theta}\) means "go to angle \(\theta\) on the unit circle".
About proof: a fully rigorous proof of \(e^{i\theta}=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta\) is usually done with power series (or an equivalent differential-equation argument). The geometric picture explains why it makes sense: \(e^{i\theta}\) behaves like a pure rotation by angle \(\theta\) with radius unchanged.

PatternFinding the argument — quadrant is everything▶

The formula \(\theta = \arctan(b/a)\) gives the right answer only in the first quadrant. For other quadrants, adjust:

DerivationWhy quadrant adjustment works (not memorisation)▶

This is the reason behind the rule table. We are not adding \(\pi\) randomly.

1
For \(z=a+bi\), the slope of the line from origin to \((a,b)\) is \[ \frac{b}{a}. \] So \(\tan\theta=\frac{b}{a}\).
2
\(\arctan(\cdot)\) returns only its principal output in \[ \left(-\frac{\pi}{2},\frac{\pi}{2}\right), \] which means it only gives a quadrant IV or quadrant I style angle.
3
But \(\tan\theta\) repeats every \(\pi\): \[ \tan(\theta)=\tan(\theta+\pi). \] So the same ratio \(b/a\) corresponds to two opposite-direction rays. We choose the one matching the signs of \(a\) and \(b\).
4
Sign check determines the quadrant:
  • \(a>0,b>0\): QI, keep \(\arctan(b/a)\).
  • \(a<0,b>0\): QII, add \(\pi\) to move from QIV-looking angle to QII.
  • \(a<0,b<0\): QIII, subtract \(\pi\) (or add \(\pi\), then convert to principal range).
  • \(a>0,b<0\): QIV, keep negative \(\arctan(b/a)\).
One-line meaning: \(\arctan(b/a)\) gives a reference direction from slope; quadrant signs tell you which opposite ray is the true complex-number direction.
Mini-example A (why add \(\pi\) in QII)
1
Take \(z=-1+i\), so \(a=-1,\ b=1\) (QII).
2
\(\arctan(b/a)=\arctan(-1)=-\pi/4\), which points to QIV, not QII.
3
Add \(\pi\): \(-\pi/4+\pi=3\pi/4\), now the angle points to QII with correct signs \((-,+)\).
Mini-example B (why subtract \(\pi\) in QIII for principal argument)
1
Take \(z=-1-i\), so \(a=-1,\ b=-1\) (QIII).
2
\(\arctan(b/a)=\arctan(1)=\pi/4\), which points to QI, not QIII.
3
Shift by \(\pi\): \(\pi/4+\pi=5\pi/4\). In principal range \((-\pi,\pi]\), this is \(-3\pi/4\), same direction, correct QIII ray.
Axis cases: if \(a=0\), do not use \(b/a\). Use geometry directly: \(b>0\Rightarrow \arg(z)=\pi/2\), \(b<0\Rightarrow \arg(z)=-\pi/2\). If \(b=0\), then angle is \(0\) (positive real axis) or \(\pi\) (negative real axis).

NoteKey facts about modulus and argument▶

When multiplying two complex numbers in polar form:

\[ |z_1 z_2| = |z_1||z_2| \qquad \arg(z_1 z_2) = \arg(z_1) + \arg(z_2) \]

Multiplication multiplies the moduli and adds the arguments. This is the geometric meaning of multiplication.

This is why multiplying by i rotates 90° anticlockwise — i has modulus 1 and argument π/2, so it adds π/2 to the argument of anything it multiplies.
Watch out: \(\arg(z)\) must be in the range \((-\pi,\pi]\). If your calculation gives an angle outside this range, add or subtract \(2\pi\) until it is inside.
Unit CircleThe unit circle in complex numbers (full detail)▶

On the complex plane, the unit circle is the set of points with modulus 1:

\[ |z|=1 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad z=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta \]

So every point on the unit circle is determined only by its angle \(\theta\). This is why angle becomes the central idea in complex multiplication.

Core interpretation: on the unit circle, the real part is \(\cos\theta\), the imaginary part is \(\sin\theta\). So trig exact values become complex-number coordinates.

Topic 7
De Moivre's Theorem
Raise complex numbers to powers and extract roots in a clean, geometric way.
Motivation: Cartesian multiplication gets long quickly for high powers. De Moivre turns repeated multiplication into a simple radius-angle rule.
DefinitionPower rule in polar form▶

If \(z=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)\), then for integer \(n\):

\[ z^n = r^n\left(\cos(n\theta)+i\sin(n\theta)\right) \]

In exponential form: \(z=re^{i\theta}\Rightarrow z^n=r^n e^{in\theta}\).

Meaning: powering multiplies the argument by \(n\) and raises modulus to \(n\).

Why this is true (derivation idea):

1
Multiply two polar numbers: \(r_1(\cos\alpha+i\sin\alpha)\cdot r_2(\cos\beta+i\sin\beta)\).
2
Using trig angle-addition identities, this becomes \(r_1r_2(\cos(\alpha+\beta)+i\sin(\alpha+\beta))\).
3
For \(z^n\), you multiply the same \(z\) by itself \(n\) times: modulus gets multiplied \(n\) times \(\to r^n\), and angle gets added \(n\) times \(\to n\theta\).
WorkedExample 1: \((1+i)^8\)▶

\(=16(\cos2\pi+i\sin2\pi)=16\)
ANSWER \(16\)
WorkedExample 2: \(\left(2\left(\cos\frac{\pi}{3}+i\sin\frac{\pi}{3}\right)\right)^5\)▶

Topic 8
Roots of Unity
Understand why \(z^n=1\) gives equally spaced points on the unit circle.
Motivation: roots of unity are central in polynomial solving, signal processing, and periodic systems.
DefinitionGeneral formula for \(n\)th roots of 1▶

Why this formula appears:

1
Write \(1\) in exponential form. Since one full rotation does not change direction, \(1=e^{i2\pi m}\) for any integer \(m\).
2
If \(z^n=1\), then \(z^n=e^{i2\pi m}\).
3
Take \(n\)th roots on both sides: \(z=e^{i\frac{2\pi m}{n}}\).
4
Different \(m\) values can give the same root because angles differing by \(2\pi\) are equivalent. So we only need one complete set: \(m=0,1,\dots,n-1\).
\[ z_k=e^{i\frac{2k\pi}{n}}=\cos\left(\frac{2k\pi}{n}\right)+i\sin\left(\frac{2k\pi}{n}\right),\quad k=0,1,\dots,n-1 \]

Now the spacing is clear: consecutive arguments differ by \(\frac{2\pi}{n}\), so the roots are equally spaced around the unit circle.

Key insight: there are exactly \(n\) distinct roots because after \(k=n\), angle \(\frac{2n\pi}{n}=2\pi\) returns to the same point as \(k=0\).

Angles: \(0,\frac{\pi}{3},\frac{2\pi}{3},\pi,\frac{4\pi}{3},\frac{5\pi}{3}\).

Roots:

\[ 1,\ \frac12+\frac{\sqrt3}{2}i,\ -\frac12+\frac{\sqrt3}{2}i,\ -1,\ -\frac12-\frac{\sqrt3}{2}i,\ \frac12-\frac{\sqrt3}{2}i \]
Check: product of all \(n\)th roots of unity is \((-1)^{n+1}\).

DefinitionHigh-frequency locus patterns▶
FormGeometry

Topic 10
Engineering Modeling with Complex Numbers
Use exponential form for phase, oscillations, and modeling workflows in mining and mechanical engineering.
Motivation: In mining and mechanical systems, vibration, rotating machinery, drill-string oscillations, and conveyor dynamics are phase-based. Complex numbers give a compact way to track amplitude and phase through these systems.
DefinitionPhasor form and phase operations▶
\[ z=Ae^{i\phi} \quad\Rightarrow\quad |z|=A,\ \arg(z)=\phi \]

Multiplication combines gain and phase shift:

\[ A_1e^{i\phi_1}\cdot A_2e^{i\phi_2}=(A_1A_2)e^{i(\phi_1+\phi_2)} \]

Division subtracts phase:

\[ \frac{A_1e^{i\phi_1}}{A_2e^{i\phi_2}}=\frac{A_1}{A_2}e^{i(\phi_1-\phi_2)} \]

Why multiply adds phase and divide subtracts phase:

1
Exponential law: \(e^{u}e^{v}=e^{u+v}\). So \(e^{i\phi_1}e^{i\phi_2}=e^{i(\phi_1+\phi_2)}\).
2
Hence the complex multipliers rotate by \(\phi_1\) then \(\phi_2\), total rotation \(\phi_1+\phi_2\).
3
For division, \(e^{i\phi_1}/e^{i\phi_2}=e^{i(\phi_1-\phi_2)}\), so you remove the denominator phase contribution.
WorkedExample: mining vibration transfer through two stages▶

A crusher-support stage has gain \(z_1=1.8e^{i15^\circ}\), and a foundation stage has gain \(z_2=0.6e^{-i10^\circ}\). Find overall response.

1
Multiply magnitudes: \(1.8\times0.6=1.08\).
2
Add phases: \(15^\circ+(-10^\circ)=5^\circ\).
RESULT \(z_1z_2=1.08e^{i5^\circ}\)
Interpretation: output vibration is amplified by \(8\%\) with a small positive phase lead of \(5^\circ\).

3
