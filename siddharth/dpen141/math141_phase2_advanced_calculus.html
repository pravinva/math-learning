Parametric Curves — Definition & First Derivatives
Parametric representation, dy/dx for parametric curves
Not every curve is the graph of a function $y=f(x)$ — a circle fails the vertical line test entirely. Parametric equations sidestep the problem by describing both $x$ and $y$ separately in terms of a third variable.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

The idea. Instead of $y=f(x)$, write $x=x(t)$ and $y=y(t)$, where $t$ is a parameter (often time). As $t$ varies, the point $(x(t),y(t))$ traces out the curve — this can loop, cross itself, or fail the vertical line test, none of which a single $y=f(x)$ could do.

Deriving $dy/dx$. By the chain rule, $\dfrac{dy}{dt}=\dfrac{dy}{dx}\cdot\dfrac{dx}{dt}$. Rearranging (dividing both sides by $dx/dt$, provided it's nonzero) gives a way to find the slope of the parametric curve without ever solving for $y$ in terms of $x$ directly.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}, \qquad \frac{dx}{dt}\ne0 \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — find dy/dx for a parametric circle

For $x=\cos t$, $y=\sin t$, find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$.

\[ \frac{dx}{dt}=-\sin t, \qquad \frac{dy}{dt}=\cos t \implies \frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{\cos t}{-\sin t}=-\cot t \]
ANSWER $-\cot t$
Example 2 — tangent line at a specific parameter value

For $x=t^2$, $y=t^3$, find the tangent line at $t=2$.

\[ \frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{3t^2}{2t}=\frac{3t}{2} \bigg|_{t=2}=3, \qquad (x,y)=(4,8) \]
ANSWER $y-8=3(x-4) \implies y=3x-4$
Note Study notes ▶
Uni Bridge
The vector line equation from Vectors ($\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{a}+\lambda\mathbf{b}$) is a special (straight) case of parametric curves; $\mathbf{r}(t)=(x(t),y(t))$ generalises the idea to curved paths, which is exactly what MATH142 does with curves in space.

Parametric Curves — Second Derivatives
d²y/dx² for parametric curves, concavity from parametric form
$\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ for a parametric curve is itself a function of $t$ — so finding its rate of change (the second derivative) means differentiating that new function with respect to $t$, then dividing by $dx/dt$ again, exactly the same trick as before.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

The key realisation. $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ is not found by simply differentiating $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ with respect to $t$ — that would give $\dfrac{d}{dt}\left(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\right)$, a rate with respect to the wrong variable. Since everything is parametrised by $t$, you need the chain rule again to convert back to a rate with respect to $x$.

1
Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ as a function of $t$, exactly as before.
2
Differentiate that expression with respect to $t$, giving $\dfrac{d}{dt}\left(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\right)$.
3
Divide by $dx/dt$ once more, to convert the rate back from "per unit $t$" to "per unit $x$".
Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{\dfrac{d}{dt}\left(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\right)}{dx/dt} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — full second derivative

For $x=t^2$, $y=t^3$, find $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$.

\[ \frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{3t}{2} \ (\text{from before}), \qquad \frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{3t}{2}\right)=\frac32 \]
\[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=\frac{3/2}{2t}=\frac{3}{4t} \]
ANSWER $\dfrac{3}{4t}$
Example 2 — concavity at a point

Is the curve from Example 1 concave up or down at $t=1$?

\[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\bigg|_{t=1}=\frac34>0 \]
ANSWER Concave up (positive second derivative)
Note Study notes ▶
Watch out
The most common error is dividing $\dfrac{d}{dt}(dy/dx)$ by $dt$ instead of by $dx/dt$ — remember you're converting a $t$-rate back into an $x$-rate, so you always divide by $dx/dt$ again, never just "cancel a $dt$."

Related Rates
Setting up and solving related rates problems
Two quantities can be linked by a fixed geometric or physical equation while both change with time — related rates is the technique for finding how fast one changes given how fast the other does, without ever solving for either quantity explicitly as a function of time.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

The strategy, as a fixed procedure:

1
Draw a diagram and label all changing quantities with variables (not numbers yet).
2
Write down an equation relating the variables — usually geometry (Pythagoras, similar triangles, volume/area formulas).
3
Differentiate both sides with respect to time $t$, using the chain rule on every variable (each picks up a $\dfrac{d(\cdot)}{dt}$ term).
4
Only now substitute the specific numerical values given in the problem, and solve for the unknown rate.
Critical order: never substitute specific numbers before differentiating — differentiating a number gives zero, destroying the relationship you need.
Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \text{Relate variables} \implies \frac{d}{dt}(\text{equation}) \implies \text{substitute numbers last} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — expanding circle

A circular ripple expands so its radius grows at 3 cm/s. How fast is the area growing when $r=5$cm?

\[ A=\pi r^2 \implies \frac{dA}{dt}=2\pi r\frac{dr}{dt} = 2\pi(5)(3)=30\pi \]
ANSWER $30\pi\ \text{cm}^2\text{/s}$
Example 2 — ladder sliding down a wall

A 10m ladder leans against a wall; the base slides away at 2 m/s. How fast is the top sliding down when the base is 6m from the wall?

Let $x$=base distance, $y$=height. $x^2+y^2=100$.

\[ 2x\frac{dx}{dt}+2y\frac{dy}{dt}=0 \]

At $x=6$: $y=\sqrt{100-36}=8$.

\[ 2(6)(2)+2(8)\frac{dy}{dt}=0 \implies \frac{dy}{dt}=-\frac{24}{16}=-1.5 \]
ANSWER Top slides down at 1.5 m/s (negative sign = decreasing height)
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
The sign of your answer always carries meaning — negative means the quantity is decreasing. If a "distance" or "height" rate comes out negative, that's not necessarily wrong; check what it means physically before assuming an error.

Second Derivative Test & Concavity
Concavity, inflection points, the Second Derivative Test
The First Derivative Test classifies critical points by checking a sign change either side — useful, but it means checking two extra points. The second derivative offers a shortcut: it directly measures whether the curve bends upward or downward right at the critical point itself.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Concavity. $f''(x)>0$ means the curve bends upward (like a smile, "concave up"); $f''(x)<0$ means it bends downward ("concave down"). This comes directly from $f''$ being the rate of change of the slope $f'$: a positive rate of change means the slope is increasing, which is exactly what "curving upward" looks like.

The Second Derivative Test. At a critical point where $f'(c)=0$: if $f''(c)>0$, the curve is concave up there, so it's a local minimum; if $f''(c)<0$, concave down, so a local maximum. If $f''(c)=0$, the test is inconclusive — fall back to the First Derivative Test.

Inflection points. Where concavity switches sign — candidates are where $f''(x)=0$ or is undefined, but (just like critical points) you must confirm an actual sign change either side, not just that $f''=0$.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ f'(c)=0,\ f''(c)>0 \implies \text{local min} \qquad f'(c)=0,\ f''(c)<0 \implies \text{local max} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — Second Derivative Test

Classify the critical points of $f(x)=x^3-3x$ using the Second Derivative Test.

\[ f'(x)=3x^2-3=0 \implies x=\pm1, \qquad f''(x)=6x \]
\[ f''(1)=6>0 \ (\text{local min}), \qquad f''(-1)=-6<0 \ (\text{local max}) \]
ANSWER Local max at $x=-1$, local min at $x=1$ (matches the earlier First Derivative Test result)
Example 2 — find an inflection point

Find the inflection point of $f(x)=x^3-3x^2+2$.

\[ f''(x)=6x-6=0 \implies x=1 \]

Check sign change: $f''(0)=-6<0$, $f''(2)=6>0$ — genuine sign change.

ANSWER Inflection point at $x=1$ (i.e. $(1,0)$)
Note Study notes ▶
Watch out
$f''(c)=0$ does not automatically mean an inflection point — check $f(x)=x^4$ at $x=0$: $f''(0)=0$, but the curve is concave up on both sides, so there's no sign change and no inflection.

Curve Sketching — Asymptotes, Discontinuities & Inflection
Vertical/horizontal asymptotes, discontinuities, inflection points as sketching features
Before attempting a full sketch, it helps to catalogue every "special" feature of a curve individually — the places it breaks, flattens, bends, or heads off toward infinity — since a sketch is really just these features stitched together.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Vertical asymptotes. Occur where the function is undefined but the limit blows up — typically where a denominator hits zero while the numerator doesn't. Check $\lim_{x\to a^\pm}f(x)=\pm\infty$.

Horizontal asymptotes. Given by $\lim_{x\to\pm\infty}f(x)$, using the limit-at-infinity techniques from Week 2.

Discontinuities. Reuse the classification from Week 2 (removable, jump, infinite) — infinite discontinuities are exactly the vertical asymptotes above.

Inflection points. Reuse the Second Derivative Test machinery just built: solve $f''(x)=0$, then confirm an actual sign change.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \text{Vertical asymptote: } \lim_{x\to a^\pm}f(x)=\pm\infty \qquad \text{Horizontal asymptote: } \lim_{x\to\pm\infty}f(x)=L \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — find all asymptotes

Find the asymptotes of $f(x)=\dfrac{2x^2}{x^2-4}$.

Vertical: denominator zero at $x=\pm2$ (numerator nonzero there) $\implies$ vertical asymptotes $x=2,x=-2$.

\[ \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{2x^2}{x^2-4} = \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{2}{1-4/x^2}=2 \]
ANSWER Vertical: $x=\pm2$; Horizontal: $y=2$
Example 2 — inflection points of a rational-free function

Find the inflection points of $f(x)=xe^{-x}$ (given $f''(x)=(x-2)e^{-x}$).

\[ f''(x)=0 \implies x=2 \ \ (\text{since } e^{-x}\ne0) \]

Sign check: $f''(0)=-2<0$, $f''(3)=e^{-3}>0$ — genuine sign change.

ANSWER Inflection point at $x=2$
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
Always simplify a rational function (cancel common factors) before hunting for vertical asymptotes — a factor that cancels indicates a removable discontinuity (a hole), not an asymptote, even though the original denominator was zero there.

Curve Sketching — Full Workflow
Combining every technique into a complete sketch
A full curve sketch is not a new technique — it's a checklist that runs every earlier tool (domain, intercepts, asymptotes, first/second derivative tests) in a fixed order, so nothing gets missed.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶
1
Domain: where is the function even defined?
2
Intercepts: set $x=0$ for the $y$-intercept, $f(x)=0$ for $x$-intercepts.
3
Symmetry: is $f$ even ($f(-x)=f(x)$), odd ($f(-x)=-f(x)$), or neither?
4
Asymptotes: vertical and horizontal, from the previous topic.
5
First derivative: find critical points, determine increasing/decreasing intervals.
6
Second derivative: find concavity intervals and inflection points.
7
Assemble: plot the key points and features found, then connect consistently with the increasing/decreasing and concavity information.
Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \text{Domain} \to \text{Intercepts} \to \text{Symmetry} \to \text{Asymptotes} \to f' \to f'' \to \text{Sketch} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — full sketch checklist, executed

Sketch $f(x)=x^3-3x$ using the full workflow.

  • Domain: all reals
  • Intercepts: $f(0)=0$; $x^3-3x=x(x^2-3)=0 \implies x=0,\pm\sqrt3$
  • Symmetry: $f(-x)=-x^3+3x=-f(x)$ — odd function
  • Asymptotes: none (polynomial)
  • $f'(x)=3x^2-3=0\implies x=\pm1$; increasing outside $(-1,1)$, decreasing inside
  • $f''(x)=6x=0\implies x=0$; concave down for $x<0$, up for $x>0$
ANSWER Local max $(-1,2)$, local min $(1,-2)$, inflection at $(0,0)$, odd symmetry about origin
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
Do the steps in order every single time, even for "easy-looking" functions — skipping symmetry or asymptote checks is exactly where marks get lost in exams, since an otherwise-correct sketch missing an asymptote is still wrong.

Complex Numbers — Definition & Operations
Definition, arithmetic operations, the complex plane
$x^2=-1$ has no solution among real numbers — rather than declaring the equation unsolvable, mathematicians extended the number system with a new number, $i$, defined precisely so that $i^2=-1$, and built a whole consistent arithmetic around it.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Definition. $i=\sqrt{-1}$, so $i^2=-1$. A complex number is $z=a+bi$, where $a$ (the real part, $\text{Re}(z)$) and $b$ (the imaginary part, $\text{Im}(z)$) are ordinary real numbers.

Arithmetic. Add/subtract by combining real and imaginary parts separately. Multiply using ordinary expansion, replacing $i^2$ with $-1$ wherever it appears. Divide by multiplying top and bottom by the complex conjugate $\bar z=a-bi$, which clears the imaginary part from the denominator (since $z\bar z=a^2+b^2$ is always real).

The complex plane. Plot $a+bi$ as the point $(a,b)$ — real part along the horizontal axis, imaginary part along the vertical. This turns complex arithmetic into a geometric picture: addition is vector addition, and (as you'll see next topic) multiplication is a rotation-and-scale.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ i^2=-1, \qquad z=a+bi, \qquad \bar z=a-bi, \qquad z\bar z=a^2+b^2 \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — multiply two complex numbers

Compute $(2+3i)(1-i)$.

\[ (2+3i)(1-i)=2-2i+3i-3i^2=2+i-3(-1)=5+i \]
ANSWER $5+i$
Example 2 — divide using the conjugate

Simplify $\dfrac{3+i}{1-2i}$.

\[ \frac{3+i}{1-2i}\cdot\frac{1+2i}{1+2i} = \frac{3+6i+i+2i^2}{1+4} = \frac{1+7i}{5} \]
ANSWER $\dfrac15+\dfrac75i$
Note Study notes ▶
Uni Bridge
The conjugate trick ($z\bar z$ is always real) is the complex-number cousin of the "rationalise the denominator" trick from Week 1's surds, and reappears constantly in MATH141/142 whenever a complex expression needs to be simplified to a real quantity.

Complex Numbers — Polar Form & De Moivre's Theorem
Modulus, argument, polar form, De Moivre's theorem
Cartesian form $a+bi$ is great for addition, but multiplication gets algebraically messy. Polar form describes the same point using a distance and an angle instead — and multiplication becomes almost embarrassingly simple.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Modulus and argument. $|z|=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$ is the distance from the origin (reusing the Pythagoras idea yet again). $\arg(z)=\theta$ is the angle from the positive real axis, found via $\tan\theta=b/a$ (with quadrant care).

Polar form. Since $a=|z|\cos\theta$ and $b=|z|\sin\theta$ (basic right-triangle trig), $z=|z|(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)$, often abbreviated $z=r\,\text{cis}\,\theta$.

Why multiplication simplifies. Multiplying two polar-form numbers and using the compound angle formulas for $\sin$ and $\cos$ reveals: multiply the moduli, add the arguments. Applying that rule $n$ times to $z^n$ gives De Moivre's Theorem directly.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ z=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta), \qquad z_1z_2=r_1r_2\big(\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2)+i\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2)\big) \]\[ z^n = r^n(\cos n\theta+i\sin n\theta) \quad \text{(De Moivre's Theorem)} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — convert to polar form

Write $z=1+i\sqrt3$ in polar form.

\[ r=\sqrt{1+3}=2, \qquad \tan\theta=\sqrt3 \implies \theta=\frac{\pi}{3} \ (\text{first quadrant}) \]
ANSWER $z=2\left(\cos\dfrac{\pi}{3}+i\sin\dfrac{\pi}{3}\right)$
Example 2 — De Moivre's Theorem

Find $(1+i)^8$ using De Moivre's Theorem.

\[ 1+i = \sqrt2\left(\cos\frac{\pi}{4}+i\sin\frac{\pi}{4}\right) \]
\[ (1+i)^8 = (\sqrt2)^8\left(\cos 2\pi+i\sin 2\pi\right) = 16(1+0i)=16 \]
ANSWER $16$
Note Study notes ▶
Uni Bridge
De Moivre's Theorem is the gateway to finding $n$-th roots of complex numbers, which then reappear when solving polynomial equations in engineering (e.g. characteristic equations of differential equations) — a topic MATH141/142 builds toward directly.

11
Find $d^2y/dx^2$ for $x=t, y=t^2$ (should match ordinary $y=x^2$).
W6
▶
Solution

$dy/dx=2t$, $\frac{d}{dt}(2t)=2$, $dx/dt=1$; $d^2y/dx^2=2$ — matches $y=x^2$'s constant concavity

12
Find $d^2y/dx^2$ for $x=2t, y=t^2-1$.
W6
▶
Solution

$dy/dx=t$, $\frac{d}{dt}(t)=1$, $dx/dt=2$; $d^2y/dx^2=1/2$

13
Find $d^2y/dx^2$ for $x=\cos t, y=\sin t$.
W6
▶
Solution

$dy/dx=-\cot t$; $\frac{d}{dt}(-\cot t)=\csc^2t$; $dx/dt=-\sin t$; $d^2y/dx^2=-\dfrac{\csc^2t}{\sin t}=-\dfrac{1}{\sin^3t}$

14
For $x=t^3, y=t^2$, find $d^2y/dx^2$ at $t=1$.
W6
▶
Solution

$dy/dx=2/(3t)$; $\frac{d}{dt}=-2/(3t^2)$; $dx/dt=3t^2$; $d^2y/dx^2=\dfrac{-2/(3t^2)}{3t^2}=\dfrac{-2}{9t^4}$; at $t=1$: $-2/9$

15
Is the curve $x=t, y=t^3$ concave up or down at $t=1$?
W6
▶
Solution

$dy/dx=3t^2$; $\frac{d}{dt}=6t$; $dx/dt=1$; $d^2y/dx^2=6t$; at $t=1$: $6>0$, concave up

16
Is the same curve ($x=t,y=t^3$) concave up or down at $t=-1$?
W6
▶
Solution

$d^2y/dx^2=6t=-6<0$ at $t=-1$ — concave down

17
Find where $x=t,y=t^3$ has an inflection point, using the previous two answers.
W6
▶
Solution

Since concavity switches sign between $t=-1$ and $t=1$ and $d^2y/dx^2=6t$, the switch is at $t=0$, i.e. the point $(0,0)$

18
Find $d^2y/dx^2$ for $x=e^t, y=e^{2t}$.
W6
▶
Solution

$dy/dx=2e^{2t}/e^t=2e^t$; $\frac{d}{dt}=2e^t$; $dx/dt=e^t$; $d^2y/dx^2=2$

19
For $x=t^2-1, y=t^3-t$, find $d^2y/dx^2$ in terms of $t$.
W6
▶
Solution

$dy/dx=\dfrac{3t^2-1}{2t}$; differentiate w.r.t. $t$ using quotient rule, then divide by $dx/dt=2t$ — gives $d^2y/dx^2=\dfrac{3t^2+1}{4t^3}$

Explain why a parametric curve can have a well-defined $d^2y/dx^2$ even at a point where the curve isn't the graph of a single-valued function nearby.

▶
Solution

The second derivative is computed purely from $t$-derivatives and the chain rule at each instant — it never requires solving for $y$ as an explicit function of $x$ globally, only locally where $dx/dt\ne0$, which is exactly where the curve behaves like a function momentarily

A cube's side grows at 2cm/s. Find the rate of volume growth when the side is 5cm.
W6
▶
Solution

$V=s^3$; $dV/dt=3s^2\,ds/dt=3(25)(2)=150\text{ cm}^3\text{/s}$

22
A spherical balloon is inflated so its radius grows at 0.5cm/s. Find $dV/dt$ when $r=4$cm.
W6
▶
Solution

$V=\tfrac43\pi r^3$; $dV/dt=4\pi r^2\,dr/dt=4\pi(16)(0.5)=32\pi\text{ cm}^3\text{/s}$

23
A point moves along $y=x^2$ so that $dx/dt=3$. Find $dy/dt$ when $x=2$.
W6
▶
Solution

$dy/dt=2x\,dx/dt=2(2)(3)=12$

24
Water drains from a conical tank (radius=height) at 2 m³/min. Find the rate the water level drops when the level is 3m.
W6
▶
Solution

$V=\tfrac13\pi r^2h=\tfrac13\pi h^3$ (since $r=h$); $dV/dt=\pi h^2\,dh/dt$; $-2=\pi(9)\,dh/dt \implies dh/dt=-\dfrac{2}{9\pi}$ m/min

25
Two cars leave an intersection, one heading north at 40km/h, one east at 30km/h. Find the rate the distance between them grows after 1 hour.
W6
▶
Solution

$D^2=x^2+y^2$; at $t=1$, $x=30,y=40,D=50$; $D\,dD/dt=x\,dx/dt+y\,dy/dt \implies 50\,dD/dt=30(30)+40(40)=2500 \implies dD/dt=50$ km/h

26
A shadow's length $s$ and a person's distance $x$ from a lamppost satisfy $s=\tfrac25x$ (similar triangles). If the person walks at 1.5 m/s, find how fast the shadow tip moves (rate of $s+x$).
W6
▶
Solution

$ds/dt=\tfrac25(1.5)=0.6$; shadow tip speed $=ds/dt+dx/dt=0.6+1.5=2.1$ m/s

27
The area of a square is increasing at 4 cm²/s. Find the rate the side length changes when the side is 6cm.
W6
▶
Solution

$A=s^2$; $dA/dt=2s\,ds/dt \implies 4=12\,ds/dt \implies ds/dt=\tfrac13$ cm/s

28
A rectangle's length grows at 3cm/s and width shrinks at 2cm/s. Find $dA/dt$ when length=10, width=5.
W6
▶
Solution

$A=lw$; $dA/dt=\dfrac{dl}{dt}w+l\dfrac{dw}{dt}=3(5)+10(-2)=15-20=-5\text{ cm}^2\text{/s}$

29
Air is pumped into a spherical balloon at 10 cm³/s. Find $dr/dt$ when $r=5$cm.
W6
▶
Solution

$dV/dt=4\pi r^2\,dr/dt \implies 10=4\pi(25)\,dr/dt \implies dr/dt=\dfrac{10}{100\pi}=\dfrac{1}{10\pi}$ cm/s

30
Explain, in terms of the four-step procedure, why substituting numbers too early in a related rates problem gives a wrong (usually zero) answer.
W6
▶
Solution

The equation relating the variables (step 2) must still be a genuine relationship between changing quantities when you differentiate (step 3) — if you plug in specific numeric values first, every 'variable' becomes a constant, and the derivative of a constant is zero, so the differentiation step produces no useful relationship between the rates at all

38

W7
▶
Solution

$f''(c)=0$ only tells you the curve is momentarily neither bending up nor down at that instant — it's consistent with a min, a max, an inflection point, or a flat saddle-like point, so you must fall back to checking the sign of $f'$ either side

Solution

No — as $x\to\infty$, $f(x)\to\infty$ (numerator degree exceeds denominator degree), so there's no finite horizontal asymptote (there is a slant asymptote $y=x$ instead)

50
Explain why a function can cross its own horizontal asymptote, but (typically) can never cross a vertical asymptote.
W7
▶
Solution

A horizontal asymptote only describes end behaviour as $x\to\pm\infty$ — nothing stops the function from equalling that value at some finite $x$ along the way. A vertical asymptote marks an $x$-value where the function is undefined (or blows up) entirely, so there's no point on the curve at that $x$ to 'cross' through
