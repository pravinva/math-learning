0 Why these topics?

The DPEN22 Calc 1 revision set already covers first principles, the power/product/quotient/chain rules, implicit and parametric differentiation, and the ordinary trig and exp/log derivatives, along with the standard applications (tangent lines, optimisation, curve sketching). That's the full DPEN22 toolkit.

MATH141's own Differentiation unit builds four more pieces on top of that foundation: a second family of functions built from $e^x$ (the hyperbolic functions), the general rule for differentiating any inverse function, the specific derivatives that rule produces for the inverse trig and inverse hyperbolic functions, and a technique, logarithmic differentiation, for handling products, quotients, and variable exponents that the ordinary rules can't reach directly. None of this needs new machinery, every derivation here leans entirely on the product, quotient, and chain rules already known from DPEN22.

1 Hyperbolic functions

The hyperbolic functions are built directly out of $e^x$, and despite the trig-sounding names ("hyperbolic sine," "hyperbolic cosine"), they are not angles at all, just specific combinations of exponentials that happen to satisfy an identity strikingly similar to $\sin^2+\cos^2=1$.

Key idea

$$\sinh x = \frac{e^x-e^{-x}}2, \qquad \cosh x = \frac{e^x+e^{-x}}2, \qquad \tanh x = \frac{\sinh x}{\cosh x} = \frac{e^x-e^{-x}}{e^x+e^{-x}}.$$

The defining identity: $$\cosh^2x - \sinh^2x = 1.$$

Why the identity holds

$$\cosh^2x-\sinh^2x = \left(\frac{e^x+e^{-x}}2\right)^2 - \left(\frac{e^x-e^{-x}}2\right)^2 = \frac{(e^{2x}+2+e^{-2x}) - (e^{2x}-2+e^{-2x})}4 = \frac44 = 1.$$

The $e^{2x}$ and $e^{-2x}$ terms cancel identically between the two squares, leaving only the $\pm2$, which is exactly where the $1$ on the right comes from.

Worked example

Evaluate $\cosh(0)$ and $\sinh(0)$, and verify $\cosh^2x-\sinh^2x=1$ at $x=\ln2$.

$\cosh(0) = \dfrac{e^0+e^0}2 = \dfrac{1+1}2=1$. $\ \sinh(0) = \dfrac{e^0-e^0}2 = 0$.

At $x=\ln2$: $e^x=2$, $e^{-x}=\dfrac12$. $\ \sinh(\ln2) = \dfrac{2-0.5}2 = 0.75$. $\ \cosh(\ln2) = \dfrac{2+0.5}2=1.25$.

$\cosh^2-\sinh^2 = 1.25^2-0.75^2 = 1.5625-0.5625 = 1$ ✓.

Practice 1

Evaluate $\sinh(0)$ and $\cosh(0)$ directly from the definitions (without looking back).

Solution

$\sinh(0) = \dfrac{e^0-e^0}2 = 0$. $\ \cosh(0) = \dfrac{e^0+e^0}2 = 1$.

Practice 2

Write $\tanh x$ purely in terms of $e^x$ and $e^{-x}$.

Solution

$$\tanh x = \frac{\sinh x}{\cosh x} = \frac{e^x-e^{-x}}{e^x+e^{-x}}.$$

Practice 3

Show that $\cosh x+\sinh x = e^x$.

Solution

$$\cosh x+\sinh x = \frac{e^x+e^{-x}}2+\frac{e^x-e^{-x}}2 = \frac{2e^x}2 = e^x.$$

Practice 4

Show that $\cosh x-\sinh x = e^{-x}$.

Solution

$$\cosh x-\sinh x = \frac{e^x+e^{-x}}2-\frac{e^x-e^{-x}}2 = \frac{2e^{-x}}2 = e^{-x}.$$

Practice 5

Evaluate $\cosh(\ln3)$ and $\sinh(\ln3)$.

Solution

$e^{\ln3}=3$, $\ e^{-\ln3}=\dfrac13$. $\ \cosh(\ln3) = \dfrac{3+1/3}2 = \dfrac{10/3}2=\dfrac53$. $\ \sinh(\ln3) = \dfrac{3-1/3}2 = \dfrac{8/3}2=\dfrac43$.

2 Derivatives of hyperbolic functions

Since $\sinh x$ and $\cosh x$ are just combinations of $e^x$ and $e^{-x}$, and both of those differentiate to themselves (up to a sign), their derivatives fall out immediately, and land on each other in a pattern that echoes $\sin$ and $\cos$ closely, but without the usual minus sign.

Key idea

$$\frac{d}{dx}\sinh x = \cosh x, \qquad \frac{d}{dx}\cosh x = \sinh x, \qquad \frac{d}{dx}\tanh x = \operatorname{sech}^2x = \frac1{\cosh^2x}.$$

Notice there is no minus sign anywhere, unlike $\dfrac{d}{dx}\cos x=-\sin x$: differentiating $\cosh x$ gives back $\sinh x$ directly, not $-\sinh x$.

Why no minus sign appears

$\dfrac{d}{dx}\sinh x = \dfrac{d}{dx}\left[\dfrac{e^x-e^{-x}}2\right] = \dfrac{e^x-(-e^{-x})}2 = \dfrac{e^x+e^{-x}}2 = \cosh x$ (the derivative of $-e^{-x}$ picks up an extra minus from the chain rule, which then cancels the minus already sitting in front of $e^{-x}$). The $\tanh x$ derivative follows from the quotient rule: $\dfrac{d}{dx}\left[\dfrac{\sinh x}{\cosh x}\right] = \dfrac{\cosh x\cdot\cosh x - \sinh x\cdot\sinh x}{\cosh^2x} = \dfrac{\cosh^2x-\sinh^2x}{\cosh^2x} = \dfrac1{\cosh^2x}$, using Section 1's identity directly in the numerator.

Worked example

Differentiate $y=\sinh(3x)$ and $y=\cosh^2x$.

$y=\sinh(3x)$: chain rule, inner derivative $3$: $\ y' = 3\cosh(3x)$.

$y=\cosh^2x$: chain rule, $u=\cosh x$: $\ y' = 2\cosh x\cdot\sinh x$.

Using the double-angle-style identity $2\sinh x\cosh x = \sinh(2x)$ (provable the same way as Section 1's identity), this simplifies to $y'=\sinh(2x)$.

Practice 6

Differentiate $y=\cosh(4x)$.

Solution

$y' = 4\sinh(4x)$.

Practice 7

Differentiate $y=\tanh(2x)$.

Solution

$y' = 2\operatorname{sech}^2(2x)$.

Practice 8

Differentiate $y=x\sinh x$.

Solution

Product rule: $y' = \sinh x + x\cosh x$.

Practice 9

Differentiate $y=\sinh^3x$.

Solution

Chain rule: $y' = 3\sinh^2x\cosh x$.

Practice 10

Differentiate $y=e^x\cosh x$, and simplify using Practice 3.

Solution

Product rule: $y' = e^x\cosh x + e^x\sinh x = e^x(\cosh x+\sinh x)$.

By Practice 3, $\cosh x+\sinh x=e^x$, so $y' = e^x\cdot e^x = e^{2x}$, a clean simplification straight from the identity.

Practice 11

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ for $y=\ln(\cosh x)$.

Solution

Chain rule ($f'/f$ pattern): $y' = \dfrac{\sinh x}{\cosh x} = \tanh x$.

3 The derivative of an inverse function

Before reaching the inverse trig and inverse hyperbolic derivatives, it's worth having the general rule they both come from. If $y=f(x)$ has an inverse, $x=f^{-1}(y)$, there is a direct relationship between how fast $y$ changes with $x$ and how fast $x$ changes with $y$, they're reciprocals of each other.

Key idea

If $y=f(x)$ and $x=f^{-1}(y)$, then, wherever $f'(x)\neq0$:

$$\big(f^{-1}\big)'(y) = \frac1{f'(x)}, \qquad \text{equivalently} \qquad \frac{dx}{dy} = \frac1{dy/dx}.$$

Why this holds

Start from the defining property of an inverse: $f^{-1}(f(x))=x$ for every $x$. Differentiate both sides with respect to $x$, using the chain rule on the left: $\big(f^{-1}\big)'(f(x))\cdot f'(x) = 1$. Dividing through by $f'(x)$ (assuming it's nonzero) gives $\big(f^{-1}\big)'(f(x)) = \dfrac1{f'(x)}$, exactly the stated formula, with $y=f(x)$.

Worked example

Given $f(x)=x^3+2$, find $\big(f^{-1}\big)'(3)$ without finding $f^{-1}$ explicitly.

Find the $x$ that gives $y=3$: $f(1) = 1+2=3$, so $f^{-1}(3)=1$.

$f'(x)=3x^2$, so $f'(1)=3$.

$$\big(f^{-1}\big)'(3) = \frac1{f'(1)} = \frac13.$$

Practice 12

Given $f(x)=x^5+x$, and noting $f(1)=2$, find $\big(f^{-1}\big)'(2)$.

Solution

$f'(x)=5x^4+1$, so $f'(1)=6$. $\ \big(f^{-1}\big)'(2) = \dfrac16$.

Practice 13

Given $f(x)=x^3$, and noting $f(2)=8$, find $\big(f^{-1}\big)'(8)$.

Solution

$f'(x)=3x^2$, so $f'(2)=12$. $\ \big(f^{-1}\big)'(8) = \dfrac1{12}$.

Practice 14

Explain why the formula cannot produce $\big(f^{-1}\big)'(0)$ for $f(x)=x^3$.

Solution

$f^{-1}(0)=0$ (since $f(0)=0$), and $f'(x)=3x^2$ gives $f'(0)=0$. The formula divides by $f'(x)$, so it breaks down exactly at $f'(0)=0$. This isn't a computational slip, it reflects something real: the inverse function $f^{-1}(y)=\sqrt[3]y$ genuinely has a vertical tangent at $y=0$, an infinite slope, which is exactly what "division by zero" is signalling here.

Practice 15

Given $f(x)=\sqrt x$ (for $x\ge0$), and noting $f(4)=2$, find $\big(f^{-1}\big)'(2)$, then check it against the known derivative of $f^{-1}(y)=y^2$.

Solution

$f'(x) = \dfrac1{2\sqrt x}$, so $f'(4) = \dfrac14$. $\ \big(f^{-1}\big)'(2) = \dfrac1{1/4} = 4$.

Check: $f^{-1}(y)=y^2$, so $\big(f^{-1}\big)'(y)=2y$, and at $y=2$: $2(2)=4$ ✓.

Practice 16

Given $f(x)=e^x$, and noting $f(0)=1$, find $\big(f^{-1}\big)'(1)$, then confirm it matches the known derivative of $\ln x$ at $x=1$.

Solution

$f'(x)=e^x$, so $f'(0)=1$. $\ \big(f^{-1}\big)'(1) = \dfrac11 = 1$.

Check: $f^{-1}(y)=\ln y$, and $\dfrac{d}{dy}\ln y = \dfrac1y$, at $y=1$: $\dfrac11=1$ ✓.

4 Derivatives of inverse trig functions

The inverse trig derivatives are the single most direct application of Section 3's formula: apply it to $\sin$, $\cos$, and $\tan$, and the strange-looking $\sqrt{1-x^2}$ and $1+x^2$ patterns fall straight out of the ordinary Pythagorean identity.

Key idea

$$\frac{d}{dx}\arcsin x = \frac1{\sqrt{1-x^2}}, \qquad \frac{d}{dx}\arccos x = -\frac1{\sqrt{1-x^2}}, \qquad \frac{d}{dx}\arctan x = \frac1{1+x^2}.$$

Deriving $\arcsin$ and $\arctan$ from Section 3

Let $y=\arcsin x$, so $x=\sin y$. By Section 3, $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac1{dx/dy} = \dfrac1{\cos y}$. Now rewrite $\cos y$ in terms of $x$: since $\cos^2y+\sin^2y=1$, $\cos y = \sqrt{1-\sin^2y}=\sqrt{1-x^2}$ (taking the positive root, since $\cos y\ge0$ for $y$ in $\arcsin$'s range $[-\frac{\pi}2,\frac{\pi}2]$). So $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac1{\sqrt{1-x^2}}$.

Let $y=\arctan x$, so $x=\tan y$. $\ \dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac1{dx/dy} = \dfrac1{\sec^2y}$. Since $\sec^2y = 1+\tan^2y = 1+x^2$: $\ \dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac1{1+x^2}$.

Worked example

Differentiate $y=\arcsin(3x)$ and $y=\arctan(x^2)$.

$y=\arcsin(3x)$: chain rule, inner derivative $3$: $\ y' = \dfrac3{\sqrt{1-9x^2}}$.

$y=\arctan(x^2)$: chain rule, inner derivative $2x$: $\ y' = \dfrac{2x}{1+x^4}$.

Practice 17

Differentiate $y=\arccos(2x)$.

Solution

$y' = -\dfrac2{\sqrt{1-4x^2}}$.

Practice 18

Differentiate $y=\arctan(5x)$.

Solution

$y' = \dfrac5{1+25x^2}$.

Practice 19

Differentiate $y=x\arcsin x$.

Solution

Product rule: $y' = \arcsin x + \dfrac{x}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}$.

Practice 20

Differentiate $y=\arcsin\left(\dfrac{x}2\right)$.

Solution

Chain rule with $u=\dfrac{x}2$, $u'=\dfrac12$: $y' = \dfrac{1/2}{\sqrt{1-x^2/4}} = \dfrac{1/2}{\sqrt{(4-x^2)/4}} = \dfrac{1/2}{\frac12\sqrt{4-x^2}} = \dfrac1{\sqrt{4-x^2}}$.

Practice 21

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ for $y=(\arctan x)^2$.

Solution

Chain rule: $y' = 2\arctan x\cdot\dfrac1{1+x^2} = \dfrac{2\arctan x}{1+x^2}$.

Practice 22

Differentiate $y=\arcsin x+\arccos x$, and explain the result.

Solution

$y' = \dfrac1{\sqrt{1-x^2}} + \left(-\dfrac1{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\right) = 0$.

This makes sense: $\arcsin x+\arccos x=\dfrac{\pi}2$ for every $x$ in the domain, a constant function, so its derivative has to be $0$ everywhere, exactly what the calculation confirms.

5 Inverse hyperbolic functions

Because the hyperbolic functions are built from $e^x$, their inverses can always be written out explicitly as logarithms, unlike the inverse trig functions, which have no simpler closed form. The derivatives follow the same Section 3 approach as before.

Key idea

$$\operatorname{arcsinh} x = \ln\!\left(x+\sqrt{x^2+1}\right), \qquad \operatorname{arccosh} x = \ln\!\left(x+\sqrt{x^2-1}\right)\ (x\ge1), \qquad \operatorname{arctanh} x = \frac12\ln\!\left(\frac{1+x}{1-x}\right)\ (-1

Their derivatives:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\operatorname{arcsinh} x = \frac1{\sqrt{x^2+1}}, \qquad \frac{d}{dx}\operatorname{arccosh} x = \frac1{\sqrt{x^2-1}}, \qquad \frac{d}{dx}\operatorname{arctanh} x = \frac1{1-x^2}.$$

Deriving the $\operatorname{arcsinh}$ derivative from Section 3

Let $y=\operatorname{arcsinh} x$, so $x=\sinh y$. By Section 3, $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac1{\cosh y}$. Since $\cosh^2y-\sinh^2y=1$ (Section 1), $\cosh y = \sqrt{\sinh^2y+1} = \sqrt{x^2+1}$ (no sign ambiguity here, since $\cosh y\ge1>0$ always). So $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac1{\sqrt{x^2+1}}$, matching the key idea.

Worked example

Differentiate $y=\operatorname{arcsinh}(2x)$, then verify $\dfrac{d}{dx}\operatorname{arcsinh} x = \dfrac1{\sqrt{x^2+1}}$ by differentiating the log form $\ln(x+\sqrt{x^2+1})$ directly.

$y=\operatorname{arcsinh}(2x)$: chain rule, inner derivative $2$: $\ y' = \dfrac2{\sqrt{4x^2+1}}$.

Direct check: $\dfrac{d}{dx}\ln\!\left(x+\sqrt{x^2+1}\right) = \dfrac{1+\dfrac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+1}}}{x+\sqrt{x^2+1}} = \dfrac{\dfrac{\sqrt{x^2+1}+x}{\sqrt{x^2+1}}}{x+\sqrt{x^2+1}} = \dfrac1{\sqrt{x^2+1}}$, exactly matching, with the $\left(x+\sqrt{x^2+1}\right)$ factor cancelling completely top and bottom.

Practice 23

Differentiate $y=\operatorname{arccosh}(3x)$.

Solution

$y' = \dfrac3{\sqrt{9x^2-1}}$.

Practice 24

Differentiate $y=\operatorname{arctanh}\left(\dfrac{x}2\right)$.

Solution

Chain rule with $u=\dfrac{x}2$, $u'=\dfrac12$: $y' = \dfrac{1/2}{1-x^2/4} = \dfrac{1/2}{(4-x^2)/4} = \dfrac2{4-x^2}$.

Practice 25

Evaluate $\operatorname{arcsinh}(0)$ directly from the log definition.

Solution

$\operatorname{arcsinh}(0) = \ln\!\left(0+\sqrt{0+1}\right) = \ln(1) = 0$.

Practice 26

Evaluate $\operatorname{arccosh}(1)$, and explain why the domain of $\operatorname{arccosh}$ requires $x\ge1$.

Solution

$\operatorname{arccosh}(1) = \ln\!\left(1+\sqrt{0}\right) = \ln(1)=0$.

The domain needs $x\ge1$ because $\cosh y$ (for real $y$) always has range $[1,\infty)$: $\cosh y=\dfrac{e^y+e^{-y}}2 \ge1$ for every real $y$ (this is exactly the AM-GM-style fact that $e^y+e^{-y}\ge2$). Since $\operatorname{arccosh}$ is the inverse of $\cosh$, it can only be defined for inputs actually in $\cosh$'s range.

Practice 27

Differentiate $y=x\operatorname{arctanh} x$.

Solution

Product rule: $y' = \operatorname{arctanh} x + \dfrac{x}{1-x^2}$.

6 Logarithmic differentiation

Every rule so far handles a function raised to a constant power ($x^2$) or a constant raised to a variable power ($2^x$). Neither the power rule nor the exponential rule can handle $y=x^x$, where both the base and the exponent depend on $x$ at once. Taking the logarithm of both sides first sidesteps the problem entirely, by turning the troublesome exponent into an ordinary product that the product rule can reach.

Key idea: the three-step method

1. Take $\ln$ of both sides of $y=f(x)$, and use log laws to break products into sums, quotients into differences, and powers into coefficients out front.

2. Differentiate both sides with respect to $x$. The left side, $\ln y$, differentiates to $\dfrac1y\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ by the chain rule (implicit differentiation, since $y$ is a function of $x$).

3. Multiply both sides by $y$ (i.e., by the original right-hand side $f(x)$) to isolate $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$.

Worked example

Differentiate $y=x^x$ ($x>0$), and $y=\dfrac{(x^2+1)^3(x-1)^5}{(2x+3)^2}$.

$y=x^x$: take logs: $\ln y = x\ln x$. Differentiate (product rule on the right, chain rule on the left): $\dfrac1y\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \ln x + x\cdot\dfrac1x = \ln x+1$.

Multiply by $y=x^x$: $$\frac{dy}{dx} = x^x(\ln x+1).$$

$y=\dfrac{(x^2+1)^3(x-1)^5}{(2x+3)^2}$: take logs, breaking the product/quotient/powers apart: $\ln y = 3\ln(x^2+1)+5\ln(x-1)-2\ln(2x+3)$.

Differentiate each term with the $f'/f$ pattern: $$\frac1y\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{6x}{x^2+1}+\frac5{x-1}-\frac4{2x+3}.$$

Multiply through by the original $y$: $$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{(x^2+1)^3(x-1)^5}{(2x+3)^2}\left(\frac{6x}{x^2+1}+\frac5{x-1}-\frac4{2x+3}\right).$$ This avoids ever having to expand the product/quotient rule for three factors directly, a substantial saving.

Practice 28

Differentiate $y=x^{\sin x}$ using logarithmic differentiation.

Solution

$\ln y = \sin x\ln x$. Differentiate (product rule on the right): $\dfrac1y y' = \cos x\ln x + \dfrac{\sin x}x$.

$$y' = x^{\sin x}\left(\cos x\ln x+\frac{\sin x}x\right).$$

Practice 29

Differentiate $y=(2x+1)^x$.

Solution

$\ln y = x\ln(2x+1)$. Differentiate: $\dfrac1y y' = \ln(2x+1) + x\cdot\dfrac2{2x+1}$.

$$y' = (2x+1)^x\left(\ln(2x+1)+\frac{2x}{2x+1}\right).$$

Practice 30

Use logarithmic differentiation to differentiate $y=\dfrac{\sqrt x\,(x+1)^4}{(x-2)^3}$.

Solution

$\ln y = \dfrac12\ln x+4\ln(x+1)-3\ln(x-2)$. Differentiate: $\dfrac1y y' = \dfrac1{2x}+\dfrac4{x+1}-\dfrac3{x-2}$.

$$y' = \frac{\sqrt x\,(x+1)^4}{(x-2)^3}\left(\frac1{2x}+\frac4{x+1}-\frac3{x-2}\right).$$

Practice 31

Differentiate $y=x^{x^2}$.

Solution

$\ln y = x^2\ln x$. Differentiate: $\dfrac1y y' = 2x\ln x + x^2\cdot\dfrac1x = 2x\ln x+x$.

$$y' = x^{x^2}(2x\ln x+x) = x^{x^2+1}(2\ln x+1).$$

Practice 32

Explain why logarithmic differentiation is necessary for $y=x^x$ but not for $y=x^2$ or $y=2^x$.

Solution

$y=x^2$ has a constant exponent, so the ordinary power rule applies directly. $y=2^x$ has a constant base, so the ordinary exponential rule ($\dfrac{d}{dx}a^x=a^x\ln a$) applies directly. $y=x^x$ has both the base and the exponent depending on $x$ simultaneously, so neither rule fits: there is no direct formula for "variable to the power of variable." Taking logs converts the troublesome exponent $x\ln x$ into an ordinary product on the right-hand side, which the product rule (already known) can differentiate without any new machinery.

Practice 33

Differentiate $y=(\ln x)^x$.

Solution

$\ln y = x\ln(\ln x)$. Differentiate (product rule; the second term needs the chain rule on $\ln(\ln x)$): $\dfrac1y y' = \ln(\ln x) + x\cdot\dfrac1{\ln x}\cdot\dfrac1x = \ln(\ln x)+\dfrac1{\ln x}$.

$$y' = (\ln x)^x\left(\ln(\ln x)+\frac1{\ln x}\right).$$
