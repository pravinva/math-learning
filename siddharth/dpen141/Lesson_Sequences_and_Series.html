1 Sequences: limits and convergence

A sequence is simply an ordered, infinite list of numbers, $a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots$, usually described by a formula for the general term $a_n$. A sequence converges if the terms settle down and approach a single finite number as $n\to\infty$; otherwise it diverges. This is exactly the same idea as a function limit at infinity, $\lim_{x\to\infty}f(x)$, just restricted to whole-number inputs, so every limit technique you already know (algebraic simplification, L'Hôpital's rule, the squeeze theorem) applies directly.

Key idea

$$\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n = L \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \text{the sequence converges to } L.$$

If no finite $L$ exists, either because $a_n\to\pm\infty$ or because the terms oscillate without settling, the sequence diverges. A useful sufficient condition worth knowing: a monotonic (always increasing, or always decreasing) sequence that is also bounded is guaranteed to converge, even if you can't immediately see what it converges to.

Worked example

Determine whether $a_n = \dfrac{2n+1}{n+3}$ converges, and find its limit if so.

Divide numerator and denominator by the highest power of $n$ present, $n^1$:

$$a_n = \frac{2+\frac1n}{1+\frac3n}.$$

As $n\to\infty$, $\dfrac1n\to0$ and $\dfrac3n\to0$, so $a_n \to \dfrac{2+0}{1+0} = 2$.

The sequence converges to $2$.

Practice 1

Determine whether $a_n = \dfrac{n}{n+1}$ converges, and find its limit if so.

Solution

Divide by $n$: $a_n = \dfrac1{1+\frac1n} \to \dfrac1{1+0}=1$. Converges to $1$.

Practice 2

Determine whether $a_n = (-1)^n$ converges.

Solution

The terms are $-1,1,-1,1,\dots$, forever alternating between two fixed values and never settling on a single number. Diverges (by oscillation, not by blowing up to infinity, a second way a sequence can fail to converge).

Practice 3

Determine whether $a_n = \dfrac{3n^2+1}{n^2+2n}$ converges, and find its limit if so.

Solution

Divide by $n^2$: $a_n = \dfrac{3+\frac1{n^2}}{1+\frac2n} \to \dfrac{3+0}{1+0}=3$. Converges to $3$.

Practice 4

Determine whether $a_n = \dfrac1{n!}$ converges, and find its limit if so.

Solution

$n!$ grows without bound as $n\to\infty$ (and grows far faster than any polynomial or exponential in $n$), so $\dfrac1{n!}\to0$. Converges to $0$.

Practice 5

Determine whether $a_n = \dfrac{n!}{n^n}$ converges, and find its limit if so.

Solution

Write out the fraction: $\dfrac{n!}{n^n} = \dfrac{n}n\cdot\dfrac{n-1}n\cdot\dfrac{n-2}n\cdots\dfrac1n$, a product of $n$ factors, every one of them at most $1$, and the very last factor is $\dfrac1n$, which shrinks to $0$. Squeezing between $0$ and $\dfrac1n$ (since every other factor is $\le1$): the whole product $\to0$. Converges to $0$.

Practice 6

Determine whether $a_n = \left(1+\dfrac1n\right)^n$ converges, and find its limit if so.

Solution

This converges to $e\approx2.71828$, one of the standard limit definitions of Euler's number. It's worth recognising this exact form on sight, since it resurfaces constantly (continuously-compounded interest is derived from precisely this limit).

Practice 7

Determine whether $a_n = \dfrac{\sin n}n$ converges, and find its limit if so.

Solution

$\sin n$ oscillates, but is always trapped between $-1$ and $1$, so $-\dfrac1n \le \dfrac{\sin n}n \le \dfrac1n$. Since both bounds $\to0$ as $n\to\infty$, the squeeze theorem forces $\dfrac{\sin n}n\to0$ as well. Converges to $0$, even though $\sin n$ itself never settles down, the shrinking denominator wins out.

Practice 8

A sequence is defined recursively by $a_1=1$, $a_{n+1}=\sqrt{2+a_n}$. Assuming this sequence converges (you can show it's increasing and bounded above by $2$), find its limit $L$.

Solution

If $a_n\to L$, then $a_{n+1}\to L$ as well (the same limit, just relabelled), so taking the limit of both sides of the recursive definition: $L = \sqrt{2+L}$.

Square both sides: $L^2 = 2+L \Rightarrow L^2-L-2=0 \Rightarrow (L-2)(L+1)=0 \Rightarrow L=2$ or $L=-1$.

Since every term of the sequence is positive (starting from $a_1=1$ and built from square roots), the limit must be non-negative, so $L=2$, not $L=-1$.

This "assume it converges, then solve for the limit algebraically" trick only works once convergence is separately justified (here, via the monotonic-bounded criterion from the key idea), it cannot prove convergence on its own, a divergent recursively-defined sequence could just as easily produce a spurious algebraic answer.

2 Series and partial sums

A series is what you get by trying to add up every term of a sequence: $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_n = a_1+a_2+a_3+\cdots$. Since you can never actually finish adding infinitely many numbers, "the sum of a series" is defined as a limit: add up the first $N$ terms (the $N$th partial sum $S_N$), and see what happens to $S_N$ as $N\to\infty$.

Key idea

$$S_N = \sum_{n=1}^{N}a_n, \qquad \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_n := \lim_{N\to\infty}S_N.$$

If this limit exists (is finite), the series converges and its value is that limit. If the limit is infinite or doesn't exist, the series diverges. This is genuinely just Section 1's sequence-convergence idea, applied to the specific sequence of partial sums rather than to the original terms $a_n$ directly.

Worked example

Compute the first four partial sums of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\dfrac1{2^n}$, and guess the value of the infinite sum from the pattern.

$S_0 = 1$. $\ S_1 = 1+\dfrac12 = \dfrac32$. $\ S_2 = \dfrac32+\dfrac14=\dfrac74$. $\ S_3 = \dfrac74+\dfrac18=\dfrac{15}8$.

The pattern: $S_N = \dfrac{2^{N+1}-1}{2^N} = 2-\dfrac1{2^N}$. As $N\to\infty$, $\dfrac1{2^N}\to0$, so $S_N\to2$.

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac1{2^n} = 2.$$

Section 3 gives a much faster way to reach this same answer, without needing to spot a partial-sum pattern by hand.

Practice 9

Compute $S_1, S_2, S_3, S_4$ for $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}n$, and determine whether the series converges.

Solution

$S_1=1,\ S_2=3,\ S_3=6,\ S_4=10$ (each partial sum growing by an increasing amount). In general $S_N=\dfrac{N(N+1)}2$, which $\to\infty$ as $N\to\infty$. Diverges.

Practice 10

A series has partial sums $S_N = \dfrac{3N}{N+1}$. Determine whether the series converges, and find its value if so.

Solution

$\displaystyle\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{3N}{N+1} = \lim_{N\to\infty}\frac3{1+1/N} = 3$. Converges to $3$.

Practice 11

You're told $S_N\to5$ as $N\to\infty$ for some series $\sum a_n$. What is the value of $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_n$?

Solution

By definition, the value of the series is the limit of its partial sums, so $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_n = 5$. There is nothing further to compute, this is the definition being applied directly, not a separate calculation.

Practice 12

If a series' terms satisfy $a_n\to0$ as $n\to\infty$, does that guarantee the series $\sum a_n$ converges? Give a reason for your answer (you don't need to prove it rigorously yet, Section 5 will).

Solution

No. The classic counterexample is the harmonic series $\sum\dfrac1n$: the individual terms $\dfrac1n\to0$, yet the series itself diverges (its partial sums grow without bound, just extremely slowly). Terms shrinking to zero is necessary for convergence (Section 4's divergence test relies on this direction), but it is not sufficient on its own, plenty of series with vanishing terms still diverge.

Practice 13

Compute $S_1,S_2,S_3$ for $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac3{4^n}$, and guess the pattern for $S_N$.

Solution

$S_1=\dfrac34$. $\ S_2=\dfrac34+\dfrac3{16}=\dfrac{15}{16}$. $\ S_3=\dfrac{15}{16}+\dfrac3{64}=\dfrac{63}{64}$.

Pattern: $S_N = 1-\dfrac1{4^N}$, approaching $1$ as $N\to\infty$ (confirmed directly via the geometric series formula in Section 3).

Practice 14

Explain, in your own words, why "the sum of infinitely many numbers" needs to be defined via a limit, rather than just being computed directly the way a finite sum is.

Solution

Ordinary addition is only ever defined for finitely many numbers at a time, there is no direct arithmetic operation for "add up infinitely many terms simultaneously." The partial-sum-limit definition sidesteps this by turning the problem into something calculus already knows how to handle: a sequence (of partial sums) and its limiting behaviour. This also explains why some infinite sums genuinely have no value at all (divergent series), the underlying limit simply fails to exist, exactly as an ordinary sequence can fail to converge.

3 Geometric series

A geometric series is one where each term is a fixed multiple $r$ (the common ratio) of the previous one: $a+ar+ar^2+ar^3+\cdots$. These are the single most important series to have a closed-form sum for, since they show up constantly (repeating decimals, compound processes, bouncing balls) and, unlike most series, can be summed exactly rather than merely classified as convergent or divergent.

Key idea

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}ar^n = \frac{a}{1-r}, \qquad \text{provided } |r|<1.$$

If $|r|\ge1$, the series diverges (the terms don't shrink toward zero fast enough, or at all, to ever settle on a finite sum).

Where the formula comes from

The $N$th partial sum is $S_N = a+ar+\cdots+ar^{N-1}$. Multiply both sides by $r$: $rS_N = ar+ar^2+\cdots+ar^N$. Subtracting, almost everything cancels: $S_N-rS_N = a-ar^N$, so $S_N(1-r)=a(1-r^N)$, giving $S_N = \dfrac{a(1-r^N)}{1-r}$. Now take $N\to\infty$: if $|r|<1$, then $r^N\to0$, leaving $S_N\to\dfrac{a}{1-r}$, exactly the key idea's formula. If $|r|\ge1$, $r^N$ does not shrink to $0$ (it either blows up or fails to settle), so $S_N$ has no finite limit, confirming the series diverges in that case.

Worked example

Find $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}3\left(\frac12\right)^n$.

This matches the geometric form with $a=3$, $r=\dfrac12$. Since $|r|=\dfrac12<1$, the series converges.

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}3\left(\frac12\right)^n = \frac{3}{1-\frac12} = \frac3{1/2} = 6.$$

Practice 15

Find $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\left(\frac23\right)^n$.

Solution

$a=1$, $r=\dfrac23$: $\displaystyle\sum = \frac1{1-\frac23} = \frac1{1/3}=3$.

Practice 16

Find $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}5\left(-\frac14\right)^n$.

Solution

$a=5$, $r=-\dfrac14$, $|r|<1$: $\displaystyle\sum = \frac5{1-(-1/4)} = \frac5{5/4} = 4$.

Practice 17

Find $\displaystyle\sum_{n=2}^{\infty}\left(\frac23\right)^n$ (note: the sum starts at $n=2$, not $n=0$).

Solution

Factor out the first two missing terms, or equivalently reindex so the sum starts at $0$: let $m=n-2$, so $n=m+2$ and the sum becomes $\displaystyle\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}\left(\frac23\right)^{m+2} = \left(\frac23\right)^2\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}\left(\frac23\right)^m = \frac49\cdot3 = \frac{12}9=\frac43$.

(Equivalently: take the full sum from Practice 15, which was $3$, and subtract off the missing $n=0$ and $n=1$ terms, $1$ and $\frac23$: $3-1-\frac23 = \frac43$, matching.)

Practice 18

Write the repeating decimal $0.777\ldots$ as a geometric series, and find its exact fractional value.

Solution

$0.777\ldots = 0.7+0.07+0.007+\cdots = \displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac7{10}\left(\frac1{10}\right)^n$, with $a=\dfrac7{10}$, $r=\dfrac1{10}$.

$$\sum = \frac{7/10}{1-1/10} = \frac{7/10}{9/10} = \frac79.$$

This is exactly the standard trick for converting any repeating decimal into a fraction, recognising it as a geometric series in disguise.

Practice 19

Does $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(1.2)^n$ converge? Explain.

Solution

No. Here $r=1.2$, and $|r|=1.2\ge1$, so the geometric series formula's convergence condition fails. The terms $(1.2)^n$ actually grow without bound as $n\to\infty$ rather than shrinking, so the partial sums grow without bound too. Diverges.

Practice 20

A ball is dropped from $10$ m and always bounces back up to $60\%$ of the height it fell from. Find the total vertical distance the ball travels (down and up, forever), using a geometric series.

Solution

The first drop contributes $10$ m. Every bounce afterward, the ball travels up some height and then back down the same height, so each bounce contributes twice its peak height. The bounce heights are $10(0.6),\ 10(0.6)^2,\ 10(0.6)^3,\dots$, a geometric sequence with $a=10(0.6)=6$, $r=0.6$.

Total distance from all the up-and-down bounces: $2\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}10(0.6)^n = 2\left(\frac{6}{1-0.6}\right) = 2(15)=30$ m.

Add the initial drop: total distance $=10+30=40$ m, a finite total distance despite the ball bouncing, in principle, infinitely many times.

Practice 21

Find $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\left[4\left(\frac13\right)^n - 2\left(\frac15\right)^n\right]$.

Solution

Split into two separate geometric series (each converges individually, since both $|r|$-values are less than $1$, so this splitting is valid):

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}4\left(\frac13\right)^n = \frac4{1-1/3}=6, \qquad \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}2\left(\frac15\right)^n = \frac2{1-1/5}=\frac52.$$

$$\text{Total: } 6-\frac52 = \frac72.$$

4 Telescoping series and the divergence test

A telescoping series is one where writing out the partial sum causes almost every term to cancel against a neighbour, collapsing down to just a couple of leftover pieces, a second family (alongside geometric series) that can be summed exactly rather than merely classified. Separately, the divergence test gives an extremely quick way to rule out convergence entirely, worth checking before reaching for any heavier machinery.

Key idea

Telescoping: if $a_n$ can be written as $b_n-b_{n+1}$ for some sequence $b_n$, then $S_N = \displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{N}(b_n-b_{n+1}) = b_1-b_{N+1}$ (every interior term cancels). If $b_{N+1}\to L$, the series converges to $b_1-L$.

Divergence test: if $\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n \neq 0$ (including if the limit doesn't exist at all), then $\sum a_n$ diverges, immediately, no further work needed. Note carefully what this test cannot do: if $a_n\to0$, the test is inconclusive, it tells you nothing (Practice 12's harmonic series is exactly this case).

Worked example

Find $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac1{n(n+1)}$.

Partial fractions: $\dfrac1{n(n+1)} = \dfrac1n-\dfrac1{n+1}$.

$$S_N = \sum_{n=1}^{N}\left(\frac1n-\frac1{n+1}\right) = \left(1-\frac12\right)+\left(\frac12-\frac13\right)+\cdots+\left(\frac1N-\frac1{N+1}\right).$$

Every middle term cancels against its neighbour, leaving $S_N = 1-\dfrac1{N+1}$.

As $N\to\infty$, $\dfrac1{N+1}\to0$, so $S_N\to1$.

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac1{n(n+1)} = 1.$$

Practice 22

Find $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac1{(n+1)(n+2)}$.

Solution

$\dfrac1{(n+1)(n+2)} = \dfrac1{n+1}-\dfrac1{n+2}$. $\ S_N = \dfrac1{2}-\dfrac1{N+2} \to \dfrac12$.

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac1{(n+1)(n+2)} = \frac12.$$

Practice 23

Find $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left(\dfrac1n-\dfrac1{n+2}\right)$ (note: the "gap" here is $2$, not $1$, so cancellation is slightly different from the worked example).

Solution

With a gap of $2$, each term cancels with one two positions later, not the very next one, so writing out enough terms:

$$S_N = \left(1-\frac13\right)+\left(\frac12-\frac14\right)+\left(\frac13-\frac15\right)+\cdots$$

The surviving (uncancelled) pieces are the first two positive terms ($1$ and $\frac12$) and the last two negative terms (which both $\to0$): $S_N \to 1+\dfrac12 = \dfrac32$.

Practice 24

Find $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac1{4n^2-1}$ (hint: factor the denominator first, then use partial fractions).

Solution

$4n^2-1 = (2n-1)(2n+1)$. Partial fractions: $\dfrac1{(2n-1)(2n+1)} = \dfrac12\left(\dfrac1{2n-1}-\dfrac1{2n+1}\right)$.

$$S_N = \frac12\sum_{n=1}^{N}\left(\frac1{2n-1}-\frac1{2n+1}\right) = \frac12\left(1-\frac1{2N+1}\right) \to \frac12.$$

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac1{4n^2-1} = \frac12.$$

Practice 25

Use the divergence test to show $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac{n}{n+1}$ diverges.

Solution

$\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n}{n+1} = 1 \neq 0$. By the divergence test, the series diverges immediately, there is no need to examine partial sums at all.

Practice 26

Use the divergence test to show $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(-1)^n$ diverges.

Solution

$\lim_{n\to\infty}(-1)^n$ doesn't exist at all (it oscillates between $-1$ and $1$ forever, exactly Practice 2's diverging sequence). Since the limit fails to equal $0$ (indeed, fails to exist as any single value), the divergence test applies: the series diverges.

Practice 27

Find $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{N}\ln\left(\dfrac{n}{n+1}\right)$ as an explicit formula in $N$ (telescoping using log properties), and determine whether the infinite series converges.

Solution

$\ln\left(\dfrac{n}{n+1}\right) = \ln n - \ln(n+1)$, so this telescopes exactly like the earlier examples:

$$S_N = \sum_{n=1}^{N}\big(\ln n - \ln(n+1)\big) = \ln1 - \ln(N+1) = -\ln(N+1).$$

As $N\to\infty$, $\ln(N+1)\to\infty$, so $S_N\to-\infty$. The series diverges (to $-\infty$), a useful reminder that telescoping doesn't automatically guarantee convergence, it only guarantees the partial sums simplify to something you can actually evaluate the limit of, which here turns out to be infinite.

Practice 28

Explain precisely why the divergence test can never be used to prove that a series converges, even when $a_n\to0$ is confirmed.

Solution

The divergence test is a one-directional statement: $a_n\not\to0$ guarantees divergence, but its logical converse ($a_n\to0$ guarantees convergence) simply isn't true, and Practice 12's harmonic series is the standard counterexample proving it false. When $a_n\to0$, the test has nothing further to say, the series might converge (like $\sum\frac1{n^2}$, established in Section 5) or might diverge (like $\sum\frac1n$), and distinguishing between these two outcomes requires an entirely different tool, which is exactly what Sections 5 through 8 provide.

5 The integral test and $p$-series

Most series can't be summed to an exact closed-form value the way geometric and telescoping series can, most of the time, the best you can hope to determine is whether a series converges, without ever finding the precise number it converges to. The integral test is the first genuinely general-purpose tool for this: it compares the series to a related improper integral, whose convergence is often much easier to determine.

Key idea

If $f(x)$ is positive, decreasing, and continuous for $x\ge1$, with $f(n)=a_n$, then $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_n$ and $\displaystyle\int_1^{\infty}f(x)\,dx$ either both converge or both diverge. (The test doesn't claim they equal the same value, only that they share the same convergence behaviour.)

The most important direct consequence is the $p$-series test: $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac1{n^p}$ converges if $p>1$, and diverges if $p\le1$. The harmonic series ($p=1$) sits right on the dividing line, and diverges.

Why the harmonic series diverges: the classic integral-test example

Take $f(x)=\dfrac1x$, positive, decreasing, continuous for $x\ge1$, matching $a_n=\dfrac1n$ exactly. Compute the improper integral:

$$\int_1^{\infty}\frac1x\,dx = \lim_{b\to\infty}\big[\ln x\big]_1^b = \lim_{b\to\infty}\big(\ln b-\ln1\big) = \lim_{b\to\infty}\ln b = \infty.$$

The integral diverges, so by the integral test, the harmonic series $\sum\frac1n$ diverges too, confirming, with a genuine proof this time, the claim used informally back in Practice 12.

Practice 29

Does $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac1{n^3}$ converge or diverge?

Solution

This is a $p$-series with $p=3>1$. Converges (though the $p$-series test doesn't reveal what value it converges to, only that a finite value exists).

Practice 30

Does $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac1{\sqrt n}$ converge or diverge?

Solution

$\dfrac1{\sqrt n} = \dfrac1{n^{1/2}}$, a $p$-series with $p=\dfrac12\le1$. Diverges.

Practice 31

Use the integral test to determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=2}^{\infty}\dfrac1{n\ln n}$ converges.

Solution

$f(x)=\dfrac1{x\ln x}$ is positive, decreasing, and continuous for $x\ge2$. Substituting $u=\ln x$, $du=\dfrac{dx}x$:

$$\int_2^{\infty}\frac{dx}{x\ln x} = \lim_{b\to\infty}\big[\ln(\ln x)\big]_2^b = \lim_{b\to\infty}\big(\ln(\ln b)-\ln(\ln2)\big) = \infty.$$

The integral diverges (very slowly, since $\ln(\ln b)\to\infty$ only extremely gradually, but it is unbounded), so by the integral test, the series diverges too.

Practice 32

Use the integral test to determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=2}^{\infty}\dfrac1{n(\ln n)^2}$ converges.

Solution

Same substitution, $u=\ln x$: $$\int_2^{\infty}\frac{dx}{x(\ln x)^2} = \lim_{b\to\infty}\left[-\frac1{\ln x}\right]_2^b = \lim_{b\to\infty}\left(-\frac1{\ln b}+\frac1{\ln2}\right) = \frac1{\ln2}.$$

A finite value, so the integral converges, and by the integral test, the series converges too. Notice how sensitive this family of series is: squaring the $\ln n$ in the denominator (Practice 31 vs.\ this one) flips the outcome from divergent to convergent, even though both series' terms shrink toward $0$.

Practice 33

Does $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac1{n^{1.5}}$ converge or diverge?

Solution

$p$-series with $p=1.5>1$. Converges.

Practice 34

Use the integral test to determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}ne^{-n^2}$ converges.

Solution

$f(x)=xe^{-x^2}$ is positive and continuous for $x\ge1$ (and can be checked to be decreasing there, since $e^{-x^2}$ shrinks faster than $x$ grows). Substituting $u=-x^2$, $du=-2x\,dx$:

$$\int_1^{\infty}xe^{-x^2}\,dx = \lim_{b\to\infty}\left[-\frac12e^{-x^2}\right]_1^b = 0-\left(-\frac12e^{-1}\right) = \frac1{2e}.$$

Finite, so the integral converges, and by the integral test, the series converges too.

Practice 35

State the three conditions $f(x)$ must satisfy for the integral test to apply, and explain briefly why the "decreasing" condition specifically is needed (what could go wrong without it?).

Solution

$f(x)$ must be positive, decreasing, and continuous on $[1,\infty)$ (or wherever the series starts).

The decreasing condition is what lets the integral genuinely bound the series terms from above and below: on each interval $[n,n+1]$, since $f$ is decreasing, $f(n+1)\le f(x)\le f(n)$ throughout, which is exactly what lets you sandwich $\int_n^{n+1}f(x)\,dx$ between consecutive series terms. Without monotonicity, $f$ could wiggle up and down between integer points in a way that makes the integral's value bear no reliable relationship to the sum of $f$ at just the integers, the comparison the whole test depends on would break down.

6 Comparison tests

Once you have a stock of series whose behaviour you already know (geometric series, $p$-series), comparison tests let you classify a new, more complicated series by comparing it to one of those known series, without ever computing an integral or a partial sum directly.

Key idea

Direct comparison: if $0\le a_n\le b_n$ for all (sufficiently large) $n$, then: $\sum b_n$ converges $\Rightarrow \sum a_n$ converges (the smaller series is squeezed under a finite ceiling); $\sum a_n$ diverges $\Rightarrow \sum b_n$ diverges (the larger series is dragged along by the smaller one blowing up).

Limit comparison: if $a_n,b_n>0$ and $\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{a_n}{b_n} = L$ for some finite $L>0$, then $\sum a_n$ and $\sum b_n$ either both converge or both diverge. This version is usually easier to apply than direct comparison, since you don't need to verify an inequality holds for every single $n$, just find the right comparison series and take one limit.

Why limit comparison works

If $\dfrac{a_n}{b_n}\to L$ with $L>0$ finite, then for large $n$, $a_n\approx Lb_n$, the two sequences are proportional to each other in the long run. Since a series' convergence only depends on the "tail" behaviour (finitely many early terms never affect whether an infinite sum ultimately converges), and multiplying every term of a series by the fixed positive constant $L$ never changes whether it converges, $\sum a_n$ behaves exactly like $\sum Lb_n$, which behaves exactly like $\sum b_n$. The key idea's rule is really just this "eventually proportional, so eventually the same convergence behaviour" observation, made rigorous.

Worked example

Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac1{n^2+1}$ converges, using direct comparison.

For every $n\ge1$: $n^2+1 > n^2$, so $\dfrac1{n^2+1} < \dfrac1{n^2}$.

$\sum\dfrac1{n^2}$ is a $p$-series with $p=2>1$, which converges (Section 5).

Since $0 < \dfrac1{n^2+1} < \dfrac1{n^2}$ and the larger series converges, the smaller series is squeezed under a finite total too.

By direct comparison, $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac1{n^2+1}$ converges.

Practice 36

Use direct comparison to determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac1{n^3+n}$ converges.

Solution

$n^3+n > n^3$ for all $n\ge1$, so $\dfrac1{n^3+n} < \dfrac1{n^3}$. Since $\sum\dfrac1{n^3}$ is a convergent $p$-series ($p=3>1$), and the given series is squeezed underneath it, the given series converges by direct comparison.

Practice 37

Use limit comparison (against $\dfrac1{n^2}$) to determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac{n+1}{n^3+2}$ converges.

Solution

$$\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{(n+1)/(n^3+2)}{1/n^2} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^2(n+1)}{n^3+2} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^3+n^2}{n^3+2} = 1.$$

Finite and positive. Since $\sum\dfrac1{n^2}$ converges ($p=2>1$), the given series converges too, by limit comparison.

Practice 38

Use direct comparison to determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac1{2^n+n}$ converges.

Solution

$2^n+n > 2^n$ for all $n\ge1$, so $\dfrac1{2^n+n} < \dfrac1{2^n}$. Since $\sum\dfrac1{2^n}$ is a convergent geometric series ($r=\dfrac12$, $|r|<1$), the given series converges by direct comparison.

Practice 39

Use limit comparison (against $\dfrac1{n^{3/2}}$) to determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac{\sqrt n}{n^2+1}$ converges.

Solution

$$\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\sqrt n/(n^2+1)}{1/n^{3/2}} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^{3/2}\sqrt n}{n^2+1} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^2}{n^2+1} = 1.$$

Finite and positive. $\sum\dfrac1{n^{3/2}}$ is a convergent $p$-series ($p=\frac32>1$), so the given series converges too.

Practice 40

Use limit comparison (against $\dfrac1n$) to determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=2}^{\infty}\dfrac1{n-\ln n}$ converges.

Solution

$$\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1/(n-\ln n)}{1/n} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n}{n-\ln n} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac1{1-\frac{\ln n}n} = 1$$

(using $\dfrac{\ln n}n\to0$). Finite and positive. Since $\sum\dfrac1n$ (the harmonic series) diverges, the given series diverges too, by limit comparison. This is a good example of a series that looks like it might behave better than the harmonic series (subtracting $\ln n$ seems like it should help), but limit comparison confirms it doesn't, in the long run $n-\ln n$ still grows essentially like $n$ itself.

Practice 41

Explain the practical difference between direct comparison and limit comparison, and when you'd reach for one over the other.

Solution

Direct comparison requires finding (and, strictly, proving) an actual inequality $a_n\le b_n$ that holds for every $n$ past some point, which can require some algebraic massaging to set up cleanly, as in the worked example's $n^2+1>n^2$. Limit comparison sidesteps needing an exact inequality at all, you only need the two series' terms to be eventually proportional, verified by a single limit computation, which is often far more mechanical (as Practice 37-40 show, mostly just dividing out the dominant powers of $n$). In practice, limit comparison is usually the faster tool once you've correctly guessed a comparison series, direct comparison is most useful when a clean inequality is already obvious by inspection, as in the worked example and Practice 36 and 38.

7 The ratio and root tests

Comparison tests need you to already have a good comparison series in mind. The ratio test and root test need no such guess, they work directly on the series' own terms, and are especially effective whenever factorials or $n$th powers are involved, exactly the situations where finding a clean comparison series is hardest.

Key idea

Ratio test: let $L=\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|$.

Root test: let $L=\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\sqrt[n]{|a_n|}$.

For either test: if $L<1$, the series converges (absolutely); if $L>1$ (including $L=\infty$), the series diverges; if $L=1$, the test is inconclusive, telling you nothing, and you must try a different method entirely.

Why $L<1$ guarantees convergence

If $\left|\dfrac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|\to L<1$, then for large $n$, consecutive terms are shrinking by roughly a factor of $L$ each step, exactly the behaviour of a geometric series with common ratio $L$. More precisely, once $n$ is large enough that the ratio is trapped below some fixed value $r$ with $L

Worked example

Use the ratio test on $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac{n}{2^n}$.

$$\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \frac{(n+1)/2^{n+1}}{n/2^n} = \frac{n+1}n\cdot\frac{2^n}{2^{n+1}} = \frac{n+1}{2n}.$$

$$L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n+1}{2n} = \frac12.$$

Since $L=\dfrac12<1$, the series converges by the ratio test.

Practice 42

Use the ratio test on $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac{3^n}{n!}$.

Solution

$$\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \frac{3^{n+1}/(n+1)!}{3^n/n!} = 3\cdot\frac{n!}{(n+1)!} = \frac3{n+1}.$$

$$L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac3{n+1} = 0 < 1.$$

Converges. This is a general pattern worth remembering: whenever $n!$ appears in the denominator (growing far faster than any fixed exponential base), the ratio test's $L$ collapses all the way to $0$, guaranteeing convergence essentially automatically.

Practice 43

Use the ratio test on $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac{n!}{n^n}$.

Solution

$$\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \frac{(n+1)!/(n+1)^{n+1}}{n!/n^n} = \frac{(n+1)\cdot n!}{n!}\cdot\frac{n^n}{(n+1)^{n+1}} = \frac{n^n}{(n+1)^n} = \left(\frac{n}{n+1}\right)^n.$$

$$L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left(\frac{n}{n+1}\right)^n = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left(1+\frac1n\right)^{-n} = \frac1e \approx 0.368.$$

(Recognising $\left(\dfrac{n+1}n\right)^n = \left(1+\dfrac1n\right)^n \to e$ from Practice 6, and this expression is its reciprocal.) Since $L=\dfrac1e<1$, the series converges.

Practice 44

Use the ratio test on $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac{n^2}{3^n}$.

Solution

$$\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \frac{(n+1)^2/3^{n+1}}{n^2/3^n} = \frac{(n+1)^2}{n^2}\cdot\frac13.$$

$$L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{(n+1)^2}{3n^2} = \frac13 < 1.$$

Converges.

Practice 45

Use the ratio test on $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac{(n!)^2}{(2n)!}$.

Solution

$$\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \frac{\big((n+1)!\big)^2/(2n+2)!}{(n!)^2/(2n)!} = \frac{(n+1)^2(n!)^2}{(n!)^2}\cdot\frac{(2n)!}{(2n+2)!} = \frac{(n+1)^2}{(2n+2)(2n+1)}.$$

$$L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{(n+1)^2}{(2n+2)(2n+1)} = \frac14 < 1.$$

Converges.

Practice 46

Use the root test on $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left(\dfrac{2n}{3n+1}\right)^n$.

Solution

The root test is the natural choice here, since $a_n$ is already an $n$th power, taking the $n$th root simplifies it immediately:

$$\sqrt[n]{|a_n|} = \frac{2n}{3n+1}.$$

$$L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2n}{3n+1} = \frac23 < 1.$$

Converges. Whenever a series' general term is itself raised to the $n$th power, that's an immediate signal to reach for the root test rather than the ratio test, since the root test removes the power in a single step, exactly the kind of structural cue worth training yourself to notice.

Practice 47

Apply the ratio test to $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac1{n^2}$, and explain what the result does and doesn't tell you, given that you already know (Section 5) this series converges.

Solution

$$\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \frac{1/(n+1)^2}{1/n^2} = \frac{n^2}{(n+1)^2}.$$

$$L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^2}{(n+1)^2} = 1.$$

$L=1$: the ratio test is inconclusive here, it neither confirms nor denies convergence. This does not contradict the fact (established in Section 5 via the $p$-series test, $p=2>1$) that the series does converge, it simply means the ratio test was the wrong tool to reach for on this particular series. This is a genuinely important limitation to internalise: getting $L=1$ is not itself a result, it's a sign to abandon the ratio test and pick a different method, exactly as happened here.

8 Alternating series and absolute convergence

Every test so far (except the divergence test) assumed positive terms. An alternating series, where the sign flips every term, needs its own dedicated test, and also raises a distinction that doesn't exist for positive-term series at all: a series can converge only because of cancellation between positive and negative terms, a strictly weaker kind of convergence than when the series would still converge even with every term made positive.

Key idea

Alternating series test (AST): if $b_n>0$, $b_n$ is eventually decreasing, and $b_n\to0$, then $\displaystyle\sum(-1)^nb_n$ converges.

$\sum a_n$ converges absolutely if $\sum|a_n|$ converges (the series would still converge even without any sign cancellation helping it along). $\sum a_n$ converges conditionally if $\sum a_n$ converges but $\sum|a_n|$ diverges (convergence here genuinely depends on the alternating signs providing cancellation). Absolute convergence is the stronger property: it always implies ordinary convergence, but not the reverse.

Worked example

Show that the alternating harmonic series $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac{(-1)^{n+1}}n$ converges, and determine whether it converges absolutely or only conditionally.

Apply the AST with $b_n=\dfrac1n$: $b_n>0$ ✓, $b_n$ is decreasing ✓, $b_n\to0$ ✓. All three conditions hold, so the series converges.

Now check absolute convergence: $\displaystyle\sum\left|\frac{(-1)^{n+1}}n\right| = \sum\frac1n$, the ordinary harmonic series, which diverges (Section 5).

The series converges, but not absolutely, so it converges conditionally. This is the single most famous example of conditional convergence: removing the alternating signs (taking absolute values) destroys convergence entirely, the cancellation between positive and negative terms was doing genuine, load-bearing work.

Practice 48

Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac{(-1)^n}{n^2}$ converges absolutely, conditionally, or diverges.

Solution

$\displaystyle\sum\left|\frac{(-1)^n}{n^2}\right| = \sum\frac1{n^2}$, a convergent $p$-series ($p=2>1$). Since the absolute-value series converges, the original series converges absolutely (and therefore converges).

Practice 49

Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(-1)^n\dfrac{n}{n+1}$ converges.

Solution

Check the divergence test first, always worth trying before AST: $\dfrac{n}{n+1}\to1\neq0$, so the overall term $(-1)^n\dfrac{n}{n+1}$ does not tend to $0$ (it oscillates between values approaching $1$ and $-1$). By the divergence test, this series diverges. (The AST cannot even be attempted here, since $b_n=\dfrac{n}{n+1}$ fails the "$b_n\to0$" requirement outright.)

Practice 50

Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac{(-1)^n}{\sqrt n}$ converges absolutely, conditionally, or diverges.

Solution

AST with $b_n=\dfrac1{\sqrt n}$: positive ✓, decreasing ✓, $\to0$ ✓. Converges.

Absolute series: $\displaystyle\sum\frac1{\sqrt n}$, a $p$-series with $p=\frac12\le1$, which diverges (Practice 30).

Converges, but not absolutely: converges conditionally.

Practice 51

Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(-1)^n\dfrac{n}{n^2+1}$ converges absolutely, conditionally, or diverges.

Solution

AST with $b_n=\dfrac{n}{n^2+1}$: positive ✓, $b_n\to0$ ✓ (numerator degree $1$, denominator degree $2$), and decreasing for $n\ge1$ (can be checked, or simply trusted from the shape, degree-$2$ denominator eventually dominates). Converges.

Absolute series: $\displaystyle\sum\frac{n}{n^2+1}$. Limit comparison against $\dfrac1n$: $\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n/(n^2+1)}{1/n} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^2}{n^2+1}=1$, finite and positive, and $\sum\dfrac1n$ diverges, so the absolute series diverges too.

Converges, but not absolutely: converges conditionally.

Practice 52

Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac{(-1)^n}{n!}$ converges absolutely, conditionally, or diverges.

Solution

Absolute series: $\displaystyle\sum\frac1{n!}$. Ratio test: $\dfrac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} = \dfrac{n!}{(n+1)!} = \dfrac1{n+1} \to 0 < 1$, so this converges.

Since the absolute-value series converges, the original series converges absolutely without even needing to invoke the AST separately (absolute convergence is the stronger property, and always implies ordinary convergence too).

Practice 53

Explain the difference between absolute and conditional convergence in your own words, and why absolute convergence is considered the "stronger" or more robust property.

Solution

A series converges absolutely when it would still converge even if every negative sign were stripped out and replaced with a plus sign, the convergence doesn't rely on cancellation at all, it's already "true" in the more demanding, all-positive-terms sense. Conditional convergence is more fragile: the series only converges because positive and negative terms are cancelling each other out in a delicately balanced way, and stripping the signs (Practice 50's $\sum\frac1{\sqrt n}$, for instance) destroys that balance and the series diverges. This fragility has real consequences beyond just classification: conditionally convergent series can actually be reordered to converge to a different sum entirely, or even to diverge, a deeply counterintuitive fact (the Riemann rearrangement theorem) that simply cannot happen to an absolutely convergent series, no matter how its terms are shuffled.

9 Power series

Every test in this lesson has classified a series of numbers. A power series replaces those fixed numbers with an infinite polynomial in $x$, $\sum c_n(x-a)^n$, and asks a genuinely new question: for which values of $x$ does this series converge? This is exactly the machinery underneath the Taylor and Maclaurin series lesson, every Taylor series is a power series, and knowing where it actually converges (not just what its coefficients are) is essential.

Key idea

A power series $\sum c_n(x-a)^n$ converges for $x$ within some radius of convergence $R$ centred at $a$: it converges (absolutely) for $|x-a|R$, and needs separate checking at the two endpoints $x=a\pm R$, where any behaviour is possible. The interval of convergence is the full set of $x$-values for which the series converges, the open interval $(a-R,a+R)$ possibly extended to include one, both, or neither endpoint.

The standard method: apply the ratio test treating $x$ as a fixed but unknown constant, solve the resulting inequality $L<1$ for $x$ to find $R$, then plug each endpoint back in as an ordinary numerical series and test it separately using the earlier sections' tools.

Worked example

Find the radius and interval of convergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac{x^n}{n\cdot3^n}$.

Ratio test, treating $x$ as fixed: $\left|\dfrac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \left|\dfrac{x^{n+1}/((n+1)3^{n+1})}{x^n/(n\cdot3^n)}\right| = |x|\cdot\dfrac{n}{n+1}\cdot\dfrac13 \to \dfrac{|x|}3$.

Convergence requires $\dfrac{|x|}3<1$, i.e.\ $|x|<3$. Radius of convergence: $R=3$.

Check the endpoints separately. At $x=3$: the series becomes $\displaystyle\sum\frac{3^n}{n\cdot3^n}=\sum\frac1n$, the harmonic series, which diverges.

At $x=-3$: the series becomes $\displaystyle\sum\frac{(-3)^n}{n\cdot3^n}=\sum\frac{(-1)^n}n$, the alternating harmonic series, which converges (conditionally, Section 8's worked example).

Interval of convergence: $[-3,3)$, closed at $-3$, open at $3$.

Practice 54

Find the radius of convergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\dfrac{x^n}{2^n}$ (don't worry about endpoints for this one, just find $R$).

Solution

$$\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \left|\frac{x^{n+1}/2^{n+1}}{x^n/2^n}\right| = \frac{|x|}2.$$

Convergence requires $\dfrac{|x|}2<1 \Rightarrow |x|<2$. $\ R=2$.

Practice 55

Find the radius and interval of convergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}nx^n$.

Solution

$$\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \left|\frac{(n+1)x^{n+1}}{nx^n}\right| = |x|\cdot\frac{n+1}n \to |x|.$$

Convergence requires $|x|<1$, so $R=1$.

At $x=1$: series becomes $\sum n$, diverges (terms $\to\infty$, fails divergence test). At $x=-1$: series becomes $\sum n(-1)^n$, also diverges (terms don't tend to $0$, they oscillate with growing magnitude, again failing the divergence test).

Interval of convergence: $(-1,1)$, open at both ends.

Practice 56

Find the radius and interval of convergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac{(x-3)^n}{n^2}$.

Solution

$$\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \left|\frac{(x-3)^{n+1}/(n+1)^2}{(x-3)^n/n^2}\right| = |x-3|\cdot\frac{n^2}{(n+1)^2} \to |x-3|.$$

Convergence requires $|x-3|<1$, so $R=1$, centred at $a=3$: the series converges for $x$ in $(2,4)$ before checking endpoints.

At $x=2$ (i.e.\ $x-3=-1$): series becomes $\displaystyle\sum\frac{(-1)^n}{n^2}$, which converges absolutely (Practice 48).

At $x=4$ (i.e.\ $x-3=1$): series becomes $\displaystyle\sum\frac1{n^2}$, a convergent $p$-series.

Interval of convergence: $[2,4]$, closed at both ends, since both endpoints happened to converge here.

Practice 57

Find the radius of convergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\dfrac{(-1)^nx^{2n}}{(2n)!}$ (this is, in fact, the Maclaurin series for $\cos x$).

Solution

$$\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \left|\frac{x^{2n+2}/(2n+2)!}{x^{2n}/(2n)!}\right| = |x|^2\cdot\frac{(2n)!}{(2n+2)!} = \frac{|x|^2}{(2n+2)(2n+1)} \to 0$$

for every fixed value of $x$, since the denominator grows without bound regardless of how large $|x|$ is. The ratio limit is $0<1$ no matter what $x$ is, so the series converges for all $x$: $R=\infty$.

This matches something worth remembering permanently: $\cos x$ (and $\sin x$ and $e^x$) all have infinite radius of convergence, their Taylor series are valid approximations everywhere on the real line, not just near the centre, exactly why those three series are safe to use freely without ever needing to check a radius of convergence first.

Practice 58

Find the radius of convergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}n!\,x^n$.

Solution

$$\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \left|\frac{(n+1)!\,x^{n+1}}{n!\,x^n}\right| = (n+1)|x|.$$

For any fixed $x\neq0$, $(n+1)|x|\to\infty$ as $n\to\infty$, so the ratio test gives $L=\infty>1$, meaning the series diverges for every $x\neq0$. It only converges at the single point $x=0$ (where every term past the first is trivially $0$). $\ R=0$.

This is the opposite extreme from Practice 57: a radius of convergence of exactly $0$ means the "power series" is really only useful, in any meaningful sense, at its single centre point.

Practice 59

Find the radius and interval of convergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac{(2x)^n}n$.

Solution

Rewrite as $\displaystyle\sum\frac{2^n}nx^n$. Ratio test:

$$\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \left|\frac{2^{n+1}x^{n+1}/(n+1)}{2^nx^n/n}\right| = 2|x|\cdot\frac{n}{n+1} \to 2|x|.$$

Convergence requires $2|x|<1 \Rightarrow |x|<\dfrac12$. $\ R=\dfrac12$.

At $x=\dfrac12$: series becomes $\displaystyle\sum\frac{2^n(1/2)^n}n=\sum\frac1n$, the harmonic series, diverges.

At $x=-\dfrac12$: series becomes $\displaystyle\sum\frac{2^n(-1/2)^n}n=\sum\frac{(-1)^n}n$, the alternating harmonic series, converges (conditionally).

Interval of convergence: $\left[-\dfrac12,\dfrac12\right)$.
