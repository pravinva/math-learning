1 Arithmetic sequences

An arithmetic sequence (or arithmetic progression, AP) is a list of numbers where you get from one term to the next by always adding the same fixed amount, the common difference $d$. Whenever a real-world quantity changes by a constant amount each step, a fixed pay rise, a fixed number of extra seats per row, a car depreciating by the same dollar amount each year, it's an arithmetic sequence underneath.

Key idea

$$T_n = a+(n-1)d$$

where $a=T_1$ is the first term, $d$ is the common difference (found by subtracting any term from the next one, $d=T_{n+1}-T_n$, and it should give the same value no matter which consecutive pair you pick), and $T_n$ is the $n$th term.

Worked example

Find the $10$th term of the sequence $5,8,11,14,\dots$

$a=5$, and $d=8-5=3$ (check: $11-8=3$ too, confirming it's genuinely arithmetic).

$$T_{10} = 5+(10-1)(3) = 5+27 = 32.$$

Practice 1

Find the $15$th term of the sequence $3,7,11,15,\dots$

Solution

$a=3$, $d=4$. $\ T_{15} = 3+(15-1)(4) = 3+56 = 59$.

Practice 2

An arithmetic sequence has $T_5=17$ and $T_9=33$. Find $a$ and $d$.

Solution

Write both conditions using $T_n=a+(n-1)d$: $a+4d=17$ and $a+8d=33$.

Subtract the first equation from the second (eliminates $a$): $4d=16 \Rightarrow d=4$.

Substitute back: $a+4(4)=17 \Rightarrow a=1$.

Practice 3

Is $100$ a term of the sequence $4,9,14,19,\dots$? Justify your answer.

Solution

$a=4,\ d=5$. If $100$ is a term, some whole number $n\ge1$ satisfies $4+(n-1)5=100$.

$$5(n-1)=96 \quad\Rightarrow\quad n-1=\frac{96}5=19.2 \quad\Rightarrow\quad n=20.2.$$

Since $n$ must be a positive whole number and $20.2$ isn't one, $100$ is not a term of this sequence.

Practice 4

Which term of the sequence $4,9,14,19,\dots$ equals $99$?

Solution

$$4+(n-1)5=99 \quad\Rightarrow\quad 5(n-1)=95 \quad\Rightarrow\quad n-1=19 \quad\Rightarrow\quad n=20.$$

$99$ is the $20$th term.

Practice 5

The expressions $2x-1$, $x+4$, $3x+2$ are three consecutive terms of an arithmetic sequence. Find $x$.

Solution

Consecutive terms of an AP share a common difference, so the gap between the 1st and 2nd terms must equal the gap between the 2nd and 3rd:

$$(x+4)-(2x-1) = (3x+2)-(x+4)$$

$$-x+5 = 2x-2 \quad\Rightarrow\quad 7=3x \quad\Rightarrow\quad x=\frac73.$$

Practice 6

Find the $20$th term of the arithmetic sequence with first term $a=-3$ and common difference $d=2$.

Solution

$T_{20} = -3+(20-1)(2) = -3+38 = 35$.

2 Arithmetic series

An arithmetic series is what you get by adding up the terms of an arithmetic sequence. There's a famous story (probably embellished, but the mathematics is real) about a young Gauss being asked to add the numbers $1$ to $100$ in a classroom, and instantly spotting a shortcut that avoids adding a hundred numbers one at a time.

Key idea

$$S_n = \frac n2\big[2a+(n-1)d\big] = \frac n2(a+l)$$

where $l=T_n$ is the last term being added. The two forms are equivalent, since $T_n=a+(n-1)d$; the second form is often faster to use once you already know the last term.

Gauss's pairing trick

Write the sum $S_n$ forwards, then write it again backwards, and add the two versions term by term:

$$S_n = a+(a+d)+(a+2d)+\cdots+(a+(n-1)d)$$

$$S_n = (a+(n-1)d)+(a+(n-2)d)+\cdots+a$$

Adding vertically, every single pair sums to exactly $2a+(n-1)d$ (the first-plus-last term of the original sequence), and there are $n$ such pairs, since there are $n$ terms in total:

$$2S_n = n\big[2a+(n-1)d\big] \quad\Rightarrow\quad S_n = \frac n2\big[2a+(n-1)d\big].$$

This is exactly the trick Gauss reportedly used on $1+2+\cdots+100$: pairing the first and last ($1+100=101$), the second and second-last ($2+99=101$), and so on, $50$ pairs each summing to $101$, giving $50\times101=5050$ almost instantly, well before his classmates finished adding term by term.

Worked example

Find the sum of the first $20$ terms of $3,7,11,15,\dots$

$a=3$, $d=4$, $n=20$.

$$S_{20} = \frac{20}2\big[2(3)+(20-1)(4)\big] = 10\big[6+76\big] = 10(82) = 820.$$

Practice 7

Find the sum of all multiples of $3$ from $3$ to $99$ inclusive.

Solution

This is an AP: $a=3$, $d=3$, last term $l=99$. Number of terms: $99=3+(n-1)3 \Rightarrow n-1=32 \Rightarrow n=33$.

$$S_{33} = \frac{33}2(3+99) = \frac{33}2(102) = 33(51) = 1683.$$

Practice 8

A theatre has $20$ seats in the front row, and each row behind it has $2$ more seats than the row in front. Find the total number of seats in the first $15$ rows.

Solution

Row sizes form an AP with $a=20$, $d=2$, $n=15$.

$$S_{15} = \frac{15}2\big[2(20)+(15-1)(2)\big] = \frac{15}2\big[40+28\big] = \frac{15}2(68) = 510 \text{ seats}.$$

Practice 9

An arithmetic series has $S_{10}=145$ and $S_5=25$. Find $a$ and $d$.

Solution

$S_{10}=145$: $\ \dfrac{10}2\big[2a+9d\big]=145 \Rightarrow 2a+9d=29$.

$S_5=25$: $\ \dfrac52\big[2a+4d\big]=25 \Rightarrow 2a+4d=10$.

Subtract: $5d=19 \Rightarrow d=\dfrac{19}5$. Substitute back: $2a+4\left(\dfrac{19}5\right)=10 \Rightarrow 2a = 10-\dfrac{76}5=-\dfrac{26}5 \Rightarrow a=-\dfrac{13}5$.

Practice 10

Find $n$ if the sum of the first $n$ terms of the arithmetic series with $a=4,\ d=2$ is $130$.

Solution

$$S_n = \frac n2\big[2(4)+(n-1)(2)\big] = \frac n2\big[8+2n-2\big] = \frac n2(2n+6) = n(n+3).$$

Set equal to $130$: $n^2+3n-130=0$.

Factorise: need two numbers multiplying to $-130$, adding to $3$: $13$ and $-10$. $\ (n+13)(n-10)=0$.

Since $n$ must be a positive whole number, $n=10$ (discard $n=-13$).

Practice 11

Show that $S_n=\dfrac n2\big[2a+(n-1)d\big]$ and $S_n=\dfrac n2(a+l)$ are the same formula, by substituting $l=T_n=a+(n-1)d$ into the second form.

Solution

$$S_n = \frac n2(a+l) = \frac n2\Big(a+\big[a+(n-1)d\big]\Big) = \frac n2\big[2a+(n-1)d\big].$$

Identical to the first form, confirming they're not two separate facts to memorise, just the same formula with $l$ either left as a symbol or expanded out in terms of $a$, $n$, and $d$.

3 Geometric sequences

A geometric sequence (or geometric progression, GP) is a list of numbers where you get from one term to the next by always multiplying by the same fixed amount, the common ratio $r$. Whenever a quantity changes by a constant percentage, compound interest, population growth, radioactive decay, depreciation by a fixed percentage, it's a geometric sequence, not an arithmetic one, and mixing the two up is one of the most common HSC exam errors.

Key idea

$$T_n = ar^{n-1}$$

where $a=T_1$ is the first term, $r$ is the common ratio (found by dividing any term by the one before it, $r=\dfrac{T_{n+1}}{T_n}$, and it should give the same value no matter which consecutive pair you pick).

Worked example

Find the $6$th term of the sequence $2,6,18,54,\dots$

$a=2$, $r=\dfrac62=3$ (check: $\dfrac{18}6=3$ too, confirming it's genuinely geometric).

$$T_6 = 2(3)^{6-1} = 2(3^5) = 2(243) = 486.$$

Practice 12

Find the $8$th term of the sequence $5,10,20,40,\dots$

Solution

$a=5$, $r=2$. $\ T_8 = 5(2)^7 = 5(128) = 640$.

Practice 13

A geometric sequence has $T_1=4$ and $T_4=32$. Find the common ratio $r$ (there is one real solution).

Solution

$T_4 = T_1\,r^3$: $\ 32=4r^3 \Rightarrow r^3=8 \Rightarrow r=2$.

Practice 14

A bacteria culture starts at $100$ cells and triples every hour. Let $P_n$ be the population after $n$ hours. Write a formula for $P_n$, and find the population after $5$ hours.

Solution

Starting population $P_0=100$, tripling ($r=3$) each hour: $P_n = 100(3)^n$.

$$P_5 = 100(3)^5 = 100(243) = 24{,}300 \text{ cells}.$$

Notice the exponent here is $n$, not $n-1$: this formula measures elapsed hours directly from a starting value $P_0$, slightly different bookkeeping from $T_n=ar^{n-1}$, which counts the starting value itself as "term $1$." Both describe the same underlying geometric growth, just indexed from a different starting point, so it's always worth checking which convention a problem is using.

Practice 15

A car worth $\$30{,}000$ depreciates by $15\%$ each year. Find its value after $4$ years, to the nearest cent.

Solution

Depreciating by $15\%$ means $85\%$ of the value remains each year, so $r=0.85$.

$$V_4 = 30{,}000(0.85)^4 \approx \$15{,}660.19.$$

Practice 16

Determine whether $2,4,8,15,\dots$ is a geometric sequence. Justify your answer.

Solution

Check the ratio between consecutive terms: $\dfrac42=2$, but $\dfrac84=2$, and $\dfrac{15}8=1.875$.

The ratio is not the same throughout ($1.875\neq2$), so this is not a geometric sequence. A single consistency check is never enough, checking just the first pair of terms would have wrongly suggested $r=2$ for the whole sequence.

Practice 17

Find the $5$th term of a geometric sequence with $a=81$ and $r=\dfrac13$.

Solution

$$T_5 = 81\left(\frac13\right)^4 = 81\times\frac1{81} = 1.$$

4 Geometric series

A geometric series adds up the terms of a geometric sequence. Gauss's trick from Section 2 doesn't work here (pairing first-and-last terms of a GP doesn't give a constant sum the way it does for an AP), so a different trick is needed, multiply the whole sum by $r$ and subtract.

Key idea

$$S_n = \frac{a(r^n-1)}{r-1} \qquad (r\neq1)$$

Either form of the formula (this one, or the algebraically identical $\dfrac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r}$) works for any $r\neq1$; people often prefer whichever form keeps the numerator and denominator both positive for the particular value of $r$ in front of them.

The multiply-and-subtract trick

Write $S_n = a+ar+ar^2+\cdots+ar^{n-1}$. Multiply every term by $r$: $rS_n = ar+ar^2+\cdots+ar^{n-1}+ar^n$.

Subtract the first equation from the second: almost every term cancels, since $rS_n$ and $S_n$ share every term except $S_n$'s leading $a$ and $rS_n$'s trailing $ar^n$:

$$rS_n-S_n = ar^n-a \quad\Rightarrow\quad S_n(r-1)=a(r^n-1) \quad\Rightarrow\quad S_n=\frac{a(r^n-1)}{r-1}.$$

Worked example

Find the sum of the first $8$ terms of $3,6,12,24,\dots$

$a=3$, $r=2$, $n=8$.

$$S_8 = \frac{3(2^8-1)}{2-1} = \frac{3(256-1)}1 = 3(255) = 765.$$

Practice 18

Find the sum of the first $10$ terms of $2,6,18,54,\dots$

Solution

$a=2$, $r=3$, $n=10$.

$$S_{10} = \frac{2(3^{10}-1)}{3-1} = \frac{2(59049-1)}2 = 59{,}048.$$

Practice 19

Find the sum of the first $6$ terms of $81,27,9,3,\dots$ (note $r<1$ here, use whichever form of the formula keeps things positive).

Solution

$a=81$, $r=\dfrac13$, $n=6$. Using $S_n=\dfrac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r}$:

$$S_6 = \frac{81\left(1-\left(\frac13\right)^6\right)}{1-\frac13} = \frac{81\left(1-\frac1{729}\right)}{2/3} = \frac{81\left(\frac{728}{729}\right)}{2/3}.$$

$81\times\dfrac{728}{729} = \dfrac{728}9$. Dividing by $\dfrac23$ means multiplying by $\dfrac32$: $\dfrac{728}9\times\dfrac32 = \dfrac{2184}{18} = \dfrac{364}3$.

$$S_6 = \frac{364}3 \approx 121.3.$$

Practice 20

A geometric series has $a=5$ and $r=2$. Find $n$ if $S_n=635$.

Solution

$$635 = \frac{5(2^n-1)}{2-1} = 5(2^n-1) \quad\Rightarrow\quad 127 = 2^n-1 \quad\Rightarrow\quad 2^n=128=2^7.$$

$n=7$.

Practice 21

Explain why Gauss's pairing trick from Section 2 (adding the series to its own reversal) fails to produce a constant sum when applied to a geometric series, while the multiply-by-$r$ trick in this section succeeds.

Solution

Gauss's trick relies on the fact that in an AP, the first-plus-last, second-plus-second-last, and so on, are all equal ($2a+(n-1)d$ every time), because moving inward from both ends adds $d$ on one side and subtracts $d$ on the other, exactly cancelling. In a GP, moving inward from both ends multiplies by $r$ on one side and divides by $r$ on the other, these don't cancel to a constant the way addition and subtraction do, so paired terms in a GP generally give different sums depending on which pair you pick. The multiply-by-$r$ trick instead exploits that shifting an entire geometric series by one position (multiplying every term by $r$) reproduces almost the same list of terms, just shifted, so subtracting the original from the shifted version causes the massive cancellation that isolates $S_n$.

Practice 22

Find the sum of the first $5$ terms of $-3,6,-12,24,\dots$ (note the negative common ratio).

Solution

$a=-3$, $r=\dfrac6{-3}=-2$, $n=5$.

$$S_5 = \frac{-3\big((-2)^5-1\big)}{-2-1} = \frac{-3(-32-1)}{-3} = \frac{-3(-33)}{-3} = \frac{99}{-3} = -33.$$

5 Sum to infinity

What happens to $S_n$ if you keep adding forever, letting $n\to\infty$? For a geometric series, the answer depends entirely on the size of $r$. If $|r|<1$, each new term is smaller than the last, and the running total settles down and approaches a single finite number, a genuinely different, and more surprising, outcome than an arithmetic series ever produces.

Key idea

$$S_\infty = \frac a{1-r}, \qquad \text{provided } |r|<1.$$

If $|r|\ge1$, there is no sum to infinity, the terms don't shrink toward zero fast enough (or at all), so the running total either grows without bound or never settles on one value.

Where the formula comes from

Start from Section 4's finite-sum formula: $S_n = \dfrac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r}$. The only piece of this expression that depends on $n$ is $r^n$. If $|r|<1$, repeatedly multiplying a number smaller than $1$ by itself makes it shrink toward $0$ (try it: $\left(\frac12\right)^{10}\approx0.001$, and it only gets smaller from there), so $r^n\to0$ as $n\to\infty$. Substituting that limit in:

$$S_\infty = \frac{a(1-0)}{1-r} = \frac a{1-r}.$$

If instead $|r|\ge1$, $r^n$ does not shrink to $0$ (it either grows without bound, or, if $r=-1$, just flips sign forever without settling), so this substitution isn't valid and no finite sum to infinity exists.

Worked example

Express the repeating decimal $0.454545\ldots$ as an exact fraction.

Write it as a geometric series: $0.454545\ldots = 0.45+0.0045+0.000045+\cdots$, with $a=0.45=\dfrac{45}{100}$ and $r=0.01=\dfrac1{100}$.

Since $|r|<1$, the sum to infinity formula applies:

$$S_\infty = \frac{45/100}{1-1/100} = \frac{45/100}{99/100} = \frac{45}{99} = \frac5{11}.$$

Practice 23

Find $S_\infty$ for the series $8+4+2+1+\cdots$

Solution

$a=8$, $r=\dfrac12$, and $|r|<1$ so a sum to infinity exists.

$$S_\infty = \frac8{1-\frac12} = \frac8{1/2} = 16.$$

Practice 24

Find $S_\infty$ for a geometric series with $a=6$, $r=-\dfrac13$.

Solution

$|r|=\dfrac13<1$, so the sum to infinity exists, even though $r$ is negative (the condition is on the size $|r|$, not the sign of $r$).

$$S_\infty = \frac6{1-\left(-\frac13\right)} = \frac6{4/3} = \frac{18}4 = \frac92.$$

Practice 25

Explain why $3+6+12+24+\cdots$ has no sum to infinity.

Solution

$r=2$, and $|r|=2\ge1$, so the condition for a sum to infinity fails. The terms don't shrink toward zero, they actually grow without bound ($3,6,12,24,48,\dots$), so the running total also grows without bound, there's no finite value it's settling toward.

Practice 26

A ball is dropped from $20$ m and always bounces back up to $70\%$ of the height it fell from. Find the total vertical distance the ball travels, forever, using a sum to infinity.

Solution

The first drop contributes $20$ m. After that, each bounce goes up and then back down the same height, so every bounce contributes twice its peak height. Bounce heights: $20(0.7),\ 20(0.7)^2,\ 20(0.7)^3,\dots$, a GP with $a=20(0.7)=14$, $r=0.7$.

Total from all the up-and-down bounces: $2S_\infty = 2\left(\dfrac{14}{1-0.7}\right) = 2\left(\dfrac{14}{0.3}\right) = \dfrac{28}{0.3} = \dfrac{280}3$.

Add the initial drop: total distance $= 20+\dfrac{280}3 = \dfrac{60}3+\dfrac{280}3 = \dfrac{340}3 \approx 113.3$ m, a finite total despite the ball bouncing infinitely many times, in principle.

Practice 27

A geometric series has $r=\dfrac14$ and $S_\infty=20$. Find $a$.

Solution

$$20 = \frac a{1-\frac14} = \frac a{3/4} \quad\Rightarrow\quad a = 20\times\frac34 = 15.$$

Practice 28

State the exact condition on $r$ required for a sum to infinity to exist, and explain in one sentence why $r=1$ specifically has to be excluded even though $|1|<1$ is false only barely (it's exactly on the boundary).

Solution

The condition is $|r|<1$, a strict inequality. At $r=1$ exactly, every term of the series equals $a$ (no shrinking happens at all, since $ar^{n-1}=a\times1=a$ for every $n$), so the partial sums are $S_n=na$, growing without bound (unless $a=0$), clearly not settling on a finite value. This confirms $r=1$ genuinely must be excluded, not just as a technicality in the formula (where it would cause division by zero in $\frac a{1-r}$ anyway), but because the series itself demonstrably fails to converge there.

6 Recognising arithmetic vs.\ geometric

Exam and real-world problems rarely announce "this is arithmetic" or "this is geometric" outright, that judgement call is often the actual skill being tested. The wording usually gives it away, once you know what to listen for.

Key idea: the wording tells you the model
Wording patternModelWhy
"increases/decreases by a fixed amount"Arithmeticconstant addition each step
"increases/decreases by a fixed percentage"Geometricconstant multiplication each step
"doubles / triples / halves"Geometricconstant multiplicative factor
"$n$ more each time" (a fixed count)Arithmeticconstant addition each step

When in doubt, compute the difference between consecutive terms and the ratio between consecutive terms from any given data; whichever one comes out constant tells you the correct model.

Worked example

A salary starts at $\$50{,}000$ and increases by $\$2000$ each year. A second salary also starts at $\$50{,}000$ but increases by $4\%$ each year. Find each salary in year $6$ (i.e.\ after $5$ increases), and compare.

First salary: "fixed amount" $\Rightarrow$ arithmetic, $a=50{,}000$, $d=2000$: $T_6 = 50{,}000+5(2000)=60{,}000$.

Second salary: "fixed percentage" $\Rightarrow$ geometric, $a=50{,}000$, $r=1.04$: $T_6 = 50{,}000(1.04)^5 \approx 60{,}832.65$.

Both salaries reach roughly the same value after $5$ years, but they arrived there via genuinely different models, and if extended for many more years, the geometric (percentage-based) salary would eventually pull well ahead, since percentage growth compounds while fixed-amount growth doesn't.

Practice 29

Classify $100,90,80,70,\dots$ as arithmetic or geometric, and state $a$ and the relevant constant ($d$ or $r$).

Solution

Consecutive differences are constant ($90-100=-10$, $80-90=-10$), while consecutive ratios are not ($\frac{90}{100}=0.9$, $\frac{80}{90}\approx0.889$, not equal). Arithmetic, $a=100$, $d=-10$.

Practice 30

Classify $100,90,81,72.9,\dots$ as arithmetic or geometric, and state $a$ and the relevant constant.

Solution

Consecutive ratios are constant ($\frac{90}{100}=0.9$, $\frac{81}{90}=0.9$, $\frac{72.9}{81}=0.9$), while consecutive differences are not ($90-100=-10$, $81-90=-9$). Geometric, $a=100$, $r=0.9$.

Notice how close this sequence looks to Practice 29 at a glance, both start at $100$ and drop by roughly similar amounts early on, but they diverge further and further as they continue, exactly the kind of pair an exam might use to test whether you're checking properly rather than guessing from the first couple of terms.

Practice 31

A savings account balance of $\$2000$ earns simple interest of $\$60$ per year (fixed dollar amount, not compounded). Model the year-end balances as a sequence, identify whether it's arithmetic or geometric, and find the balance after $10$ years.

Solution

"Fixed dollar amount" each year is the arithmetic signal: $a=2000$, $d=60$.

Balance after $10$ years corresponds to $T_{11}$ (the starting balance is $T_1$, before any interest has been added, so $10$ years of interest lands on term $11$): $T_{11} = 2000+10(60) = 2600$.

Simple interest is the classic real-world arithmetic sequence, exactly because it adds the same dollar amount every period, unlike compound interest, which multiplies.

Practice 32

The same $\$2000$ instead earns compound interest at $3\%$ per year. Model the year-end balances, identify the correct sequence type, and find the balance after $10$ years, to the nearest cent.

Solution

"Percentage" growth each year is the geometric signal: $a=2000$, $r=1.03$.

$$T_{11} = 2000(1.03)^{10} \approx \$2687.83.$$

Compare with Practice 31's simple-interest result of exactly $\$2600$ for the same $10$-year period: compound interest already pulls ahead by roughly $\$88$, and that gap would only widen further over a longer time frame, the practical consequence of geometric (multiplicative) growth eventually overtaking arithmetic (additive) growth.

Practice 33

A sequence begins $5,5,5,5,\dots$ (every term identical). Is this sequence arithmetic, geometric, both, or neither? Justify your answer using the definitions of $d$ and $r$.

Solution

Both. As an arithmetic sequence: $d=5-5=0$, a perfectly valid (if unexciting) common difference. As a geometric sequence: $r=\dfrac55=1$, a perfectly valid common ratio. A constant sequence satisfies both definitions simultaneously, since adding $0$ and multiplying by $1$ both leave a number unchanged, it sits at the one special point where the two families of sequences overlap.

Practice 34

A tank contains $500$ L of water. Each day, $40$ L is drained out. Separately, a second tank also starts with $500$ L, and $8\%$ of the remaining water is drained out each day. After $6$ days, which tank has more water left? (Assume both start counting from "day $0$" as $T_1$.)

Solution

Tank 1 (fixed amount drained): arithmetic, $a=500$, $d=-40$. After $6$ days is $T_7$: $T_7 = 500+6(-40) = 500-240=260$ L.

Tank 2 (fixed percentage drained, so $92\%$ remains each day): geometric, $a=500$, $r=0.92$. $T_7 = 500(0.92)^6 \approx 500(0.6064) \approx 303.2$ L.

Tank 2 (the percentage-based drain) has noticeably more water left, $\approx303.2$ L versus $260$ L, because a percentage-based drain removes progressively less water each day as the tank empties (it's a percentage of a shrinking amount), while the fixed-amount drain keeps removing the same $40$ L regardless of how little water remains.

7 The harmonic series

The harmonic sequence is the sequence of reciprocals of the natural numbers, $1,\dfrac12,\dfrac13,\dfrac14,\dots$ (more generally, the reciprocals of any arithmetic sequence). Adding these up gives the harmonic series, and it's one of the most famous series in mathematics precisely because it behaves in a way that feels like it shouldn't be true: the individual terms shrink to $0$, exactly like a convergent geometric series, and yet the sum still grows without any bound at all.

Key idea

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac1n = 1+\frac12+\frac13+\frac14+\cdots \quad\text{diverges (has no finite sum), despite } \frac1n\to0.$$

This is a genuinely important warning, already hinted at back in Section 5: terms shrinking to zero is necessary for an infinite sum to converge, but it is not sufficient on its own. The harmonic series is the single most famous counterexample proving that point.

Why it diverges: a grouping argument

Group the terms in blocks that double in length each time: $1,\ \left(\dfrac12\right),\ \left(\dfrac13+\dfrac14\right),\ \left(\dfrac15+\dfrac16+\dfrac17+\dfrac18\right),\dots$

In the block $\left(\dfrac13+\dfrac14\right)$, every term is at least as large as the smallest one, $\dfrac14$, and there are $2$ terms, so the block totals at least $2\times\dfrac14=\dfrac12$.

In the block $\left(\dfrac15+\dfrac16+\dfrac17+\dfrac18\right)$, every term is at least as large as the smallest, $\dfrac18$, and there are $4$ terms, so the block totals at least $4\times\dfrac18=\dfrac12$.

This pattern continues forever, no matter how far out you go, the next block (twice as long as the last) still totals at least $\dfrac12$, because it's always $2^k$ terms each at least $\dfrac1{2^{k+1}}$.

So the harmonic series is at least $1+\dfrac12+\dfrac12+\dfrac12+\cdots$, adding an unlimited number of $\dfrac12$'s. That clearly grows without bound, so the harmonic series, being even larger, must diverge too. (This argument dates back to the French scholar Nicole Oresme, in the 14th century, centuries before calculus formalised the idea of a limit.)

Worked example

Compute the exact value of $S_4=1+\dfrac12+\dfrac13+\dfrac14$, and compare the growth of the partial sums $S_1,S_2,S_4,S_8,S_{16}$ to see just how slowly the harmonic series grows.

Common denominator $12$: $\dfrac{12}{12}+\dfrac6{12}+\dfrac4{12}+\dfrac3{12} = \dfrac{25}{12} \approx 2.083$.

$S_1=1$. $\ S_2=1.5$. $\ S_4\approx2.083$. $\ S_8\approx2.718$. $\ S_{16}\approx3.381$.

Doubling the number of terms each time only adds roughly $0.6$–$0.7$ to the total, and that increment keeps shrinking further (this matches the grouping argument's "$\ge\frac12$ per doubling" bound closely). The series is still growing without any upper limit, it's just doing so at a punishingly slow pace, which is exactly why it feels, intuitively, like it "should" converge, right up until you prove otherwise.

Practice 36

Show that $\dfrac15+\dfrac16+\dfrac17+\dfrac18 > \dfrac12$, using the same reasoning as the "why it diverges" grouping argument.

Solution

Every one of the $4$ terms is at least as large as the smallest, $\dfrac18$: $\dfrac15>\dfrac18$, $\dfrac16>\dfrac18$, $\dfrac17>\dfrac18$, and $\dfrac18=\dfrac18$.

$$\frac15+\frac16+\frac17+\frac18 \;>\; \frac18+\frac18+\frac18+\frac18 = 4\times\frac18 = \frac12.$$

(The exact value is $\dfrac{533}{840}\approx0.634$, comfortably above $\dfrac12$, matching the inequality.)

Practice 37

Contrast the harmonic series with the geometric series $1+\dfrac12+\dfrac14+\dfrac18+\cdots$. Both have terms shrinking to $0$ at a glance, so why does one converge and the other diverge?

Solution

The geometric series $1+\dfrac12+\dfrac14+\dfrac18+\cdots$ has $a=1$, $r=\dfrac12$, and $|r|<1$, so by Section 5's formula, $S_\infty = \dfrac1{1-\frac12}=2$, a genuine finite sum.

The key difference is how fast the terms shrink. The geometric series' terms shrink exponentially (each term is exactly half the last), while the harmonic series' terms shrink only reciprocally ($\frac1n$ shrinks far more slowly than $\left(\frac12\right)^n$ does, for large $n$). The grouping argument in this section's "why" box shows precisely that the harmonic series' terms don't shrink fast enough, every doubling-length block still contributes at least $\frac12$ forever, while a geometric series' doubling-length blocks would themselves shrink toward $0$. Terms merely tending to $0$ is not enough; the rate at which they shrink is what actually determines convergence.

Solution

Common denominator $12$: $\dfrac{12}{12}+\dfrac6{12}+\dfrac4{12}+\dfrac3{12} = \dfrac{25}{12}$.

Practice 39

Consider the series formed from the reciprocals of the arithmetic sequence $2,5,8,11,\dots$ (i.e.\ $\dfrac12+\dfrac15+\dfrac18+\dfrac1{11}+\cdots$). Based on how this section's grouping argument worked, would you expect this series to converge or diverge? Explain your reasoning informally.

Solution

Diverge, by essentially the same reasoning as the ordinary harmonic series. The terms of $2,5,8,11,\dots$ grow linearly in $n$ (roughly proportional to $3n$, since $d=3$), exactly like the plain natural numbers $1,2,3,\dots$ grow proportional to $n$, just scaled and shifted. Since $\dfrac1{3n}$ is just a constant multiple of $\dfrac1n$, and multiplying every term of a divergent series by a fixed positive constant can never make it converge (the partial sums still grow without bound, merely more slowly), a series built from the reciprocals of any arithmetic sequence will diverge by the same grouping-into-doubling-blocks argument, not just the specific case $1,2,3,4,\dots$.

Practice 40

Referring to the worked example's partial sums ($S_1=1,\ S_2=1.5,\ S_4\approx2.083,\ S_8\approx2.718,\ S_{16}\approx3.381$), roughly how many terms would you guess are needed to push the harmonic series' partial sum past $S=4$? (No need for an exact answer, just reason from the pattern of how much each doubling adds.)

Solution

Each doubling of the term count seems to add roughly $0.6$–$0.7$ to the running total ($1\to1.5$ adds $0.5$; $1.5\to2.083$ adds about $0.58$; $2.083\to2.718$ adds about $0.63$; $2.718\to3.381$ adds about $0.66$, a slowly increasing increment approaching, though never quite reaching, $\ln2\approx0.693$ per doubling in the limit). Extrapolating, $S_{32}$ would land somewhere around $3.38+0.68\approx4.06$, so passing $S=4$ should take roughly $32$ terms, in fact the true value is $S_{31}\approx4.027$, confirming the estimate lands in the right neighbourhood. The broader point: the harmonic series does eventually pass any target value you name, however large, it just takes exponentially more terms to add a fixed extra amount as the sum grows, exactly the signature of divergence-in-slow-motion this whole section has been building toward.

Practice 41

Tie the lesson together: in one or two sentences, explain how Section 5 (sum to infinity) and this section (the harmonic series) together demonstrate that "does the sum converge?" is a genuinely separate question from "do the terms shrink to zero?"

Solution

Section 5 showed that a geometric series with $|r|<1$ has terms shrinking to $0$ and converges to a finite sum, which might tempt someone into believing "terms $\to0$" is the whole story. The harmonic series in this section has terms shrinking to $0$ just as clearly, $\frac1n\to0$, yet its sum diverges, proving that shrinking terms alone guarantee nothing about the sum's behaviour. What actually matters, as Practice 37 worked out directly, is how fast the terms shrink, exponential shrinkage (geometric, $|r|<1$) is fast enough to force convergence, while merely reciprocal shrinkage (harmonic) is not, a distinction with no analogue at all in the finite, term-by-term world of Sections 1 through 4.
