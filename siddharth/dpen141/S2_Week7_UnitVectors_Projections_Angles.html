0 Unit Vectors

A unit vector has magnitude exactly $1$ — it carries pure direction information, with no size. Converting any nonzero vector to a unit vector is the standard first move whenever you only care about a direction, not a magnitude.

Key formula

$$\hat{\mathbf{v}} = \frac{\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|}.$$

The standard basis vectors $\mathbf{i}=(1,0,0)$, $\mathbf{j}=(0,1,0)$, $\mathbf{k}=(0,0,1)$ are already unit vectors, each pointing along one coordinate axis.

Worked example

Find the unit vector in the direction of $\mathbf{v}=(2,-3,6)$, and use it to find a vector of magnitude $10$ pointing the same way.

$|\mathbf{v}|=\sqrt{4+9+36}=\sqrt{49}=7$.

$\hat{\mathbf{v}} = \dfrac17(2,-3,6) = \left(\dfrac27,-\dfrac37,\dfrac67\right)$.

A vector of magnitude $10$ in the same direction: $10\hat{\mathbf{v}} = \left(\dfrac{20}{7},-\dfrac{30}{7},\dfrac{60}{7}\right)$.

Practice 1

Find the unit vector in the direction of $(0,3,4)$.

Solution

$|\mathbf{v}|=5$. Unit vector: $(0,0.6,0.8)$.

Practice 2

Find the unit vector in the direction of $(1,1,1)$.

Solution

$|\mathbf{v}|=\sqrt3$. Unit vector: $\dfrac{1}{\sqrt3}(1,1,1)$.

Practice 3

A force of magnitude $50$ N acts in the direction of $(3,4,0)$. Express this force as a vector.

Solution

Unit direction: $\dfrac15(3,4,0)$. Force vector: $50\times\dfrac15(3,4,0)=(30,40,0)$ N.

Practice 4

Find the unit vector in the direction opposite to $(2,-1,2)$.

Solution

$|\mathbf{v}|=3$. Original unit vector $\dfrac13(2,-1,2)$; opposite direction: $-\dfrac13(2,-1,2)=\left(-\dfrac23,\dfrac13,-\dfrac23\right)$.

Practice 5

A cable runs from $A=(0,0,0)$ to $B=(6,8,0)$ (metres) under tension $200$ N. Express the tension as a vector acting along $\overrightarrow{AB}$.

Solution

$\overrightarrow{AB}=(6,8,0)$, $|\overrightarrow{AB}|=10$. Unit vector: $(0.6,0.8,0)$. Tension vector: $200(0.6,0.8,0)=(120,160,0)$ N.

1 Scalar and Vector Projections

Projecting $\mathbf{a}$ onto $\mathbf{b}$ answers the question: "how much of $\mathbf{a}$ points in the direction of $\mathbf{b}$?" There are two versions of the answer — a scalar (just the size of that component) and a vector (the actual component vector itself).

Key formulas

Scalar projection of $\mathbf{a}$ onto $\mathbf{b}$: $$\text{comp}_{\mathbf{b}}\mathbf{a} = \frac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{b}|}.$$

Vector projection of $\mathbf{a}$ onto $\mathbf{b}$: $$\text{proj}_{\mathbf{b}}\mathbf{a} = \frac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{b}|^2}\,\mathbf{b} = (\text{comp}_{\mathbf{b}}\mathbf{a})\,\hat{\mathbf{b}}.$$

Worked example

Find the scalar and vector projections of $\mathbf{a}=(4,3,0)$ onto $\mathbf{b}=(1,1,0)$.

$\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=4+3+0=7$. $|\mathbf{b}|=\sqrt2$.

Scalar projection: $\text{comp}_{\mathbf{b}}\mathbf{a} = \dfrac{7}{\sqrt2}$.

Vector projection: $\text{proj}_{\mathbf{b}}\mathbf{a} = \dfrac{7}{2}(1,1,0) = \left(\dfrac72,\dfrac72,0\right)$.

Practice 6

Find the scalar projection of $\mathbf{a}=(3,4,0)$ onto $\mathbf{b}=(0,1,0)$.

Solution

$\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=4$, $|\mathbf{b}|=1$. Scalar projection $=4$.

Practice 7

Find the vector projection of $\mathbf{a}=(2,2,1)$ onto $\mathbf{b}=(1,0,0)$.

Solution

$\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=2$, $|\mathbf{b}|^2=1$. Vector projection $=\dfrac21(1,0,0)=(2,0,0)$.

Practice 8

Find the scalar projection of $\mathbf{a}=(1,2,2)$ onto $\mathbf{b}=(2,-1,2)$.

Solution

$\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=2-2+4=4$. $|\mathbf{b}|=3$. Scalar projection $=\dfrac43$.

Practice 9

A ramp's direction is $\mathbf{d}=(4,3,0)$ (metres, horizontal-vertical cross-section), and gravity acts as $\mathbf{g}=(0,-9.8,0)$. Find the scalar projection of $\mathbf{g}$ onto $\mathbf{d}$ (the component of gravity along the ramp).

Solution

$\mathbf{g}\cdot\mathbf{d}=0(4)+(-9.8)(3)+0=-29.4$. $|\mathbf{d}|=5$. Scalar projection $=\dfrac{-29.4}{5}=-5.88$ (negative, meaning it acts down the slope, opposite to the direction $\mathbf{d}$ was measured).

Practice 10

Find the vector projection of $\mathbf{a}=(5,0,0)$ onto $\mathbf{b}=(3,4,0)$, and hence decompose $\mathbf{a}$ into components parallel and perpendicular to $\mathbf{b}$.

Solution

$\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=15$, $|\mathbf{b}|^2=25$. Vector projection (parallel component): $\dfrac{15}{25}(3,4,0)=(1.8,2.4,0)$. Perpendicular component: $\mathbf{a}-\text{proj}=(5-1.8,0-2.4,0)=(3.2,-2.4,0)$.

2 Angle Between Vectors: Further Practice

Building directly on the dot-product angle formula from last week, this section applies it to slightly more involved settings: angles in triangles formed by position vectors, and direction angles made with the coordinate axes themselves.

Key idea — direction angles

The angles a vector $\mathbf{v}=(v_1,v_2,v_3)$ makes with the positive $x$, $y$, $z$ axes satisfy $$\cos\alpha = \frac{v_1}{|\mathbf{v}|}, \quad \cos\beta=\frac{v_2}{|\mathbf{v}|}, \quad \cos\gamma=\frac{v_3}{|\mathbf{v}|}.$$ These are found simply by taking the dot product of $\mathbf{v}$ with each standard basis vector in turn.

Worked example

Find the angle that $\mathbf{v}=(1,2,2)$ makes with the positive $z$-axis.

$|\mathbf{v}|=\sqrt{1+4+4}=3$.

$\cos\gamma = \dfrac{v_3}{|\mathbf{v}|} = \dfrac{2}{3}$.

$\gamma = \cos^{-1}\!\left(\dfrac23\right)\approx48.2^\circ$.

Practice 11

Find the angle that $(3,0,4)$ makes with the positive $x$-axis.

Solution

$|\mathbf{v}|=5$. $\cos\alpha=\dfrac35 \Rightarrow \alpha=\cos^{-1}(0.6)\approx53.1^\circ$.

Practice 12

Find the angle that $(1,1,0)$ makes with the positive $y$-axis.

Solution

$|\mathbf{v}|=\sqrt2$. $\cos\beta=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt2} \Rightarrow \beta=45^\circ$.

Practice 13

For triangle with vertices $A=(0,0,0)$, $B=(2,0,0)$, $C=(0,2,0)$, find the interior angle at vertex $A$, using vectors $\overrightarrow{AB}$ and $\overrightarrow{AC}$.

Solution

$\overrightarrow{AB}=(2,0,0)$, $\overrightarrow{AC}=(0,2,0)$. Dot product $=0$. Angle $=90^\circ$.

Practice 14

For triangle with vertices $A=(1,0,0)$, $B=(0,1,0)$, $C=(0,0,1)$, find the interior angle at vertex $A$.

Solution

$\overrightarrow{AB}=(-1,1,0)$, $\overrightarrow{AC}=(-1,0,1)$. Dot product $=1+0+0=1$. $|\overrightarrow{AB}|=\sqrt2$, $|\overrightarrow{AC}|=\sqrt2$. $\cos\theta=\dfrac{1}{2} \Rightarrow \theta=60^\circ$.

Practice 15

Verify that the sum $\cos^2\alpha+\cos^2\beta+\cos^2\gamma=1$ for the direction angles of $\mathbf{v}=(2,3,6)$.

Solution

$|\mathbf{v}|=\sqrt{4+9+36}=7$. $\cos\alpha=\dfrac27,\ \cos\beta=\dfrac37,\ \cos\gamma=\dfrac67$. Sum of squares: $\dfrac{4+9+36}{49}=\dfrac{49}{49}=1$. ✓ (This identity always holds — it's exactly $\dfrac{v_1^2+v_2^2+v_3^2}{|\mathbf{v}|^2}=1$.)

Practice 16

Given A=[[1,5],[5,6]], (a) find det(A), (b) state invertibility, (c) compute A^(-1) if possible.

Solution

det(A)=1*6-5*5.

If det!=0 matrix is invertible.

Use 2x2 inverse formula (1/det)[[d,-b],[-c,a]].

Practice 17

Row-reduce augmented matrix to RREF, classify system (unique/infinite/inconsistent), and write parametric solution if needed.

Solution

Perform elimination to echelon then pivot normalization/back-elimination.

Check for row [0 0 ... | nonzero] (inconsistent) or free variables (infinite).

Express free-variable family in vector/parametric form.

Practice 18

For vectors u and v in R^3, compute dot and cross products, then determine angle and area of parallelogram spanned by them.

Solution

u·v gives cos(theta)= (u·v)/(|u||v|).

u×v gives normal vector; |u×v| is parallelogram area.

Check orthogonality (dot=0) or parallelism (cross=0) where relevant.

Practice 19

Find eigenvalues and eigenvectors of a 2x2 matrix, then diagonalize if eigenvectors are independent.

Solution

Solve det(A-lambda I)=0 for eigenvalues.

Solve (A-lambda I)v=0 for each eigenvector basis.

If two independent eigenvectors exist, form P and D with A=PDP^{-1}.

Practice 20

Given two lines in vector form, determine if they intersect, are parallel, or skew; compute minimum distance if skew.

Solution

Equate component forms with parameters; test consistency.

Parallel if direction vectors scalar multiples.

If skew, distance = |(r2-r1)·(d1×d2)|/|d1×d2|.

Practice 21

Construct plane equation through three points, then compute angle between this plane and another given plane.

Solution

Form two direction vectors in plane and cross them for normal n.

Use n·(r-r0)=0 for plane equation.

Angle between planes = angle between normals via dot formula.

Practice 22

Use Cramer's rule to solve 3x3 system and compare with determinant test for singular/non-singular behavior.

Solution

Compute main determinant Delta.

If Delta!=0, x=Delta_x/Delta etc.

If Delta=0 inspect numerator determinants to classify infinite vs none.

Practice 23

For a transformation matrix T in R^2, map basis vectors, sketch geometric effect, and infer determinant sign/area scaling.

Solution

Images of e1,e2 are matrix columns.

Parallelogram area scale = |det(T)|.

det<0 implies orientation reversal (reflection component).
