Projectile Motion

A from-scratch lesson on projectile motion: how launch speed and angle determine everything about the flight, and how eliminating time from the equations of motion gives the shape of the path itself. Everything is derived from the same two starting facts, constant horizontal velocity and constant downward acceleration. Solutions stay hidden until you click.

1 Time equations

A projectile launched with speed $v_0$ at angle $\theta$ above the horizontal experiences two completely independent motions at once: horizontally, nothing pushes or slows it (ignoring air resistance), so it moves at constant velocity; vertically, gravity pulls it down with constant acceleration $g$, exactly like an object thrown straight up. The entire trick of this section is that these two directions never interact, you can analyse each one separately using nothing more than the constant-velocity and constant-acceleration formulas you already know, then combine the results.

Key idea: velocity and position as functions of time

Splitting the initial velocity into components, $v_{0x}=v_0\cos\theta$ (horizontal) and $v_{0y}=v_0\sin\theta$ (vertical):

$$v_x(t) = v_0\cos\theta \quad\text{(constant)}, \qquad v_y(t) = v_0\sin\theta - gt.$$

$$x(t) = (v_0\cos\theta)\,t, \qquad y(t) = (v_0\sin\theta)\,t - \frac12gt^2.$$

(These position formulas are just $x=x_0+v_{0x}t$ and $y=y_0+v_{0y}t-\frac12gt^2$ with the launch point taken as the origin, $x_0=y_0=0$.)

Why time to reach maximum height, time of flight, and range follow directly

The projectile reaches its maximum height at the instant $v_y=0$ (it is momentarily neither rising nor falling, exactly as a ball thrown straight up pauses at its peak): setting $v_0\sin\theta-gt=0$ gives $t_{\max} = \dfrac{v_0\sin\theta}g$. Substituting this back into $y(t)$ gives the maximum height itself, $H = \dfrac{(v_0\sin\theta)^2}{2g}$.

The time of flight $T$ (assuming launch and landing at the same height) is when $y(t)$ returns to $0$: solving $0=(v_0\sin\theta)t-\frac12gt^2 = t\left(v_0\sin\theta-\frac12gt\right)$ gives $t=0$ (the launch itself) or $t=T=\dfrac{2v_0\sin\theta}g$, exactly double $t_{\max}$, a reflection of the fact that (again ignoring air resistance) the upward and downward halves of the trajectory are perfect time-reverses of each other.

The range $R$ (horizontal distance travelled) is simply $x(T)$, the constant horizontal velocity multiplied by the total flight time: $R = (v_0\cos\theta)\cdot\dfrac{2v_0\sin\theta}g = \dfrac{2v_0^2\sin\theta\cos\theta}g = \dfrac{v_0^2\sin(2\theta)}g$, using the double-angle identity $2\sin\theta\cos\theta=\sin(2\theta)$.

Worked example

A projectile is launched at $v_0=25$m/s, at an angle $\theta$ with $\sin\theta=\frac45$, $\cos\theta=\frac35$ (a 3-4-5 triangle angle, about $53.13^\circ$), with $g=10$m/s$^2$. Find the velocity components, the time and height of the peak, and the time of flight and range.

$v_{0x} = 25\times\frac35 = 15$m/s. $\ v_{0y} = 25\times\frac45=20$m/s.

$t_{\max} = \dfrac{20}{10} = 2$s. $\ H = \dfrac{20^2}{2\times10} = \dfrac{400}{20} = 20$m.

$T = 2t_{\max} = 4$s. $\ R = v_{0x}\cdot T = 15\times4 = 60$m.

Notice every number came out as a clean whole number, this was a deliberately chosen "nice" 3-4-5 triangle angle so the arithmetic stays transparent; real problems usually need a calculator for $\sin\theta$ and $\cos\theta$, but the method is identical.

Practice 1

A projectile is launched at $v_0=20$m/s at $\theta=30^\circ$, with $g=10$m/s$^2$. Find $v_{0x}$ and $v_{0y}$.

Solution

$v_{0x} = 20\cos30^\circ = 20\times\dfrac{\sqrt3}2 = 10\sqrt3 \approx 17.32$m/s.

$v_{0y} = 20\sin30^\circ = 20\times\dfrac12 = 10$m/s.

Practice 2

Using Practice 1's numbers ($v_0=20$m/s, $\theta=30^\circ$, $g=10$m/s$^2$), find the time to reach maximum height and the maximum height.

Solution

$t_{\max} = \dfrac{v_{0y}}g = \dfrac{10}{10} = 1$s.

$H = \dfrac{v_{0y}^2}{2g} = \dfrac{100}{20} = 5$m.

Practice 3

Continuing Practice 1-2, find the total time of flight and the range.

Solution

$T = 2t_{\max} = 2$s.

$R = v_{0x}\cdot T = 10\sqrt3\times2 = 20\sqrt3 \approx 34.64$m.

(Check against the range formula directly: $R=\dfrac{v_0^2\sin(2\theta)}g = \dfrac{400\sin60^\circ}{10} = \dfrac{400\times\frac{\sqrt3}2}{10} = 20\sqrt3$, matching.)

Practice 4

A ball is thrown at $v_0=40$m/s at $\theta=45^\circ$, with $g=9.8$m/s$^2$. Find the time of flight and the maximum height.

Solution

$v_{0y} = 40\sin45^\circ = 40\times\dfrac{\sqrt2}2 = 20\sqrt2 \approx 28.28$m/s.

$T = \dfrac{2v_{0y}}g = \dfrac{2\times20\sqrt2}{9.8} \approx \dfrac{56.57}{9.8} \approx 5.77$s.

$H = \dfrac{v_{0y}^2}{2g} = \dfrac{800}{19.6} \approx 40.82$m.

Practice 5

Find $x(t)$ and $y(t)$ for a projectile launched at $v_0=30$m/s, $\theta=60^\circ$, $g=10$m/s$^2$, and evaluate the position at $t=1$s.

Solution

$v_{0x}=30\cos60^\circ=15$m/s, $\ v_{0y}=30\sin60^\circ = 15\sqrt3$m/s.

$x(t)=15t$, $\ y(t)=15\sqrt3\,t - 5t^2$.

At $t=1$: $x(1)=15$m. $\ y(1) = 15\sqrt3-5 \approx 25.98-5 = 20.98$m.

Practice 6

For the projectile in Practice 5, find $v_y$ at $t=1$s, and state whether the projectile is still rising or already falling at that instant.

Solution

$v_y(t) = 15\sqrt3-10t$. At $t=1$: $v_y = 15\sqrt3-10 \approx 25.98-10 = 15.98$m/s.

Since $v_y>0$, the projectile is still rising at $t=1$s, it has not yet reached its peak (peak occurs at $t_{\max}=\dfrac{15\sqrt3}{10}\approx2.6$s, which is later than $t=1$).

Practice 7

Two projectiles are launched with the same speed $v_0=20$m/s, one at $\theta=30^\circ$ and one at $\theta=60^\circ$, with $g=10$m/s$^2$. Show that they have the same range, without fully recomputing each one from scratch (use the range formula's structure).

Solution

$R=\dfrac{v_0^2\sin(2\theta)}g$. For $\theta=30^\circ$: $2\theta=60^\circ$, $\sin60^\circ=\dfrac{\sqrt3}2$. For $\theta=60^\circ$: $2\theta=120^\circ$, $\sin120^\circ=\dfrac{\sqrt3}2$ as well (since $\sin(180^\circ-x)=\sin x$, and $120^\circ=180^\circ-60^\circ$).

Since $\sin(2\theta)$ is identical for both angles, and $v_0$ and $g$ are the same, the ranges must be equal: $R=\dfrac{400\times\frac{\sqrt3}2}{10}=20\sqrt3$m for both. This is a general fact: any two launch angles that add up to $90^\circ$ (here $30^\circ+60^\circ=90^\circ$) give the same range, since $\sin(2\theta)$ and $\sin(2(90^\circ-\theta))=\sin(180^\circ-2\theta)=\sin(2\theta)$ always match.

Practice 8

At what launch angle $\theta$ is the range $R=\dfrac{v_0^2\sin(2\theta)}g$ maximised, for a fixed launch speed $v_0$? Explain your reasoning.

Solution

$R$ is proportional to $\sin(2\theta)$, and $\sin$ reaches its largest possible value, $1$, when its argument is $90^\circ$. So $R$ is maximised when $2\theta=90^\circ$, i.e.\ $\theta=45^\circ$. This matches the everyday intuition that throwing something at a $45^\circ$ angle sends it the farthest, now justified directly from the range formula rather than just asserted.

Practice 9

A projectile launched at $g=10$m/s$^2$ has a maximum height of $H=45$m. Find $v_{0y}$ (the initial vertical velocity component), and the total time of flight.

Solution

From $H=\dfrac{v_{0y}^2}{2g}$: $45 = \dfrac{v_{0y}^2}{20} \Rightarrow v_{0y}^2 = 900 \Rightarrow v_{0y}=30$m/s.

$T = \dfrac{2v_{0y}}g = \dfrac{60}{10}=6$s.

Practice 10

A projectile is launched at $v_0=50$m/s, $\theta=37^\circ$ (take $\sin37^\circ\approx0.6$, $\cos37^\circ\approx0.8$), $g=10$m/s$^2$. Find the range.

Solution

$v_{0x}=50\times0.8=40$m/s, $\ v_{0y}=50\times0.6=30$m/s.

$T=\dfrac{2\times30}{10}=6$s. $\ R = v_{0x}\times T = 40\times6=240$m.

2 Equation of the path

Section 1 described the projectile's motion as two separate functions of time, $x(t)$ and $y(t)$. But the actual visible shape of the trajectory, the curve you would see if you traced the path in the air, is a relationship between $x$ and $y$ directly, with no reference to time at all. Eliminating $t$ between the two time-equations gives exactly that curve, and it turns out to always be a parabola.

Key idea

$$y = x\tan\theta - \frac{g}{2v_0^2\cos^2\theta}\,x^2.$$

This is a quadratic in $x$ with negative leading coefficient (since $g,v_0^2,\cos^2\theta$ are all positive), confirming that the path is a downward-opening parabola, exactly the shape you would sketch by eye for a thrown object.

Deriving the equation of the path

Start from the two time-equations: $x=(v_0\cos\theta)t$ and $y=(v_0\sin\theta)t-\frac12gt^2$.

Solve the $x$ equation for $t$: $t = \dfrac{x}{v_0\cos\theta}$.

Substitute into the $y$ equation:

$$y = (v_0\sin\theta)\cdot\frac{x}{v_0\cos\theta} - \frac12g\left(\frac{x}{v_0\cos\theta}\right)^2.$$

The first term simplifies since $\dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}=\tan\theta$ and the $v_0$'s cancel: $x\tan\theta$. The second term becomes $\dfrac{gx^2}{2v_0^2\cos^2\theta}$. So

$$y = x\tan\theta - \frac{g}{2v_0^2\cos^2\theta}\,x^2,$$

exactly the key idea's formula. Every quantity here ($\theta$, $v_0$, $g$) is a constant fixed by the launch conditions, so this really is an equation purely in $x$ and $y$, with time nowhere in sight, exactly the visible shape of the path.

Worked example

Using the Section 1 worked example's numbers ($v_0=25$m/s, $\sin\theta=\frac45$, $\cos\theta=\frac35$, $g=10$m/s$^2$), find the equation of the path, and verify it passes through the launch point, the peak, and the landing point already found.

$\tan\theta = \dfrac{4/5}{3/5}=\dfrac43$. $\ v_0^2\cos^2\theta = 625\times\dfrac9{25}=225$.

$$y = \frac43x - \frac{10}{2\times225}x^2 = \frac43x - \frac{x^2}{45}.$$

At $x=0$: $y=0$ ✓ (the launch point). At $x=60$ (the range found in Section 1): $y=\dfrac43(60)-\dfrac{3600}{45} = 80-80=0$ ✓ (lands back at ground level). At $x=30$ (halfway to the range, where the peak should sit by symmetry): $y=\dfrac43(30)-\dfrac{900}{45}=40-20=20$ ✓, exactly matching the maximum height $H=20$m found in Section 1.

Practice 11

Find the equation of the path for a projectile launched at $v_0=20$m/s, $\theta=30^\circ$, $g=10$m/s$^2$ (Practice 1's numbers).

Solution

$\tan30^\circ = \dfrac1{\sqrt3}$. $\ v_0^2\cos^2\theta = 400\times\dfrac34=300$.

$$y = \frac{x}{\sqrt3} - \frac{10}{2\times300}x^2 = \frac{x}{\sqrt3} - \frac{x^2}{60}.$$

Practice 12

Using Practice 11's path equation, verify that $y=0$ at both $x=0$ and $x=20\sqrt3$ (the range found in Practice 3).

Solution

At $x=0$: $y=0-0=0$ ✓.

At $x=20\sqrt3$: $y = \dfrac{20\sqrt3}{\sqrt3} - \dfrac{(20\sqrt3)^2}{60} = 20 - \dfrac{1200}{60} = 20-20=0$ ✓.

Practice 13

A projectile has path equation $y=x-\dfrac{x^2}{100}$ (with $x,y$ in metres). Find where it lands (the nonzero root), and its maximum height (using the parabola's symmetry, the peak sits halfway between the two roots).

Solution

Set $y=0$: $x-\dfrac{x^2}{100}=0 \Rightarrow x\left(1-\dfrac{x}{100}\right)=0 \Rightarrow x=0$ or $x=100$. Lands at $x=100$m.

By symmetry, the peak is at $x=50$: $y=50-\dfrac{2500}{100}=50-25=25$m.

Practice 14

Find the equation of the path for a projectile launched at $v_0=50$m/s, $\theta=37^\circ$ ($\sin37^\circ\approx0.6$, $\cos37^\circ\approx0.8$), $g=10$m/s$^2$ (Practice 10's numbers), and verify it gives $y=0$ at the range found there ($x=240$m).

Solution

$\tan37^\circ \approx \dfrac{0.6}{0.8}=0.75$. $\ v_0^2\cos^2\theta = 2500\times0.64=1600$.

$$y = 0.75x - \frac{10}{3200}x^2 = 0.75x - \frac{x^2}{320}.$$

At $x=240$: $y=0.75(240)-\dfrac{240^2}{320} = 180-\dfrac{57600}{320}=180-180=0$ ✓.

Practice 15

A path equation is $y=2x-0.1x^2$. Rewrite it in the standard form $y=x\tan\theta - \dfrac{g}{2v_0^2\cos^2\theta}x^2$ terms by identifying $\tan\theta$, and explain what a coefficient comparison like this tells you even without knowing $v_0$ or $g$ individually.

Solution

Matching the linear coefficient: $\tan\theta=2$, so $\theta=\arctan2\approx63.43^\circ$, fully determined without needing to know $v_0$ or $g$ at all. The quadratic coefficient, $\dfrac{g}{2v_0^2\cos^2\theta}=0.1$, links $v_0$ and $g$ together but cannot separate them individually from this equation alone; you would need one more piece of information (such as knowing $g$, or measuring $v_0$ directly) to pin down $v_0$ on its own. This illustrates that the launch angle is recoverable from the path shape alone, while speed and gravity are only recoverable as a combined ratio.

Practice 16

Using the path equation from Practice 15 ($y=2x-0.1x^2$), find the $x$-coordinate of the peak and the maximum height, using the same symmetry idea as Practice 13.

Solution

Find the landing point: $2x-0.1x^2=0 \Rightarrow x(2-0.1x)=0 \Rightarrow x=0$ or $x=20$. Peak sits halfway, at $x=10$.

$y(10) = 2(10)-0.1(100) = 20-10=10$m.

Practice 17

Explain why the path equation $y=x\tan\theta - \dfrac{g}{2v_0^2\cos^2\theta}x^2$ has no linear term missing and no constant term at all (i.e.\ why there is no "$+c$" added on), tying your answer back to the physical setup.

Solution

The constant term of the path equation is exactly its $y$-value at $x=0$, and $x=0$ corresponds to $t=0$, the instant of launch, where by construction $y(0)=0$ (the launch point is taken as the origin). Since there is no constant added, the parabola automatically passes through the origin, exactly matching the physical fact that the projectile starts its flight from height $0$ at horizontal position $0$. If the launch point were elevated (fired from a height $y_0>0$, off a cliff for instance), the path equation would need an explicit "$+y_0$" term added, since then $y(0)=y_0\neq0$.

Practice 18

A projectile is launched at $v_0=30$m/s, $\theta=60^\circ$, $g=10$m/s$^2$. Find the equation of the path, and use it to find the height when $x=10$m.

Solution

$\tan60^\circ=\sqrt3$. $\ v_0^2\cos^2\theta = 900\times\dfrac14=225$.

$$y = \sqrt3\,x - \frac{10}{450}x^2 = \sqrt3\,x - \frac{x^2}{45}.$$

At $x=10$: $y = 10\sqrt3 - \dfrac{100}{45} \approx 17.32-2.22 \approx 15.10$m.

Practice 19

Two projectiles are launched from the same point with the same $v_0$, at angles $\theta$ and $90^\circ-\theta$ (complementary angles). Explain, using the path equation's quadratic coefficient $\dfrac{g}{2v_0^2\cos^2\theta}$, why the two parabolas are generally not mirror images of each other (even though Practice 7 showed they share the same range).

Solution

The linear coefficient $\tan\theta$ and the quadratic coefficient $\dfrac{g}{2v_0^2\cos^2\theta}$ both depend on $\theta$ in a way that is not simply "flip the sign" under $\theta\to90^\circ-\theta$: for instance $\tan\theta$ and $\tan(90^\circ-\theta)=\cot\theta=\dfrac1{\tan\theta}$ are reciprocals, not equal (unless $\theta=45^\circ$, where they coincide). So while both parabolas start and end at the same two points on the ground (equal range, from Practice 7), one of them is generally taller and narrower (the steeper launch angle) while the other is shorter and wider (the shallower angle), not mirror images, they are two genuinely different parabolas that happen to share the same two roots.

Practice 20

A projectile's path equation is $y=1.5x-0.05x^2$. Find $v_0$ given that $g=10$m/s$^2$ (first find $\theta$ from the linear term, then use the quadratic term).

Solution

$\tan\theta=1.5$, so $\cos^2\theta = \dfrac1{1+\tan^2\theta} = \dfrac1{1+2.25}=\dfrac1{3.25}$ (using $\sec^2\theta=1+\tan^2\theta$).

From the quadratic term: $\dfrac{g}{2v_0^2\cos^2\theta}=0.05 \Rightarrow v_0^2 = \dfrac{g}{2\times0.05\times\cos^2\theta} = \dfrac{10}{0.1\times\frac1{3.25}} = \dfrac{10\times3.25}{0.1} = 325$.

$v_0 = \sqrt{325} \approx 18.03$m/s.
