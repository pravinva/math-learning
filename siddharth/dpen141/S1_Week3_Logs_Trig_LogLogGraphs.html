0 Laws of Logarithms

A logarithm just answers the question "what power do I raise the base to, to get this number?" Every log law is a restatement of an index law from Week 1, translated into log language.

Key laws

$\log_a(xy)=\log_a x+\log_a y$, $\quad \log_a\!\left(\dfrac{x}{y}\right)=\log_a x-\log_a y$, $\quad \log_a(x^n)=n\log_a x$.

Change of base: $\log_a x = \dfrac{\log_b x}{\log_b a}$ for any convenient base $b$ (usually $10$ or $e$, matching your calculator).

$\log_a x$ is only defined for $x>0$, and $\log_a a=1$, $\ \log_a 1=0$.

Worked example

Solve $x+\ln y=\ln(x+2y)+3$ for $y$ in terms of $x$.

Isolate the logs: $\ln y - \ln(x+2y) = 3-x$.

Combine using the quotient law: $\ln\!\left(\dfrac{y}{x+2y}\right)=3-x$.

Exponentiate both sides: $\dfrac{y}{x+2y}=e^{3-x}$.

$y = e^{3-x}(x+2y) \ \Rightarrow\ y = xe^{3-x}+2ye^{3-x} \ \Rightarrow\ y-2ye^{3-x}=xe^{3-x} \ \Rightarrow\ y(1-2e^{3-x})=xe^{3-x}$.

$$y = \frac{xe^{3-x}}{1-2e^{3-x}}.$$

Practice 1

Simplify $\log_2 32 - \log_2 4$.

Solution

$\log_2 32=5$, $\log_2 4=2$. Difference $=3$. (Or directly: $\log_2(32/4)=\log_2 8=3$.)

Practice 2

Solve $\log_3(2x-1)=2$ for $x$.

Solution

$2x-1=3^2=9 \Rightarrow x=5$.

Practice 3

Solve $2\log x - \log(x-3)=\log 4$ for $x$ (base $10$), checking for extraneous solutions.

Solution

$\log(x^2)-\log(x-3)=\log4 \Rightarrow \log\!\left(\dfrac{x^2}{x-3}\right)=\log4 \Rightarrow \dfrac{x^2}{x-3}=4 \Rightarrow x^2=4x-12 \Rightarrow x^2-4x+12=0$. Discriminant $16-48<0$: no real solutions. (Checking domain: we'd also need $x>0$ and $x>3$ for the logs to be defined, but the equation itself has no real roots at all.)

Practice 4

Solve the equation $x+\ln 2y=\ln(x+y)-3$ for $y$ in terms of $x$.

Solution

$\ln2y-\ln(x+y)=-3-x \Rightarrow \ln\!\left(\dfrac{2y}{x+y}\right)=-3-x \Rightarrow \dfrac{2y}{x+y}=e^{-3-x}$. So $2y=e^{-3-x}(x+y) \Rightarrow 2y-ye^{-3-x}=xe^{-3-x} \Rightarrow y(2-e^{-3-x})=xe^{-3-x} \Rightarrow y=\dfrac{xe^{-3-x}}{2-e^{-3-x}}$.

Practice 5

An earthquake's magnitude on the Richter scale is $M=\log_{10}\!\left(\dfrac{A}{A_0}\right)$. If earthquake $X$ has amplitude $1000$ times that of earthquake $Y$, find $M_X-M_Y$.

Solution

$M_X-M_Y = \log_{10}(A_X/A_0)-\log_{10}(A_Y/A_0)=\log_{10}(A_X/A_Y)=\log_{10}(1000)=3$.

Practice 6

Solve $e^{2y}+5x=e^x e^{2y}-2$ for $y$ in terms of $x$.

Solution

Collect $e^{2y}$ terms: $e^{2y}-e^xe^{2y}=-2-5x \Rightarrow e^{2y}(1-e^x)=-(2+5x) \Rightarrow e^{2y}=\dfrac{2+5x}{e^x-1}$. Taking $\ln$: $y=\dfrac12\ln\!\left(\dfrac{5x+2}{e^x-1}\right)$.

Practice 7

Simplify $\log_5 x + \log_5(x-4) = \log_5 21$ and solve for $x$, checking domain restrictions.

Solution

$\log_5[x(x-4)]=\log_5 21 \Rightarrow x^2-4x=21 \Rightarrow x^2-4x-21=0 \Rightarrow (x-7)(x+3)=0 \Rightarrow x=7$ or $x=-3$. Domain requires $x>0$ and $x>4$, so only $x=7$ is valid.

1 Basic Trigonometry

Trigonometric identities let you rewrite an expression in a more useful form without changing its value — essential for solving equations, simplifying, and (later in the course) integrating. A small set of core identities generates almost everything else you need.

Core identities

Pythagorean: $\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta=1$. Compound angle: $\sin(A\pm B)=\sin A\cos B\pm\cos A\sin B$, $\ \cos(A\pm B)=\cos A\cos B\mp\sin A\sin B$. Double angle: $\sin2A=2\sin A\cos A$, $\ \cos2A=\cos^2A-\sin^2A=1-2\sin^2A=2\cos^2A-1$.

Worked example

Solve $2\sin^2x - \sin x - 1=0$ for $0\le x\le 2\pi$.

Let $u=\sin x$: $2u^2-u-1=0 \Rightarrow (2u+1)(u-1)=0 \Rightarrow u=-\tfrac12$ or $u=1$.

$\sin x = 1 \Rightarrow x=\dfrac{\pi}{2}$.

$\sin x = -\tfrac12 \Rightarrow x = \pi+\dfrac{\pi}{6}=\dfrac{7\pi}{6}$ or $x=2\pi-\dfrac{\pi}{6}=\dfrac{11\pi}{6}$ (third and fourth quadrants, where sine is negative).

Practice 8

Solve $2\cos x - 1 = 0$ for $0\le x\le2\pi$.

Solution

$\cos x=\tfrac12 \Rightarrow x=\dfrac{\pi}{3}$ or $x=2\pi-\dfrac{\pi}{3}=\dfrac{5\pi}{3}$.

Practice 9

Simplify $\dfrac{\sin2\theta}{1+\cos2\theta}$ using double angle identities.

Solution

$\sin2\theta=2\sin\theta\cos\theta$, $\ 1+\cos2\theta=1+(2\cos^2\theta-1)=2\cos^2\theta$. Ratio: $\dfrac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{2\cos^2\theta}=\dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}=\tan\theta$.

Practice 10

Solve $\sin^2x = \cos^2x$ for $0\le x\le2\pi$.

Solution

Using $\sin^2x-\cos^2x=0 \Rightarrow -\cos2x=0 \Rightarrow \cos2x=0 \Rightarrow 2x=\dfrac{\pi}{2},\dfrac{3\pi}{2},\dfrac{5\pi}{2},\dfrac{7\pi}{2} \Rightarrow x=\dfrac{\pi}{4},\dfrac{3\pi}{4},\dfrac{5\pi}{4},\dfrac{7\pi}{4}$.

Practice 11

Show that $\cot 3\theta = \dfrac{\cot^3\theta-3\cot\theta}{3\cot^2\theta-1}$, given $\sin3\theta=3\sin\theta-4\sin^3\theta$ and $\cos3\theta=4\cos^3\theta-3\cos\theta$.

Solution

$\cot3\theta=\dfrac{\cos3\theta}{\sin3\theta}=\dfrac{4\cos^3\theta-3\cos\theta}{3\sin\theta-4\sin^3\theta}$. Divide numerator and denominator by $\sin^3\theta$: numerator becomes $4\cot^3\theta-3\cot\theta\csc^2\theta=4\cot^3\theta-3\cot\theta(1+\cot^2\theta)=\cot^3\theta-3\cot\theta$ (using $\csc^2\theta=1+\cot^2\theta$); denominator becomes $3\csc^2\theta-4=3(1+\cot^2\theta)-4=3\cot^2\theta-1$. So $\cot3\theta=\dfrac{\cot^3\theta-3\cot\theta}{3\cot^2\theta-1}$, as required.

Practice 12

Find all solutions to $\tan x = \sqrt3$ for $0\le x\le2\pi$.

Solution

Reference angle $\dfrac{\pi}{3}$; tangent is positive in quadrants I and III: $x=\dfrac{\pi}{3}$ or $x=\pi+\dfrac{\pi}{3}=\dfrac{4\pi}{3}$.

Practice 13

A wave is modelled by $y(t)=3\sin(2t)+4\cos(2t)$. Write $y(t)$ in the form $R\sin(2t+\phi)$, finding $R$ and $\phi$ (in radians, to 2 d.p.).

Solution

$R=\sqrt{3^2+4^2}=5$. $\tan\phi=\dfrac{4}{3} \Rightarrow \phi\approx0.93$ rad. So $y(t)\approx5\sin(2t+0.93)$.

2 Log-Log Graphs and Fitting Power/Exponential Laws to Data

A huge number of physical relationships have the form $y=Ax^n$ (a power law) or $y=Ab^x$ (exponential growth/decay). Neither is a straight line on ordinary axes, but taking logs of both sides turns each into a straight line — which is exactly what makes log-log and semi-log graphs so useful for fitting a model to experimental data.

Key idea

Power law $y=Ax^n$: taking $\log$ of both sides gives $\log y = \log A + n\log x$, a straight line in $(\log x,\log y)$ with gradient $n$ and $\log y$-intercept $\log A$.

Exponential $y=Ab^x$: taking $\log$ gives $\log y = \log A + x\log b$, a straight line in $(x,\log y)$ with gradient $\log b$ and $\log y$-intercept $\log A$.

Worked example

For the data set below, calculate a formula relating $x$ and $y$.

$x$: $0.03,\ 0.7,\ 5,\ 11,\ 85$. $\ y$: $0.14,\ 1.5,\ 7,\ 12,\ 56$. $\ \log x$: $-1.52,-0.15,0.70,1.04,1.93$. $\ \log y$: $-0.85,0.18,0.85,1.08,1.75$.

Plotting $\log y$ against $\log x$ gives an almost perfectly straight line, so a power law $y=Ax^n$ is suggested. Estimate the gradient using the first and last points: $n\approx\dfrac{1.75-(-0.85)}{1.93-(-1.52)}=\dfrac{2.60}{3.45}\approx0.75$.

The $\log y$-intercept (at $\log x=0$): reading off the line, $\log A \approx 0.18-0.75(-0.15)\approx0.29$, so $A\approx10^{0.29}\approx1.95$.

Model: $y\approx1.95\,x^{0.75}$.

Practice 14

A power law $y=Ax^n$ passes through $(1,3)$ and $(100,300)$. Find $n$ using $\log$s.

Solution

Taking logs: $\log y=\log A+n\log x$. At $(1,3)$: $\log3=\log A+0 \Rightarrow \log A=\log3$. At $(100,300)$: $\log300=\log3+2n \Rightarrow n=\dfrac{\log300-\log3}{2}=\dfrac{\log100}{2}=\dfrac{2}{2}=1$. (So actually $y=3x$, a linear, not curved, power law.)

Practice 15

An exponential model $y=Ab^x$ passes through $(0,5)$ and $(3,40)$. Find $A$ and $b$.

Solution

At $x=0$: $y=A=5$. At $x=3$: $40=5b^3 \Rightarrow b^3=8 \Rightarrow b=2$. Model: $y=5(2)^x$.

Practice 16

A quantity decays as $y=Ae^{-kx}$. Measurements give $y=100$ at $x=0$ and $y=13.5$ at $x=4$. Find $k$ (to 3 d.p.).

Solution

$A=100$. $13.5=100e^{-4k} \Rightarrow e^{-4k}=0.135 \Rightarrow -4k=\ln(0.135)\approx-2.002 \Rightarrow k\approx0.500$.

Practice 17

A student plots $\log y$ against $x$ (not $\log x$) for a data set and gets a straight line with gradient $0.3$ and intercept $1$. What is the relationship between $y$ and $x$?

Solution

$\log y = 1+0.3x \Rightarrow y = 10^{1+0.3x} = 10\cdot10^{0.3x} = 10\,(10^{0.3})^x \approx 10(1.995)^x$: an exponential relationship, $y\approx10(1.995)^x$.

Practice 18

Explain why a straight-line fit on a $\log$-$\log$ plot reveals a power-law relationship $y=Ax^n$, but the same straight-line test on a plot of $\log y$ versus (linear) $x$ instead reveals an exponential relationship.

Solution

$\log y=\log A+n\log x$ is linear in $\log x$ (both axes logged), matching a power law. $\log y=\log A + x\log b$ is linear in $x$ itself (only the $y$-axis logged), matching an exponential. The distinguishing test is which axis you log: both axes → power law; only the $y$-axis → exponential.

Practice 19

A cooling object's temperature excess $\theta$ (°C above ambient) is modelled as $\theta=\theta_0e^{-kt}$. If $\theta_0=80$ and $\theta=20$ after $10$ minutes, find $k$ and hence predict $\theta$ at $t=20$ minutes.

Solution

$20=80e^{-10k} \Rightarrow e^{-10k}=0.25 \Rightarrow k=\dfrac{-\ln0.25}{10}=\dfrac{\ln4}{10}\approx0.1386$. At $t=20$: $\theta=80e^{-0.1386(20)}=80e^{-2.773}\approx80(0.0625)=5$°C (as expected: two half-lives of the same decay, so temperature excess quarters twice, $80\to20\to5$).
