Matrices as Transformations

Every matrix you've added, scaled, and multiplied so far has been a grid of numbers obeying rules. This lesson is the reframe: a matrix is a function that moves every point in the plane somewhere else, all at once, in a completely predictable way. Once that picture is in place, matrix multiplication, non-commutativity, rank, and the determinant all stop being rules to memorise and start being things you can see. 0 A matrix is a function

In Matrix Fundamentals, you learned that $A\mathbf{x}$ means: take row $i$ of $A$, dot it with $\mathbf{x}$, and that gives you the $i$th entry of the output. That's the mechanical definition, and it's correct, but it hides something worth seeing directly: $A\mathbf{x}$ is really a rule that eats a vector and spits out a new vector. Feed it any point in the plane, and it tells you exactly where that point goes.

That reframing, matrix as transformation rather than matrix as grid of numbers, is what this whole lesson is about. Nothing here contradicts what you already know about matrix arithmetic; it's the same arithmetic, viewed as motion instead of bookkeeping. And once you can see a matrix move a point, a lot of what felt arbitrary in Matrix Fundamentals (why does $AB\neq BA$?) and a lot of what's coming in Row Operations (what does it actually mean for a matrix to be "singular"?) stops being a rule and starts being a picture you already understand.

1 Where do the basis vectors go?

Here is the single most useful fact in this entire lesson, and everything else builds on it. Every vector in the plane can be written as a combination of the two special vectors $\mathbf{e}_1=\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{e}_2=\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}$ (a horizontal step and a vertical step). If you know where a transformation sends $\mathbf{e}_1$ and $\mathbf{e}_2$, you automatically know where it sends every other vector, because linearity lets you build any vector's image out of those two images.

Key idea

For a $2\times2$ matrix $A$, $A\mathbf{e}_1$ is exactly the first column of $A$, and $A\mathbf{e}_2$ is exactly the second column. That's not a coincidence, it's what matrix-vector multiplication does: $A\mathbf{e}_1$ picks out column 1 because $\mathbf{e}_1$ has a $1$ in the first slot and $0$s everywhere else.

Once you know the two columns are the images of $\mathbf{e}_1,\mathbf{e}_2$, any vector $\mathbf{v}=\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\end{pmatrix}=x\mathbf{e}_1+y\mathbf{e}_2$ transforms as $A\mathbf{v} = x(A\mathbf{e}_1)+y(A\mathbf{e}_2)$: just scale the two column-images by $x$ and $y$ and add them.

Worked example

For $A=\begin{pmatrix}2&1\\0&3\end{pmatrix}$, find $A\mathbf{e}_1$ and $A\mathbf{e}_2$ directly from the columns, then use that to find $A\mathbf{v}$ for $\mathbf{v}=\begin{pmatrix}3\\-2\end{pmatrix}$ without multiplying $A$ by $\mathbf{v}$ from scratch.

Column 1 is $\begin{pmatrix}2\\0\end{pmatrix}$, so $A\mathbf{e}_1=\begin{pmatrix}2\\0\end{pmatrix}$. Column 2 is $\begin{pmatrix}1\\3\end{pmatrix}$, so $A\mathbf{e}_2=\begin{pmatrix}1\\3\end{pmatrix}$.

$\mathbf{v}=3\mathbf{e}_1-2\mathbf{e}_2$, so $A\mathbf{v} = 3(A\mathbf{e}_1)-2(A\mathbf{e}_2) = 3\begin{pmatrix}2\\0\end{pmatrix}-2\begin{pmatrix}1\\3\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}6\\0\end{pmatrix}-\begin{pmatrix}2\\6\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}4\\-6\end{pmatrix}$.

This matches ordinary matrix multiplication exactly, $A\mathbf{v}$ computed the usual way also gives $\begin{pmatrix}4\\-6\end{pmatrix}$, but here you never had to redo a dot-product calculation: once you know where the two basis vectors land, every other vector's landing spot is just a scaled combination of those two answers.

Practice 1

For $A=\begin{pmatrix}1&4\\3&2\end{pmatrix}$, state where $\mathbf{e}_1$ and $\mathbf{e}_2$ land, reading straight off the columns.

Solution

$A\mathbf{e}_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\3\end{pmatrix}$ (column 1). $\ A\mathbf{e}_2 = \begin{pmatrix}4\\2\end{pmatrix}$ (column 2).

Practice 2

A transformation sends $\mathbf{e}_1$ to $\begin{pmatrix}2\\5\end{pmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{e}_2$ to $\begin{pmatrix}-1\\3\end{pmatrix}$. Write down its matrix $A$.

Solution

The images of $\mathbf{e}_1,\mathbf{e}_2$ become the columns, in order: $$A = \begin{pmatrix}2&-1\\5&3\end{pmatrix}.$$

Practice 3

For $A=\begin{pmatrix}3&0\\1&2\end{pmatrix}$, use the column images (not full matrix multiplication) to find where $\begin{pmatrix}4\\1\end{pmatrix}$ lands.

Solution

$A\mathbf{e}_1=\begin{pmatrix}3\\1\end{pmatrix}$, $\ A\mathbf{e}_2=\begin{pmatrix}0\\2\end{pmatrix}$. $\begin{pmatrix}4\\1\end{pmatrix}=4\mathbf{e}_1+1\mathbf{e}_2$, so the image is $4\begin{pmatrix}3\\1\end{pmatrix}+1\begin{pmatrix}0\\2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}12\\4\end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}0\\2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}12\\6\end{pmatrix}$.

Practice 4

A $3\times3$ transformation sends $\mathbf{e}_1\to(1,0,0)$, $\mathbf{e}_2\to(0,2,0)$, $\mathbf{e}_3\to(0,0,-1)$. Write the matrix, and describe in words what it does to space.

Solution

$$A = \begin{pmatrix}1&0&0\\0&2&0\\0&0&-1\end{pmatrix}$$

It leaves the $x$-direction alone, doubles the $y$-direction, and flips the $z$-direction backward (a reflection along $z$ combined with no change in $x$ and a stretch in $y$). This is a preview of Section 2, every diagonal matrix is exactly this kind of "stretch each axis independently" transformation.

Practice 5

Explain, in your own words, why knowing where $\mathbf{e}_1$ and $\mathbf{e}_2$ land is enough information to know where every vector in the plane lands.

Solution

Every vector $\mathbf{v}=\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\end{pmatrix}$ can be written as $x\mathbf{e}_1+y\mathbf{e}_2$, a specific scaled combination of the two basis vectors, no vector in the plane is left out of this description. Matrix multiplication respects scaling and addition (this is exactly the distributive and scalar-factoring properties from Matrix Fundamentals), so $A\mathbf{v} = A(x\mathbf{e}_1+y\mathbf{e}_2) = x(A\mathbf{e}_1)+y(A\mathbf{e}_2)$. Once $A\mathbf{e}_1$ and $A\mathbf{e}_2$ are known, this formula hands you $A\mathbf{v}$ for absolutely any $\mathbf{v}$, with no further guessing about what the transformation does elsewhere.

Practice 6

A matrix has columns $\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$ and $\begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\end{pmatrix}$. Find where $\begin{pmatrix}2\\0\end{pmatrix}$ and $\begin{pmatrix}0\\2\end{pmatrix}$ each land.

Solution

$\begin{pmatrix}2\\0\end{pmatrix}=2\mathbf{e}_1$, so it lands at $2\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}2\\2\end{pmatrix}$.

$\begin{pmatrix}0\\2\end{pmatrix}=2\mathbf{e}_2$, so it lands at $2\begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}2\\-2\end{pmatrix}$.

2 Scaling

The simplest transformation of all: stretch or shrink along each axis independently, leaving the axes themselves pointing the same direction. A diagonal matrix always does exactly this, and Section 1's column trick tells you why immediately: the columns of a diagonal matrix are $\begin{pmatrix}k_1\\0\end{pmatrix}$ and $\begin{pmatrix}0\\k_2\end{pmatrix}$, meaning $\mathbf{e}_1$ just gets stretched by $k_1$ along its own direction, and $\mathbf{e}_2$ gets stretched by $k_2$ along its own direction, with no mixing between the axes at all.

Key idea

$$\begin{pmatrix}k_1&0\\0&k_2\end{pmatrix}$$ scales the $x$-direction by $k_1$ and the $y$-direction by $k_2$. If $k_1=k_2=k$, every direction scales by the same amount, a uniform scaling. A negative $k$ doesn't just shrink, it flips that axis to point the other way, scaling and reflecting in one move.

Worked example

Find where $\begin{pmatrix}4\\5\end{pmatrix}$ lands under the scaling matrix $\begin{pmatrix}2&0\\0&3\end{pmatrix}$.

$$\begin{pmatrix}2&0\\0&3\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}4\\5\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}2(4)\\3(5)\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}8\\15\end{pmatrix}.$$

The $x$-coordinate doubled, the $y$-coordinate tripled, exactly matching the two diagonal entries, with no cross-influence between them.

Practice 7

Write the matrix that scales $x$ by $3$ and leaves $y$ completely unchanged.

Solution

Leaving $y$ unchanged means scaling it by $1$: $$\begin{pmatrix}3&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix}.$$

Practice 8

Find where $\begin{pmatrix}2\\-1\end{pmatrix}$ lands under $\begin{pmatrix}4&0\\0&\frac12\end{pmatrix}$.

Solution

$$\begin{pmatrix}4(2)\\\frac12(-1)\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}8\\-\frac12\end{pmatrix}.$$

Practice 9

A transformation sends $\mathbf{e}_1\to\begin{pmatrix}5\\0\end{pmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{e}_2\to\begin{pmatrix}0\\5\end{pmatrix}$. Write the matrix, and name what kind of scaling this is.

Solution

$$A = \begin{pmatrix}5&0\\0&5\end{pmatrix}$$

Both axes scale by the same factor, $5$: this is a uniform scaling, everything gets $5\times$ bigger with its shape completely preserved, just like zooming in on a photo.

Practice 10

What does $\begin{pmatrix}-2&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix}$ do geometrically? Find the image of $\begin{pmatrix}3\\4\end{pmatrix}$.

Solution

It stretches $x$ by a factor of $2$ and flips it to the opposite side (the negative sign), while leaving $y$ untouched, a combination of scaling and reflecting across the $y$-axis in one matrix.

$$\begin{pmatrix}-2(3)\\1(4)\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}-6\\4\end{pmatrix}.$$

Practice 11

What matrix would collapse every point in the plane down to the single point at the origin? What do its columns look like, and why does that make sense given Section 1?

Solution

The zero matrix, $\begin{pmatrix}0&0\\0&0\end{pmatrix}$. Its columns are both $\begin{pmatrix}0\\0\end{pmatrix}$, meaning $\mathbf{e}_1$ and $\mathbf{e}_2$ both land on the origin. Since Section 1 showed every vector's image is built from scaled copies of $A\mathbf{e}_1$ and $A\mathbf{e}_2$, and both of those are already $\mathbf{0}$, every combination of them is $\mathbf{0}$ too, no matter what $x$ and $y$ were. The whole plane really does get crushed to a single point.

Practice 12

The unit square with corners $(0,0),(1,0),(1,1),(0,1)$ (area $1$) is transformed by $\begin{pmatrix}2&0\\0&3\end{pmatrix}$. Find the new corners, and the new area.

Solution

New corners: $(0,0),\ (2,0),\ (2,3),\ (0,3)$, still a rectangle (scaling never bends straight lines).

New area: width $2$ times height $3$ gives area $6$, exactly the product of the two scale factors, $2\times3$. Section 7 turns this observation into a general rule connecting the determinant to area.

3 Reflections

A reflection flips every point across a fixed line, like a mirror. It's really just a special case of scaling: flipping across the $x$-axis, for instance, leaves $x$ alone (scale factor $1$) and flips $y$ (scale factor $-1$).

Key idea

Reflect across the $x$-axis: $\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}$. Reflect across the $y$-axis: $\begin{pmatrix}-1&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix}$. Reflect across the line $y=x$: $\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}$ (this one swaps the two coordinates directly, since it sends $\mathbf{e}_1\to\mathbf{e}_2$ and $\mathbf{e}_2\to\mathbf{e}_1$).

Worked example

Reflect $\begin{pmatrix}3\\4\end{pmatrix}$ across the $x$-axis.

$$\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}3\\4\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}3\\-4\end{pmatrix}.$$

The $x$-coordinate is untouched, the $y$-coordinate flips sign, exactly matching what "reflecting across the $x$-axis" should mean physically.

Practice 13

Reflect $\begin{pmatrix}2\\5\end{pmatrix}$ across the $y$-axis.

Solution

$$\begin{pmatrix}-1&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}2\\5\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}-2\\5\end{pmatrix}.$$

Practice 14

Reflect $\begin{pmatrix}-3\\1\end{pmatrix}$ across the line $y=x$.

Solution

Reflecting across $y=x$ swaps the coordinates: $\begin{pmatrix}-3\\1\end{pmatrix} \to \begin{pmatrix}1\\-3\end{pmatrix}$.

Practice 15

Verify that reflecting across the $x$-axis twice in a row returns a point to where it started, using $\begin{pmatrix}5\\2\end{pmatrix}$ as an example, and explain why that has to happen for any reflection, not just this one.

Solution

First reflection: $\begin{pmatrix}5\\2\end{pmatrix}\to\begin{pmatrix}5\\-2\end{pmatrix}$. Second reflection: $\begin{pmatrix}5\\-2\end{pmatrix}\to\begin{pmatrix}5\\2\end{pmatrix}$, back to the start.

This must always happen for any reflection, because a mirror flip undoes itself: flipping to the other side of a line and then flipping again brings you right back, there's no accumulating drift the way there might be with a rotation or scaling. Algebraically, the reflection matrix $R$ always satisfies $R^2=I$ for exactly this reason.

Practice 16

A point lands at $\begin{pmatrix}4\\-4\end{pmatrix}$ after being reflected across $y=x$. What was the original point?

Solution

Reflecting across $y=x$ swaps coordinates, and (as Practice 15 showed) doing it twice undoes it. So the original point also had its coordinates swapped relative to the result: original $=\begin{pmatrix}-4\\4\end{pmatrix}$. Check: reflecting $\begin{pmatrix}-4\\4\end{pmatrix}$ across $y=x$ swaps to $\begin{pmatrix}4\\-4\end{pmatrix}$ ✓.

Practice 17

Using Section 1's column trick, explain why the reflection-across-the-$x$-axis matrix has to have columns $\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$ and $\begin{pmatrix}0\\-1\end{pmatrix}$.

Solution

$\mathbf{e}_1=\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$ already sits exactly on the $x$-axis, the mirror line itself, so reflecting it across that same line leaves it completely unmoved: its image is still $\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$, giving the first column. $\mathbf{e}_2=\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}$ sits directly above the $x$-axis; reflecting it across that axis sends it to directly below, $\begin{pmatrix}0\\-1\end{pmatrix}$, giving the second column. The columns aren't an arbitrary rule to memorise, they're just the reflection applied to the two simplest possible test points.

Practice 18

Reflect $\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$ across the $y$-axis, then reflect the result across the $x$-axis. What single, more familiar transformation does doing both in sequence match?

Solution

Reflect across $y$-axis: $\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}\to\begin{pmatrix}-1\\1\end{pmatrix}$. Reflect that across the $x$-axis: $\begin{pmatrix}-1\\1\end{pmatrix}\to\begin{pmatrix}-1\\-1\end{pmatrix}$.

This matches a $180^\circ$ rotation directly: rotating $\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$ by $180^\circ$ also gives $\begin{pmatrix}-1\\-1\end{pmatrix}$. Two mirror flips across perpendicular lines always combine into a half-turn, a first taste of Section 6's idea that composing simple transformations can produce a completely different-looking one.

4 Rotations

A rotation spins every point around the origin by the same fixed angle. Working out its matrix is a nice payoff for Section 1's column trick: just track where $\mathbf{e}_1$ and $\mathbf{e}_2$ land after rotating by $\theta$, using ordinary right-triangle trigonometry, and those landing spots become the columns.

Key idea

Rotating $\mathbf{e}_1=\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$ counter-clockwise by angle $\theta$ lands it at $\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta\\\sin\theta\end{pmatrix}$ (standard unit-circle coordinates). Rotating $\mathbf{e}_2=\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}$, which starts $90^\circ$ ahead of $\mathbf{e}_1$, lands it at $\begin{pmatrix}-\sin\theta\\\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}$. So:

$$R_\theta = \begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta&-\sin\theta\\\sin\theta&\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}$$

This picture shows exactly those four vectors on the unit circle: the original basis vectors $\mathbf{e}_1,\mathbf{e}_2$ and their rotated images $R_\theta\mathbf{e}_1,\ R_\theta\mathbf{e}_2$.

Worked example

Find the $90^\circ$ rotation matrix, and use it to rotate $\begin{pmatrix}2\\0\end{pmatrix}$ by $45^\circ$ instead.

At $\theta=90^\circ$: $\cos90^\circ=0$, $\sin90^\circ=1$, so $R_{90} = \begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}$.

For the $45^\circ$ case: $\cos45^\circ=\sin45^\circ=\dfrac{\sqrt2}2$, so $R_{45} = \begin{pmatrix}\frac{\sqrt2}2&-\frac{\sqrt2}2\\\frac{\sqrt2}2&\frac{\sqrt2}2\end{pmatrix}$.

$$R_{45}\begin{pmatrix}2\\0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}\sqrt2\\\sqrt2\end{pmatrix}.$$

This makes sense on sight: a point $2$ units out along the positive $x$-axis, spun halfway toward the positive $y$-axis, should land equally spaced between the two axes, at $(\sqrt2,\sqrt2)$, still at distance $2$ from the origin.

Practice 19

Rotate $\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$ by $180^\circ$.

Solution

$\cos180^\circ=-1$, $\sin180^\circ=0$: $R_{180}=\begin{pmatrix}-1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}$.

$$R_{180}\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}-1\\0\end{pmatrix}.$$ A half-turn sends every point to its exact opposite, as expected.

Practice 20

Rotate $\begin{pmatrix}0\\2\end{pmatrix}$ by $90^\circ$ counter-clockwise.

Solution

$$R_{90}\begin{pmatrix}0\\2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}0\\2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}-2\\0\end{pmatrix}.$$

Practice 21

Write the rotation matrix for $30^\circ$, and use it to rotate $\begin{pmatrix}2\\0\end{pmatrix}$.

Solution

$\cos30^\circ=\dfrac{\sqrt3}2$, $\sin30^\circ=\dfrac12$: $R_{30}=\begin{pmatrix}\frac{\sqrt3}2&-\frac12\\\frac12&\frac{\sqrt3}2\end{pmatrix}$.

$$R_{30}\begin{pmatrix}2\\0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}\sqrt3\\1\end{pmatrix}.$$

Practice 22

Using Section 1's column trick directly, explain why the columns of $R_\theta$ are $\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta\\\sin\theta\end{pmatrix}$ and $\begin{pmatrix}-\sin\theta\\\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}$, rather than treating this as a formula to just memorise.

Solution

The first column is, by Section 1, exactly where $\mathbf{e}_1$ lands. $\mathbf{e}_1$ sits at angle $0^\circ$ on the unit circle; rotating it by $\theta$ moves it to angle $\theta$, which by definition of sine and cosine on the unit circle is the point $(\cos\theta,\sin\theta)$. The second column is where $\mathbf{e}_2$ lands; $\mathbf{e}_2$ already sits at angle $90^\circ$, so after rotating by $\theta$ it sits at angle $90^\circ+\theta$, and using the angle-addition identities, $\cos(90^\circ+\theta)=-\sin\theta$ and $\sin(90^\circ+\theta)=\cos\theta$, giving exactly the second column. Nothing here is an arbitrary rule, it's the unit circle applied to the two simplest test vectors, same idea as Practice 17's reflection columns.

Practice 23

Rotate $\begin{pmatrix}3\\4\end{pmatrix}$ by $90^\circ$ twice in a row, and compare to rotating it by $180^\circ$ directly.

Solution

First $90^\circ$: $\begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}3\\4\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}-4\\3\end{pmatrix}$. Second $90^\circ$: $\begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}-4\\3\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}-3\\-4\end{pmatrix}$.

Directly by $180^\circ$: $\begin{pmatrix}-1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}3\\4\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}-3\\-4\end{pmatrix}$, matching exactly. Two $90^\circ$ turns really do add up to one $180^\circ$ turn, angles of rotation combine by simple addition when rotations are composed, a fact Section 6 makes precise for transformations in general.

Practice 24

A rotation matrix has first column $\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}$. What angle is this, and what must the second column be?

Solution

The first column is $(\cos\theta,\sin\theta)=(0,1)$, so $\theta=90^\circ$. The second column must then be $(-\sin90^\circ,\cos90^\circ)=(-1,0)$, matching $R_{90}=\begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}$ exactly, the same matrix from the worked example.

5 Shears

A shear is the transformation behind the "leaning" effect in italic text or a deck of cards fanned out sideways: one axis stays completely fixed, and points get pushed sideways by an amount proportional to how far they are from that fixed axis.

Key idea

Horizontal shear: $\begin{pmatrix}1&k\\0&1\end{pmatrix}$. Every point's $x$-coordinate shifts by $k$ times its $y$-coordinate; points already on the $x$-axis ($y=0$) don't move at all.

Vertical shear: $\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\k&1\end{pmatrix}$. Every point's $y$-coordinate shifts by $k$ times its $x$-coordinate; points on the $y$-axis stay fixed.

Worked example

Apply the horizontal shear $\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\0&1\end{pmatrix}$ to $\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$, and to $\begin{pmatrix}5\\0\end{pmatrix}$.

$$\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\0&1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1+2\\1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}3\\1\end{pmatrix}.$$

$$\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\0&1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}5\\0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}5+0\\0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}5\\0\end{pmatrix}.$$

The point already on the $x$-axis didn't move at all, exactly matching the key idea, while the point with $y=1$ got pushed sideways by $2$ (the shear factor times its $y$-coordinate).

Practice 25

Apply the horizontal shear $\begin{pmatrix}1&3\\0&1\end{pmatrix}$ to $\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix}$.

Solution

$$\begin{pmatrix}1&3\\0&1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}2+3\\1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}5\\1\end{pmatrix}.$$

Practice 26

Which points are left completely unchanged by a horizontal shear? Explain using the matrix's columns.

Solution

The first column of $\begin{pmatrix}1&k\\0&1\end{pmatrix}$ is $\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$, meaning $\mathbf{e}_1$ itself doesn't move. Every point on the $x$-axis is just a scalar multiple of $\mathbf{e}_1$, and scaling an unmoved vector still leaves it on the same line, unmoved. So the entire $x$-axis is left fixed, point for point, only points with a nonzero $y$-coordinate get pushed sideways.

Practice 27

Write the vertical shear matrix with shear factor $2$, and apply it to $\begin{pmatrix}3\\0\end{pmatrix}$.

Solution

$$\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\2&1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}3\\0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}3\\2(3)+0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}3\\6\end{pmatrix}.$$

The $x$-coordinate ($3$) pushed the $y$-coordinate from $0$ up to $6$, exactly matching "shift $y$ by the shear factor times $x$."

Practice 28

The unit square is sheared horizontally with $k=1$. Describe in words what happens to its shape, and find the area of the resulting parallelogram.

Solution

The bottom edge (on the $x$-axis) stays exactly where it is, since points there are unmoved (Practice 26). The top edge, sitting at $y=1$, slides sideways by $1\times k=1$: the corner $(0,1)$ moves to $(1,1)$, and $(1,1)$ moves to $(2,1)$. The square leans over into a parallelogram, like a stack of cards pushed sideways.

The area stays exactly $1$: the base is still $1$ and the height is still $1$ (shears never change how far a point sits from the fixed axis, only how far sideways it slides), so base times height is unchanged even though the shape is no longer a square. Section 7 explains this in terms of the determinant.

Practice 29

Is a shear matrix ever also a scaling matrix? Explain using what makes each one geometrically distinctive.

Solution

No (except in the trivial case $k=0$, where the shear matrix is just $I$ and does nothing at all, which isn't a very interesting kind of scaling either). A genuine scaling matrix moves every point along its own axis direction, stretching distances from the origin along $x$ and $y$ independently, but never introduces sideways sliding between the axes. A genuine shear does the opposite: it leaves distances along the fixed axis completely alone and only slides points sideways, parallel to that axis, by an amount depending on their distance from it. The two effects, stretching along an axis versus sliding parallel to one, are fundamentally different kinds of motion, which is exactly why they get different names and different matrix shapes (diagonal vs. one off-diagonal entry).

6 Composition: what multiplication means

Matrix Fundamentals taught you the mechanical rule for computing $AB$. Here is what that product actually means: $(AB)\mathbf{v}$ is "first transform $\mathbf{v}$ by $B$, then transform the result by $A$." Reading a product of matrices is a right-to-left instruction: whichever matrix sits closest to the vector gets applied first.

Key idea

$(AB)\mathbf{v} = A(B\mathbf{v})$: apply $B$ first, then $A$. Since $AB\neq BA$ in general (Matrix Fundamentals, Section 6), doing transformation $B$ then $A$ is generally a genuinely different overall motion from doing $A$ then $B$, not just the same result computed in a different order.

Why order visibly matters here

Take $A=$ rotate $90^\circ$ and $B=$ scale $x$ by $2$ (leave $y$ alone), and track $\mathbf{v}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$.

Scale then rotate ($A(B\mathbf{v})$, i.e.\ matrix $AB$): first $B\mathbf{v}=\begin{pmatrix}2\\0\end{pmatrix}$ (stretched along $x$), then rotate that $90^\circ$: $\begin{pmatrix}0\\2\end{pmatrix}$.

Rotate then scale ($B(A\mathbf{v})$, i.e.\ matrix $BA$): first $A\mathbf{v}=\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}$ (rotated onto the $y$-axis), then scale $x$ by $2$: scaling $x$ does nothing to a vector that's already sitting entirely on the $y$-axis, giving $\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}$.

Two completely different landing spots, $\begin{pmatrix}0\\2\end{pmatrix}$ versus $\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}$, from the exact same two building-block transformations, just applied in the opposite order. This is $AB\neq BA$ made visible: stretching before you turn is not the same motion as turning before you stretch, because by the time you turn, the stretch has already happened along a direction that turning then carries somewhere the un-stretched rotation never visits.

Practice 30

Apply reflection-across-the-$x$-axis, then rotation-by-$90^\circ$, to the point $\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$ (i.e.\ compute rotate(reflect($\mathbf{v}$))).

Solution

Reflect first: $\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$ (unchanged, since it's already on the $x$-axis). Then rotate: $\begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}$.

Practice 31

Now reverse the order from Practice 30: apply rotation-by-$90^\circ$ first, then reflection-across-the-$x$-axis, to the same point $\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$. Confirm you get a different answer.

Solution

Rotate first: $\begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}$. Then reflect: $\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0\\-1\end{pmatrix}$.

$\begin{pmatrix}0\\-1\end{pmatrix}$, not $\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}$ from Practice 30: genuinely different, confirming order matters here too, not just in the rotate/scale example from the "why" box.

Practice 32

Apply a horizontal shear ($k=1$), then a vertical shear ($k=1$), to $\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$.

Solution

Horizontal shear first: $\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$ is on the $x$-axis, unmoved (Practice 26): still $\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$.

Vertical shear: $\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\1&1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$.

Practice 33

Explain, using the "apply $B$ first, then $A$" reading of $(AB)\mathbf{v}$, why the definition $(AB)\mathbf{v} = A(B\mathbf{v})$ is exactly what you'd want associativity, $A(B\mathbf{v})=(AB)\mathbf{v}$, to mean geometrically.

Solution

$A(B\mathbf{v})$ is an instruction carried out in two separate steps: transform $\mathbf{v}$ by $B$, then take that result and transform it by $A$. $(AB)\mathbf{v}$ is a single matrix, $AB$, applied once. Associativity says these must give the same final landing spot for $\mathbf{v}$, which matches the everyday meaning of "do $B$, then do $A$" being interchangeable with "do the single combined motion $AB$": whether you think of a compound motion as two separate steps or as one pre-computed combined transformation, the vector ends up in the same place either way. This is exactly why Matrix Fundamentals could state associativity, $A(BC)=(AB)C$, as a general algebraic fact, it's the geometric statement that grouping a sequence of motions doesn't change where anything ends up, only whether you compute the combined matrix ahead of time or apply each step one at a time.

Practice 34

Compose reflection-across-the-$x$-axis with reflection-across-the-$y$-axis (either order, check it doesn't matter here). Which single familiar transformation does the combination match? Verify with the test point $\begin{pmatrix}2\\3\end{pmatrix}$.

Solution

Reflect $\begin{pmatrix}2\\3\end{pmatrix}$ across the $x$-axis: $\begin{pmatrix}2\\-3\end{pmatrix}$. Then across the $y$-axis: $\begin{pmatrix}-2\\-3\end{pmatrix}$.

This matches a $180^\circ$ rotation of $\begin{pmatrix}2\\3\end{pmatrix}$, which is also $\begin{pmatrix}-2\\-3\end{pmatrix}$. Unlike Practice 30–31, this particular pair of reflections happens to give the same result regardless of order (you can check the other order lands the same place too), because reflecting across two perpendicular lines is a special case where the combined motion, a half-turn, is symmetric enough not to care which mirror you use first.

7 The determinant as area and orientation

Row Operations introduces the determinant as a number that decides whether a system has a unique solution. Geometrically, that same number is answering a much more visual question: how much does this transformation stretch or shrink area, and does it flip the picture over like a mirror, or keep it right-side up?

Key idea

For a $2\times2$ matrix $A$, $|\det A|$ is exactly the factor by which $A$ scales area, any shape's area gets multiplied by $|\det A|$ after the transformation. The sign of $\det A$ tells you about orientation: positive means the picture keeps its original handedness (going counter-clockwise around a shape stays counter-clockwise), negative means the transformation flips it, like a reflection.

Worked example

Interpret $\det\begin{pmatrix}3&1\\1&2\end{pmatrix} = 5$ geometrically, and contrast with $\det\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}=-1$.

The first matrix scales every area by a factor of $5$, and since $\det>0$, it preserves orientation, no flipping. Any shape you transform by this matrix comes out $5\times$ bigger in area, but still "the right way round."

The second matrix is the reflection-across-$y=x$ matrix from Section 3. Its determinant, $-1$, has magnitude $1$: area is completely unchanged. But the sign is negative, confirming what you already know about reflections: they flip orientation, exactly what the negative sign is built to detect.

Practice 35

Find $\det\begin{pmatrix}4&0\\0&3\end{pmatrix}$, and interpret it as an area factor, connecting back to Practice 12.

Solution

$\det = 4(3)-0(0) = 12$. This matches Practice 12's scaling matrix, where the unit square's area of $1$ became $6$ under $\mathrm{diag}(2,3)$ ($\det=6$ there); here, scaling by $4$ and $3$ instead multiplies area by $4\times3=12$, exactly the determinant.

Practice 36

Find $\det$ of the reflection-across-$y=x$ matrix, and explain the negative sign in terms of orientation.

Solution

$\det\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix} = 0(0)-1(1) = -1$. The magnitude, $1$, says area is unchanged, exactly what you'd expect from a mirror flip (it doesn't stretch anything, just flips it). The negative sign is the determinant's way of flagging that a reflection reverses handedness: trace a counter-clockwise loop around any shape, and after reflecting, that same loop reads clockwise, the defining signature of every reflection matrix.

Practice 37

A transformation has determinant $0$. What does that tell you about what happens to the unit square? (This connects directly to invertibility in Row Operations.)

Solution

The unit square gets squashed down to something with zero area, a line segment, or in the most extreme case, a single point (exactly like Practice 11's zero matrix, whose determinant is also $0$). A transformation that collapses area to nothing has genuinely destroyed information, you cannot tell two different starting points apart anymore once they've both been squashed onto the same flattened line. This is precisely why $\det=0$ signals a non-invertible matrix in Row Operations: there's no way to reverse a transformation that has erased information by collapsing a 2D region down to something with less than 2 dimensions.

Practice 38

Find $\det$ of the shear matrix $\begin{pmatrix}1&5\\0&1\end{pmatrix}$, and explain why shears never change area, connecting to Practice 28.

Solution

$\det = 1(1)-5(0) = 1$. A determinant of exactly $1$ means area is completely preserved, no stretching, no shrinking, matching Practice 28's direct finding that the sheared unit square kept area $1$ despite changing shape into a parallelogram. Every shear matrix has this same triangular shape with $1$s on the diagonal, so its determinant is always $1\times1-k\times0=1$, regardless of the shear factor $k$: sliding a shape sideways parallel to a fixed axis never changes its base or its height, only how slanted it becomes.

Practice 39

A transformation doubles every area and reverses orientation. Give a possible value for its determinant.

Solution

$\det = -2$: the magnitude $2$ accounts for doubling every area, and the negative sign accounts for the orientation flip.

Practice 40

Two transformations $A$ and $B$ have $\det(A)=2$ and $\det(B)=3$. Using the fact that $\det(AB)=\det(A)\det(B)$, find $\det(AB)$, and explain what that says about area scaling when the two transformations are applied one after the other.

Solution

$\det(AB) = 2\times3 = 6$. This makes complete sense from the area-scaling picture: if $B$ triples every area and then $A$ doubles whatever area it's handed, applying both in sequence multiplies area by $3$ and then by $2$, a combined factor of $6$, exactly matching the product of the two individual determinants. Area-scaling factors combine by multiplication under composition, precisely because composing transformations means applying one scaling effect on top of the other.

8 Preview: directions that don't rotate

Most vectors, under most transformations, end up pointing in a completely new direction. But for many matrices, a small number of special directions are the exception: whatever's along one of these directions gets stretched or shrunk, and possibly flipped backward, but it never gets rotated off its own line. These special directions are the single most important idea the rest of the Linear Algebra track is building toward.

A first look, no new machinery required

Take $A=\begin{pmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{pmatrix}$. Try the vector $\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$:

$$A\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}2+1\\1+2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}3\\3\end{pmatrix} = 3\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}.$$

The output is just $3$ times the input, same direction, just $3\times$ longer. Now try $\begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\end{pmatrix}$:

$$A\begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}2-1\\1-2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\end{pmatrix} = 1\begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\end{pmatrix}.$$

Unchanged entirely. But try an ordinary vector like $\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$: $A\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix}$, pointing in a visibly different direction from where it started, a genuinely new direction, not just a stretched or flipped copy of the original. Most starting directions behave like this last one; only $\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$ and $\begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\end{pmatrix}$ (and anything lying along those same two lines) survive with their direction intact.

These surviving directions are called eigenvectors, and the stretch factor each one experiences ($3$ and $1$, in the example above) is its eigenvalue. Finding them systematically, for any matrix, needs two tools you're about to build in the next two lessons: solving $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$-style systems (Row Operations) and the determinant's connection to singularity (also Row Operations), plus, for the matrices where these special directions turn out to be complex rather than real, everything the Complex Numbers lesson builds. That's exactly the path this track takes from here, and exactly why Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors sits at the very end as the capstone: it's the payoff for every tool built along the way.

Practice 41

Verify that $A=\begin{pmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{pmatrix}$ sends $\begin{pmatrix}2\\2\end{pmatrix}$ to a scalar multiple of itself, and state the scale factor.

Solution

$$A\begin{pmatrix}2\\2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}4+2\\2+4\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}6\\6\end{pmatrix} = 3\begin{pmatrix}2\\2\end{pmatrix}.$$

Scale factor $3$, matching the "why" box's finding for $\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$ exactly, since $\begin{pmatrix}2\\2\end{pmatrix}$ lies along that same surviving direction, just twice as long.

Practice 42

For the $90^\circ$ rotation matrix, explain why no real, nonzero vector can survive with its direction unchanged.

Solution

A $90^\circ$ rotation turns every direction in the plane by the same fixed angle, with no exceptions built in anywhere: whichever direction you start with, after a quarter-turn it's pointing somewhere genuinely new, at a right angle to where it began. There's no special starting line that a quarter-turn happens to leave alone, unlike Section 8's matrix, which had two such lines. This is a genuine preview of Section 5's complex eigenvalues in the Eigenvalues lesson: a pure rotation turns out to have eigenvalues that are complex, not real, exactly because there's no real direction left standing still for it to stretch instead of rotate.

Practice 43

For $A=\begin{pmatrix}5&0\\0&3\end{pmatrix}$, which two directions obviously survive unrotated, and what are their stretch factors?

Solution

The $x$-axis direction $\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$ and the $y$-axis direction $\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}$: since $A$ is diagonal, it stretches each axis independently with no mixing (Section 2), so $A\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}5\\0\end{pmatrix}=5\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$ and $A\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0\\3\end{pmatrix}=3\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}$. Stretch factors $5$ and $3$, read straight off the diagonal. This is no accident: for any diagonal matrix, the eigenvectors are always exactly the coordinate axes, and the eigenvalues are always exactly the diagonal entries, the easiest possible case, and exactly the case the Eigenvalues lesson uses as its own first checkpoint.

Practice 44

For the shear matrix $\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\0&1\end{pmatrix}$, does $\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$ survive with its direction unchanged? Verify directly.

Solution

$$\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\0&1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix} = 1\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}.$$

Yes, completely unchanged (stretch factor exactly $1$), matching Practice 26's finding that shears leave every point on the fixed axis exactly where it is. This vector is an eigenvector of the shear matrix with eigenvalue $1$, but it's the only independent direction that survives, a shear matrix turns out to be an example of exactly the "defective" case the Eigenvalues lesson's Section 5 (Repeated eigenvalues) is built around: one repeated eigenvalue, but only one surviving direction to show for it, not two.
