0 Why roots-coefficients matter

You often need information about roots without solving fully. Roots-coefficients identities let you do that immediately from polynomial coefficients. This is especially useful when characteristic polynomials are hard to factor exactly.

Bridge to eigenvalues

For $\det(A-\lambda I)=\lambda^n+c_{n-1}\lambda^{n-1}+\cdots+c_0$, coefficients encode sums/products of eigenvalues.

In particular, trace and determinant relationships become transparent via these identities.

1 Quadratic and cubic identities
Key identities

For $ax^2+bx+c=0$ with roots $\alpha,\beta$:

$$\alpha+\beta=-\frac{b}{a}, \qquad \alpha\beta=\frac{c}{a}.$$

For $ax^3+bx^2+cx+d=0$ with roots $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$:

$$\alpha+\beta+\gamma=-\frac{b}{a},\quad \alpha\beta+\alpha\gamma+\beta\gamma=\frac{c}{a},\quad \alpha\beta\gamma=-\frac{d}{a}.$$

Worked example A

If roots of $x^2-7x+10=0$ are $\alpha,\beta$, find $\alpha+\beta$, $\alpha\beta$, and $\alpha^2+\beta^2$.

$\alpha+\beta=7,\ \alpha\beta=10$.

$\alpha^2+\beta^2=(\alpha+\beta)^2-2\alpha\beta=49-20=29$.

Worked example B

For $2x^3-5x^2+4x-1=0$ with roots $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$, find $\alpha+\beta+\gamma$, $\alpha\beta+\alpha\gamma+\beta\gamma$, and $\alpha\beta\gamma$.

$\alpha+\beta+\gamma=\dfrac{5}{2}$, $\alpha\beta+\alpha\gamma+\beta\gamma=2$, $\alpha\beta\gamma=\dfrac{1}{2}$.

2 Quartic identities and symmetric sums
Quartic form

For $ax^4+bx^3+cx^2+dx+e=0$ with roots $\alpha,\beta,\gamma,\delta$:

$$\alpha+\beta+\gamma+\delta=-\frac{b}{a},$$

$$\sum_{\text{pairs}}\alpha\beta=\frac{c}{a},\qquad \sum_{\text{triples}}\alpha\beta\gamma=-\frac{d}{a},\qquad \alpha\beta\gamma\delta=\frac{e}{a}.$$

Worked example C

For $x^4-3x^3+2x^2+5x-6=0$, find $\alpha+\beta+\gamma+\delta$ and $\alpha\beta\gamma\delta$.

Sum of roots: $3$.

Product of roots: $-6$.

3 Derived expressions from roots
Worked example D

If $\alpha,\beta$ are roots of $3x^2-5x+2=0$, find $\dfrac1\alpha+\dfrac1\beta$.

$\alpha+\beta=\dfrac53,\ \alpha\beta=\dfrac23$.

$\dfrac1\alpha+\dfrac1\beta=\dfrac{\alpha+\beta}{\alpha\beta}=\dfrac{5/3}{2/3}=\dfrac52$.

Worked example E

If $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$ are roots of $x^3-4x^2+x+6=0$, find $\dfrac1\alpha+\dfrac1\beta+\dfrac1\gamma$.

$\alpha\beta+\alpha\gamma+\beta\gamma=1$, and $\alpha\beta\gamma=-6$.

So $\dfrac1\alpha+\dfrac1\beta+\dfrac1\gamma=\dfrac{\alpha\beta+\alpha\gamma+\beta\gamma}{\alpha\beta\gamma}=-\dfrac16$.

4 Constructing new equations from transformed roots
Standard method

Given roots $\alpha,\beta$, if you need a new equation with roots $f(\alpha),f(\beta)$, first compute the new sum and product, then build

$$x^2-(\text{new sum})x+(\text{new product})=0.$$

Worked example F

If $\alpha,\beta$ are roots of $x^2-4x+1=0$, form the equation with roots $\alpha+1,\beta+1$.

$\alpha+\beta=4,\ \alpha\beta=1$.

New sum $=(\alpha+1)+(\beta+1)=6$.

New product $=(\alpha+1)(\beta+1)=\alpha\beta+\alpha+\beta+1=1+4+1=6$.

Required equation: $x^2-6x+6=0$.

Practice 1

For $x^2-9x+14=0$ with roots $\alpha,\beta$, find $\alpha+\beta$ and $\alpha\beta$.

Solution

$\alpha+\beta=9$, $\alpha\beta=14$.

Practice 2

If $\alpha,\beta$ solve $2x^2+3x-5=0$, find $\alpha+\beta$ and $\alpha\beta$.

Solution

$\alpha+\beta=-\frac{3}{2}$, $\alpha\beta=-\frac{5}{2}$.

Practice 3

For roots $\alpha,\beta$ of $x^2-6x+8=0$, find $\alpha^2+\beta^2$.

Solution

$\alpha+\beta=6,\ \alpha\beta=8$. Then $\alpha^2+\beta^2=36-16=20$.

Practice 4

For roots $\alpha,\beta$ of $3x^2-x-2=0$, compute $\frac1\alpha+\frac1\beta$.

Solution

$\alpha+\beta=\frac13,\ \alpha\beta=-\frac23$. So $\frac1\alpha+\frac1\beta=\frac{1/3}{-2/3}=-\frac12$.

Practice 5

For $x^3-3x^2-10x+24=0$ with roots $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$, find $\alpha+\beta+\gamma$, $\alpha\beta+\alpha\gamma+\beta\gamma$, and $\alpha\beta\gamma$.

Solution

Sum $=3$, pair-sum $=-10$, product $=-24$.

Practice 6

If roots of $x^3+ax^2+bx+6=0$ satisfy $\alpha+\beta+\gamma=-2$ and $\alpha\beta\gamma=-6$, find $a,b$ when $\alpha\beta+\alpha\gamma+\beta\gamma=1$.

Solution

For monic cubic, $a=-(\alpha+\beta+\gamma)=2$, and $b=\alpha\beta+\alpha\gamma+\beta\gamma=1$.

Practice 7

For roots $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$ of $2x^3+x^2-7x+3=0$, find $\frac1\alpha+\frac1\beta+\frac1\gamma$.

Solution

Pair-sum $=c/a=-7/2$, product $=-d/a=-3/2$. Reciprocal sum $=\frac{-7/2}{-3/2}=\frac73$.

Practice 8

For quartic $x^4-2x^3-5x^2+6x+8=0$, find sum of roots and product of roots.

Solution

Sum $=2$, product $=8$.

Practice 9

If $x^4+px^3+qx^2+rx+10=0$ has roots with sum $-3$ and pairwise-sum $4$, find $p,q$.

Solution

$-p=-3\Rightarrow p=3$. Also $q=4$.

Practice 10

If $\alpha,\beta$ are roots of $x^2-5x+6=0$, form the equation with roots $\alpha+2,\beta+2$.

Solution

Old sum/product: $5,6$. New sum: $9$. New product: $(\alpha+2)(\beta+2)=6+10+4=20$. Equation: $x^2-9x+20=0$.

Practice 11

If $\alpha,\beta$ are roots of $x^2+4x+1=0$, form equation with roots $\frac1\alpha,\frac1\beta$.

Solution

Old sum/product: $-4,1$. Reciprocal roots have sum $-4$ and product $1$. Equation: $x^2+4x+1=0$ (self-reciprocal here).

Practice 12

If $\alpha,\beta$ are roots of $x^2-3x-2=0$, form equation with roots $\alpha^2,\beta^2$.

Solution

$\alpha+\beta=3,\ \alpha\beta=-2$. Then $\alpha^2+\beta^2=9+4=13$, and $\alpha^2\beta^2=4$. Equation: $x^2-13x+4=0$.

Practice 13

Given roots $\alpha,\beta$ of $x^2+px+q=0$, express $\alpha^2+\beta^2$ in terms of $p,q$.

Solution

$\alpha+\beta=-p,\ \alpha\beta=q$. So $\alpha^2+\beta^2=(\alpha+\beta)^2-2\alpha\beta=p^2-2q$.

Practice 14

Given roots $\alpha,\beta$ of $x^2+px+q=0$, express $(\alpha+1)(\beta+1)$ in terms of $p,q$.

Solution

$(\alpha+1)(\beta+1)=\alpha\beta+\alpha+\beta+1=q-p+1$.

Practice 15

If $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$ are roots of $x^3-6x^2+11x-6=0$, find $(\alpha+1)(\beta+1)(\gamma+1)$ without solving roots directly.

Solution

Expand: $(\alpha+1)(\beta+1)(\gamma+1)=\alpha\beta\gamma+(\alpha\beta+\alpha\gamma+\beta\gamma)+(\alpha+\beta+\gamma)+1=6+11+6+1=24$.

Practice 16

If $x^3-4x^2+mx-8=0$ has roots $1,1,2$, find $m$ and verify coefficient consistency.

Solution

Pairwise sum $=1\cdot1+1\cdot2+1\cdot2=5$, so $m=5$. Sum is $4$ and product is $2$, giving constant $-2$ for monic cubic, not $-8$; therefore such roots are inconsistent with this polynomial. No valid $m$ under stated roots.

Practice 17

For $2x^3-7x^2+7x-2=0$, find sum and product of roots.

Solution

Sum $=7/2$, product $=1$.

Practice 18

If $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$ are roots of $x^3+3x^2-4x-12=0$, compute $\alpha\beta+\alpha\gamma+\beta\gamma$ and $\frac1\alpha+\frac1\beta+\frac1\gamma$.

Solution

Pair-sum $=c/a=-4$. Product $=-d/a=12$. Reciprocal sum $=-4/12=-1/3$.

Practice 19

Build the quadratic with roots $\alpha-3,\beta-3$ if $\alpha,\beta$ satisfy $x^2+2x-15=0$.

Solution

Old sum/product: $-2,-15$. New sum $=-8$. New product $(\alpha-3)(\beta-3)=\alpha\beta-3(\alpha+\beta)+9=-15+6+9=0$. Equation: $x^2+8x=0$.

Practice 20

If roots of $x^2-ax+12=0$ differ by $2$, find $a$.

Solution

Let roots $r,s$. Then $r+s=a,\ rs=12,\ (r-s)^2=4$. So $a^2=(r+s)^2=(r-s)^2+4rs=4+48=52$. Hence $a=\pm2\sqrt{13}$.

Practice 21

For monic cubic with roots $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$, show $\alpha^2+\beta^2+\gamma^2=(\alpha+\beta+\gamma)^2-2(\alpha\beta+\alpha\gamma+\beta\gamma)$.

Solution

Expand $(\alpha+\beta+\gamma)^2$ then rearrange: $\alpha^2+\beta^2+\gamma^2+2(\alpha\beta+\alpha\gamma+\beta\gamma)$. Move the pair term across.

Practice 22

If characteristic polynomial is $\lambda^3-9\lambda^2+26\lambda-24$, find sum and product of eigenvalues.

Solution

Sum of eigenvalues $=9$, product of eigenvalues $=24$.

Practice 23

If characteristic polynomial is $\lambda^2-7\lambda+10$, and one eigenvalue is $5$, find the other without solving quadratically.

Solution

Sum is $7$, so other eigenvalue is $2$. (Also product $10$ checks: $5\cdot2=10$.)

Practice 24

For $x^3+px^2+qx+r=0$ with roots in arithmetic progression, set roots as $a-d,a,a+d$. Write formulas for $p,q,r$ in terms of $a,d$.

Solution

Sum $=3a=-p\Rightarrow p=-3a$. Pair-sum $=(a-d)a+(a-d)(a+d)+a(a+d)=3a^2-d^2=q$. Product $=(a-d)a(a+d)=a(a^2-d^2)=-r$ so $r=-a(a^2-d^2)$.
