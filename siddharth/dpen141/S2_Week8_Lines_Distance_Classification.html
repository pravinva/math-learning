0 Direction Cosines and the Vector Equation of a Line

A line in 3D is completely determined by one point on it and one direction vector along it. Direction cosines (from last week's direction-angle work) give a standardised way to describe that direction using the cosines of the angles made with each axis.

Key formula

The vector equation of the line through point $A$ (position vector $\mathbf{a}$) with direction $\mathbf{d}$: $$\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a}+t\mathbf{d}, \qquad t\in\mathbb{R}.$$

Writing this out in components gives the parametric equations $x=a_1+td_1,\ y=a_2+td_2,\ z=a_3+td_3$, and eliminating $t$ gives the symmetric equations $\dfrac{x-a_1}{d_1}=\dfrac{y-a_2}{d_2}=\dfrac{z-a_3}{d_3}$ (where each $d_i\ne0$).

Worked example

Find the vector, parametric, and symmetric equations of the line through $A=(1,-2,3)$ in the direction $\mathbf{d}=(2,1,-1)$.

Vector form: $\mathbf{r} = (1,-2,3)+t(2,1,-1)$.

Parametric form: $x=1+2t,\ y=-2+t,\ z=3-t$.

Symmetric form: solve each for $t$ and equate: $\dfrac{x-1}{2}=\dfrac{y+2}{1}=\dfrac{z-3}{-1}$.

Practice 1

Solution

$\mathbf{r}=(2,0,-1)+t(1,3,2)$.

Find the parametric equations of the line through $(0,1,4)$ with direction $(-1,2,0)$.

$x=-t,\ y=1+2t,\ z=4$.

Practice 3

Find the symmetric equations of the line through $(3,-1,2)$ with direction $(2,-2,4)$.

Solution

Find the vector equation of the line passing through the two points $A=(1,2,3)$ and $B=(4,0,-1)$.

Solution

Direction: $\overrightarrow{AB}=(3,-2,-4)$. $\mathbf{r}=(1,2,3)+t(3,-2,-4)$.

Practice 5

For the line $x=3-t,\ y=t,\ z=1+2t$, find the direction vector and a point on the line, then find where the line crosses the $xy$-plane (where $z=0$).

Solution

Direction $(-1,1,2)$; point $(3,0,1)$ (at $t=0$). $z=0 \Rightarrow 1+2t=0 \Rightarrow t=-\tfrac12$. Then $x=3+\tfrac12=\tfrac72$, $y=-\tfrac12$. Crossing point: $\left(\tfrac72,-\tfrac12,0\right)$.

1 Distance from a Point to a Line

Finding how far a point sits from a line combines the cross product (Week 6) with the geometric fact that the cross product's magnitude gives a parallelogram's area — divide that area by the base length, and you get the height, which is exactly the distance you want.

Key formula

For a line through point $A$ with direction $\mathbf{d}$, the distance from an external point $P$ to the line is $$\text{dist} = \frac{|\overrightarrow{AP}\times\mathbf{d}|}{|\mathbf{d}|}.$$

Worked example

Find the distance between the point $P(-2,1,1)$ and the line $x=3-t,\ y=t,\ z=1+2t$.

Point on line ($t=0$): $A=(3,0,1)$, direction $\mathbf{d}=(-1,1,2)$. $\overrightarrow{AP}=P-A=(-5,1,0)$.

$\overrightarrow{AP}\times\mathbf{d}$: $\mathbf{i}$: $(1)(2)-(0)(1)=2$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(-5)(2)-(0)(-1)]=10$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(-5)(1)-(1)(-1)=-5+1=-4$. Result: $(2,10,-4)$.

$|\overrightarrow{AP}\times\mathbf{d}|=\sqrt{4+100+16}=\sqrt{120}$. $|\mathbf{d}|=\sqrt{1+1+4}=\sqrt6$.

Distance $=\dfrac{\sqrt{120}}{\sqrt6}=\sqrt{20}=2\sqrt5$.

Practice 6

Find the distance from $P=(0,0,0)$ to the line through $A=(1,1,1)$ with direction $(1,0,0)$.

Solution

$\overrightarrow{AP}=(-1,-1,-1)$. $\overrightarrow{AP}\times(1,0,0)$: $\mathbf{i}$: $(-1)(0)-(-1)(0)=0$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(-1)(0)-(-1)(1)]=-1$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(-1)(0)-(-1)(1)=1$. Result $(0,-1,1)$, magnitude $\sqrt2$. $|\mathbf{d}|=1$. Distance $=\sqrt2$.

Practice 7

Find the distance from $P=(2,3,1)$ to the line through $A=(0,0,0)$ with direction $(0,0,1)$ (the $z$-axis).

Solution

Intuitively, distance to the $z$-axis is just $\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ at the point: $\sqrt{4+9}=\sqrt{13}$. (Confirms the formula: $\overrightarrow{AP}=(2,3,1)$, cross with $(0,0,1)$ gives $(3,-2,0)$, magnitude $\sqrt{13}$, and $|\mathbf{d}|=1$.)

Practice 8

Explain, without computing anything, why the distance formula $\dfrac{|\overrightarrow{AP}\times\mathbf{d}|}{|\mathbf{d}|}$ gives zero when $P$ lies exactly on the line.

Solution

If $P$ is on the line, $\overrightarrow{AP}$ is itself parallel to $\mathbf{d}$ (both point along the same line), and the cross product of two parallel vectors is always $\mathbf{0}$ — giving a distance of $0$, as expected.

2 Parallel, Perpendicular, Skew, and Intersecting Lines

In 3D, two lines that aren't parallel don't have to intersect — they might simply miss each other entirely, a possibility that never arises in 2D. Classifying the relationship between two lines is a short, systematic checklist.

Key checklist

1) Parallel: direction vectors are scalar multiples of each other. 2) Perpendicular: direction vectors have zero dot product. 3) If not parallel, check for intersection by setting the two parametric forms equal and solving for both parameters consistently. 4) If no consistent solution exists, the lines are skew (non-parallel, non-intersecting).

Worked example

Consider $\mathcal{L}_1: \mathbf{r}=(0,1,0)+s(1,1,0)$ and $\mathcal{L}_2: \mathbf{r}=(0,1,1)+t(1,0,1)$. Check whether they are parallel, intersecting, or skew.

Directions $(1,1,0)$ and $(1,0,1)$: not scalar multiples (ratios $1/1, 1/0$ don't match) — not parallel.

Set equal, component by component: $s = t$ (from $x$), $\ 1+s=1$ (from $y$) $\Rightarrow s=0$, $\ 0=1+t$ (from $z$) $\Rightarrow t=-1$.

From $x$: $s=t$, but we found $s=0$ and $t=-1$ — inconsistent. No common solution exists, so the lines are skew.

Practice 9

Determine whether the lines with directions $(2,4,6)$ and $(1,2,3)$ are parallel.

Solution

$(2,4,6)=2(1,2,3)$: parallel.

Practice 10

Determine whether the lines with directions $(1,2,-1)$ and $(3,-1,1)$ are perpendicular.

Solution

Dot product: $1(3)+2(-1)+(-1)(1)=3-2-1=0$. Perpendicular.

Practice 11

Determine whether $\mathcal{L}_1: x=1+t,\ y=2-t,\ z=t$ and $\mathcal{L}_2: x=2+s,\ y=1+s,\ z=1-s$ intersect, and if so, find the point.

Solution

Set equal: $1+t=2+s \Rightarrow t-s=1$. $2-t=1+s \Rightarrow -t-s=-1 \Rightarrow t+s=1$. Adding: $2t=2\Rightarrow t=1$, so $s=0$. Check $z$: $t=1$ gives $z=1$; $s=0$ gives $z=1-0=1$. Consistent! Intersection point (using $t=1$ in $\mathcal{L}_1$): $(2,1,1)$.

Practice 12

Determine whether the lines $\mathcal{L}_1: \mathbf{r}=(1,0,0)+s(1,1,1)$ and $\mathcal{L}_2: \mathbf{r}=(0,1,0)+t(2,2,2)$ are parallel, and if so, whether they're actually the same line.

Solution

Directions $(1,1,1)$ and $(2,2,2)=2(1,1,1)$: parallel. Check if $\mathcal{L}_2$'s point $(0,1,0)$ lies on $\mathcal{L}_1$: need $(0,1,0)=(1,0,0)+s(1,1,1)$ for some $s$, i.e. $1+s=0,\ s=1,\ s=0$ — inconsistent ($s$ can't be both $-1$ and $1$ and $0$). So the lines are parallel but distinct (never meeting).

Practice 13

Determine whether $\mathcal{L}_1: \mathbf{r}=(1,2,0)+s(1,0,1)$ and $\mathcal{L}_2: \mathbf{r}=(0,0,3)+t(0,1,1)$ intersect, are parallel, or are skew.

Solution

Directions $(1,0,1)$, $(0,1,1)$: not parallel. Equate: $1+s=0 \Rightarrow s=-1$. $2=t \Rightarrow t=2$. Check $z$: $s=-1$ gives $z=0+(-1)=-1$; $t=2$ gives $z=3+2=5$. $-1\ne5$: inconsistent. Skew.

Practice 14

Find distance from point \(P(2,1,-1)\) to line \(\mathbf r=(1,0,2)+t(2,-1,2)\).

Solution

Take \(\mathbf a=(1,0,2),\mathbf d=(2,-1,2),\overrightarrow{AP}=(1,1,-3)\).

Distance \(d=\dfrac{\|\overrightarrow{AP}\times\mathbf d\|}{\|\mathbf d\|}\).

\(\overrightarrow{AP}\times\mathbf d=(-1,-8,-3)\), so \(d=\dfrac{\sqrt{74}}{3}\).

Practice 15

Determine whether lines \(\mathbf r=(0,1,2)+t(1,2,-1)\) and \(\mathbf s=(2,0,1)+u(2,4,-2)\) are parallel, identical, or distinct parallel.

Solution

Direction vectors satisfy \( (2,4,-2)=2(1,2,-1)\), so lines are parallel/identical.

Check a point: \((2,0,1)\) is not on \(\mathbf r\) (component equations inconsistent), so distinct parallel lines.

Practice 16

Find line through \(A(1,-2,0)\), \(B(4,1,6)\), then compute a unit direction vector.

Solution

Direction \(\overrightarrow{AB}=(3,3,6)=3(1,1,2)\).

Line: \(\mathbf r=(1,-2,0)+t(1,1,2)\).

Unit direction \(=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt6}(1,1,2)\).

Practice 17

Check if lines \(\mathbf r=(1,2,0)+t(2,-1,1)\), \(\mathbf s=(3,1,1)+u(1,0,2)\) intersect; if yes, find point.

Solution

Solve component system: \(1+2t=3+u\), \(2-t=1\), \(t=1+2u\).

From middle equation \(t=1\). Then first gives \(u=0\), third gives \(1=1\), consistent.

Intersection point at \(t=1\): \((3,1,1)\).

Practice 18

For line \(\mathbf r=(2,-1,3)+t(4,2,-2)\), find its symmetric cartesian form.

Solution

Parametric equations: \(x=2+4t,\ y=-1+2t,\ z=3-2t\).

Hence symmetric form \(\dfrac{x-2}{4}=\dfrac{y+1}{2}=\dfrac{3-z}{2}\).

Practice 19

A cable follows line \(\mathbf r=(0,0,1)+t(1,2,2)\). Find point on cable closest to origin.

Solution

Point is \(\mathbf p(t)=(t,2t,1+2t)\). Minimize \(\|\mathbf p(t)\|^2=t^2+4t^2+(1+2t)^2=9t^2+4t+1\).

Derivative \(18t+4=0\Rightarrow t=-2/9\).

Closest point \(=(-2/9,-4/9,5/9)\).
