0 The Definite Integral and Area Under a Curve

The definite integral $\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ is defined as the limit of a sum of thin rectangle areas (a Riemann sum) as the rectangles get infinitely thin. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus is what makes this practical: it says you never actually need to compute that limit directly — an antiderivative does the job instead.

Key theorem — the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

If $F'(x)=f(x)$, then $$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b)-F(a) = \big[F(x)\big]_a^b.$$

For $f(x)\ge0$ on $[a,b]$, this integral equals the area between the curve and the $x$-axis. If $f(x)<0$ somewhere, the integral subtracts that region's area instead of adding it.

Worked example

Find the area under $y=x^2$ between $x=1$ and $x=3$.

Antiderivative: $F(x)=\dfrac{x^3}{3}$.

$$\int_1^3 x^2\,dx = \left[\frac{x^3}{3}\right]_1^3 = \frac{27}{3}-\frac{1}{3} = 9-\frac13 = \frac{26}{3}.$$

Area $=\dfrac{26}{3}$ square units.

Practice 1

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^2(3x^2-2x)\,dx$.

Solution

$\left[x^3-x^2\right]_0^2 = (8-4)-0=4$.

Practice 2

Find the area under $y=\sqrt{x}$ between $x=0$ and $x=4$.

Solution

$\displaystyle\int_0^4x^{1/2}\,dx = \left[\dfrac23x^{3/2}\right]_0^4=\dfrac23(8)-0=\dfrac{16}{3}$.

Practice 3

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_{-1}^{2}(x^3-x)\,dx$, and explain why the result isn't simply the total area between the curve and the axis.

Solution

$\left[\dfrac{x^4}{4}-\dfrac{x^2}{2}\right]_{-1}^2 = \left(4-2\right)-\left(\dfrac14-\dfrac12\right)=2-\left(-\dfrac14\right)=2.25$. This is the signed area: since $x^3-x$ is negative on part of $[-1,2]$ (e.g. between $0$ and $1$), that region subtracts from the total rather than adding, so it isn't the same as the geometric (unsigned) area enclosed.

Practice 4

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi}\sin x\,dx$, and interpret the result as an area.

Solution

$\left[-\cos x\right]_0^{\pi}=(-\cos\pi)-(-\cos0)=1-(-1)=2$. Since $\sin x\ge0$ on $[0,\pi]$, this is the actual area under one arch of the sine curve.

Practice 5

Find the area enclosed between $y=x^2-4$ and the $x$-axis, for $-2\le x\le2$ (note the curve dips below the axis here).

Solution

Since $y\le0$ throughout $[-2,2]$ (the curve is below the axis), the actual area is $-\displaystyle\int_{-2}^2(x^2-4)\,dx$. Compute the integral: $\left[\dfrac{x^3}{3}-4x\right]_{-2}^2=\left(\dfrac83-8\right)-\left(-\dfrac83+8\right)=\left(-\dfrac{16}{3}\right)-\left(\dfrac{16}{3}\right)=-\dfrac{32}{3}$. Area $=\left|-\dfrac{32}{3}\right|=\dfrac{32}{3}$.

1 Area Between Two Curves

Finding the area trapped between two curves is just Section 0's idea applied to the difference of the two functions — integrate (top minus bottom) across the region where they overlap.

Key formula

If $f(x)\ge g(x)$ on $[a,b]$: $$\text{Area} = \int_a^b\big[f(x)-g(x)\big]\,dx.$$ Always find the intersection points first (solving $f(x)=g(x)$) to determine the correct limits and confirm which curve is on top.

Worked example

Find the area enclosed between $y=x+2$ and $y=x^2$.

Find intersections: $x+2=x^2 \Rightarrow x^2-x-2=0 \Rightarrow (x-2)(x+1)=0 \Rightarrow x=-1,2$.

Test which is on top (e.g. at $x=0$): line gives $2$, parabola gives $0$ — the line is on top.

$$\text{Area} = \int_{-1}^{2}\big[(x+2)-x^2\big]\,dx = \left[\frac{x^2}{2}+2x-\frac{x^3}{3}\right]_{-1}^2.$$

At $x=2$: $2+4-\dfrac83=6-\dfrac83=\dfrac{10}{3}$. At $x=-1$: $\dfrac12-2+\dfrac13=-\dfrac76$. Area $=\dfrac{10}{3}-\left(-\dfrac76\right)=\dfrac{20}{6}+\dfrac{7}{6}=\dfrac{27}{6}=\dfrac92$.

Practice 6

Find the area enclosed between $y=x^2$ and $y=2x$.

Solution

Intersections: $x^2=2x \Rightarrow x(x-2)=0 \Rightarrow x=0,2$. Line on top (check $x=1$: line gives $2$, parabola gives $1$). Area $=\displaystyle\int_0^2(2x-x^2)\,dx=\left[x^2-\dfrac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^2=4-\dfrac83=\dfrac43$.

Practice 7

Find the area enclosed between $y=4-x^2$ and the $x$-axis (i.e. between the curve and $y=0$).

Solution

Intersections with $x$-axis: $4-x^2=0 \Rightarrow x=\pm2$. Area $=\displaystyle\int_{-2}^{2}(4-x^2)\,dx=\left[4x-\dfrac{x^3}{3}\right]_{-2}^2$. At $x=2$: $8-\dfrac83=\dfrac{16}{3}$. At $x=-2$: $-8+\dfrac83=-\dfrac{16}{3}$. Area $=\dfrac{16}{3}-\left(-\dfrac{16}{3}\right)=\dfrac{32}{3}$.

Practice 8

Find the area enclosed between $y=x^2-2x$ and $y=x$.

Solution

Intersections: $x^2-2x=x \Rightarrow x^2-3x=0 \Rightarrow x(x-3)=0 \Rightarrow x=0,3$. Line on top (check $x=1$: line gives $1$, parabola gives $-1$). Area $=\displaystyle\int_0^3\big[x-(x^2-2x)\big]dx=\int_0^3(3x-x^2)\,dx=\left[\dfrac{3x^2}{2}-\dfrac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^3=\dfrac{27}{2}-9=\dfrac92$.

Practice 9 EXAM-STYLE

[Exam-style, multi-part] Consider the curves $y=x^2$ and $y=8-x^2$. (a) Find their points of intersection. (b) Find the area enclosed between them. (c) State, without recomputing, what the area would be if both curves were shifted up by $5$ units each (justify briefly).

Solution

(a) $x^2=8-x^2 \Rightarrow 2x^2=8 \Rightarrow x^2=4 \Rightarrow x=\pm2$.

(b) Area $=\displaystyle\int_{-2}^2\big[(8-x^2)-x^2\big]dx=\int_{-2}^2(8-2x^2)\,dx=\left[8x-\dfrac{2x^3}{3}\right]_{-2}^2$. At $x=2$: $16-\dfrac{16}{3}=\dfrac{32}{3}$. At $x=-2$: $-16+\dfrac{16}{3}=-\dfrac{32}{3}$. Area $=\dfrac{32}{3}-\left(-\dfrac{32}{3}\right)=\dfrac{64}{3}$.

(c) Unchanged, still $\dfrac{64}{3}$: shifting both curves by the same constant doesn't change the difference between them at any $x$, so the enclosed area is identical.

2 Integration by Substitution

Substitution is the chain rule run in reverse: if an integrand is (up to a constant factor) the derivative of some inner function times a function of that inner function, replacing the inner function with a single letter $u$ often turns a hard integral into a simple one.

Key method

1) Spot a substitution $u=g(x)$ such that $du=g'(x)\,dx$ appears (up to a constant) elsewhere in the integrand. 2) Rewrite the whole integral in terms of $u$ only. 3) Integrate in terms of $u$. 4) Substitute back $u=g(x)$. For definite integrals, you can instead change the limits to match $u$ and skip substituting back.

Worked example

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^1 x\sqrt{x^2+3}\,dx$, changing the limits to match the substitution.

Let $u=x^2+3$, so $du=2x\,dx \Rightarrow x\,dx=\dfrac{du}{2}$.

Change limits: at $x=0$, $u=3$; at $x=1$, $u=4$.

$$\int_3^4 \sqrt{u}\cdot\frac{du}{2} = \frac12\left[\frac23u^{3/2}\right]_3^4 = \frac13\Big[u^{3/2}\Big]_3^4 = \frac13\big(8-3\sqrt3\big).$$

$$= \frac{8-3\sqrt3}{3}.$$

Practice 10

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int(2x+1)^5\,dx$ using substitution $u=2x+1$.

Solution

$du=2\,dx$. $\displaystyle\int u^5\cdot\dfrac{du}{2}=\dfrac{1}{12}u^6+C=\dfrac{(2x+1)^6}{12}+C$.

Practice 11

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int\frac{2x}{x^2+1}\,dx$ using substitution $u=x^2+1$.

Solution

$du=2x\,dx$. $\displaystyle\int\dfrac{du}{u}=\ln|u|+C=\ln(x^2+1)+C$.

Practice 12

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^3x\cos x\,dx$ using $u=\sin x$.

Solution

$du=\cos x\,dx$. Limits: $x=0\Rightarrow u=0$; $x=\pi/2\Rightarrow u=1$. $\displaystyle\int_0^1u^3\,du=\left[\dfrac{u^4}{4}\right]_0^1=\dfrac14$.

Practice 13

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int x^2\sqrt{x^3+1}\,dx$ using $u=x^3+1$.

Solution

$du=3x^2\,dx \Rightarrow x^2\,dx=\dfrac{du}{3}$. $\displaystyle\int\sqrt{u}\cdot\dfrac{du}{3}=\dfrac13\cdot\dfrac23u^{3/2}+C=\dfrac29(x^3+1)^{3/2}+C$.

Practice 14

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int e^{5x-2}\,dx$ using $u=5x-2$.

Solution

$du=5\,dx$. $\displaystyle\int e^u\cdot\dfrac{du}{5}=\dfrac15e^u+C=\dfrac15e^{5x-2}+C$.

Practice 15

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_1^e\frac{\ln x}{x}\,dx$ using $u=\ln x$.

Solution

$du=\dfrac1x\,dx$. Limits: $x=1\Rightarrow u=0$; $x=e\Rightarrow u=1$. $\displaystyle\int_0^1u\,du=\left[\dfrac{u^2}{2}\right]_0^1=\dfrac12$.

Practice 16 EXAM-STYLE

[Exam-style, multi-part] (a) Evaluate $\displaystyle\int\cos(3x)\sin^4(3x)\,dx$ using $u=\sin(3x)$. (b) Hence, or otherwise, evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/6}\cos(3x)\sin^4(3x)\,dx$.

Solution

(a) $du=3\cos(3x)\,dx$. $\displaystyle\int u^4\cdot\dfrac{du}{3}=\dfrac{u^5}{15}+C=\dfrac{\sin^5(3x)}{15}+C$.

(b) Using the antiderivative from (a): $\left[\dfrac{\sin^5(3x)}{15}\right]_0^{\pi/6}$. At $x=\pi/6$: $\sin(\pi/2)=1$, giving $\dfrac{1}{15}$. At $x=0$: $\sin(0)=0$, giving $0$. Result: $\dfrac{1}{15}$.

3 Mixed Exam-Style Review: Integration

Multi-part problems combining substitution, area calculations, and the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus — testing the full arc of this lesson the way a real exam question would.

Practice 17 EXAM-STYLE

[Exam-style, multi-part] Let $f(x)=x^2$ and $g(x)=6-x$. (a) Find the points of intersection of $y=f(x)$ and $y=g(x)$. (b) Determine which curve is on top between these intersection points. (c) Find the area enclosed between the curves.

Solution

(a) $x^2=6-x \Rightarrow x^2+x-6=0 \Rightarrow (x+3)(x-2)=0 \Rightarrow x=-3,2$.

(b) At $x=0$: $g(0)=6$, $f(0)=0$. Line $g$ is on top.

(c) Area $=\displaystyle\int_{-3}^{2}\big[(6-x)-x^2\big]dx=\left[6x-\dfrac{x^2}{2}-\dfrac{x^3}{3}\right]_{-3}^2$. At $x=2$: $12-2-\dfrac83=\dfrac{22}{3}$. At $x=-3$: $-18-4.5+9=-13.5$. Area $=\dfrac{22}{3}-(-13.5)=\dfrac{22}{3}+\dfrac{27}{2}=\dfrac{44+81}{6}=\dfrac{125}{6}$.

Practice 18 EXAM-STYLE

[Exam-style, multi-part] (a) Evaluate $\displaystyle\int x(x^2-1)^3\,dx$ using substitution. (b) Hence find the area under $y=x(x^2-1)^3$ between $x=1$ and $x=2$.

Solution

(a) Let $u=x^2-1$, $du=2x\,dx$. $\displaystyle\int u^3\cdot\dfrac{du}{2}=\dfrac{u^4}{8}+C=\dfrac{(x^2-1)^4}{8}+C$.

(b) $\left[\dfrac{(x^2-1)^4}{8}\right]_1^2 = \dfrac{3^4}{8}-\dfrac{0^4}{8}=\dfrac{81}{8}$.

Practice 19 EXAM-STYLE

[Exam-style, answer only] Evaluate: (a) $\displaystyle\int_0^2(3x^2+1)\,dx$. (b) $\displaystyle\int 4x(x^2+5)^2\,dx$ (leave in terms of $x$, don't evaluate the constant). (c) The area under $y=2x$ between $x=0$ and $x=3$.

Solution

(a) $\left[x^3+x\right]_0^2=8+2=10$.

(b) Let $u=x^2+5$: $\displaystyle\int 2u^2\,du=\dfrac{2u^3}{3}+C=\dfrac23(x^2+5)^3+C$.

(c) $\left[x^2\right]_0^3=9$.

Practice 20 EXAM-STYLE

[Exam-style, multi-part] A particle moves with velocity $v(t)=6t^2-6t$ (m/s) for $t\ge0$. (a) Find the particle's displacement between $t=0$ and $t=2$ (i.e. $\displaystyle\int_0^2v(t)\,dt$). (b) Find the total distance travelled over the same interval (accounting for any change in direction).

Solution

(a) $\left[2t^3-3t^2\right]_0^2=(16-12)-0=4$ m.

(b) $v(t)=6t(t-1)$, zero at $t=0,1$; negative on $(0,1)$ (moving backward), positive on $(1,2)$. Distance $=\left|\displaystyle\int_0^1v\,dt\right|+\left|\displaystyle\int_1^2v\,dt\right|$. $\displaystyle\int_0^1v\,dt=\left[2t^3-3t^2\right]_0^1=2-3=-1$, so $|-1|=1$. $\displaystyle\int_1^2v\,dt=4-(-1)=5$. Total distance $=1+5=6$ m.

Practice 21

Evaluate integral I=integral ( (4x+8)/(x^2+4x+8) ) dx using decomposition into derivative-of-denominator part plus remainder.

Solution

Write numerator as A(2x+c)+B.

Integrate A ln(denominator) + B*integral 1/(quadratic) dx after completing square.

Express final answer using ln and arctan (or artanh if discriminant positive pattern requires).

Practice 22

Solve inequality ln(x-2) + ln(x+5) >= ln(45) and express solution set in interval notation.

Solution

Domain: x>2 and x>-5; hence x>2.

Combine logs: ln((x-2)(x+5)) >= ln(45) => (x-2)(x+5)>=45.

Solve quadratic inequality with sign chart, then intersect with domain.

Practice 23

For parametric curve x=t^2-3t, y=t^3-2t, (a) find dy/dx, (b) find stationary points in y with respect to x, (c) tangent equations.

Solution

Compute dx/dt and dy/dt; then dy/dx=(dy/dt)/(dx/dt).

Stationary in y wrt x occurs when dy/dt=0 and dx/dt!=0.

Substitute t-values into (x,y) and slope for tangent equations.

Practice 24

Use substitution to evaluate integral from 0 to 2: x*sqrt(x^2+4) dx, then interpret geometric meaning of positive result.

Solution

Let u=x^2+4, du=2x dx.

Convert limits: x=0 -> u=4, x=2 -> u=8.

Integrate (1/2) integral u^(1/2) du and back-substitute/check units.

Practice 25

For f(x)=((x^2-3x+5)/(x-1)), (a) state domain, (b) simplify f(x) for x!= 1, (c) solve f(x)= 8.

Solution

(a) Domain excludes denominator zero: x != 1.

(b) Factor numerator where possible, then cancel only if a common factor with (x-1) exists; keep restriction x != 1.

(c) Set simplified expression equal to 8, solve resulting equation, then reject any root violating x != 1.

Practice 26

Solve exactly: 2sin(2x)-1=0 for 0<=x<2pi, then verify each solution on unit-circle quadrants.

Solution

Rewrite as sin(2x)=1/2.

Find principal solutions for 2x in [0,4pi): 2x=pi/6,5pi/6,13pi/6,17pi/6.

Divide by 2: x=pi/12,5pi/12,13pi/12,17pi/12 and confirm all are in [0,2pi).

Practice 27

Given z=5+7i, (a) convert to polar form, (b) find z^3 using De Moivre, (c) give argument in principal range.

Solution

(a) r=sqrt(25+49) and theta=atan2(7,5).

(b) z^3=r^3(cos(3theta)+i sin(3theta)).

(c) Reduce angle modulo 2pi to principal argument range.

Practice 28

Differentiate y=(x^6+8x)^(4) and evaluate y' at x=1; then classify stationary points of y' on x>0.

Solution

Use chain rule: y'=4(x^6+8x)^(3)(6x^5+8).

Substitute x=1 directly.

For stationary analysis of y, set y'=0 and inspect multiplicity/sign changes.
