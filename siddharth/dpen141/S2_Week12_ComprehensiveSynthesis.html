0 Synthesis: Lines, Planes, and the Objects They Build

Real vector geometry problems rarely test a single formula in isolation — they combine several ideas from across the whole sequence (vectors, lines, planes, distances) into one connected question. This section works through fully synthesised problems that draw on the entire strand.

Key idea — a general problem-solving strategy

1) Identify exactly what geometric objects are involved (points? lines? planes?) and what's being asked (an equation? a distance? an angle? a classification?). 2) Convert every given piece of information into vector form (points to position vectors, "through $A$ and $B$" to a direction vector, etc.). 3) Match the question to the right formula from the toolkit. 4) Compute, then sanity-check the answer's units and rough size.

Worked example

A drone at $A=(0,0,10)$ flies in a straight line toward $B=(30,40,0)$ (metres). A no-fly cylindrical zone is centred on the vertical line through $(10,10,z)$ for all $z$. Find the closest horizontal distance the drone's flight path comes to this restricted line.

Flight line: direction $\mathbf{d}=\overrightarrow{AB}=(30,40,-10)$, through $A=(0,0,10)$.

Restricted line: through $C=(10,10,0)$, direction $(0,0,1)$.

These lines are skew (not parallel, and checking intersection would require the drone's path to pass exactly through the vertical line, which isn't guaranteed). Using the skew-line distance formula: $\overrightarrow{AC}=(10,10,-10)$. $\mathbf{d}\times(0,0,1)$: $\mathbf{i}$: $(40)(1)-(-10)(0)=40$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(30)(1)-(-10)(0)]=-30$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(30)(0)-(40)(0)=0$. Result $(40,-30,0)$, magnitude $50$.

$\overrightarrow{AC}\cdot(40,-30,0)=10(40)+10(-30)+(-10)(0)=400-300=100$. Distance $=\dfrac{|100|}{50}=2$ m.

Practice 1

Find the equation of the line through $A=(1,2,3)$ and $B=(4,6,3)$, and determine whether this line is parallel to the plane $x+y+z=10$ (check whether the direction vector is perpendicular to the plane's normal).

Solution

Direction $\overrightarrow{AB}=(3,4,0)$. Plane normal $(1,1,1)$. Dot product: $3+4+0=7\ne0$: not perpendicular to the normal, so the line is not parallel to the plane (it will cross it somewhere).

Practice 2

Find where the line through $(1,2,3)$ with direction $(3,4,0)$ meets the plane $x+y+z=10$.

Solution

Parametrise: $x=1+3t,\ y=2+4t,\ z=3$. Substitute: $(1+3t)+(2+4t)+3=10 \Rightarrow 6+7t=10 \Rightarrow t=\dfrac47$. Point: $x=1+\dfrac{12}{7}=\dfrac{19}{7}$, $y=2+\dfrac{16}{7}=\dfrac{30}{7}$, $z=3$.

Practice 3

A plane contains the point $(2,0,0)$ and is perpendicular to the line joining $(0,0,0)$ and $(1,1,1)$. Find its equation.

Solution

The line's direction $(1,1,1)$ becomes the plane's normal (since the plane is perpendicular to it). Equation: $1(x-2)+1(y)+1(z)=0 \Rightarrow x+y+z=2$.

Practice 4

Find the shortest distance from the point $(5,5,5)$ to the plane found in the previous question ($x+y+z=2$).

Solution

$|\mathbf{n}|=\sqrt3$. Distance $=\dfrac{|5+5+5-2|}{\sqrt3}=\dfrac{13}{\sqrt3}=\dfrac{13\sqrt3}{3}$.

Practice 5

Two aircraft fly along $\mathbf{r}_1=(0,0,1000)+t(200,0,0)$ and $\mathbf{r}_2=(0,500,800)+t(0,200,0)$ (metres, $t$ in seconds). Determine whether their flight paths ever cross (ignoring timing, purely as geometric lines), and if not, find the minimum separation between the paths.

Solution

Directions $(200,0,0)$ and $(0,200,0)$: not parallel. Check intersection: from $\mathbf{r}_1$, $z=1000$ always; from $\mathbf{r}_2$, $z=800$ always. Since $1000\ne800$, the paths never share a common point — they're skew (in fact, since both directions lie in horizontal planes at different heights, they never even get close vertically). $\overrightarrow{A_1A_2}=(0,500,-200)$. $\mathbf{d}_1\times\mathbf{d}_2=(200,0,0)\times(0,200,0)$: this is $200(1,0,0)\times200(0,1,0)=40000(0,0,1)$. Dot: $(0,500,-200)\cdot(0,0,40000)=-8{,}000{,}000$. $|\mathbf{d}_1\times\mathbf{d}_2|=40000$. Distance $=\dfrac{8{,}000{,}000}{40000}=200$ m.

1 Matrices and Vector Geometry Together

Vectors and matrices aren't separate topics — a matrix acting on a vector (Lesson 2 of this strand) is exactly the transformation picture from your Matrices lesson, and understanding both together deepens each. This section revisits that connection with genuinely combined problems.

Worked example

A linear transformation $T$ has matrix $A=\begin{pmatrix}2&0&0\\0&3&0\\0&0&1\end{pmatrix}$. Find the image of the plane $x+y+z=1$ under $T$ (i.e. find the equation satisfied by every point $T(\mathbf{v})$ for $\mathbf{v}$ on the original plane).

If $(x,y,z)$ is on the original plane, its image is $(x',y',z')=(2x,3y,z)$, so $x=\dfrac{x'}{2},\ y=\dfrac{y'}{3},\ z=z'$.

Substituting into $x+y+z=1$: $\dfrac{x'}{2}+\dfrac{y'}{3}+z'=1$. The image is the plane $\dfrac{x}{2}+\dfrac{y}{3}+z=1$ (relabelling $x',y',z'$ back to $x,y,z$).

Practice 6

A transformation matrix $A=\begin{pmatrix}1&0&0\\0&1&0\\0&0&2\end{pmatrix}$ acts on the line through $(0,0,0)$ with direction $(1,1,1)$. Find the direction vector of the image line.

Solution

$A(1,1,1)=(1,1,2)$. The image line has direction $(1,1,2)$.

Practice 7

Find the determinant of $A=\begin{pmatrix}2&0&0\\0&3&0\\0&0&1\end{pmatrix}$ (from the worked example), and interpret it in terms of how volumes transform under $T$.

Solution

$\det A=2\times3\times1=6$ (diagonal matrix). Any volume in 3D space is scaled by a factor of $6$ under this transformation — e.g. a unit cube maps to a box of volume $6$.

Practice 8

A $3\times3$ transformation matrix has determinant $0$. Explain what this implies about the image of any 3D solid region under the transformation.

Solution

A determinant of $0$ means every volume gets scaled by a factor of $0$ — the transformation collapses all of 3D space onto a lower-dimensional object (a plane, a line, or a point), so no solid region can retain any volume after the transformation.

Practice 9

The eigenvectors of a matrix $A$ give directions unaffected in orientation by the transformation (Strand 2, Lesson 5). If $A=\begin{pmatrix}2&0&0\\0&3&0\\0&0&1\end{pmatrix}$, state its eigenvalues and eigenvectors directly (recall: diagonal matrices have their diagonal entries as eigenvalues).

Solution

Eigenvalues $2,3,1$, with eigenvectors $(1,0,0),(0,1,0),(0,0,1)$ respectively (the standard basis vectors, since a diagonal matrix scales each axis independently).

2 Comprehensive Exam-Style Review: Vector Geometry

Full multi-part questions synthesising vectors, lines, planes, matrices, and distances — mirroring the "answer only" and "full working" sections of a genuine MATH141 exam paper.

Practice 10 EXAM-STYLE

[Exam-style, answer only] Write down only the final answer for each: (a) $|\mathbf{v}|$ for $\mathbf{v}=(3,4,12)$. (b) The normal vector of $3x-y+2z=7$. (c) $\mathbf{i}\times\mathbf{j}$. (d) The value of $k$ making $(1,2,k)$ and $(2,4,6)$ parallel. (e) The determinant of $\begin{pmatrix}5&0\\0&5\end{pmatrix}$.

Solution

(a) $\sqrt{9+16+144}=\sqrt{169}=13$.

(b) $(3,-1,2)$.

(c) $\mathbf{k}$.

(d) $(2,4,6)=2(1,2,3)$, so $k=3$.

(e) $25$.

Practice 11 EXAM-STYLE

[Exam-style, multi-part] Let $A=(1,1,0)$, $B=(3,1,2)$, $C=(1,3,2)$. (a) Find $\overrightarrow{AB}$ and $\overrightarrow{AC}$. (b) Find the area of triangle $ABC$. (c) Find the equation of the plane through $A,B,C$. (d) Find the distance from the point $(0,0,0)$ to this plane.

Solution

(a) $\overrightarrow{AB}=(2,0,2)$, $\overrightarrow{AC}=(0,2,2)$.

(b) $\overrightarrow{AB}\times\overrightarrow{AC}$: $\mathbf{i}$: $(0)(2)-(2)(2)=-4$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(2)(2)-(2)(0)]=-4$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(2)(2)-(0)(0)=4$. Result $(-4,-4,4)$, magnitude $\sqrt{48}=4\sqrt3$. Area $=\dfrac{4\sqrt3}{2}=2\sqrt3$.

(c) Normal $(-4,-4,4)$, simplify to $(1,1,-1)$ (dividing by $-4$). Equation using $A$: $1(x-1)+1(y-1)-1(z)=0 \Rightarrow x+y-z=2$.

(d) $|\mathbf{n}|=\sqrt3$. Distance $=\dfrac{|0+0-0-2|}{\sqrt3}=\dfrac{2}{\sqrt3}=\dfrac{2\sqrt3}{3}$.

Practice 12 EXAM-STYLE

[Exam-style, multi-part] Consider the planes $\mathcal{P}_1: x+y-z=2$ and $\mathcal{P}_2: 2x-y+z=1$. (a) Show the planes are not parallel. (b) Find the direction of their line of intersection. (c) Find a point on the line of intersection. (d) Find the angle between the two planes.

Solution

(a) Normals $(1,1,-1)$ and $(2,-1,1)$ are not scalar multiples of each other.

(b) $\mathbf{d}=(1,1,-1)\times(2,-1,1)$: $\mathbf{i}$: $(1)(1)-(-1)(-1)=0$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(1)(1)-(-1)(2)]=-3$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(1)(-1)-(1)(2)=-3$. $\mathbf{d}=(0,-3,-3)$, or simplified $(0,1,1)$.

(c) Set $x=0$: $y-z=2$ and $-y+z=1$. Adding: $0=3$ — inconsistent! Set $y=0$ instead: $x-z=2$ and $2x+z=1$. Adding: $3x=3 \Rightarrow x=1$, then $z=x-2=-1$. Point: $(1,0,-1)$.

(d) $\mathbf{n}_1\cdot\mathbf{n}_2=2-1-1=0$: the planes are perpendicular, angle $=90^\circ$.

Practice 13 EXAM-STYLE

[Exam-style, multi-part] A tetrahedron has vertices $O=(0,0,0)$, $A=(2,0,0)$, $B=(0,2,0)$, $C=(1,1,3)$. (a) Find the volume of the tetrahedron using $\dfrac16|\overrightarrow{OA}\cdot(\overrightarrow{OB}\times\overrightarrow{OC})|$. (b) Find the equation of the plane $ABC$. (c) Find the distance from $O$ to the plane $ABC$, and verify it's consistent with your answer to (a) using $V=\dfrac13\times\text{base area}\times\text{height}$.

Solution

(a) $\overrightarrow{OB}\times\overrightarrow{OC}=(0,2,0)\times(1,1,3)$: $\mathbf{i}$: $(2)(3)-(0)(1)=6$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(0)(3)-(0)(1)]=0$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(0)(1)-(2)(1)=-2$. Result $(6,0,-2)$. $\overrightarrow{OA}\cdot(6,0,-2)=2(6)+0+0=12$. $V=\dfrac16(12)=2$.

(b) $\overrightarrow{AB}=(-2,2,0)$, $\overrightarrow{AC}=(-1,1,3)$. Normal: $\mathbf{i}$: $(2)(3)-(0)(1)=6$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(-2)(3)-(0)(-1)]=6$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(-2)(1)-(2)(-1)=0$. $\mathbf{n}=(6,6,0)$, simplify to $(1,1,0)$. Equation using $A=(2,0,0)$: $(x-2)+y=0 \Rightarrow x+y=2$.

(c) $|\mathbf{n}|=\sqrt2$. Distance from $O$: $\dfrac{|0+0-2|}{\sqrt2}=\dfrac{2}{\sqrt2}=\sqrt2$. Base area (triangle $ABC$, using $|\overrightarrow{AB}\times\overrightarrow{AC}|/2$ with the un-simplified normal $(6,6,0)$, magnitude $6\sqrt2$): area $=3\sqrt2$. Check: $V=\dfrac13(3\sqrt2)(\sqrt2)=\dfrac13(3)(2)=2$. ✓ Matches (a).
