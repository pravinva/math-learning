0 Further Trigonometric Equations

Beyond the basic "solve $\sin x=k$" equations from Week 3, engineering problems often produce trig equations mixing multiple angles, multiple functions, or requiring an identity substitution before they become solvable at all. The strategy is always the same: reduce to a single trig function of a single angle, then solve.

Key idea

For an equation involving $\sin(nx)$ or $\cos(nx)$, solve for $nx$ over the extended range first (e.g. if $0\le x\le 2\pi$ and the equation involves $\sin(2x)$, solve over $0\le 2x\le4\pi$), then divide by $n$ at the very end.

Worked example

Solve $\cos(2x) = \dfrac12$ for $0\le x\le 2\pi$.

Let $u=2x$, so $0\le u\le4\pi$. Solve $\cos u=\tfrac12$: reference angle $\dfrac{\pi}{3}$, cosine positive in Q1 and Q4, repeating every $2\pi$: $u=\dfrac{\pi}{3},\ 2\pi-\dfrac{\pi}{3}=\dfrac{5\pi}{3},\ 2\pi+\dfrac{\pi}{3}=\dfrac{7\pi}{3},\ 4\pi-\dfrac{\pi}{3}=\dfrac{11\pi}{3}$.

Divide by $2$: $x=\dfrac{\pi}{6},\ \dfrac{5\pi}{6},\ \dfrac{7\pi}{6},\ \dfrac{11\pi}{6}$.

Practice 1

Solve $2\sin(3x)=1$ for $0\le x\le\dfrac{2\pi}{3}$.

Solution

Let $u=3x$, so $0\le u\le2\pi$. $\sin u=\tfrac12 \Rightarrow u=\dfrac{\pi}{6}$ or $\dfrac{5\pi}{6}$. So $x=\dfrac{\pi}{18}$ or $\dfrac{5\pi}{18}$.

Practice 2

Solve $2\cos^2x + 3\sin x = 3$ for $0\le x\le2\pi$.

Solution

Use $\cos^2x=1-\sin^2x$: $2(1-\sin^2x)+3\sin x=3 \Rightarrow -2\sin^2x+3\sin x-1=0 \Rightarrow 2\sin^2x-3\sin x+1=0 \Rightarrow (2\sin x-1)(\sin x-1)=0$. So $\sin x=\tfrac12 \Rightarrow x=\dfrac{\pi}{6},\dfrac{5\pi}{6}$, or $\sin x=1 \Rightarrow x=\dfrac{\pi}{2}$.

Practice 3

Solve $\sin x + \cos x = 1$ for $0\le x\le2\pi$ by squaring both sides (and check for extraneous roots).

Solution

Square: $\sin^2x+2\sin x\cos x+\cos^2x=1 \Rightarrow 1+\sin2x=1 \Rightarrow \sin2x=0 \Rightarrow 2x=0,\pi,2\pi,3\pi,4\pi \Rightarrow x=0,\dfrac{\pi}{2},\pi,\dfrac{3\pi}{2},2\pi$. Checking each in the original (unsquared) equation: $x=0$: $0+1=1$✓. $x=\dfrac{\pi}{2}$: $1+0=1$✓. $x=\pi$: $0-1=-1$✗. $x=\dfrac{3\pi}{2}$: $-1+0=-1$✗. $x=2\pi$: $0+1=1$✓. Valid solutions: $x=0,\dfrac{\pi}{2},2\pi$.

Practice 4

Solve $\tan^2x - 1 = 0$ for $0\le x\le2\pi$.

Solution

$\tan x=\pm1$. $\tan x=1$: $x=\dfrac{\pi}{4},\dfrac{5\pi}{4}$. $\tan x=-1$: $x=\dfrac{3\pi}{4},\dfrac{7\pi}{4}$.

Practice 5

A pendulum's angular displacement is $\theta(t)=0.2\cos(3t)$. Find the smallest positive $t$ for which $\theta(t)=0.1$.

Solution

$0.2\cos(3t)=0.1 \Rightarrow \cos(3t)=0.5 \Rightarrow 3t=\dfrac{\pi}{3}$ (smallest positive solution) $\Rightarrow t=\dfrac{\pi}{9}$.

1 Complex Numbers: Cartesian Form and Arithmetic

A complex number extends the real numbers by introducing $j=\sqrt{-1}$ (engineers typically write $j$ rather than $i$, to avoid clashing with current $i$). Arithmetic with complex numbers works exactly like arithmetic with real binomials, with one extra rule: $j^2=-1$.

Key definitions

$z=a+bj$ has real part $\text{Re}(z)=a$ and imaginary part $\text{Im}(z)=b$. The conjugate $z^*=a-bj$. To divide by a complex number, multiply top and bottom by the conjugate of the denominator, since $(a+bj)(a-bj)=a^2+b^2$ is always real.

Worked example

Given $z_1=1-j$ and $z_2=-2+4j$, evaluate $\alpha=2z_2-3z_1^*$ and $\beta=\text{Im}\!\left(\dfrac{z_1}{z_2}\right)$.

$z_1^*=1+j$. $2z_2=-4+8j$. $3z_1^*=3+3j$. $\alpha = (-4+8j)-(3+3j) = -7+5j$.

$\dfrac{z_1}{z_2}=\dfrac{1-j}{-2+4j}\times\dfrac{-2-4j}{-2-4j}=\dfrac{(1-j)(-2-4j)}{4+16}=\dfrac{-2-4j+2j+4j^2}{20}=\dfrac{-2-2j-4}{20}=\dfrac{-6-2j}{20}=-\dfrac{3}{10}-\dfrac{1}{10}j$.

$\beta = \text{Im}(z_1/z_2) = -\dfrac{1}{10}$.

Practice 6

For $z_1=3+2j$ and $z_2=1-4j$, find $z_1+z_2$ and $z_1z_2$.

Solution

$z_1+z_2=4-2j$. $z_1z_2=(3)(1)+(3)(-4j)+(2j)(1)+(2j)(-4j)=3-12j+2j-8j^2=3-10j+8=11-10j$.

Practice 7

Find $(2-3j)^2$.

Solution

$(2-3j)^2=4-12j+9j^2=4-12j-9=-5-12j$.

Practice 8

Simplify $\dfrac{5}{2+j}$.

Solution

$\dfrac{5}{2+j}\times\dfrac{2-j}{2-j}=\dfrac{10-5j}{4+1}=\dfrac{10-5j}{5}=2-j$.

Practice 9

If $z=3-4j$, find $z\cdot z^*$ and $|z|$, and verify $|z|^2=z\cdot z^*$.

Solution

$z^*=3+4j$. $z\cdot z^* = 9+16=25$. $|z|=\sqrt{9+16}=5$. Check: $|z|^2=25=z\cdot z^*$. ✓

Practice 10

Solve $z+3j\bar z = 4-2j$ for the complex number $z=x+yj$ (find $x$ and $y$).

Solution

$\bar z=x-yj$. $z+3j\bar z=(x+yj)+3j(x-yj)=x+yj+3xj-3yj^2=x+3y+(y+3x)j$. Equate to $4-2j$: $x+3y=4$ and $3x+y=-2$. From the second, $y=-2-3x$. Substitute: $x+3(-2-3x)=4 \Rightarrow x-6-9x=4 \Rightarrow-8x=10 \Rightarrow x=-\tfrac54$. Then $y=-2-3(-\tfrac54)=-2+\tfrac{15}{4}=\tfrac74$.

Practice 11

An AC circuit has impedance $Z_1=3+4j\ \Omega$ and $Z_2=2-j\ \Omega$ in series. Find the total impedance $Z_1+Z_2$ and its magnitude.

Solution

$Z_1+Z_2=5+3j\ \Omega$. Magnitude: $\sqrt{25+9}=\sqrt{34}\ \Omega$.

2 Polar (Modulus-Argument) Form

Cartesian form ($a+bj$) is natural for addition and subtraction, but multiplication and division are far cleaner in polar form, which represents a complex number by its distance from the origin ($r$, the modulus) and its angle from the positive real axis ($\theta$, the argument).

Key idea

$z=a+bj = r(\cos\theta+j\sin\theta)$ where $r=|z|=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$ and $\theta=\arg z$, found from $\tan\theta=\dfrac{b}{a}$, with the quadrant of $(a,b)$ determining the correct angle.

Multiplication/division in polar form: $r_1(\cos\theta_1+j\sin\theta_1)\times r_2(\cos\theta_2+j\sin\theta_2) = r_1r_2\big(\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2)+j\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2)\big)$ — moduli multiply, arguments add.

Worked example

Find the polar form of $z=-1+j\sqrt3$.

$r=\sqrt{(-1)^2+(\sqrt3)^2}=\sqrt{1+3}=2$.

$\tan\theta=\dfrac{\sqrt3}{-1}$: reference angle $\dfrac{\pi}{3}$, but since $a<0,b>0$, $z$ is in the second quadrant, so $\theta=\pi-\dfrac{\pi}{3}=\dfrac{2\pi}{3}$.

$$z = 2\left(\cos\frac{2\pi}{3}+j\sin\frac{2\pi}{3}\right).$$

Practice 12

Find the polar form of $z=3+3j$.

Solution

$r=\sqrt{9+9}=3\sqrt2$. Both parts positive: Q1, $\theta=\dfrac{\pi}{4}$. $z=3\sqrt2\left(\cos\dfrac{\pi}{4}+j\sin\dfrac{\pi}{4}\right)$.

Practice 13

Find the polar form of $z=-4$.

Solution

$r=4$. Purely negative real: $\theta=\pi$. $z=4(\cos\pi+j\sin\pi)$.

Practice 14

Find the polar form of $z=2-2j\sqrt3$.

Solution

$r=\sqrt{4+12}=4$. $a>0,b<0$: Q4. Reference angle $\arctan(\sqrt3)=\dfrac{\pi}{3}$, so $\theta=-\dfrac{\pi}{3}$. $z=4\left(\cos(-\tfrac{\pi}{3})+j\sin(-\tfrac{\pi}{3})\right)$.

Practice 15

If $z_1=2(\cos\tfrac{\pi}{6}+j\sin\tfrac{\pi}{6})$ and $z_2=5(\cos\tfrac{\pi}{3}+j\sin\tfrac{\pi}{3})$, find $z_1z_2$ and $\dfrac{z_1}{z_2}$ in polar form.

Solution

$z_1z_2=10\left(\cos\tfrac{\pi}{2}+j\sin\tfrac{\pi}{2}\right)$ (moduli multiply: $2\times5=10$; arguments add: $\tfrac{\pi}{6}+\tfrac{\pi}{3}=\tfrac{\pi}{2}$). $\dfrac{z_1}{z_2}=\dfrac25\left(\cos(-\tfrac{\pi}{6})+j\sin(-\tfrac{\pi}{6})\right)$ (moduli divide, arguments subtract).

Practice 16

Convert $z=5\left(\cos\dfrac{5\pi}{6}+j\sin\dfrac{5\pi}{6}\right)$ to Cartesian form.

Solution

$\cos\tfrac{5\pi}{6}=-\dfrac{\sqrt3}{2}$, $\sin\tfrac{5\pi}{6}=\dfrac12$. $z=5\left(-\dfrac{\sqrt3}{2}+\dfrac12j\right)=-\dfrac{5\sqrt3}{2}+\dfrac52j$.

Practice 17

A phasor voltage is $V=100(\cos40^\circ+j\sin40^\circ)$ V and a phasor current is $I=5(\cos10^\circ+j\sin10^\circ)$ A. Find the complex power $S=VI^*$ (using the conjugate of $I$), in polar form.

Solution

$I^*=5(\cos(-10^\circ)+j\sin(-10^\circ))$ (conjugating negates the argument). $S=VI^*$: moduli multiply $100\times5=500$; arguments add $40^\circ+(-10^\circ)=30^\circ$. $S=500(\cos30^\circ+j\sin30^\circ)$ VA.

3 Solving Polynomial Equations Over $\mathbb{C}$, and Simple Loci

Complex numbers guarantee that every polynomial equation has a solution — a quadratic with a negative discriminant, which has no real roots, always has exactly two complex conjugate roots instead. Loci (paths traced by $z$ satisfying a given condition) are a natural extension of the Argand-diagram picture of a complex number as a point in the plane.

Worked example

Solve $z^2-4z+13=0$.

Quadratic formula: $z=\dfrac{4\pm\sqrt{16-52}}{2}=\dfrac{4\pm\sqrt{-36}}{2}=\dfrac{4\pm6j}{2}=2\pm3j$.

Two complex conjugate roots: $z=2+3j$ and $z=2-3j$, as expected whenever the discriminant is negative.

Practice 18

Solve $z^2+2z+5=0$.

Solution

$z=\dfrac{-2\pm\sqrt{4-20}}{2}=\dfrac{-2\pm4j}{2}=-1\pm2j$.

Practice 19

Solve $z^2-6z+25=0$.

Solution

$z=\dfrac{6\pm\sqrt{36-100}}{2}=\dfrac{6\pm8j}{2}=3\pm4j$.

Practice 20

If $z=3+4j$ is one root of a quadratic with real coefficients, state the other root, and hence find the quadratic.

Solution

Complex roots of a real-coefficient polynomial occur in conjugate pairs, so the other root is $3-4j$. Sum $=6$, product $=(3+4j)(3-4j)=9+16=25$. Quadratic: $z^2-6z+25=0$.

Practice 21

Describe the locus of points $z$ in the Argand plane satisfying $|z-2-3j|=5$.

Solution

This is the set of points at distance $5$ from the fixed point $2+3j$: a circle of radius $5$ centred at $(2,3)$.

Practice 22

Describe the locus of points $z$ satisfying $|z-1|=|z+3|$.

Solution

This is the set of points equidistant from $1$ (i.e. $(1,0)$) and $-3$ (i.e. $(-3,0)$): the perpendicular bisector of the segment joining them, i.e. the vertical line $x=-1$.
