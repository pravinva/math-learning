0 Why do we even need this?

You already know how to add two matrices together, entry by entry, and how to multiply a matrix by a scalar (multiply every entry by that number). That is genuinely almost everything you need to get started here. Everything in this lesson is really just those two operations, applied cleverly, to rows of numbers.

Here is the motivating problem. Suppose you have a system of linear equations, like

$$x + 2y + z = 4, \qquad 3x + 7y + 8z = 18, \qquad x + 2y + 3z = 6.$$

You could try to solve this by substitution, the way you probably did in high school: solve the first equation for $x$, substitute into the other two, and repeat. That works, but it gets messy fast, especially once you have four, five, or twenty equations. We want a systematic, mechanical procedure that always works, is easy to check, and scales to any number of equations. That procedure is called Gaussian elimination, and it is built entirely out of simple row manipulations of a matrix.

Along the way we will also meet the determinant, a single number attached to a square matrix that tells you, among other things, whether a system like the one above has exactly one solution, no solution, or infinitely many. The two ideas, row reduction and determinants, turn out to be deeply connected: the fastest way to compute a determinant by hand for anything bigger than a $3\times3$ matrix is to row-reduce it.

We will build up in this order: legal row operations first, then the "staircase" shape called echelon form, then the elimination algorithm itself, then how all of this connects to determinants and to the question of whether a system of equations is solvable at all.

1 The three legal row operations

First, notice that the system of equations above can be written as an augmented matrix, just by stripping away the variable names and the plus signs, and keeping the numbers in their positions:

$$\left(\begin{array}{ccc|c}1&2&1&4\\3&7&8&18\\1&2&3&6\end{array}\right)$$

Each row of this matrix is just one of the original equations, written compactly. This means that anything you are allowed to do to an equation without changing its solution, you are also allowed to do to a row of the matrix. Think back to solving equations: you can swap the order you write two equations in, you can multiply both sides of an equation by a nonzero number, and you can add a multiple of one equation to another. All three of these are completely ordinary algebra, and none of them change what values of $x$, $y$, $z$ satisfy the system. Translated into matrix language, these become the three elementary row operations:

Key idea

1. Swap two rows. $R_i \leftrightarrow R_j$. This just reorders which equation you write first; it changes nothing about which $x,y,z$ satisfy all of them.

2. Scale a row by a nonzero constant. $R_i \to kR_i$. Multiplying both sides of an equation by the same nonzero number preserves the equation.

3. Add a multiple of one row to another. $R_i \to R_i + kR_j$. If two equations both hold, then adding $k$ times one of them to the other still holds, since you are adding equal amounts to both sides.

These are exactly the same moves you already know from adding and scaling matrices, just restricted to specific rows instead of the whole matrix at once. Operation 2 is scalar multiplication of a $1\times n$ row. Operation 3 is a scalar multiple of one row added to another, which is literally matrix addition applied to two rows. There is no new arithmetic here, only a new way of using arithmetic you already have.

The single most important fact about these three operations is this: none of them change the solution set of the system. If $(x,y,z)$ satisfies the original system, it satisfies every system you get afterwards by applying any sequence of these operations, and vice versa. That is the whole reason row operations are useful for solving equations: you are allowed to keep transforming the matrix into a simpler and simpler shape, and whatever solution you eventually read off is guaranteed to be the solution to the equations you started with.

Worked example

Solve $x+y=3$ and $2x+5y=12$ using row operations, and check that the solution really does satisfy both original equations.

Write the augmented matrix: $\left(\begin{array}{cc|c}1&1&3\\2&5&12\end{array}\right)$.

Apply $R_2 \to R_2 - 2R_1$: the new row 2 is $(2-2(1),\,5-2(1),\,12-2(3)) = (0,3,6)$.

Result: $\left(\begin{array}{cc|c}1&1&3\\0&3&6\end{array}\right)$, which says $x+y=3$ and $3y=6$.

From the second row, $y=2$. Substituting into the first row, $x+2=3$, so $x=1$.

Check against the original equations: $x+y = 1+2=3$ ✓, and $2x+5y = 2(1)+5(2)=2+10=12$ ✓. The row operation did not change the answer, it just made the second equation easier to read.

2 Row echelon form (REF)

Row operations are the tools. The goal, when solving a system, is to use those tools to push the matrix into a shape that is easy to read off an answer from. That target shape is called row echelon form, often abbreviated REF. "Echelon" just means a staircase pattern, and that is exactly what the matrix looks like once it is done.

In the worked example above, we turned $\left(\begin{array}{cc|c}1&1&3\\2&5&12\end{array}\right)$ into $\left(\begin{array}{cc|c}1&1&3\\0&3&6\end{array}\right)$. Notice the shape on the left of the second matrix: the first row starts with a nonzero entry in column 1, and the second row's first nonzero entry has moved over to column 2. That rightward staircase pattern of leading entries is the defining feature of echelon form.

Key idea: definition of REF

The first nonzero entry in a row (reading left to right) is called that row's pivot or leading entry. A matrix is in row echelon form when:

1. Every entry below a pivot, in the same column, is zero.

2. Each pivot sits strictly to the right of the pivot in the row above it.

3. Any rows that are entirely zero are gathered at the bottom.

Why does this shape matter? Because once a matrix is in echelon form, the corresponding system of equations can be solved from the bottom up, one variable at a time, without any more guessing. The bottom nonzero row only involves the last variable or two, so you can solve for it immediately; then you substitute that value upward into the row above, which now only has one unknown left in it, and so on. This process is called back-substitution, and we will use it constantly starting in the next section.

Worked example: is it REF?

Consider $\begin{pmatrix}1&2&3\\0&0&5\\0&0&0\end{pmatrix}$. The pivots are $1$ (row 1, column 1) and $5$ (row 2, column 3); row 2's pivot is strictly right of row 1's pivot, everything below each pivot in its column is zero, and the zero row sits at the bottom. This matrix is in REF, even though row 2 "skips" column 2 entirely, since REF only requires the pivots to move strictly rightward, not to move by exactly one column each time.

Practice 19

Is $\begin{pmatrix}0&0\\0&0\end{pmatrix}$ in row echelon form?

Solution

Yes, trivially. There are no pivots at all, so there is nothing for conditions 1 and 2 to apply to, and both zero rows are (vacuously) "gathered at the bottom," since there is no nonzero row anywhere for them to sit above. The all-zero matrix is always in REF, no matter its size.

3 Gaussian elimination

Gaussian elimination is simply the name for the systematic procedure of applying row operations, in a fixed order, to push any matrix into row echelon form. Once you reach REF, back-substitution finishes the job. Here is the procedure in words before we see it in action:

Key idea: the algorithm

1. Look at the first column. If the entry in row 1 is zero but some entry below it is not, swap rows so that a nonzero entry is on top. (If the whole column is zero, move to the next column.)

2. Use operation 3 (add a multiple of the pivot row to the rows below it) to turn every entry below that pivot into zero.

3. Move down and right to the next row and column, and repeat steps 1 and 2, ignoring rows and columns you have already finished with.

4. Once no more pivots can be created, the matrix is in echelon form. Solve for the last variable, then substitute upward, row by row, to find the rest.

Let's run this on the system we started the whole lesson with, since it is a clean example that uses every idea so far, and we will keep coming back to it later on.

Worked example: full elimination

Solve

$$x + 2y + z = 4, \qquad 3x + 7y + 8z = 18, \qquad x + 2y + 3z = 6.$$

Write the augmented matrix: $\left(\begin{array}{ccc|c}1&2&1&4\\3&7&8&18\\1&2&3&6\end{array}\right)$.

The pivot in row 1 is already the $1$ in the top-left corner, so we use it to clear out everything below it in column 1: $R_2 \to R_2 - 3R_1$ gives new row 2 $= (3-3,\,7-6,\,8-3,\,18-12) = (0,1,5,6)$. Then $R_3 \to R_3 - R_1$ gives new row 3 $= (1-1,\,2-2,\,3-1,\,6-4) = (0,0,2,2)$.

$$\left(\begin{array}{ccc|c}1&2&1&4\\0&1&5&6\\0&0&2&2\end{array}\right)$$

This is already in echelon form: the pivots ($1$, then $1$, then $2$) move strictly rightward down the rows. No more elimination is needed.

Back-substitute. The last row says $2z=2$, so $z=1$. Substitute into the middle row: $y + 5(1) = 6$, so $y = 1$. Substitute both into the top row: $x + 2(1) + 1 = 4$, so $x = 1$.

Solution: $(x,y,z) = (1,1,1)$. You can check this satisfies all three original equations directly.

Why this always works

Every step we used was one of the three legal row operations from Section 1, so the solution set never changed from the very first matrix to the very last. We are not solving a "new," different system by the time we reach echelon form: it is the exact same system, just rewritten in a form that happens to be trivial to finish by hand. That is the entire point of elimination: it trades algebraic cleverness for a completely mechanical, repeatable recipe.

4 Reduced row echelon form (RREF)

Row echelon form is enough to solve a system with back-substitution, but we can push the same idea one step further and make the answer appear directly, with no substitution required at all. If, on top of the REF conditions, we also insist that every pivot equals $1$, and that every entry directly above a pivot is also zero (not just below), the matrix is said to be in reduced row echelon form, or RREF. Reaching RREF is sometimes called Gauss–Jordan elimination, to distinguish it from ordinary Gaussian elimination that stops at plain REF.

Key idea: definition of RREF

A matrix is in reduced row echelon form when it satisfies the REF conditions, and in addition:

4. Every pivot is exactly $1$.

5. Every pivot is the only nonzero entry in its column, that is, entries above a pivot are zero as well as below.

When the augmented matrix of a system reaches RREF, the last column simply is the solution, with no further work needed.

Worked example: continuing to RREF

Take the echelon form we reached in Section 3: $\left(\begin{array}{ccc|c}1&2&1&4\\0&1&5&6\\0&0&2&2\end{array}\right)$. Instead of stopping to back-substitute, let's push all the way to RREF.

Make every pivot a 1. Scale $R_3 \to \frac12 R_3$: $(0,0,1,1)$.

$$\left(\begin{array}{ccc|c}1&2&1&4\\0&1&5&6\\0&0&1&1\end{array}\right)$$

REF or RREF: which should you use?

RREF is more satisfying because the answer appears automatically, but it usually takes more arithmetic steps to get there than plain REF plus back-substitution. In practice, by hand, REF with back-substitution is often faster for a one-off system. RREF becomes genuinely useful when you want a completely mechanical, foolproof answer with no separate back-substitution step, for example when working with a computer, or when you want to read off other structural information about the matrix (like the rank, coming up next) at a glance.

5 Rank

Once a matrix is in echelon form, one very natural thing to count is how many nonzero rows it ended up with, that is, how many pivots you actually found. This number is called the rank of the matrix, and it does not depend on which particular sequence of row operations you used to get there. No matter how you row-reduce a given matrix, you always end up with the same number of pivots.

Rank tells you how many of the original equations were actually giving you new information. If you start with three equations in three unknowns but end up with only two pivots after elimination, that means one of your original equations was secretly a combination of the other two, it was not adding anything new, and the system does not pin down a unique solution.

Key idea

The rank of a matrix is the number of nonzero rows in its row echelon form, equivalently, the number of pivots. For a square $n\times n$ matrix, if the rank equals $n$ (every row produced a pivot), the matrix has "full rank." If the rank is less than $n$, some rows became entirely zero during elimination, and the matrix is missing information; we call it "rank deficient" or singular.

Worked example

Find the rank of $\begin{pmatrix}1&2&3\\2&4&6\\1&1&1\end{pmatrix}$.

$R_2 \to R_2 - 2R_1$: $(0,0,0)$. Already this row has vanished entirely, a warning sign.

$R_3 \to R_3 - R_1$: $(0,-1,-2)$.

$$\begin{pmatrix}1&2&3\\0&0&0\\0&-1&-2\end{pmatrix}$$

Swap $R_2$ and $R_3$ to move the zero row to the bottom (standard practice for tidy echelon form): $\begin{pmatrix}1&2&3\\0&-1&-2\\0&0&0\end{pmatrix}$.

There are two nonzero rows, so the rank is $2$, even though the matrix is $3\times3$. It is rank deficient. This happened because row 2 of the original matrix was exactly twice row 1, it carried no new information at all.

6 Determinants: what and why

So far, every question we have asked has been about solving a system of equations. The determinant answers a slightly different, and in some ways more basic, question: without actually solving anything, can you tell just by looking at the coefficient matrix whether the system is guaranteed to have exactly one solution?

The determinant is a single number computed from a square matrix, and it turns out that this one number completely settles the question: a square matrix is invertible, meaning $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ has a unique solution for every choice of $\mathbf{b}$, exactly when its determinant is nonzero. We will make this precise in Section 8. For now, let's see what the determinant actually is and how to compute it for small matrices.

For a $2\times2$ matrix, the determinant has a wonderfully simple formula, and an equally simple geometric meaning.

Key idea: the 2×2 determinant

$$\det\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix} = ad - bc.$$

Geometrically, if you think of the rows $(a,b)$ and $(c,d)$ as two vectors drawn from the origin, then $|ad-bc|$ is exactly the area of the parallelogram those two vectors span. The sign tells you the orientation: positive if going from the first row-vector to the second is a counter-clockwise turn, negative if clockwise.

Here is that geometric picture made concrete. Take the vectors $(3,1)$ and $(1,2)$ as the two rows.

$$\det\begin{pmatrix}3&1\\1&2\end{pmatrix} = 3(2)-1(1) = 5.$$

And indeed, the shaded parallelogram in the picture above has area exactly $5$. This is not a coincidence, it is what the determinant is, for a $2\times2$ matrix: a signed area.

For a $3\times3$ matrix, the determinant is computed by cofactor expansion: pick any row (row 1 is the usual choice), and for each entry in that row, multiply it by the determinant of the smaller $2\times2$ matrix left over when you delete that entry's row and column, with alternating signs $+,-,+$ across the row.

Key idea: the 3×3 determinant by cofactor expansion

$$\det\begin{pmatrix}a&b&c\\d&e&f\\g&h&i\end{pmatrix} = a\begin{vmatrix}e&f\\h&i\end{vmatrix} - b\begin{vmatrix}d&f\\g&i\end{vmatrix} + c\begin{vmatrix}d&e\\g&h\end{vmatrix}$$

Each $2\times2$ piece is the "minor" left over after deleting that entry's row and column, and you already know how to compute those from the formula above.

Worked example

Compute $\det\begin{pmatrix}2&0&1\\1&3&2\\0&1&1\end{pmatrix}$ by expanding along the first row.

$$= 2\begin{vmatrix}3&2\\1&1\end{vmatrix} - 0\begin{vmatrix}1&2\\0&1\end{vmatrix} + 1\begin{vmatrix}1&3\\0&1\end{vmatrix}$$

$\begin{vmatrix}3&2\\1&1\end{vmatrix} = 3(1)-2(1) = 1$, and $\begin{vmatrix}1&3\\0&1\end{vmatrix} = 1(1)-3(0)=1$.

$$= 2(1) - 0 + 1(1) = 2+1 = 3.$$

7 Determinants by row reduction

Cofactor expansion works, but it gets painful fast: a $4\times4$ determinant by cofactor expansion requires computing four separate $3\times3$ determinants, each of which requires three $2\times2$ determinants. For anything bigger than $3\times3$, there is a much faster route, and it uses exactly the row operations you already know from Gaussian elimination.

The key fact is that the determinant of a matrix in echelon form (upper triangular, with zeros everywhere below the diagonal) is just the product of the diagonal entries. That is dramatically easier than cofactor expansion. So the strategy is: row-reduce the matrix to echelon form exactly as in Section 3, multiply the diagonal entries together, and then correct for whatever the row operations did to the determinant along the way.

That correction is the only new thing to learn here, and we actually already worked it out in an earlier lesson. Each of the three row operations affects the determinant in a specific, predictable way:

Why row operations affect the determinant the way they do

Swapping two rows flips the sign of the determinant ($\times-1$). This is because the determinant is an alternating function of the rows: a matrix with two identical rows always has determinant $0$ (its rows are linearly dependent, so it cannot be invertible), and swapping two nearly-identical rows must flip the sign to stay consistent with that fact.

Scaling a row by a constant $k$ scales the whole determinant by $k$. This is linearity: every term in the determinant's expansion uses exactly one entry from that row, so scaling the row scales every one of those terms by $k$.

Adding a multiple of one row to another ($R_i \to R_i + kR_j$) leaves the determinant completely unchanged. This also follows from linearity: $\det(\dots,R_i+kR_j,\dots) = \det(\dots,R_i,\dots) + k\det(\dots,R_j,\dots,R_j,\dots)$, and that second term is automatically zero because it has a repeated row $R_j$.

Putting this together: as you row-reduce, keep a running tally of how many row swaps you performed, and by what constants you scaled any rows. At the end, $\det(\text{original}) = (-1)^{\#\text{swaps}} \times \dfrac{1}{\prod(\text{scaling factors used})} \times (\text{product of the final diagonal})$. In practice, most of the time you will avoid scaling rows entirely and only use swaps and add-a-multiple operations, since those are free (they do not change the determinant at all, apart from the sign flip from a swap), which keeps the bookkeeping to just counting swaps.

One more thing needs explaining: when do you actually need to swap? The answer is, whenever the entry sitting in the pivot position is exactly zero, but some entry below it in the same column is not. You cannot use a zero pivot to eliminate the entries below it (there is nothing to work with), so you swap it out for a nonzero one first. Here is a worked example where that happens.

Worked example: a swap is necessary

Compute $\det\begin{pmatrix}0&2&1\\1&3&2\\2&1&4\end{pmatrix}$ by row reduction.

The $(1,1)$ entry is $0$, so we cannot eliminate below it yet. Swap $R_1 \leftrightarrow R_2$ (this flips the sign of the determinant):

$$\begin{pmatrix}1&3&2\\0&2&1\\2&1&4\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \det(\text{original}) = -\det(\text{this matrix}).$$

Eliminate below the new pivot: $R_3 \to R_3 - 2R_1$: $(2-2,\,1-6,\,4-4) = (0,-5,0)$. This operation does not change the determinant.

$$\begin{pmatrix}1&3&2\\0&2&1\\0&-5&0\end{pmatrix}$$

One more elimination: $R_3 \to R_3 + \frac52 R_2$: $\left(0,\,-5+\frac52(2),\,0+\frac52(1)\right) = (0,0,\tfrac52)$. Again, no change to the determinant.

$$\begin{pmatrix}1&3&2\\0&2&1\\0&0&\frac52\end{pmatrix}$$

This is upper triangular. Its determinant is the diagonal product: $1 \times 2 \times \frac52 = 5$.

We performed exactly one swap along the way, so $\det(\text{original}) = -5$. (You can double-check this using cofactor expansion on the original matrix; it agrees.)

Now let's use this to tackle something a plain cofactor expansion would make you dread: a $4\times4$ determinant.

Worked example: a full 4×4 determinant

Compute $\det\begin{pmatrix}2&4&2&6\\0&0&3&3\\1&1&1&1\\2&2&0&2\end{pmatrix}$.

Swap $R_1 \leftrightarrow R_3$ to get a convenient leading $1$ (sign flips once):

$$\begin{pmatrix}1&1&1&1\\0&0&3&3\\2&4&2&6\\2&2&0&2\end{pmatrix}$$

Eliminate column 1 below the pivot: $R_3 \to R_3-2R_1$: $(0,2,0,4)$. $\ R_4 \to R_4-2R_1$: $(0,0,-2,0)$. (No change to the determinant.)

$$\begin{pmatrix}1&1&1&1\\0&0&3&3\\0&2&0&4\\0&0&-2&0\end{pmatrix}$$

$$\begin{pmatrix}1&1&1&1\\0&2&0&4\\0&0&3&3\\0&0&-2&0\end{pmatrix}$$

This is exactly the same determinant, and the same method, from Question 1 of the SGTA Week 6 worksheet, worked through here in full detail so you can see precisely where every step comes from.

8 The big picture: invertibility

We now have three completely different-looking ways to investigate the same underlying question about a square matrix $A$: does the system $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ have exactly one solution? It turns out all three methods, row reduction, rank, and the determinant, always agree with each other. That is not a coincidence, and seeing why ties this whole lesson together.

Key idea: the equivalence theorem

For a square $n\times n$ matrix $A$, the following statements are all equivalent, meaning if any one of them is true, all of the others are true as well:

1. $\det(A) \neq 0$.

2. The row echelon form of $A$ has no zero rows, that is, $A$ has $n$ pivots.

3. $\mathrm{rank}(A) = n$ (full rank).

4. $A$ is invertible (there exists a matrix $A^{-1}$ with $AA^{-1}=A^{-1}A=I$).

5. $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ has exactly one solution, for every possible $\mathbf{b}\in\mathbb{R}^n$.

6. $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{0}$ has only the trivial solution $\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{0}$.

And, if any one of these fails, all of them fail together: $\det(A)=0$, some row vanishes during elimination, the rank is less than $n$, $A$ has no inverse, and $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ has either no solution or infinitely many solutions depending on $\mathbf{b}$ (never exactly one).

Why these all have to agree

The thread connecting everything is row reduction itself. When you row-reduce a square matrix, you either end up with a pivot in every row, or you end up with at least one zero row. Nothing else can happen.

If every row gets a pivot, the rank is automatically $n$ (full rank). Since the determinant of the echelon form is the product of the pivots, and none of the pivots are zero, the determinant is nonzero, and swaps or scalings along the way only ever change it by a nonzero factor (a sign, or a nonzero scalar), so the original determinant is nonzero too. A full set of pivots also means back-substitution can always solve for every variable, no matter what the right-hand side $\mathbf{b}$ is, giving exactly one solution.

If instead some row becomes entirely zero during elimination, the rank drops below $n$, the "diagonal product" for the determinant includes that zero row's missing pivot (effectively a zero factor), so the determinant is $0$, and the system no longer pins down a unique solution: that zero row translates into either an equation like $0=0$ (meaning infinitely many solutions remain, one free variable at least), or an equation like $0 = c$ for some nonzero $c$ (meaning no solution exists at all), depending on the specific right-hand side.

So all six statements are really just different ways of describing the exact same fork in the road that shows up the moment you start row-reducing: either you get a pivot in every row, or you don't.

Worked example: putting it all together

Recall $\begin{pmatrix}1&2&1\\3&7&8\\1&2&3\end{pmatrix}$, the coefficient matrix from our running system in Sections 3 and 4.

Row reducing (as in Section 3) gives $\begin{pmatrix}1&2&1\\0&1&5\\0&0&2\end{pmatrix}$, with a pivot in every row: rank $=3=n$, full rank.

The determinant is the diagonal product, with zero swaps used, so $\det = 1\times1\times2 = 2 \neq 0$.

By the theorem, this matrix is invertible, and indeed we found in Section 3 that $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ had exactly one solution, $(1,1,1)$, for the particular $\mathbf{b}$ we chose. The theorem guarantees this would hold for any choice of $\mathbf{b}$, not just that one.
