Direction Cosines & Vector Components
Angles a vector makes with each axis
Plane/space vectors, the dot product, angles, and projections were already built up in the Vectors in 3D guide (5A–5D) — this topic adds one new idea the tracker introduces in Week 10: describing a vector's direction purely by the angles it makes with each axis.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

The idea. For a vector $\mathbf{a}=(a_1,a_2,a_3)$, let $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$ be the angles it makes with the $x$, $y$, $z$ axes respectively. Since $\mathbf{i}=(1,0,0)$ is a unit vector along the $x$-axis, the angle-finding formula already built in the Vectors guide (5D) applies directly:

\[ \cos\alpha = \frac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{i}}{|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{i}|} = \frac{a_1}{|\mathbf{a}|} \]

and identically $\cos\beta=\dfrac{a_2}{|\mathbf{a}|}$, $\cos\gamma=\dfrac{a_3}{|\mathbf{a}|}$ — nothing new is being derived here, just the 5D angle formula applied three times, once per axis.

Why they satisfy a Pythagoras-like identity. $\cos^2\alpha+\cos^2\beta+\cos^2\gamma = \dfrac{a_1^2+a_2^2+a_3^2}{|\mathbf{a}|^2}=\dfrac{|\mathbf{a}|^2}{|\mathbf{a}|^2}=1$ — the direction cosines are literally just the components of the unit vector $\hat{\mathbf{a}}$ from the Vectors guide (5B), so of course they satisfy this identity.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \cos\alpha=\frac{a_1}{|\mathbf{a}|}, \quad \cos\beta=\frac{a_2}{|\mathbf{a}|}, \quad \cos\gamma=\frac{a_3}{|\mathbf{a}|}, \qquad \cos^2\alpha+\cos^2\beta+\cos^2\gamma=1 \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — find direction cosines

Find the direction cosines of $\mathbf{a}=(2,-1,2)$.

\[ |\mathbf{a}|=\sqrt{4+1+4}=3, \qquad \cos\alpha=\frac23,\ \cos\beta=-\frac13,\ \cos\gamma=\frac23 \]
ANSWER $\left(\tfrac23,-\tfrac13,\tfrac23\right)$
Example 2 — verify the identity

Check $\cos^2\alpha+\cos^2\beta+\cos^2\gamma=1$ for Example 1.

\[ \left(\frac23\right)^2+\left(-\frac13\right)^2+\left(\frac23\right)^2 = \frac49+\frac19+\frac49=\frac99=1 \]
ANSWER Confirmed
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
Direction cosines are literally the same three numbers as the unit vector $\hat{\mathbf{a}}$ from the Vectors guide — if you already know how to normalise a vector, you already know how to find direction cosines; it's the same computation with a new name attached.

Cross Product — Definition, Properties & Right-Hand Rule
A second vector product — this one returns a vector, not a scalar
The dot product measures "how aligned" two vectors are and returns a number. Sometimes what you actually need is a brand new vector perpendicular to both originals — direction and all — and that's exactly what the cross product supplies.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Motivating question. Given two non-parallel vectors $\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b}$, is there a systematic way to produce a third vector perpendicular to both? In 2D this makes no sense (there's no unique perpendicular direction), but in 3D, the plane containing $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ has exactly two perpendicular directions (opposite to each other) — the cross product picks one of them by a fixed convention.

The formula, and where it comes from. Built directly from the requirement "perpendicular to both $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$" — i.e. $(\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b})\cdot\mathbf{a}=0$ and $(\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b})\cdot\mathbf{b}=0$ simultaneously. Solving those two equations for the unknown vector's components produces:

\[ \mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix}a_2b_3-a_3b_2\\a_3b_1-a_1b_3\\a_1b_2-a_2b_1\end{pmatrix} \]

Right-hand rule. Point your right-hand fingers along $\mathbf{a}$, curl them toward $\mathbf{b}$; your thumb points along $\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}$ — this resolves the "which of the two perpendicular directions" ambiguity from the motivating question.

Key properties. $\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}=-(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{a})$ (anti-commutative — swapping order flips the direction), $|\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}|=|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin\theta$, and $\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}=\mathbf{0}$ exactly when $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ are parallel (no unique perpendicular direction exists).

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b} = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\a_1&a_2&a_3\\b_1&b_2&b_3\end{vmatrix}, \qquad |\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}|=|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin\theta \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — compute a cross product

Find $\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}$ for $\mathbf{a}=(1,0,2)$, $\mathbf{b}=(3,1,-1)$.

\[ \mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix}(0)(-1)-(2)(1)\\(2)(3)-(1)(-1)\\(1)(1)-(0)(3)\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}-2\\7\\1\end{pmatrix} \]
ANSWER $(-2,7,1)$
Example 2 — verify perpendicularity

Confirm the result from Example 1 is perpendicular to both $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$.

\[ (-2,7,1)\cdot(1,0,2) = -2+0+2=0, \qquad (-2,7,1)\cdot(3,1,-1)=-6+7-1=0 \]
ANSWER Both dot products are zero — confirmed perpendicular to both
Note Study notes ▶
Uni Bridge
This is the cross product teased back in the Vectors guide (5C). Note it's genuinely a 3D-only operation — there's no direct 2D analogue, since "perpendicular to a single 2D vector" isn't unique the way "perpendicular to two 3D vectors" is.

Cross Product — Geometric Interpretation & Applications
Area of a parallelogram/triangle, finding normals to planes
The formula $|\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}|=|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin\theta$ isn't just an algebraic curiosity — it's exactly the area formula for a parallelogram with sides $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$, which unlocks a fast way to compute areas and to find plane normals.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Area connection. A parallelogram with adjacent sides $\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b}$ and included angle $\theta$ has area (base $\times$ height) $=|\mathbf{a}|\cdot(|\mathbf{b}|\sin\theta) = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin\theta$ — precisely $|\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}|$. A triangle with the same two sides is exactly half that parallelogram, so its area is $\tfrac12|\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}|$.

Finding a plane's normal. The Vectors guide (5G) needed a normal vector $\mathbf{n}$ to write a plane's equation, but didn't say how to find one if you're only given three points on the plane. Now you can: take two vectors lying in the plane (e.g. $\overrightarrow{AB}$ and $\overrightarrow{AC}$), and their cross product is automatically perpendicular to both — hence perpendicular to the whole plane.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \text{Area of parallelogram} = |\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}|, \qquad \text{Area of triangle} = \tfrac12|\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}| \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — area of a triangle

Find the area of the triangle with vertices $A(0,0,0)$, $B(2,0,0)$, $C(0,3,0)$.

\[ \overrightarrow{AB}=(2,0,0), \ \overrightarrow{AC}=(0,3,0), \qquad \overrightarrow{AB}\times\overrightarrow{AC}=(0,0,6) \]
ANSWER Area $=\tfrac12|(0,0,6)|=3$ (matches $\tfrac12\times2\times3=3$ from basic geometry)
Example 2 — find a plane's equation from three points

Find the equation of the plane through $A(1,0,0)$, $B(0,1,0)$, $C(0,0,1)$.

\[ \overrightarrow{AB}=(-1,1,0), \ \overrightarrow{AC}=(-1,0,1), \qquad \mathbf{n}=\overrightarrow{AB}\times\overrightarrow{AC}=(1,1,1) \]

Using the 5G plane formula $\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{a}$: $\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{a}=1$.

ANSWER $x+y+z=1$
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
This example closes a gap that might have bothered you in the Vectors guide's 5G: there, the normal was always just given. Now you know the normal for a plane defined by three points is $\overrightarrow{AB}\times\overrightarrow{AC}$ — every plane-through-three-points problem now reduces to a cross product followed by the 5G recipe.

Scalar Triple Product — Volume of a Parallelepiped
Combining dot and cross products to measure 3D volume
A parallelogram's area came from the cross product; extending to three dimensions, a parallelepiped's volume comes from combining the cross product (which finds an area) with the dot product (which projects a third vector onto the direction perpendicular to that area) — exactly "base times height," dressed in vector notation.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Building the formula. Take a parallelepiped with edges $\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b},\mathbf{c}$ from a common vertex. Volume $=$ (area of base) $\times$ (height). Let the base be the parallelogram spanned by $\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b}$: its area is $|\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}|$, and $\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}$ points perpendicular to that base. The height is exactly the length of the projection of $\mathbf{c}$ onto that perpendicular direction — which the projection formula from the Vectors guide (5D) computes as $\dfrac{|\mathbf{c}\cdot(\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b})|}{|\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}|}$.

Multiply base area by height and the $|\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}|$ cancels, leaving a strikingly compact result:

\[ V = \big|\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c})\big| \]

Coplanarity test. Three vectors are coplanar (lie in the same plane, i.e. the "parallelepiped" is flattened to zero volume) exactly when $\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c})=0$.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c}) = \begin{vmatrix}a_1&a_2&a_3\\b_1&b_2&b_3\\c_1&c_2&c_3\end{vmatrix}, \qquad V=\left|\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c})\right| \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — volume of a parallelepiped

Find the volume with edges $\mathbf{a}=(1,0,0)$, $\mathbf{b}=(0,2,0)$, $\mathbf{c}=(0,0,3)$.

\[ \mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c}=(6,0,0), \qquad \mathbf{a}\cdot(6,0,0)=6 \]
ANSWER $6$ (matches $1\times2\times3$ from a rectangular box, as expected)
Example 2 — test for coplanarity

Are $\mathbf{a}=(1,1,0)$, $\mathbf{b}=(0,1,1)$, $\mathbf{c}=(1,2,1)$ coplanar?

\[ \mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c}=(1(1)-1(2),\ 1(1)-0(1),\ 0(2)-1(1)) = (-1,1,-1) \]
\[ \mathbf{a}\cdot(-1,1,-1)=-1+1+0=0 \]
ANSWER Yes — coplanar, since the scalar triple product is zero
Note Study notes ▶
Uni Bridge
The determinant form of the scalar triple product is your first hint of what's coming in Week 12–13: determinants of $3\times3$ matrices are computed with exactly this pattern, and the "zero determinant means degenerate/no unique solution" theme reappears constantly with matrices and linear systems.

Angle Between Planes & Line-Plane Intersection
Using normals to compare planes, finding where a line pierces a plane
A plane's normal vector fully captures its orientation — so comparing two planes' orientations, or checking where a line meets a plane, both reduce to normal-vector calculations already built in the Vectors guide.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Angle between two planes. The angle between two planes is defined as the angle between their normal vectors (or its supplement, by convention usually taking the acute angle) — directly reusing the 5D angle formula: $\cos\theta=\dfrac{\mathbf{n}_1\cdot\mathbf{n}_2}{|\mathbf{n}_1||\mathbf{n}_2|}$.

Parallel and perpendicular planes. Planes are parallel exactly when their normals are parallel ($\mathbf{n}_1=k\mathbf{n}_2$); perpendicular exactly when their normals are perpendicular ($\mathbf{n}_1\cdot\mathbf{n}_2=0$) — the same tests from 5F applied to normals instead of direction vectors.

Line-plane intersection. Substitute the line's vector equation $\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{a}+\lambda\mathbf{b}$ (5F) directly into the plane's equation $\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{v}=d$ (5G), and solve for the single unknown $\lambda$ — this gives the specific point where they meet (or reveals no solution/infinitely many if the line is parallel to or lies within the plane).

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \cos\theta=\frac{\mathbf{n}_1\cdot\mathbf{n}_2}{|\mathbf{n}_1||\mathbf{n}_2|}, \qquad \mathbf{n}\cdot(\mathbf{a}+\lambda\mathbf{b})=d \ \implies\ \text{solve for }\lambda \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — angle between two planes

Find the angle between $x+y+z=1$ and $x-y+2z=3$.

\[ \mathbf{n}_1=(1,1,1),\ \mathbf{n}_2=(1,-1,2), \qquad \mathbf{n}_1\cdot\mathbf{n}_2=1-1+2=2 \]
\[ \cos\theta=\frac{2}{\sqrt3\cdot\sqrt6}=\frac{2}{\sqrt{18}}=\frac{2}{3\sqrt2} \implies \theta\approx61.9^\circ \]
ANSWER $\approx61.9^\circ$
Example 2 — line-plane intersection

Find where $\mathbf{r}=(1,0,1)+\lambda(1,1,-1)$ meets the plane $x+2y+z=6$.

\[ (1+\lambda)+2(\lambda)+(1-\lambda)=6 \implies 2+2\lambda=6 \implies \lambda=2 \]
\[ \mathbf{r}=(1,0,1)+2(1,1,-1)=(3,2,-1) \]
ANSWER $(3,2,-1)$
Note Study notes ▶
Watch out
If substituting the line into the plane equation gives an equation with no $\lambda$ left (a contradiction like $0=5$), the line is parallel to the plane and never meets it; if it gives an identity true for all $\lambda$ (like $0=0$), the entire line lies within the plane.

Matrices — Definition, Types & Notation
Rows, columns, notation, types of matrices, the Kronecker delta
A matrix is simply a rectangular grid of numbers — but the specific vocabulary around its shape, special cases, and index notation is what makes every later rule (addition, multiplication, inverses) precise enough to state without ambiguity.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Notation. A matrix $A$ with $m$ rows and $n$ columns is "$m\times n$." The entry in row $i$, column $j$ is written $a_{ij}$ — row index always comes first.

Special types. Square ($m=n$), row vector ($1\times n$), column vector ($m\times1$ — literally the same object as the column vectors from the Vectors guide, now given a formal name), zero matrix (all entries $0$), identity matrix $I$ (ones on the diagonal, zeros elsewhere), diagonal matrix (nonzero entries only on the diagonal), symmetric matrix ($a_{ij}=a_{ji}$ for all $i,j$).

The Kronecker delta. $\delta_{ij}=1$ if $i=j$, and $0$ otherwise — a compact way to define the identity matrix: $I_{ij}=\delta_{ij}$.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ A = \begin{pmatrix}a_{11}&a_{12}\\a_{21}&a_{22}\end{pmatrix}, \qquad I=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \delta_{ij}=\begin{cases}1&i=j\\0&i\ne j\end{cases} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — identify entries and shape

For $A=\begin{pmatrix}2&-1&0\\3&5&7\end{pmatrix}$, state the shape and find $a_{12}$, $a_{23}$.

ANSWER $2\times3$; $a_{12}=-1$, $a_{23}=7$
Example 2 — check symmetry

Is $A=\begin{pmatrix}1&2&3\\2&4&5\\3&5&6\end{pmatrix}$ symmetric?

Check $a_{ij}=a_{ji}$ for every off-diagonal pair: $a_{12}=2=a_{21}$, $a_{13}=3=a_{31}$, $a_{23}=5=a_{32}$.

ANSWER Yes, symmetric
Note Study notes ▶
Uni Bridge
The column vector notation from the Vectors guide was always secretly matrix notation ($n\times1$) — this topic makes that connection official. Everything you already know about adding/scaling column vectors is a special case of the general matrix rules coming next.

Matrix Addition, Scalar Multiplication & Matrix Multiplication
Component-wise operations, and the row-times-column multiplication rule
Addition and scalar multiplication extend the vector rules you already know component-by-component to a full grid. Matrix multiplication looks nothing like that at first — but it's built from a specific real-world need: composing linear transformations, which forces a "row dot column" rule rather than an entry-by-entry one.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Addition and scalar multiplication. Exactly like vectors: add matching entries, or scale every entry — but only matrices of the same shape can be added.

Why multiplication is NOT entry-by-entry. If matrices represent linear transformations (each row of $A$ is really a set of dot-product instructions), then "apply transformation $A$, then transformation $B$" needs $(AB)$'s entry $(i,j)$ to be the dot product of row $i$ of $A$ with column $j$ of $B$ — this is forced by wanting composition of transformations to correspond to matrix multiplication.

\[ (AB)_{ij} = \sum_{k} a_{ik}b_{kj} \]

Dimension requirement. For $AB$ to be defined, the number of columns of $A$ must equal the number of rows of $B$ — otherwise there's no way to pair up a "row of $A$" with a "column of $B$" for the dot product.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ (A+B)_{ij}=a_{ij}+b_{ij}, \qquad (kA)_{ij}=k\,a_{ij}, \qquad (AB)_{ij}=\sum_k a_{ik}b_{kj} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — matrix multiplication

Compute $\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\3&4\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}5&6\\7&8\end{pmatrix}$.

\[ \begin{pmatrix}1(5)+2(7)&1(6)+2(8)\\3(5)+4(7)&3(6)+4(8)\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}19&22\\43&50\end{pmatrix} \]
ANSWER $\begin{pmatrix}19&22\\43&50\end{pmatrix}$
Example 2 — check whether AB and BA are both defined

For $A$ ($2\times3$) and $B$ ($3\times2$), which of $AB$, $BA$ are defined, and what shape is each?

$AB$: columns of $A$ (3) match rows of $B$ (3) — defined, shape $2\times2$. $BA$: columns of $B$ (2) match rows of $A$ (2) — also defined, shape $3\times3$.

ANSWER Both defined, but different shapes — $AB\ne BA$ in general, even when both exist
Note Study notes ▶
Watch out
Matrix multiplication is not commutative — $AB\ne BA$ in general, sometimes not even the same shape. This is the single biggest mindset shift from ordinary number arithmetic; always keep track of the order.
W12
Inverse Matrices — 2×2 Formula & Properties
Definition, the 2×2 shortcut, invertibility conditions
For numbers, "dividing by $a$" really means "multiplying by $a^{-1}$," where $a\cdot a^{-1}=1$. Matrices don't have division, but they do have an analogous idea: a matrix $A^{-1}$ such that $AA^{-1}=A^{-1}A=I$ — provided such a matrix exists at all.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Definition. $A^{-1}$ is the matrix satisfying $AA^{-1}=A^{-1}A=I$. Only square matrices can have inverses, and even then, not every square matrix does.

The $2\times2$ shortcut. For $A=\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix}$, define $\det(A)=ad-bc$ (the determinant — a preview of Week 13). Then:

\[ A^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{pmatrix}d&-b\\-c&a\end{pmatrix} \]

Invertibility condition. $A^{-1}$ exists exactly when $\det(A)=ad-bc\ne0$ — dividing by zero is exactly as forbidden here as it is for ordinary numbers, and this determinant is precisely the thing you'd be dividing by.

Key properties. $(A^{-1})^{-1}=A$, $(AB)^{-1}=B^{-1}A^{-1}$ (note the order reverses — provable by checking $(AB)(B^{-1}A^{-1})=A(BB^{-1})A^{-1}=AIA^{-1}=AA^{-1}=I$).

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ AA^{-1}=A^{-1}A=I, \qquad A^{-1}=\frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{pmatrix}d&-b\\-c&a\end{pmatrix} \ (\text{for }2\times2) \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — find a 2×2 inverse

Find the inverse of $A=\begin{pmatrix}2&1\\5&3\end{pmatrix}$.

\[ \det(A)=2(3)-1(5)=1, \qquad A^{-1}=\frac{1}{1}\begin{pmatrix}3&-1\\-5&2\end{pmatrix} \]
ANSWER $\begin{pmatrix}3&-1\\-5&2\end{pmatrix}$
Example 2 — verify the inverse

Confirm $AA^{-1}=I$ for Example 1.

\[ \begin{pmatrix}2&1\\5&3\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}3&-1\\-5&2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}6-5&-2+2\\15-15&-5+6\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix} \]
ANSWER Confirmed — equals $I$
Note Study notes ▶
Uni Bridge
The condition $\det(A)\ne0$ for invertibility is the same condition that guarantees a linear system $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ has a unique solution — Week 13 develops both determinants and linear systems in tandem, and this connection is why.
W13
Linear Systems — Matrix Representation & Row Operations
Writing systems as Ax=b, elementary row operations, augmented matrices
A system of linear equations is really just several dot-product conditions stacked together — matrix multiplication (Week 12) packages the whole system into a single compact equation, and elementary row operations give a systematic way to simplify that system without changing its solution set.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Matrix representation. The system $\begin{cases}a_1x+b_1y=c_1\\a_2x+b_2y=c_2\end{cases}$ is exactly the statement $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$, where $A=\begin{pmatrix}a_1&b_1\\a_2&b_2\end{pmatrix}$, $\mathbf{x}=\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\end{pmatrix}$, $\mathbf{b}=\begin{pmatrix}c_1\\c_2\end{pmatrix}$ — check this is exactly right by expanding the matrix multiplication and comparing to the original equations.

The augmented matrix. Write $A$ and $\mathbf{b}$ side by side as $[A\,|\,\mathbf{b}]$ — a compact record of the whole system without needing to keep rewriting the variable names.

Elementary row operations. Three moves that are guaranteed not to change the solution set, because each corresponds to a valid algebraic manipulation of the original equations: (1) swap two rows (reorder the equations — doesn't change anything), (2) scale a row by a nonzero constant (multiply an equation by a nonzero number — still true), (3) add a multiple of one row to another (add a true equation to another true equation — still true).

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}, \qquad [A\,|\,\mathbf{b}], \qquad R_i \leftrightarrow R_j,\ \ kR_i,\ \ R_i+kR_j \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — write the matrix form

Write $\begin{cases}2x+3y=7\\x-y=1\end{cases}$ as $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ and as an augmented matrix.

\[ \begin{pmatrix}2&3\\1&-1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}7\\1\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \left[\begin{array}{cc|c}2&3&7\\1&-1&1\end{array}\right] \]
ANSWER As above
Example 2 — apply a row operation

Row Echelon Form & Gaussian Elimination
Systematic use of row operations to solve systems
Row operations alone are just legal moves — Gaussian elimination is the strategy that applies them in a fixed, systematic order to reach a simplified "staircase" shape from which the solution can be read off by simple back-substitution.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Row echelon form (REF). A matrix is in REF if: all-zero rows are at the bottom, and each row's first nonzero entry (its "pivot") sits strictly to the right of the pivot in the row above — producing a staircase pattern of leading entries.

Gaussian elimination — the strategy. Work column by column, left to right: use row operations to turn every entry below the current pivot into zero, then move to the next column and repeat, only ever using rows below the current one.

Back-substitution. Once in REF, the bottom row usually gives one variable directly; substitute it upward into the row above to find the next variable, and so on, working from the bottom row to the top.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c}\ast&\ast&\ast&\ast\\0&\ast&\ast&\ast\\0&0&\ast&\ast\end{array}\right] \quad\text{(staircase pattern of pivots)} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — reduce to REF and back-substitute

Solve $\begin{cases}x+y+z=6\\2x-y+z=3\\x+2y-z=2\end{cases}$.

\[ \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c}1&1&1&6\\2&-1&1&3\\1&2&-1&2\end{array}\right] \xrightarrow{R_2-2R_1,\ R_3-R_1} \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c}1&1&1&6\\0&-3&-1&-9\\0&1&-2&-4\end{array}\right] \]
\[ \xrightarrow{R_3+\tfrac13R_2} \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c}1&1&1&6\\0&-3&-1&-9\\0&0&-\tfrac73&-7\end{array}\right] \]

Back-substitute: $-\tfrac73 z=-7\implies z=3$; $-3y-3=-9\implies y=2$; $x+2+3=6\implies x=1$.

ANSWER $x=1,\ y=2,\ z=3$
Note Study notes ▶
Watch out
If Gaussian elimination produces a row like $(0,0,0\,|\,5)$ — all zeros on the left, nonzero on the right — that row represents $0=5$, a contradiction, meaning the system has no solution. A row of all zeros (including the right side) instead signals infinitely many solutions.

Reduced Row Echelon Form & Gauss-Jordan
Taking elimination one step further to read off the solution directly
Row echelon form still requires back-substitution to finish solving a system. Pushing the elimination a little further — clearing entries *above* each pivot too, and scaling every pivot to exactly $1$ — produces a form where the solution can be read off immediately, with no further algebra.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Reduced row echelon form (RREF). Row echelon form, with two extra conditions: every pivot equals exactly $1$, and every entry directly above a pivot is also zero (not just below, as in REF).

Gauss-Jordan elimination. The same strategy as Gaussian elimination, but after clearing below each pivot, also clear above it (working from the bottom pivot back up), and scale each pivot row so its pivot becomes $1$.

Why RREF is worth the extra work. Once a system's augmented matrix is in RREF, each row directly states one variable's value (or its relationship to any free variables) — no back-substitution step is needed at all, unlike with plain REF.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c}1&0&0&\ast\\0&1&0&\ast\\0&0&1&\ast\end{array}\right] \quad\text{(solution read off directly)} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — continue REF to RREF

Continue Example 1 from the REF topic: $\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c}1&1&1&6\\0&-3&-1&-9\\0&0&-\tfrac73&-7\end{array}\right]$ to RREF.

Scale $R_3$ to make its pivot $1$: $R_3\to-\tfrac37R_3$ gives $(0,0,1\,|\,3)$. Clear above: $R_2\to R_2+R_3$, $R_1\to R_1-R_3$, then scale $R_2$, clear above that pivot too.

\[ \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c}1&0&0&1\\0&1&0&2\\0&0&1&3\end{array}\right] \]
ANSWER $x=1,y=2,z=3$ — read off directly, matching the back-substitution answer
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
RREF and REF always agree on the final solution — RREF is purely a convenience that trades extra row-operation work for skipping the back-substitution step. On paper under exam time pressure, REF plus quick back-substitution is often faster; RREF is more useful when a computer is doing the arithmetic.

Determinants — 2×2 and 3×3
Definition, cofactor expansion, geometric meaning
The determinant is a single number computed from a square matrix that answers "does this matrix have an inverse?" — and, not coincidentally, it also measures the volume-scaling factor of the linear transformation the matrix represents, tying directly back to the scalar triple product from Week 11.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

$2\times2$ determinant. $\det\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix}=ad-bc$ — already used as the denominator in the Week 12 inverse formula.

$3\times3$ determinant via cofactor expansion. Expand along the first row, alternating signs:

\[ \det\begin{pmatrix}a&b&c\\d&e&f\\g&h&i\end{pmatrix} = a(ei-fh) - b(di-fg) + c(dh-eg) \]

Each $2\times2$ piece is the determinant of the matrix left after deleting the row and column of the entry you're expanding by ("minor"), and the alternating $+,-,+$ signs come from the checkerboard cofactor pattern.

Connection to the scalar triple product. This is exactly the same computation as $\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c})$ from Week 11 — the $3\times3$ determinant of the matrix with rows $\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b},\mathbf{c}$ literally is that scalar triple product.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \det\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix}=ad-bc, \qquad \det(3\times3) = a(ei-fh)-b(di-fg)+c(dh-eg) \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — 2×2 determinant

Find $\det\begin{pmatrix}4&3\\2&5\end{pmatrix}$.

\[ 4(5)-3(2)=14 \]
ANSWER $14$
Example 2 — 3×3 determinant

Find $\det\begin{pmatrix}1&2&0\\3&1&1\\0&2&2\end{pmatrix}$.

\[ 1(1\cdot2-1\cdot2) - 2(3\cdot2-1\cdot0) + 0(\cdots) = 1(0)-2(6)+0 = -12 \]
ANSWER $-12$
Note Study notes ▶
Uni Bridge
Determinants generalise to $n\times n$ matrices with the same cofactor-expansion recipe (recursively, using smaller determinants), and $\det(A)=0$ is the universal test for "no inverse exists" / "linear system doesn't have a unique solution" — the single most important number attached to a square matrix.

Cramer's Rule
Solving linear systems directly using determinants
Gaussian elimination works for any system, but for small systems where you only need one or two of the variables (not the full solution process), Cramer's Rule offers a direct determinant-based formula — built by systematically tracking how each variable's coefficient column affects the overall determinant.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

The rule. For $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ with $\det(A)\ne0$, each variable $x_i$ is found by replacing the $i$-th column of $A$ with $\mathbf{b}$, taking the determinant of that new matrix, and dividing by $\det(A)$:

\[ x_i = \frac{\det(A_i)}{\det(A)}, \qquad A_i = A \text{ with column } i \text{ replaced by } \mathbf{b} \]

Why it only works when $\det(A)\ne0$. Exactly the same restriction as matrix inverses (Week 12) — and this is no coincidence: Cramer's Rule is really just an explicit formula for $\mathbf{x}=A^{-1}\mathbf{b}$, worked out entry by entry using cofactor expansions.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ x = \frac{\det(A_x)}{\det(A)}, \quad y = \frac{\det(A_y)}{\det(A)} \quad \text{(for a } 2\times2 \text{ system)} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — solve a 2×2 system with Cramer's Rule

Solve $\begin{cases}2x+3y=8\\x-y=-1\end{cases}$.

\[ \det(A)=\begin{vmatrix}2&3\\1&-1\end{vmatrix}=-2-3=-5 \]
\[ x=\frac{\begin{vmatrix}8&3\\-1&-1\end{vmatrix}}{-5}=\frac{-8+3}{-5}=\frac{-5}{-5}=1, \qquad y=\frac{\begin{vmatrix}2&8\\1&-1\end{vmatrix}}{-5}=\frac{-2-8}{-5}=\frac{-10}{-5}=2 \]
ANSWER $x=1,\ y=2$
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
Cramer's Rule is elegant for $2\times2$ and manageable for $3\times3$ systems, but the number of determinants needed grows fast (four $3\times3$ determinants for a $3\times3$ system) — for larger systems, or systems needing every variable, Gaussian elimination is almost always faster in practice.
