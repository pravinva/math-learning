W1
Algebraic Foundations
Indices, surds, quadratics, binomial expansion, absolute value
Before any calculus can happen, you need total fluency manipulating expressions — every derivative and integral problem hides an algebra problem inside it.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Indices. $a^m\cdot a^n=a^{m+n}$, $(a^m)^n=a^{mn}$, $a^{-n}=\dfrac{1}{a^n}$, $a^{1/n}=\sqrt[n]{a}$ — all of these follow from "multiplying is repeated counting of factors," so $a^m\cdot a^n$ just concatenates $m$ copies of $a$ with $n$ copies of $a$, giving $m+n$ copies total.

Surds. Simplify by pulling out perfect-square factors: $\sqrt{50}=\sqrt{25\cdot2}=5\sqrt2$. Rationalise denominators by multiplying by a conjugate: $\dfrac{1}{\sqrt2}=\dfrac{\sqrt2}{2}$, and $\dfrac{1}{1+\sqrt2}=\dfrac{1-\sqrt2}{(1+\sqrt2)(1-\sqrt2)}=\dfrac{1-\sqrt2}{-1}=\sqrt2-1$.

Quadratics. Three tools: factorising, completing the square, and the formula $x=\dfrac{-b\pm\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}$ — all three are really the same statement, just extracted at different points of the same derivation (complete the square first, and the formula falls out).

Binomial expansion. $(a+b)^n=\sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k}a^{n-k}b^k$ — coefficients come from Pascal's triangle, or directly from $\binom{n}{k}=\dfrac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$.

Absolute value. $|x|$ is the distance from $x$ to $0$ — never negative. Solving $|x-3|=5$ means "distance from $3$ is $5$," giving $x=8$ or $x=-2$.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ a^m a^n=a^{m+n},\quad (ab)^n=a^nb^n,\quad \sqrt[n]{a}=a^{1/n} \]\[ (a+b)^2=a^2+2ab+b^2,\quad x=\frac{-b\pm\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \]\[ |x|=\begin{cases}x & x\ge0\\-x& x<0\end{cases} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — rationalise and simplify

Simplify $\dfrac{3}{\sqrt5-1}$.

\[ \frac{3}{\sqrt5-1}\cdot\frac{\sqrt5+1}{\sqrt5+1} = \frac{3(\sqrt5+1)}{5-1}=\frac{3(\sqrt5+1)}{4} \]
ANSWER $\frac{3(\sqrt5+1)}{4}$
Example 2 — solve an absolute value equation

Solve $|2x+1|=7$.

\[ 2x+1=7 \text{ or } 2x+1=-7 \implies x=3 \text{ or } x=-4 \]
ANSWER $x=3$ or $x=-4$
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
Every mistake in a calculus problem that "shouldn't have happened" is almost always an algebra slip from this topic, not a calculus misunderstanding — index laws and surd manipulation need to be automatic before Week 2.
W1
Coordinate Geometry
Lines, circles, intersection of curves
Calculus lives on the coordinate plane, so fluency describing lines and circles algebraically is the visual vocabulary everything else builds on.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Lines. Gradient $m=\dfrac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}$ measures steepness. Point-gradient form: $y-y_1=m(x-x_1)$. Parallel lines share gradients; perpendicular lines have gradients multiplying to $-1$.

Circles. A circle is "every point a fixed distance $r$ from a centre $(h,k)$" — translate directly using the distance formula: $(x-h)^2+(y-k)^2=r^2$.

Intersections. To find where two curves meet, set their equations equal (or substitute one into the other) and solve simultaneously — the solutions are the shared $(x,y)$ points.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ y-y_1=m(x-x_1), \qquad (x-h)^2+(y-k)^2=r^2 \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — equation of a line

Find the equation of the line through $(1,2)$ and $(4,8)$.

\[ m=\frac{8-2}{4-1}=2, \qquad y-2=2(x-1) \implies y=2x \]
ANSWER $y=2x$
Example 2 — circle-line intersection

Find where $x^2+y^2=25$ meets $y=x+1$.

\[ x^2+(x+1)^2=25 \implies 2x^2+2x-24=0 \implies x^2+x-12=0 \implies (x+4)(x-3)=0 \]
ANSWER $(-4,-3)$ and $(3,4)$
Note Study notes ▶
Uni Bridge
Circles reappear as level curves in multivariable calculus (e.g. $x^2+y^2=c$ for a function $f(x,y)$), and the perpendicular-gradient condition here is the 2D shadow of the orthogonality ideas from vectors.
W1
Functions
Domain, range, composition, one-to-one, inverse functions, trig review
A function is a precise promise — one output per input. Everything calculus does (limits, derivatives, integrals) is a statement about how that promise behaves.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Domain and range. Domain = every input the rule can accept without breaking (no division by zero, no square root of a negative). Range = every output that actually gets produced.

Composition. $(f\circ g)(x)=f(g(x))$ — apply $g$ first, then feed the result into $f$. The domain of the composite is restricted to $x$ where $g(x)$ itself lands in $f$'s domain.

One-to-one and inverses. A function is one-to-one if no two different inputs give the same output (passes the horizontal line test). Only one-to-one functions have genuine inverses — swap $x$ and $y$ and solve for $y$ to build $f^{-1}$.

Trig review. $\sin,\cos,\tan$ and their reciprocals, the unit circle, and identities like $\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta=1$ are assumed fluent from here on.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ (f\circ g)(x)=f(g(x)), \qquad f^{-1}(f(x))=x \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — domain

Find the domain of $f(x)=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{x-3}}$.

Need $x-3>0$ (strictly, since it's also a denominator).

ANSWER $x>3$
Example 2 — find an inverse

Find $f^{-1}(x)$ for $f(x)=2x+3$.

\[ y=2x+3 \implies x=\frac{y-3}{2} \implies f^{-1}(x)=\frac{x-3}{2} \]
ANSWER $f^{-1}(x)=\dfrac{x-3}{2}$
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
The chain rule (Week 3) is literally the derivative rule for composite functions — get comfortable identifying "outer function" and "inner function" now, since that skill is 80% of applying the chain rule correctly.
W2
Limits
Intuitive definition, algebra of limits, one-sided limits, limits at infinity
A derivative is defined as a limit. Before differentiating anything, you need a solid working sense of what a limit is actually asking.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Intuitive idea. $\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L$ means: as $x$ gets arbitrarily close to $a$ (from either side), $f(x)$ gets arbitrarily close to $L$ — regardless of what $f(a)$ itself is, or even whether $f(a)$ exists.

Algebra of limits. Limits of sums, products, and quotients behave exactly as you'd hope: $\lim(f+g)=\lim f+\lim g$, provided both limits exist (and for quotients, the denominator's limit isn't zero).

One-sided limits. $\lim_{x\to a^-}$ approaches from below, $\lim_{x\to a^+}$ from above. The two-sided limit exists only if both one-sided limits exist and agree.

Limits at infinity. $\lim_{x\to\infty}f(x)$ asks what $f(x)$ approaches as $x$ grows without bound — for rational functions, divide every term by the highest power of $x$ in the denominator.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L \iff \lim_{x\to a^-}f(x)=\lim_{x\to a^+}f(x)=L \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — factor and cancel

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2}\dfrac{x^2-4}{x-2}$.

\[ \frac{x^2-4}{x-2}=\frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2}=x+2 \ \ (x\ne2) \implies \lim_{x\to2}(x+2)=4 \]
ANSWER 4
Example 2 — limit at infinity

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{3x^2+1}{x^2-5}$.

\[ \frac{3x^2+1}{x^2-5} = \frac{3+1/x^2}{1-5/x^2} \to \frac{3+0}{1-0}=3 \]
ANSWER 3
Note Study notes ▶
Watch out
A limit existing at $a$ says nothing about $f(a)$ — the function can be undefined right at that point (as in Example 1, where $f(2)$ is undefined) and the limit still exists perfectly well.
W2
Continuity
Definition, examples and non-examples, algebra of continuous functions
Continuity is the formal way of saying "you could draw this graph without lifting your pen" — and it turns out to be exactly the condition that makes limits and function values agree.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Definition. $f$ is continuous at $x=a$ if three things all hold: $f(a)$ exists, $\lim_{x\to a}f(x)$ exists, and they're equal. Fail any one of the three and $f$ is discontinuous at $a$.

Types of discontinuity. Removable (a hole — the limit exists but doesn't match $f(a)$, or $f(a)$ is undefined), jump (the one-sided limits disagree), and infinite (the function blows up).

Algebra of continuous functions. Sums, products, quotients (where the denominator isn't zero), and compositions of continuous functions are continuous — this is inherited directly from the algebra of limits.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ f \text{ continuous at } a \iff \lim_{x\to a}f(x) = f(a) \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — check continuity at a point

Is $f(x)=\dfrac{x^2-1}{x-1}$ (with $f(1)=2$ defined separately) continuous at $x=1$?

\[ \lim_{x\to1}\frac{x^2-1}{x-1}=\lim_{x\to1}(x+1)=2=f(1) \]
ANSWER Yes — continuous, since the limit equals $f(1)$
Example 2 — find a value making a piecewise function continuous

Find $k$ so $f(x)=\begin{cases}x+k & x<2\\x^2 & x\ge2\end{cases}$ is continuous at $x=2$.

\[ \lim_{x\to2^-}(x+k)=2+k, \qquad \lim_{x\to2^+}x^2=4 \implies 2+k=4 \implies k=2 \]
ANSWER $k=2$
Note Study notes ▶
Uni Bridge
Continuity is the minimum requirement for the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus and for Rolle's/Mean Value Theorem later — always check it before applying either.
W2-3
Derivatives — Definition & Rules
Definition of derivative, algebra of derivatives, chain rule, implicit differentiation, higher order derivatives
The derivative starts as a limit describing the slope of a tangent line — but computing every derivative from that limit directly would be exhausting, so we build a toolkit of shortcut rules derived from it once and reused forever.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Definition. $f'(x)=\lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$ — the slope of the secant line through $(x,f(x))$ and $(x+h,f(x+h))$, as $h\to0$.

Algebra of derivatives. Power rule $\frac{d}{dx}x^n=nx^{n-1}$, sum rule, product rule $\frac{d}{dx}(uv)=u'v+uv'$, quotient rule $\frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{u}{v}\right)=\frac{u'v-uv'}{v^2}$ — all provable from the limit definition, but used as shortcuts from here on.

Chain rule. For a composite $f(g(x))$: $\frac{d}{dx}f(g(x))=f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x)$ — differentiate the outer function (leaving the inner alone), then multiply by the derivative of the inner function.

Implicit differentiation. When $y$ isn't isolated, differentiate both sides with respect to $x$, treating $y$ as a function of $x$ (so every $y$-term picks up a $\frac{dy}{dx}$ via the chain rule), then solve for $\frac{dy}{dx}$.

Higher order derivatives. $f''(x)$ is just the derivative of $f'(x)$ — repeat the process as many times as needed.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ f'(x)=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}, \qquad \frac{d}{dx}x^n=nx^{n-1} \]\[ (uv)'=u'v+uv', \qquad \left(\frac{u}{v}\right)'=\frac{u'v-uv'}{v^2}, \qquad \frac{d}{dx}f(g(x))=f'(g(x))g'(x) \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — chain rule

Differentiate $y=(3x^2-1)^5$.

\[ \frac{dy}{dx}=5(3x^2-1)^4\cdot 6x = 30x(3x^2-1)^4 \]
ANSWER $30x(3x^2-1)^4$
Example 2 — implicit differentiation

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ for $x^2+y^2=25$.

\[ 2x+2y\frac{dy}{dx}=0 \implies \frac{dy}{dx}=-\frac{x}{y} \]
ANSWER $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-\dfrac{x}{y}$
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
Misidentifying "outer" and "inner" functions is the single most common chain rule error — always ask "what's the very last operation applied, reading the expression left to right?" That's your outer function.
W3
Applications of Derivatives — Max/Min
Maximum and minimum problems, First Derivative Test
A derivative being zero flags a flat tangent — the natural place to look for a peak or valley. Turning that observation into a reliable procedure is what this topic builds.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Critical points. Where $f'(x)=0$ or $f'(x)$ is undefined — candidates for local maxima or minima.

First Derivative Test. Check the sign of $f'$ just before and after a critical point: $+$ to $-$ means local max, $-$ to $+$ means local min, no sign change means neither.

Optimisation problems. Translate a word problem into a function of one variable (using constraints to eliminate extra variables), then find critical points and test them — remembering to check the domain's endpoints too if it's a closed interval.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ f'(c)=0 \text{ or undefined} \implies c \text{ is a critical point} \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — find and classify critical points

Find and classify the critical points of $f(x)=x^3-3x$.

\[ f'(x)=3x^2-3=0 \implies x=\pm1 \]

Sign of $f'$: positive for $x<-1$, negative for $-11$.

ANSWER Local max at $x=-1$, local min at $x=1$
Example 2 — optimisation

A farmer has 40m of fencing to enclose a rectangular field against a wall (one side needs no fence). Maximise the area.

Let width $x$ (perpendicular to wall), length $40-2x$. Area $A=x(40-2x)=40x-2x^2$.

\[ A'(x)=40-4x=0 \implies x=10 \]
ANSWER Width $10$m, length $20$m, max area $200\text{m}^2$
Note Study notes ▶
Watch out
A critical point is only a candidate — always test it (sign change, or the Second Derivative Test from Week 7) before declaring it a max or min. Not every flat point is a turning point (e.g. $y=x^3$ at $x=0$).

Integration — Antiderivatives, Definite Integral & FTC
Antiderivatives, basic rules, summation notation, definite integral, Fundamental Theorem of Calculus
Differentiation answers "what's the instantaneous rate of change?" Integration asks the reverse question — and, remarkably, also answers "what's the total area under a curve?" The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus is the proof that these two questions have the same answer.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Antiderivatives. $F$ is an antiderivative of $f$ if $F'=f$. Since constants vanish under differentiation, antiderivatives come in families: $\int f(x)\,dx=F(x)+C$.

Basic rules. $\int x^n\,dx=\dfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}+C$ ($n\ne-1$) — the power rule for derivatives, run in reverse.

Summation and the definite integral. Approximate the area under a curve with $n$ thin rectangles, sum their areas ($\sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i)\Delta x$), then let $n\to\infty$ (rectangles get infinitely thin) — that limit is defined as $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$.

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus. If $F'=f$, then $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b)-F(a)$ — meaning you never actually have to take that limit of a sum; just find any antiderivative and evaluate it at the endpoints.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \int x^n\,dx=\frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}+C \ (n\ne-1), \qquad \int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b)-F(a) \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — indefinite integral

Find $\int (3x^2-4x+1)\,dx$.

\[ \int (3x^2-4x+1)\,dx = x^3-2x^2+x+C \]
ANSWER $x^3-2x^2+x+C$
Example 2 — definite integral via FTC

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^2 (x^2+1)\,dx$.

\[ \left[\frac{x^3}{3}+x\right]_0^2 = \left(\frac{8}{3}+2\right)-0 = \frac{14}{3} \]
ANSWER $\dfrac{14}{3}$
Note Study notes ▶
Uni Bridge
The Riemann-sum construction here (rectangles, limit as $n\to\infty$) is the exact template used to define more general integrals later, including line and surface integrals — the "chop it up, sum it, take the limit" pattern never really changes.
W4
Integration by Substitution
Recognising composite structure, u-substitution technique
Substitution is the integral counterpart of the chain rule — since differentiation "unwraps" a composite function by multiplying by the inner derivative, integration has to "rewrap" it by dividing that same inner derivative back out.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

The pattern to look for. If the integrand contains a function $g(x)$ and (up to a constant) its derivative $g'(x)$ multiplied in, that's the signal to substitute $u=g(x)$.

The mechanics. Let $u=g(x)$, so $du=g'(x)\,dx$. Rewrite the whole integral in terms of $u$ only, integrate normally, then substitute back $u=g(x)$ at the end.

Definite integrals. Either substitute back to $x$ before evaluating at the original limits, or convert the limits themselves to $u$-values and skip the substitute-back step entirely.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \int f(g(x))g'(x)\,dx = \int f(u)\,du, \qquad u=g(x) \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — basic substitution

Find $\int 2x(x^2+1)^4\,dx$.

Let $u=x^2+1$, $du=2x\,dx$.

\[ \int u^4\,du = \frac{u^5}{5}+C = \frac{(x^2+1)^5}{5}+C \]
ANSWER $\dfrac{(x^2+1)^5}{5}+C$
Example 2 — definite integral, convert limits

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^1 x\sqrt{x^2+1}\,dx$.

Let $u=x^2+1$, $du=2x\,dx$. When $x=0,u=1$; when $x=1,u=2$.

\[ \frac12\int_1^2 \sqrt{u}\,du = \frac12\left[\frac{2}{3}u^{3/2}\right]_1^2 = \frac13\left(2\sqrt2-1\right) \]
ANSWER $\dfrac{2\sqrt2-1}{3}$
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
If, after substituting, leftover $x$'s remain that won't cancel or convert cleanly into $u$'s, that's a strong signal you've picked the wrong $u$ — try a different piece of the integrand.
W4
Applications of Integration — Areas Between Curves
Setting up and evaluating area-between-curves integrals
A single integral gives area under one curve, measured from the x-axis. Subtracting two such integrals — top curve minus bottom curve — gives the area trapped between two curves instead.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Setup. If $f(x)\ge g(x)$ on $[a,b]$, the area between them is $\int_a^b\big(f(x)-g(x)\big)\,dx$ — think of it as an infinite stack of vertical strips, each of height "top minus bottom" and width $dx$.

Finding the limits. The bounds $a,b$ are usually the $x$-coordinates where the two curves intersect — solve $f(x)=g(x)$ first.

When curves cross inside the interval. If which curve is "on top" switches partway through, split the integral at the crossing point and swap the order of subtraction in each piece.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ A = \int_a^b \big(f(x)-g(x)\big)\,dx, \qquad f(x)\ge g(x) \text{ on } [a,b] \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — standard setup

Find the area between $y=x^2$ and $y=2x$ for $x\ge0$.

Intersections: $x^2=2x \implies x=0,2$. On $(0,2)$, $2x\ge x^2$.

\[ A=\int_0^2(2x-x^2)\,dx = \left[x^2-\frac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^2 = 4-\frac83 = \frac43 \]
ANSWER $\dfrac43$
Example 2 — curves that swap which is on top

Find the area between $y=x$ and $y=x^3$ for $-1\le x\le1$.

They cross at $x=-1,0,1$. On $(-1,0)$, $x^3\ge x$; on $(0,1)$, $x\ge x^3$.

\[ A=\int_{-1}^0(x^3-x)dx+\int_0^1(x-x^3)dx = \tfrac14+\tfrac14=\tfrac12 \]
ANSWER $\dfrac12$
Note Study notes ▶
Watch out
Always sketch (even roughly) which curve is on top before setting up the integral — the sign of the answer will be wrong if you subtract in the wrong order, even though the algebra all looks correct.
W5
Exponential & Logarithmic Functions
Graphs, properties, derivatives, integrals
Exponential functions are the unique functions whose rate of change is proportional to their own size — that single defining property drives every derivative and integral rule in this topic.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Graphs and properties. $e^x$ is always positive, always increasing, passes through $(0,1)$, and $\lim_{x\to-\infty}e^x=0$. $\ln x$ is its inverse: defined only for $x>0$, passes through $(1,0)$, increasing but ever more slowly.

Derivatives. $\dfrac{d}{dx}e^x=e^x$ — the defining property, stated as a derivative. By the chain rule, $\dfrac{d}{dx}e^{g(x)}=g'(x)e^{g(x)}$. And $\dfrac{d}{dx}\ln x=\dfrac1x$ — provable from the inverse function relationship with $e^x$.

Integrals. Running the derivative rules backwards: $\int e^x\,dx=e^x+C$, and $\int \dfrac1x\,dx=\ln|x|+C$.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \frac{d}{dx}e^x=e^x, \qquad \frac{d}{dx}\ln x=\frac1x, \qquad \int e^x dx=e^x+C, \qquad \int \frac1x dx=\ln|x|+C \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — chain rule with exponentials

Differentiate $y=e^{3x^2-1}$.

\[ y'=6x\,e^{3x^2-1} \]
ANSWER $6xe^{3x^2-1}$
Example 2 — log derivative via chain rule

Differentiate $y=\ln(x^2+1)$.

\[ y'=\frac{2x}{x^2+1} \]
ANSWER $\dfrac{2x}{x^2+1}$
Note Study notes ▶
Uni Bridge
The differential equation $y'=ky$ (rate of change proportional to size) that defines $e^x$ reappears constantly in engineering: population growth, radioactive decay, RC circuits — all solved by an exponential, for exactly this reason.
W5
Hyperbolic Functions
Definitions, graphs, derivatives, integrals
Hyperbolic functions are built directly from $e^x$ and $e^{-x}$ — designed so their derivative and identity structure mirrors ordinary trig functions almost exactly, just with a persistent sign difference.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Definitions. $\sinh x=\dfrac{e^x-e^{-x}}{2}$, $\cosh x=\dfrac{e^x+e^{-x}}{2}$, $\tanh x=\dfrac{\sinh x}{\cosh x}$. Both are built purely from exponentials, so every hyperbolic derivative traces back to $\frac{d}{dx}e^x=e^x$.

Key identity. $\cosh^2x-\sinh^2x=1$ (compare to $\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta=1$ — same shape, opposite sign in the middle, which is why they're called "hyperbolic": this identity describes a hyperbola the way the trig one describes a circle).

Derivatives. $\dfrac{d}{dx}\sinh x=\cosh x$, $\dfrac{d}{dx}\cosh x=\sinh x$ — no minus sign anywhere, unlike ordinary trig derivatives, because differentiating $e^x$ never introduces one.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \sinh x=\frac{e^x-e^{-x}}{2}, \quad \cosh x=\frac{e^x+e^{-x}}{2}, \quad \cosh^2x-\sinh^2x=1 \]\[ \frac{d}{dx}\sinh x=\cosh x, \qquad \frac{d}{dx}\cosh x=\sinh x \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — derivative via definition

Show $\dfrac{d}{dx}\sinh x=\cosh x$ directly from the definition.

\[ \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{e^x-e^{-x}}{2}\right) = \frac{e^x+e^{-x}}{2} = \cosh x \]
ANSWER Confirmed directly from the exponential definitions
Example 2 — integral

Find $\int \cosh(2x)\,dx$.

\[ \int \cosh(2x)\,dx = \frac12\sinh(2x)+C \]
ANSWER $\dfrac12\sinh(2x)+C$
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
Whenever you're unsure of a hyperbolic identity or derivative sign, you can always fall back to the exponential definitions and re-derive it in seconds — there's nothing to memorise that isn't already implied by $\frac{d}{dx}e^x=e^x$.

Inverse Trigonometric Functions
Definitions, domains, derivatives, integrals
Ordinary trig functions aren't one-to-one over all of $\mathbb{R}$ — so before an inverse can exist, the domain has to be restricted to a piece where the function is one-to-one, and only then does the inverse make sense.
Pattern Build it: where these rules come from ▶

Restricted domains. $\sin x$ is restricted to $[-\pi/2,\pi/2]$ to define $\arcsin x$ (range matches this restricted domain); $\cos x$ to $[0,\pi]$ for $\arccos x$; $\tan x$ to $(-\pi/2,\pi/2)$ for $\arctan x$.

Derivatives. Derived via implicit differentiation on the defining equation (e.g. $y=\arcsin x \iff \sin y=x$, differentiate both sides, solve for $y'$, then convert back using the Pythagorean identity):

\[ \frac{d}{dx}\arcsin x=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}, \qquad \frac{d}{dx}\arctan x=\frac{1}{1+x^2} \]

Integrals. Running those derivatives backwards gives two extremely useful standard integral forms.

Definition The formal statement ▶
\[ \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}dx=\arcsin x+C, \qquad \int \frac{1}{1+x^2}dx=\arctan x+C \]
Example Worked examples ▶
Example 1 — derive the arcsin derivative

Show $\dfrac{d}{dx}\arcsin x=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}$.

Let $y=\arcsin x$, so $\sin y=x$. Differentiate implicitly: $\cos y\cdot y'=1 \implies y'=\dfrac{1}{\cos y}$.

\[ \cos y=\sqrt{1-\sin^2y}=\sqrt{1-x^2} \implies y'=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} \]
ANSWER Derived directly from implicit differentiation
Example 2 — a substitution-flavoured integral

Find $\int \dfrac{1}{4+x^2}\,dx$.

\[ \int \frac{1}{4+x^2}dx = \frac14\int\frac{1}{1+(x/2)^2}dx = \frac12\arctan\left(\frac{x}{2}\right)+C \]
ANSWER $\dfrac12\arctan\left(\dfrac{x}{2}\right)+C$
Note Study notes ▶
Study tip
Any integral with $\sqrt{a^2-x^2}$ or $a^2+x^2$ in the denominator is a strong visual cue to reach for arcsin or arctan — recognising the pattern instantly saves a lot of time under exam conditions.
