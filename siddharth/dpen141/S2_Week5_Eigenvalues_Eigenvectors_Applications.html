0 Eigenvalues

Recall from Matrices as Transformations: most vectors change direction when a matrix acts on them. An eigenvector is a special exception — a direction the matrix doesn't rotate at all, only stretches or shrinks (or flips). The stretch factor is the corresponding eigenvalue.

Key definition

$\lambda$ is an eigenvalue of $A$, with eigenvector $\mathbf{v}\ne\mathbf{0}$, if $$A\mathbf{v}=\lambda\mathbf{v}.$$ Rearranging: $(A-\lambda I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$. Since $\mathbf{v}\ne\mathbf{0}$, the matrix $A-\lambda I$ must be singular, giving the characteristic equation $$\det(A-\lambda I)=0.$$ Solving this polynomial equation in $\lambda$ gives every eigenvalue of $A$.

Worked example

Find the eigenvalues of $A=\begin{pmatrix}2&3\\3&2\end{pmatrix}$.

$A-\lambda I = \begin{pmatrix}2-\lambda&3\\3&2-\lambda\end{pmatrix}$.

$\det(A-\lambda I) = (2-\lambda)^2-9 = 0 \ \Rightarrow\ (2-\lambda)^2=9 \ \Rightarrow\ 2-\lambda=\pm3$.

$\lambda=2-3=-1$ or $\lambda=2+3=5$. Two eigenvalues: $\lambda_1=5,\ \lambda_2=-1$.

Practice 1

Find the eigenvalues of $\begin{pmatrix}4&0\\0&7\end{pmatrix}$, and explain why they could be read off without any calculation.

Solution

Eigenvalues $4$ and $7$. For a diagonal matrix, the diagonal entries themselves are always the eigenvalues — the standard basis vectors are already eigenvectors, since a diagonal matrix scales each axis independently without mixing them.

Practice 2

Find the eigenvalues of $\begin{pmatrix}5&2\\2&2\end{pmatrix}$.

Solution

$\det(A-\lambda I)=(5-\lambda)(2-\lambda)-4 = \lambda^2-7\lambda+10-4=\lambda^2-7\lambda+6=(\lambda-1)(\lambda-6)=0$. Eigenvalues $\lambda=1,6$.

Practice 3

Find the eigenvalues of $\begin{pmatrix}1&4\\2&3\end{pmatrix}$.

Solution

$(1-\lambda)(3-\lambda)-8=\lambda^2-4\lambda+3-8=\lambda^2-4\lambda-5=(\lambda-5)(\lambda+1)=0$. Eigenvalues $\lambda=5,-1$.

Practice 4

Find the eigenvalues of $\begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}$ (the $90^\circ$ rotation matrix), and interpret why they aren't real.

Solution

$(0-\lambda)(0-\lambda)-(-1)(1) = \lambda^2+1=0 \Rightarrow \lambda=\pm j$. A genuine rotation (other than by $0$ or $\pi$) never leaves any real direction unchanged — every real vector gets rotated to a new direction — so there can be no real eigenvalues at all, consistent with getting complex ones instead.

Practice 5

A $3\times3$ matrix has characteristic equation $\lambda^3-6\lambda^2+11\lambda-6=0$. Verify that $\lambda=1$ is a root, then find the remaining two eigenvalues.

Solution

$1-6+11-6=0$. ✓ Factor out $(\lambda-1)$: $\lambda^3-6\lambda^2+11\lambda-6=(\lambda-1)(\lambda^2-5\lambda+6)=(\lambda-1)(\lambda-2)(\lambda-3)$. Eigenvalues: $1,2,3$.

1 Eigenvectors

Once you have an eigenvalue $\lambda$, its eigenvectors are found by solving $(A-\lambda I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$ — a homogeneous system that (because $A-\lambda I$ is singular by construction) always has infinitely many solutions, forming a line (or higher-dimensional space) through the origin.

Key idea

Eigenvectors are only ever determined up to a scalar multiple — if $\mathbf{v}$ is an eigenvector, so is $c\mathbf{v}$ for any $c\ne0$. It's conventional to present the simplest integer-entry version.

Worked example

Find the eigenvectors of $A=\begin{pmatrix}2&3\\3&2\end{pmatrix}$ corresponding to $\lambda_1=5$ and $\lambda_2=-1$ (eigenvalues found in Section 0).

For $\lambda_1=5$: solve $(A-5I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$: $\begin{pmatrix}-3&3\\3&-3\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}v_1\\v_2\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0\\0\end{pmatrix}$. Both rows give $-3v_1+3v_2=0 \Rightarrow v_1=v_2$. Eigenvector: $\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$.

For $\lambda_2=-1$: solve $(A+I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$: $\begin{pmatrix}3&3\\3&3\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}v_1\\v_2\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0\\0\end{pmatrix}$. Both rows give $3v_1+3v_2=0 \Rightarrow v_1=-v_2$. Eigenvector: $\begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\end{pmatrix}$.

Check: $A\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}5\\5\end{pmatrix}=5\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$. ✓

Practice 6

Find the eigenvector of $\begin{pmatrix}4&0\\0&7\end{pmatrix}$ corresponding to $\lambda=4$.

Solution

$(A-4I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$: $\begin{pmatrix}0&0\\0&3\end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0} \Rightarrow 3v_2=0 \Rightarrow v_2=0$, $v_1$ free. Eigenvector: $\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$.

Practice 7

Find the eigenvectors of $\begin{pmatrix}5&2\\2&2\end{pmatrix}$ (eigenvalues $\lambda=1,6$ from Practice 2).

Solution

For $\lambda=1$: $(A-I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$: $\begin{pmatrix}4&2\\2&1\end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0} \Rightarrow 4v_1+2v_2=0 \Rightarrow v_2=-2v_1$. Eigenvector: $\begin{pmatrix}1\\-2\end{pmatrix}$. For $\lambda=6$: $\begin{pmatrix}-1&2\\2&-4\end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0} \Rightarrow -v_1+2v_2=0 \Rightarrow v_1=2v_2$. Eigenvector: $\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix}$.

Practice 8

Find the eigenvectors of $\begin{pmatrix}1&4\\2&3\end{pmatrix}$ (eigenvalues $\lambda=5,-1$ from Practice 3).

Solution

For $\lambda=5$: $\begin{pmatrix}-4&4\\2&-2\end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}\Rightarrow -4v_1+4v_2=0\Rightarrow v_1=v_2$. Eigenvector $\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$. For $\lambda=-1$: $\begin{pmatrix}2&4\\2&4\end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}\Rightarrow 2v_1+4v_2=0\Rightarrow v_1=-2v_2$. Eigenvector $\begin{pmatrix}2\\-1\end{pmatrix}$ (taking $v_2=-1$).

Practice 9

Verify directly that $\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix}$ is an eigenvector of $A=\begin{pmatrix}5&2\\2&2\end{pmatrix}$, and state its eigenvalue.

Solution

$A\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}10+2\\4+2\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}12\\6\end{pmatrix}=6\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix}$. ✓ Eigenvalue $6$.

Practice 10

For a $3\times3$ matrix with eigenvalue $\lambda=2$, the equation $(A-2I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$ reduces (after row operations) to the single equation $v_1-2v_2+v_3=0$. Describe the eigenspace (the set of all valid eigenvectors) for this eigenvalue, and give two linearly independent eigenvectors.

Solution

Since there's only one equation constraining three unknowns, two free parameters remain: the eigenspace is a whole plane through the origin, $v_1=2v_2-v_3$. Two independent eigenvectors, found by choosing free values: $v_2=1,v_3=0 \Rightarrow \begin{pmatrix}2\\1\\0\end{pmatrix}$; and $v_2=0,v_3=1 \Rightarrow \begin{pmatrix}-1\\0\\1\end{pmatrix}$.

2 Applications of Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

Eigenvalues and eigenvectors aren't just an algebraic curiosity — they're the natural language for describing repeated application of a matrix (population models, Markov chains, vibration modes) and for simplifying matrix powers dramatically via diagonalisation.

Key idea — diagonalisation and matrix powers

If $A$ has eigenvalues $\lambda_1,\lambda_2$ with independent eigenvectors $\mathbf{v}_1,\mathbf{v}_2$, forming $P=(\mathbf{v}_1\ \mathbf{v}_2)$ gives $$A = PDP^{-1}, \qquad D=\begin{pmatrix}\lambda_1&0\\0&\lambda_2\end{pmatrix}.$$ Then $A^n = PD^nP^{-1}$, and $D^n$ is trivial to compute (just raise each diagonal entry to the power $n$) — turning an otherwise painful repeated matrix multiplication into three easy steps.

Worked example

Given $A=\begin{pmatrix}2&3\\3&2\end{pmatrix}$ has eigenvalues $5,-1$ with eigenvectors $\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix},\begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\end{pmatrix}$ (from Sections 0–1), describe how you would compute $A^{10}$ efficiently, without multiplying $A$ by itself ten times.

Form $P=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{pmatrix}$ and $D=\begin{pmatrix}5&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}$, so $A=PDP^{-1}$.

Then $A^{10}=PD^{10}P^{-1}$, and $D^{10}=\begin{pmatrix}5^{10}&0\\0&(-1)^{10}\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}9765625&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix}$ — instantly, with no repeated matrix multiplication at all.

Only $P^{-1}$ and two matrix multiplications remain, versus nine successive matrix-matrix multiplications the direct way — a dramatic saving for large powers.

Practice 11

A population model has transition matrix $A=\begin{pmatrix}0.8&0.3\\0.2&0.7\end{pmatrix}$ with an eigenvalue $\lambda=1$. Explain the significance of an eigenvalue exactly equal to $1$ for the long-term behaviour of the population.

Solution

An eigenvalue of $1$ means there's a direction (a particular population distribution) that the matrix leaves completely unchanged, $A\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{v}$ — a steady state. Over time, repeated application of $A$ tends to drive the population distribution toward this eigenvector, provided the other eigenvalue has magnitude less than $1$ (so its contribution decays away).

Practice 12

If $A$ has eigenvalues $3$ and $-2$, state the eigenvalues of $A^2$, and explain the general rule.

Solution

Eigenvalues of $A^2$ are $3^2=9$ and $(-2)^2=4$. In general, if $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue of $A$ with eigenvector $\mathbf{v}$, then $A^2\mathbf{v}=A(\lambda\mathbf{v})=\lambda A\mathbf{v}=\lambda^2\mathbf{v}$, so $\lambda^2$ is an eigenvalue of $A^2$ with the same eigenvector.

Practice 13

The trace of a matrix (sum of diagonal entries) always equals the sum of its eigenvalues, and the determinant always equals their product. Use this shortcut to set up an equation for the eigenvalues of $\begin{pmatrix}7&2\\-3&-2\end{pmatrix}$, without computing $\det(A-\lambda I)$ directly.

Solution

Trace $=7+(-2)=5$. Determinant $=7(-2)-2(-3)=-14+6=-8$. Every $2\times2$ matrix's eigenvalues satisfy $\lambda^2-(\text{trace})\lambda+\det=0$, i.e. $\lambda^2-5\lambda-8=0$, giving $\lambda=\dfrac{5\pm\sqrt{25+32}}{2}=\dfrac{5\pm\sqrt{57}}{2}$.

Practice 14

Explain why a matrix with a repeated eigenvalue does not necessarily fail to be diagonalisable, but a matrix that cannot supply enough independent eigenvectors does.

Solution

Diagonalisability requires, for an $n\times n$ matrix, a full set of $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors — not necessarily $n$ distinct eigenvalues. A repeated eigenvalue can still be diagonalisable if its eigenspace has dimension matching the repetition (e.g. the identity matrix has eigenvalue $1$ repeated $n$ times, yet is trivially diagonal already). Failure occurs only when a repeated eigenvalue's eigenspace is smaller than its multiplicity, leaving too few independent directions to build $P$.

Practice 15

A vibrating system's mode shapes are the eigenvectors of its stiffness matrix $K=\begin{pmatrix}3&-1\\-1&3\end{pmatrix}$. Find the eigenvalues (representing squared natural frequencies) and describe the physical meaning of the eigenvector for the smaller eigenvalue.

Solution

$(3-\lambda)^2-1=0 \Rightarrow 3-\lambda=\pm1 \Rightarrow \lambda=2$ or $\lambda=4$. For $\lambda=2$: $(K-2I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0} \Rightarrow \begin{pmatrix}1&-1\\-1&1\end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}\Rightarrow v_1=v_2$, eigenvector $\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$: physically, both masses move together, in phase — the lower-frequency, ‘softer’ mode, since it requires less relative stretching of the connecting spring.
