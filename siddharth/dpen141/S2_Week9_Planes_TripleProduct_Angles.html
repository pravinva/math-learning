0 The Equation of a Plane

Just as a line is determined by a point and a direction, a plane is determined by a point and a normal vector — a vector perpendicular to every line lying within the plane. This single perpendicularity condition is all it takes to pin down the plane's equation.

Key formula

For a plane through point $A$ (position vector $\mathbf{a}$) with normal vector $\mathbf{n}=(n_1,n_2,n_3)$: any point $P=(x,y,z)$ on the plane satisfies $\mathbf{n}\cdot\overrightarrow{AP}=0$, giving $$n_1(x-a_1)+n_2(y-a_2)+n_3(z-a_3)=0, \quad\text{i.e.}\quad n_1x+n_2y+n_3z=d$$ for a constant $d=\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{a}$.

Worked example

Find the equation of the plane through $(1,-2,3)$ with normal vector $(2,1,-1)$.

$2(x-1)+1(y+2)-1(z-3)=0$.

Expand: $2x-2+y+2-z+3=0 \ \Rightarrow\ 2x+y-z+3=0$.

Practice 1

Find the equation of the plane through $(0,0,0)$ with normal vector $(1,2,3)$.

Solution

$1(x)+2(y)+3(z)=0 \Rightarrow x+2y+3z=0$.

Practice 2

Find the equation of the plane through $(2,1,-1)$ with normal vector $(3,-1,2)$.

Solution

$3(x-2)-1(y-1)+2(z+1)=0 \Rightarrow 3x-6-y+1+2z+2=0 \Rightarrow 3x-y+2z-3=0$.

Practice 3

State the normal vector to the plane $2x-3y+z=6$, and determine whether the point $(1,1,1)$ lies on it.

Solution

Normal vector $(2,-3,1)$ (the coefficients of $x,y,z$). At $(1,1,1)$: $2-3+1=0\ne6$, so the point does not lie on the plane.

Practice 4

Find the equation of the plane parallel to $x+2y-2z=5$ that passes through $(1,0,3)$.

Solution

Parallel planes share the same normal vector $(1,2,-2)$. Equation: $1(x-1)+2(y-0)-2(z-3)=0 \Rightarrow x-1+2y-2z+6=0 \Rightarrow x+2y-2z+5=0$.

Practice 5

Find the distance from the point $A(1,-2,3)$ to the plane $2x-2y+z=4$.

Solution

Using $\text{dist}=\dfrac{|n_1a_1+n_2a_2+n_3a_3-d|}{|\mathbf{n}|}$: $|2(1)-2(-2)+1(3)-4|=|2+4+3-4|=5$. $|\mathbf{n}|=\sqrt{4+4+1}=3$. Distance $=\dfrac53$.

1 Finding a Plane Through Three Points

Three non-collinear points determine a unique plane. The strategy: build two vectors lying in the plane from the three points, then cross them to get a normal vector — converting a "three points" description into the "point + normal" form from Section 0.

Key method

Given $A,B,C$, form $\overrightarrow{AB}$ and $\overrightarrow{AC}$ (both lie in the plane). The normal is $\mathbf{n}=\overrightarrow{AB}\times\overrightarrow{AC}$. Then use $A$ and $\mathbf{n}$ in the point-normal equation.

Worked example

Find the equation of the plane through $A=(1,0,0)$, $B=(0,1,0)$, $C=(0,0,1)$.

$\overrightarrow{AB}=(-1,1,0)$, $\ \overrightarrow{AC}=(-1,0,1)$.

$\mathbf{n}=\overrightarrow{AB}\times\overrightarrow{AC}$: $\mathbf{i}$: $(1)(1)-(0)(0)=1$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(-1)(1)-(0)(-1)]=1$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(-1)(0)-(1)(-1)=1$. $\mathbf{n}=(1,1,1)$.

Plane equation using point $A=(1,0,0)$: $1(x-1)+1(y)+1(z)=0 \ \Rightarrow\ x+y+z=1$.

Practice 6

Find the equation of the plane through $A=(1,1,1)$, $B=(2,0,1)$, $C=(1,2,0)$.

Solution

$\overrightarrow{AB}=(1,-1,0)$, $\overrightarrow{AC}=(0,1,-1)$. $\mathbf{n}$: $\mathbf{i}$: $(-1)(-1)-(0)(1)=1$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(1)(-1)-(0)(0)]=1$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(1)(1)-(-1)(0)=1$. $\mathbf{n}=(1,1,1)$. Equation: $1(x-1)+1(y-1)+1(z-1)=0 \Rightarrow x+y+z=3$.

Practice 7

Find the equation of the plane through $A=(2,0,0)$, $B=(0,3,0)$, $C=(0,0,4)$.

Solution

$\overrightarrow{AB}=(-2,3,0)$, $\overrightarrow{AC}=(-2,0,4)$. $\mathbf{n}$: $\mathbf{i}$: $(3)(4)-(0)(0)=12$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(-2)(4)-(0)(-2)]=8$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(-2)(0)-(3)(-2)=6$. $\mathbf{n}=(12,8,6)$. Equation using $A$: $12(x-2)+8y+6z=0 \Rightarrow 12x+8y+6z=24$, or dividing by $2$: $6x+4y+3z=12$.

Practice 8

Determine whether the three points $A=(1,1,1)$, $B=(2,2,2)$, $C=(3,3,3)$ determine a unique plane. Explain your reasoning.

Solution

$\overrightarrow{AB}=(1,1,1)$ and $\overrightarrow{AC}=(2,2,2)=2(1,1,1)$: parallel. All three points are collinear (they lie on a single line), so they do not determine a unique plane — infinitely many planes contain this line.

Practice 9

Find the equation of the plane through $A=(0,0,1)$, $B=(1,0,0)$, $C=(0,1,0)$, and compare it to the worked example's plane.

Solution

$\overrightarrow{AB}=(1,0,-1)$, $\overrightarrow{AC}=(0,1,-1)$. $\mathbf{n}$: $\mathbf{i}$: $(0)(-1)-(-1)(1)=1$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(1)(-1)-(-1)(0)]=1$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(1)(1)-(0)(0)=1$. $\mathbf{n}=(1,1,1)$, same normal as the worked example (as expected, since these are the same three points relabelled). Equation: $x+y+z=1$.

2 The Scalar Triple Product

Combining the dot and cross products in one expression, $\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c})$, gives a single number with a beautiful geometric meaning: it's (up to sign) the volume of the parallelepiped spanned by the three vectors. When that volume is zero, the three vectors are coplanar.

Key formula

$$\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c}) = \det\begin{pmatrix}a_1&a_2&a_3\\b_1&b_2&b_3\\c_1&c_2&c_3\end{pmatrix}.$$

Volume of the parallelepiped $= |\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c})|$. If this equals $0$, the three vectors are coplanar (lie in the same plane).

Worked example

Find the scalar triple product of $\mathbf{a}=(1,2,3)$, $\mathbf{b}=(0,1,4)$, $\mathbf{c}=(2,0,1)$, and interpret the result.

$$\det\begin{pmatrix}1&2&3\\0&1&4\\2&0&1\end{pmatrix}.$$ Expand along row 1: $1\det\begin{pmatrix}1&4\\0&1\end{pmatrix}-2\det\begin{pmatrix}0&4\\2&1\end{pmatrix}+3\det\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\2&0\end{pmatrix}$.

$= 1(1-0)-2(0-8)+3(0-2) = 1+16-6=11$.

The volume of the parallelepiped spanned by $\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b},\mathbf{c}$ is $|11|=11$ (cubic units); since it's nonzero, the three vectors are not coplanar.

Practice 10

Find $\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c})$ for $\mathbf{a}=(1,0,0)$, $\mathbf{b}=(0,1,0)$, $\mathbf{c}=(0,0,1)$.

Solution

$\det\begin{pmatrix}1&0&0\\0&1&0\\0&0&1\end{pmatrix}=1$ (the determinant of the identity matrix).

Practice 11

Determine whether $\mathbf{a}=(1,2,3)$, $\mathbf{b}=(2,4,6)$, $\mathbf{c}=(1,0,1)$ are coplanar.

Solution

$\mathbf{b}=2\mathbf{a}$, so row 2 of the determinant is a multiple of row 1: the determinant is automatically $0$. Coplanar.

Practice 12

Find the volume of the parallelepiped spanned by $\mathbf{a}=(2,0,0)$, $\mathbf{b}=(0,3,0)$, $\mathbf{c}=(0,0,4)$, and verify it matches simple geometry.

Solution

$\det\begin{pmatrix}2&0&0\\0&3&0\\0&0&4\end{pmatrix}=2\times3\times4=24$ (diagonal matrix, product of diagonal entries). Matches: this is just a $2\times3\times4$ rectangular box, volume $24$. ✓

Practice 13

Determine whether the four points $A=(0,0,0)$, $B=(1,1,0)$, $C=(1,0,1)$, $D=(2,1,1)$ are coplanar, using the scalar triple product of $\overrightarrow{AB},\overrightarrow{AC},\overrightarrow{AD}$.

Solution

$\overrightarrow{AB}=(1,1,0)$, $\overrightarrow{AC}=(1,0,1)$, $\overrightarrow{AD}=(2,1,1)$. $\det\begin{pmatrix}1&1&0\\1&0&1\\2&1&1\end{pmatrix}$: expand along row 1: $1(0-1)-1(1-2)+0=-1+1+0=0$. Coplanar.

3 Angle Between Planes

The angle between two planes is defined as the angle between their normal vectors — reducing yet another 3D geometry question straight back to the dot-product angle formula from Week 6.

Key formula

For planes with normals $\mathbf{n}_1,\mathbf{n}_2$: $$\cos\theta = \frac{|\mathbf{n}_1\cdot\mathbf{n}_2|}{|\mathbf{n}_1||\mathbf{n}_2|}.$$ (The absolute value ensures the reported angle is the acute one between the planes, by convention.) Two planes are parallel iff their normals are parallel, and perpendicular iff their normals are perpendicular.

Worked example

Find the angle between the planes $\mathcal{P}_1: 2x-y+z=3$ and $\mathcal{P}_2: x+y-2z=4$.

$\mathbf{n}_1=(2,-1,1)$, $\ \mathbf{n}_2=(1,1,-2)$.

$\mathbf{n}_1\cdot\mathbf{n}_2 = 2-1-2=-1$. $|\mathbf{n}_1|=\sqrt6$, $|\mathbf{n}_2|=\sqrt6$.

$\cos\theta = \dfrac{|-1|}{\sqrt6\times\sqrt6}=\dfrac16 \ \Rightarrow\ \theta=\cos^{-1}\!\left(\dfrac16\right)\approx80.4^\circ$.

Practice 14

Determine whether the planes $2x+3y-z=5$ and $4x+6y-2z=1$ are parallel.

Solution

Normals $(2,3,-1)$ and $(4,6,-2)=2(2,3,-1)$: parallel (though the planes themselves are distinct, since the constants aren't in the same ratio).

Practice 15

Determine whether the planes $x-y+2z=3$ and $2x+2y-2z=1$ are perpendicular.

Solution

Normals $(1,-1,2)$ and $(2,2,-2)$: dot product $=2-2-4=-4\ne0$. Not perpendicular.

Practice 16

Find the angle between the planes $x+y+z=1$ and $x-y+z=2$, to the nearest degree.

Solution

$\mathbf{n}_1=(1,1,1)$, $\mathbf{n}_2=(1,-1,1)$. Dot product $=1-1+1=1$. $|\mathbf{n}_1|=|\mathbf{n}_2|=\sqrt3$. $\cos\theta=\dfrac{1}{3} \Rightarrow \theta\approx70.5^\circ$.

Practice 17

Find the value of $k$ for which the planes $x+2y+kz=1$ and $2x-y+3z=4$ are perpendicular.

Solution

Normals $(1,2,k)$ and $(2,-1,3)$: dot product $=2-2+3k=3k$. Set to $0$: $k=0$.

Find equation of plane through points \(A(1,0,2), B(3,1,0), C(0,2,1)\).

Solution

\(\overrightarrow{AB}=(2,1,-2),\overrightarrow{AC}=(-1,2,-1)\).

Plane: \(3(x-1)+4(y-0)+5(z-2)=0\Rightarrow 3x+4y+5z=13\).

Practice 19

Compute angle between planes \(2x-y+2z=5\) and \(x+2y-2z=1\).

Solution

Normals are \(\mathbf n_1=(2,-1,2),\mathbf n_2=(1,2,-2)\).

\(\cos\theta=\dfrac{|\mathbf n_1\cdot\mathbf n_2|}{\|\mathbf n_1\|\|\mathbf n_2\|}=4/9\).

\(\theta=\cos^{-1}(4/9)\).

Practice 20

Given vectors \(\mathbf a=(1,2,0),\mathbf b=(0,1,3),\mathbf c=(2,-1,1)\), compute scalar triple product and interpret.

Solution

\([\mathbf a,\mathbf b,\mathbf c]=\mathbf a\cdot(\mathbf b\times\mathbf c)\).

\(\mathbf b\times\mathbf c=(4,6,-2)\), so triple product \(=1\cdot4+2\cdot6+0\cdot(-2)=16\).

Volume of parallelepiped is \(|16|=16\), nonzero so vectors are not coplanar.

Practice 21

Find line of intersection of planes \(x+y+z=4\) and \(x-y+z=2\).

Solution

Subtract equations: \(2y=2\Rightarrow y=1\).

Then \(x+z=3\). Let \(z=t\Rightarrow x=3-t\).

Line: \((x,y,z)=(3,1,0)+t(-1,0,1)\).

Practice 22

Construct plane through point \(P(2,-1,1)\) parallel to plane \(3x+4y+5z=7\).

Solution

Parallel planes share normal \(\mathbf n=(3,4,5)\).

Equation: \(3x+4y+5z=d\). Substitute point: \(d=3(2)+4(-1)+5(1)=7\).

Required plane is \(3x+4y+5z=7\).

Practice 23

Find angle between vectors \(\mathbf u=(1,1,2)\) and \(\mathbf v=(2,-1,2)\), then state if they are orthogonal.

Solution

\(\mathbf u\cdot\mathbf v=1\cdot2+1(-1)+2\cdot2=5\).

\(\|u\|=\sqrt6, \|v\|=3\), so \(\cos\theta=5/(3\sqrt6)\).

Dot product is not zero, so not orthogonal.
