0 Integration Summary: Choosing the Right Technique

By this point you have several integration tools — direct antidifferentiation from the standard table, substitution, and pattern recognition. The hardest part of integration is often not the mechanics but the diagnosis: recognising which tool a given integral needs before you start.

A decision checklist

1) Can you match it directly against the standard table? 2) Is the integrand (up to a constant) of the form $g'(x)\cdot f(g(x))$ — i.e. does a substitution $u=g(x)$ simplify it? 3) Can a trig identity rewrite the integrand into a directly integrable form (e.g. $\sin^2x\to\tfrac{1-\cos2x}{2}$)? 4) Can you split a fraction into simpler pieces first (e.g. by polynomial division, or splitting a numerator sum across the same denominator)?

Worked example

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int\frac{x+3}{x^2+1}\,dx$ by splitting into two integrals that each match a known form.

Split: $\displaystyle\int\frac{x}{x^2+1}\,dx + \int\frac{3}{x^2+1}\,dx$.

First integral: numerator is half the derivative of $x^2+1$: $\dfrac12\ln(x^2+1)$.

Second integral: standard arctan form: $3\arctan x$. Total: $$\frac12\ln(x^2+1)+3\arctan x+C.$$

Practice 1

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int\frac{2x+5}{x^2+4}\,dx$.

Solution

Split: $\displaystyle\int\frac{2x}{x^2+4}\,dx+\int\frac{5}{x^2+4}\,dx = \ln(x^2+4)+\dfrac52\arctan\!\left(\dfrac{x}{2}\right)+C$.

Practice 2

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int\cos^2x\,dx$ using the identity $\cos^2x=\dfrac{1+\cos2x}{2}$.

Solution

$\displaystyle\int\frac{1+\cos2x}{2}\,dx=\frac{x}{2}+\frac{\sin2x}{4}+C$.

Practice 3

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int\frac{x^3+x}{x^2}\,dx$ by simplifying the fraction first.

Solution

$\dfrac{x^3+x}{x^2}=x+\dfrac1x$. $\displaystyle\int\left(x+\frac1x\right)dx=\frac{x^2}{2}+\ln|x|+C$.

Practice 4

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int\sin x\cos x\,dx$ two different ways: (a) using $u=\sin x$; (b) using the identity $\sin x\cos x=\tfrac12\sin2x$. Confirm the two answers are equivalent.

Solution

(a) $u=\sin x, du=\cos x\,dx$: $\displaystyle\int u\,du=\dfrac{u^2}{2}+C=\dfrac{\sin^2x}{2}+C$.

(b) $\displaystyle\int\tfrac12\sin2x\,dx=-\dfrac14\cos2x+C$. These look different but are equivalent: using $\cos2x=1-2\sin^2x$, $-\tfrac14(1-2\sin^2x)+C = \tfrac{\sin^2x}{2}-\tfrac14+C$, which matches (a) up to relabelling the constant.

Practice 5

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int\frac{4x}{(x^2+1)^2}\,dx$ using substitution.

Solution

$u=x^2+1$, $du=2x\,dx$. $\displaystyle\int\frac{2\,du}{u^2}=-\frac{2}{u}+C=-\frac{2}{x^2+1}+C$.

1 Average Value of a Function, and an Introduction to Volumes of Revolution

Two further uses of the definite integral: finding the "typical" value a function takes over an interval (its average value), and — as an enrichment beyond the core syllabus, useful preview for MATH142 — finding the volume swept out when a curve is spun around an axis.

Key formulas

Average value of $f$ on $[a,b]$: $$f_{\text{avg}} = \frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b f(x)\,dx.$$

Volume of revolution about the $x$-axis (disk method): $$V = \pi\int_a^b \big[f(x)\big]^2\,dx.$$

Worked example

Find the average value of $f(x)=x^2$ on $[0,3]$.

$\displaystyle\int_0^3x^2\,dx = \left[\frac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^3 = 9$.

$$f_{\text{avg}} = \frac{1}{3-0}(9) = 3.$$

Practice 6

Find the average value of $f(x)=3x+1$ on $[0,4]$.

Solution

$\displaystyle\int_0^4(3x+1)\,dx=\left[\dfrac32x^2+x\right]_0^4=24+4=28$. Average $=\dfrac{28}{4}=7$.

Practice 7

Find the average value of $f(x)=\sin x$ on $[0,\pi]$.

Solution

$\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi}\sin x\,dx=\left[-\cos x\right]_0^{\pi}=2$. Average $=\dfrac{2}{\pi}$.

Practice 8

Find the volume of the solid formed by rotating $y=\sqrt{x}$ about the $x$-axis, for $0\le x\le4$.

Solution

$V=\pi\displaystyle\int_0^4(\sqrt{x})^2\,dx=\pi\int_0^4x\,dx=\pi\left[\dfrac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^4=\pi(8)=8\pi$.

Practice 9

Find the volume of the solid formed by rotating $y=x$ about the $x$-axis for $0\le x\le3$, and verify it matches the formula for the volume of a cone ($V=\tfrac13\pi r^2h$ with $r=3,h=3$).

Solution

$V=\pi\displaystyle\int_0^3x^2\,dx=\pi\left[\dfrac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^3=9\pi$. Cone formula: $\tfrac13\pi(3)^2(3)=9\pi$. ✓

Practice 10

A component's temperature over an 8-hour shift is modelled by $T(t)=20+5\sin\!\left(\dfrac{\pi t}{8}\right)$ (°C) for $0\le t\le8$. Find the average temperature over the shift.

Solution

Average $=\dfrac18\displaystyle\int_0^8\left[20+5\sin\!\left(\dfrac{\pi t}{8}\right)\right]dt$. $\displaystyle\int_0^820\,dt=160$. $\displaystyle\int_0^85\sin\!\left(\dfrac{\pi t}{8}\right)dt=5\left[-\dfrac{8}{\pi}\cos\!\left(\dfrac{\pi t}{8}\right)\right]_0^8=-\dfrac{40}{\pi}\left[\cos\pi-\cos0\right]=-\dfrac{40}{\pi}(-1-1)=\dfrac{80}{\pi}$. Total $=160+\dfrac{80}{\pi}$. Average $=\dfrac{1}{8}\left(160+\dfrac{80}{\pi}\right)=20+\dfrac{10}{\pi}\approx23.18$°C.

2 Comprehensive Exam-Style Review: Calculus

These questions draw on everything from this lesson and earlier ones — differentiation and integration together — in the style of genuine mixed exam questions, including answer-only sections and full-working sections, matching the format of a real MATH141 paper.

Practice 11 EXAM-STYLE

[Exam-style, answer only] Write down only the final answer for each: (a) $\displaystyle\int(6x-4)\,dx$. (b) $\dfrac{d}{dx}\left[\ln(3x)\right]$. (c) $\displaystyle\int_0^1 3x^2\,dx$. (d) $\dfrac{d}{dx}\left[\cosh(2x)\right]$. (e) The critical point of $h(x)=(x-4)^2$.

Solution

(a) $3x^2-4x+C$.

(b) $\dfrac1x$ (the constant $3$ cancels via the chain rule/log law).

(c) $\left[x^3\right]_0^1=1$.

(d) $2\sinh(2x)$.

(e) $x=4$.

Practice 12 EXAM-STYLE

[Exam-style, multi-part] Let $f(x)=x^3-3x^2-9x+2$. (a) Find $f'(x)$ and hence all critical points. (b) Classify each critical point using the second derivative test. (c) Find $\displaystyle\int_0^2f(x)\,dx$.

Solution

(a) $f'(x)=3x^2-6x-9=3(x-3)(x+1)$. Critical points $x=3,-1$.

(b) $f''(x)=6x-6$. At $x=3$: $f''=12>0$, local min. At $x=-1$: $f''=-12<0$, local max.

(c) $\displaystyle\int_0^2(x^3-3x^2-9x+2)\,dx=\left[\dfrac{x^4}{4}-x^3-\dfrac{9x^2}{2}+2x\right]_0^2=(4-8-18+4)-0=-18$.

Practice 13 EXAM-STYLE

[Exam-style, multi-part] A manufacturer's cost function is $C(x)=x^3-15x^2+96x+50$ (dollars, for $x$ units, $0\le x\le10$). (a) Find the marginal cost function $C'(x)$. (b) Find the production level minimising marginal cost, and state that minimum marginal cost. (c) Find the average cost per unit over $[0,10]$, i.e. $\dfrac{1}{10}\displaystyle\int_0^{10}C(x)\,dx$.

Solution

(a) $C'(x)=3x^2-30x+96$.

(b) Minimise $C'(x)$: differentiate again, $C''(x)=6x-30=0 \Rightarrow x=5$. Since $C'$ is an upward parabola in $x$, this is its minimum. $C'(5)=75-150+96=21$.

(c) $\displaystyle\int_0^{10}(x^3-15x^2+96x+50)\,dx=\left[\dfrac{x^4}{4}-5x^3+48x^2+50x\right]_0^{10}=2500-5000+4800+500=1800$. Average $=\dfrac{1800}{10}=180$.

Practice 14 EXAM-STYLE

[Exam-style, multi-part] (a) Use substitution to evaluate $\displaystyle\int\cos x\,e^{\sin x}\,dx$. (b) Hence evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/2}\cos x\,e^{\sin x}\,dx$. (c) Verify your answer to (b) is consistent with the fact that the integrand is always positive on $[0,\pi/2]$.

Solution

(a) $u=\sin x$, $du=\cos x\,dx$: $\displaystyle\int e^u\,du=e^u+C=e^{\sin x}+C$.

(b) $\left[e^{\sin x}\right]_0^{\pi/2}=e^1-e^0=e-1$.

(c) $e-1\approx1.718>0$, consistent with integrating a positive function over a positive-length interval.

Practice 15 EXAM-STYLE

[Exam-style, multi-part] Consider the curve $y=x^2-4$ and the line $y=x-2$. (a) Find their points of intersection. (b) Find the area enclosed between them. (c) A solid is formed by rotating the region enclosed by $y=x^2-4$ and the $x$-axis (between its roots) about the $x$-axis. Find the volume of this solid.

Solution

(a) $x^2-4=x-2 \Rightarrow x^2-x-2=0 \Rightarrow (x-2)(x+1)=0 \Rightarrow x=2,-1$.

(b) At $x=0$: line gives $-2$, parabola gives $-4$: line on top. Area $=\displaystyle\int_{-1}^2\big[(x-2)-(x^2-4)\big]dx=\int_{-1}^2(x-x^2+2)\,dx=\left[\dfrac{x^2}{2}-\dfrac{x^3}{3}+2x\right]_{-1}^2$. At $x=2$: $2-\tfrac83+4=\tfrac{10}{3}$. At $x=-1$: $0.5+\tfrac13-2=-\tfrac76$. Area $=\tfrac{10}{3}+\tfrac76=\tfrac{20+7}{6}=\tfrac{27}{6}=\tfrac92$.

(c) Roots of $x^2-4=0$: $x=\pm2$. $V=\pi\displaystyle\int_{-2}^2(x^2-4)^2\,dx$. Expand: $x^4-8x^2+16$. $\displaystyle\int_{-2}^2(x^4-8x^2+16)\,dx=\left[\dfrac{x^5}{5}-\dfrac{8x^3}{3}+16x\right]_{-2}^2$. At $x=2$: $\tfrac{32}{5}-\tfrac{64}{3}+32$. At $x=-2$: the negative of that (odd powers flip sign, $16x$ flips too, giving the exact negative). So the bracket evaluates to $2\times\left(\tfrac{32}{5}-\tfrac{64}{3}+32\right) = 2\times\tfrac{96-320+480}{15}=2\times\tfrac{256}{15}=\tfrac{512}{15}$. $V=\dfrac{512\pi}{15}$.
