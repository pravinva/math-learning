0 The Line of Intersection of Two Planes

Two non-parallel planes always meet in exactly one line. Finding that line combines two ideas you already have: the direction of the line must be perpendicular to both normals (so it's their cross product), and any point satisfying both plane equations simultaneously will do as the line's basepoint.

Key method

1) Direction of the line of intersection: $\mathbf{d} = \mathbf{n}_1\times\mathbf{n}_2$. 2) Find one point on both planes by setting one coordinate to a convenient value (often $0$) and solving the resulting $2\times2$ system for the other two.

Worked example

Find the line of intersection of $\mathcal{P}_1: 2x-y+z=3$ and $\mathcal{P}_2: x+y-2z=4$.

$\mathbf{n}_1=(2,-1,1)$, $\mathbf{n}_2=(1,1,-2)$. Direction: $\mathbf{d}=\mathbf{n}_1\times\mathbf{n}_2$: $\mathbf{i}$: $(-1)(-2)-(1)(1)=1$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(2)(-2)-(1)(1)]=5$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(2)(1)-(-1)(1)=3$. $\mathbf{d}=(1,5,3)$.

Find a point: set $z=0$. Then $2x-y=3$ and $x+y=4$. Adding: $3x=7 \Rightarrow x=\tfrac73$, so $y=4-\tfrac73=\tfrac53$.

Line of intersection: $\mathbf{r} = \left(\dfrac73,\dfrac53,0\right)+t(1,5,3)$.

Practice 1

Find the direction of the line of intersection of $x+y+z=1$ and $x-y+z=2$ (you need only find $\mathbf{n}_1\times\mathbf{n}_2$).

Solution

$\mathbf{n}_1=(1,1,1)$, $\mathbf{n}_2=(1,-1,1)$. $\mathbf{i}$: $(1)(1)-(1)(-1)=2$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(1)(1)-(1)(1)]=0$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(1)(-1)-(1)(1)=-2$. Direction $(2,0,-2)$, or simplified $(1,0,-1)$.

Practice 2

Find the line of intersection of $2x-y+z=3$ and $x+2y-z=1$, choosing $z=0$ to find a point.

Solution

$\mathbf{n}_1=(2,-1,1)$, $\mathbf{n}_2=(1,2,-1)$. Direction: $\mathbf{i}$: $(-1)(-1)-(1)(2)=-1$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(2)(-1)-(1)(1)]=3$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(2)(2)-(-1)(1)=5$. $\mathbf{d}=(-1,3,5)$. At $z=0$: $2x-y=3$, $x+2y=1$. From the first, $y=2x-3$. Substitute: $x+2(2x-3)=1 \Rightarrow 5x-6=1 \Rightarrow x=\tfrac75$, $y=\tfrac{14}{5}-3=-\tfrac15$. Line: $\mathbf{r}=\left(\tfrac75,-\tfrac15,0\right)+t(-1,3,5)$.

Practice 3

Check whether the points $A=(2,1,1)$ and $B=(3,5,2)$ lie on the line of intersection of $2x-y+z=3$ and $x+y-2z=4$ (from the worked example).

Solution

Check $A=(2,1,1)$ in both: $2(2)-1+1=4\ne3$ — already fails $\mathcal{P}_1$, so $A$ is not on the line. Check $B=(3,5,2)$: $2(3)-5+2=3$✓ and $3+5-2(2)=4$✓. $B$ satisfies both, so $B$ is on the line.

Practice 4

Determine whether the planes $x+2y-z=3$ and $2x+4y-2z=6$ intersect in a line, are identical, or are parallel and distinct.

Solution

Normals $(1,2,-1)$ and $(2,4,-2)=2(1,2,-1)$: parallel normals. Check if the equations are proportional as full equations: $2\times(x+2y-z=3)$ gives $2x+4y-2z=6$, exactly matching the second equation. The planes are identical (the same plane, described twice).

1 Systems of Three Planes: Geometric Interpretation

Solving a $3\times3$ linear system (Strand 2, Lesson 3) has a direct geometric meaning once each equation is read as a plane: the solution is exactly where all three planes meet. Row reduction's outcome (unique solution, no solution, infinitely many) corresponds to a specific geometric picture of how three planes can sit relative to each other.

Key idea — the geometric pictures

Unique solution (rank $3$): three planes meeting at a single point. Infinitely many solutions (rank $<3$, consistent): the planes share a common line, or are all identical. No solution (rank of coefficient matrix $<$ rank of augmented matrix): the planes might be pairwise intersecting in three different parallel lines (forming a triangular "prism" with no common point), or some parallel and distinct, or various other non-concurrent arrangements.

Worked example

Determine the geometric relationship between the planes $x+y+z=1$, $x+y+z=2$, and $x-y+z=0$.

The first two planes have identical left-hand sides ($x+y+z$) but different constants ($1$ vs $2$) — they're parallel and distinct (never meeting each other at all).

Since two of the three planes never meet, the full system of three planes has no common solution, regardless of what the third plane does.

Practice 5

Without solving, explain why the system $x+y+z=1,\ 2x+2y+2z=5,\ x-y=0$ has no solution.

Solution

The first two equations have proportional left-hand sides ($2\times$ the first equals $2x+2y+2z=2$), but the constants $2$ and $5$ aren't in that same ratio: the corresponding planes are parallel and distinct, so no point can lie on both, regardless of the third equation.

Practice 6

The system $x+y+z=6,\ 2x-y+z=3,\ x+2y=8$ reduces (after row operations) to $\left(\begin{array}{ccc|c}1&1&1&6\\0&-3&-1&-9\\0&0&0&0\end{array}\right)$. Describe the geometric relationship between the three original planes.

Solution

The bottom row of zeros means the system is consistent but has rank $2<3$: the three planes share a common line of intersection, rather than a single point.

Practice 7

The system $x+y+z=3,\ 2x+2y+2z=6,\ x-y+z=1$ reduces to rank $1$ overall (after removing the redundant second equation, which is just $2\times$ the first). What does this suggest about the relationship between the first two planes, and what remains to determine the solution set?

Solution

Planes 1 and 2 are identical (the second equation is exactly double the first, describing the same plane). The solution set of the whole system is then just the intersection of plane 1 (equivalently plane 2) with plane 3 — generically a line, found by solving those two plane equations together as in Section 0.

Practice 8

A $3\times3$ system has coefficient matrix of rank $3$. State, without further calculation, what this guarantees about the geometric picture of the three planes.

Solution

Rank $3$ (full rank) guarantees the three planes meet at exactly one point — a unique solution.

2 Mixed Exam-Style Review: Planes

Multi-part questions combining plane equations, normal vectors, the scalar triple product, and line-of-intersection work — the way these ideas actually get tested together on an exam.

Practice 9 EXAM-STYLE

[Exam-style, multi-part] Consider the planes $\mathcal{P}_1: x-2y+2z=3$ and $\mathcal{P}_2: 2x+y-2z=1$. (a) Find the normal vectors of each plane. (b) Find the angle between the planes, to the nearest degree. (c) Find the direction of the line of intersection. (d) Find one point on the line of intersection.

Solution

(a) $\mathbf{n}_1=(1,-2,2)$, $\mathbf{n}_2=(2,1,-2)$.

(b) $\mathbf{n}_1\cdot\mathbf{n}_2=2-2-4=-4$. $|\mathbf{n}_1|=3$, $|\mathbf{n}_2|=3$. $\cos\theta=\dfrac{|-4|}{9}=\dfrac49 \Rightarrow \theta\approx63.6^\circ$.

(c) $\mathbf{d}=\mathbf{n}_1\times\mathbf{n}_2$: $\mathbf{i}$: $(-2)(-2)-(2)(1)=2$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(1)(-2)-(2)(2)]=6$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(1)(1)-(-2)(2)=5$. $\mathbf{d}=(2,6,5)$.

(d) Set $z=0$: $x-2y=3$, $2x+y=1$. From the second, $y=1-2x$. Substitute: $x-2(1-2x)=3 \Rightarrow 5x-2=3 \Rightarrow x=1$, $y=-1$. Point: $(1,-1,0)$.

Practice 10 EXAM-STYLE

[Exam-style, multi-part] Points $A=(1,0,0)$, $B=(0,2,0)$, $C=(0,0,3)$ define a plane. (a) Find the equation of this plane. (b) Find the distance from the origin to this plane. (c) Find the volume of the tetrahedron $OABC$ (where $O$ is the origin), using $V=\dfrac16|\overrightarrow{OA}\cdot(\overrightarrow{OB}\times\overrightarrow{OC})|$.

Solution

(a) $\overrightarrow{AB}=(-1,2,0)$, $\overrightarrow{AC}=(-1,0,3)$. Normal: $\mathbf{i}$: $(2)(3)-(0)(0)=6$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(-1)(3)-(0)(-1)]=3$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(-1)(0)-(2)(-1)=2$. $\mathbf{n}=(6,3,2)$. Equation using $A$: $6(x-1)+3y+2z=0 \Rightarrow 6x+3y+2z=6$.

(b) Distance from origin: $\dfrac{|6(0)+3(0)+2(0)-6|}{\sqrt{36+9+4}}=\dfrac{6}{7}$.

(c) $\overrightarrow{OB}\times\overrightarrow{OC}$: for $(0,2,0)\times(0,0,3)$: $\mathbf{i}$: $(2)(3)-(0)(0)=6$; $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(0)(3)-(0)(0)]=0$; $\mathbf{k}$: $(0)(0)-(2)(0)=0$. Result $(6,0,0)$. $\overrightarrow{OA}\cdot(6,0,0)=1(6)+0+0=6$. $V=\dfrac16|6|=1$ cubic unit.

Practice 11 EXAM-STYLE

[Exam-style, answer only] For the planes $3x-y+2z=5$ and $6x-2y+4z=1$: (a) state whether they are parallel. (b) state whether they intersect.

Solution

(a) Normals $(3,-1,2)$ and $(6,-2,4)=2(3,-1,2)$: parallel.

(b) Constants aren't proportional in the same ratio ($2\times5=10\ne1$): parallel and distinct, so they do not intersect.

Practice 12

Find eigenvalues and eigenvectors of a 2x2 matrix, then diagonalize if eigenvectors are independent.

Solution

Solve det(A-lambda I)=0 for eigenvalues.

Solve (A-lambda I)v=0 for each eigenvector basis.

If two independent eigenvectors exist, form P and D with A=PDP^{-1}.

Practice 13

Given two lines in vector form, determine if they intersect, are parallel, or skew; compute minimum distance if skew.

Solution

Equate component forms with parameters; test consistency.

Parallel if direction vectors scalar multiples.

If skew, distance = |(r2-r1)·(d1×d2)|/|d1×d2|.

Practice 14

Construct plane equation through three points, then compute angle between this plane and another given plane.

Solution

Form two direction vectors in plane and cross them for normal n.

Use n·(r-r0)=0 for plane equation.

Angle between planes = angle between normals via dot formula.

Practice 15

Use Cramer's rule to solve 3x3 system and compare with determinant test for singular/non-singular behavior.

Solution

Compute main determinant Delta.

If Delta!=0, x=Delta_x/Delta etc.

If Delta=0 inspect numerator determinants to classify infinite vs none.

Practice 16

For a transformation matrix T in R^2, map basis vectors, sketch geometric effect, and infer determinant sign/area scaling.

Solution

Images of e1,e2 are matrix columns.

Parallelogram area scale = |det(T)|.

det<0 implies orientation reversal (reflection component).

Practice 17

Given A=[[1,3],[7,4]], (a) find det(A), (b) state invertibility, (c) compute A^(-1) if possible.

Solution

det(A)=1*4-3*7.

If det!=0 matrix is invertible.

Use 2x2 inverse formula (1/det)[[d,-b],[-c,a]].

Practice 18

Row-reduce augmented matrix to RREF, classify system (unique/infinite/inconsistent), and write parametric solution if needed.

Solution

Perform elimination to echelon then pivot normalization/back-elimination.

Check for row [0 0 ... | nonzero] (inconsistent) or free variables (infinite).

Express free-variable family in vector/parametric form.

Practice 19

For vectors u and v in R^3, compute dot and cross products, then determine angle and area of parallelogram spanned by them.

Solution

u·v gives cos(theta)= (u·v)/(|u||v|).

u×v gives normal vector; |u×v| is parallelogram area.

Check orthogonality (dot=0) or parallelism (cross=0) where relevant.
