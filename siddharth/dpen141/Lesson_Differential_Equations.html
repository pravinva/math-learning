1 Introduction to differential equations

Every equation you have solved so far has asked you to find a number: solve $2x+3=7$ for the number $x$. A differential equation asks a bigger question: find a function $y(x)$ that satisfies some relationship involving the function and its derivatives. For example, $y'=2y$ is a differential equation, it is asking: which functions $y(x)$ have the property that their derivative is always exactly twice the function's own value?

This is a genuinely different kind of problem, but it is everywhere in practice: population growth, radioactive decay, the temperature of a cooling cup of coffee, the motion of a falling object, all of these are naturally described by a relationship between a quantity and its rate of change, which is exactly what a differential equation captures.

Key idea: vocabulary

The order of a differential equation is the highest derivative that appears. $y'=2y$ is first order; $y''+3y'-y=0$ is second order. This lesson deals only with first order equations, those involving $y'$ but no higher derivative.

A general solution is a family of functions, usually containing one arbitrary constant $C$, that satisfies the equation for every value of $C$. A particular solution is one specific member of that family, pinned down by an extra piece of information called an initial condition, typically the value $y(x_0)=y_0$ at some starting point. A differential equation together with an initial condition is called an initial value problem (IVP).

Why solutions always come with a constant

Solving a differential equation is, at its core, a form of integration (Sections 3-5 make this completely explicit), and every integration introduces an arbitrary constant, since a function and that same function plus any constant have identical derivatives. So a first order differential equation never has just one solution, it has an entire one-parameter family of them. Geometrically, each value of $C$ traces out a different curve, and all of these curves together fill up the plane without crossing (for well-behaved equations), each one satisfying the same relationship between $y$ and $y'$. An initial condition selects exactly one curve out of that family, the one passing through the specified point.

Worked example

Verify that $y=Ce^{2x}$ solves $y'=2y$ for every value of $C$, then find the particular solution with $y(0)=5$.

Differentiate: $y'=2Ce^{2x}$. Compare with $2y=2(Ce^{2x})=2Ce^{2x}$. These match exactly, for any $C$, so $y=Ce^{2x}$ really is a solution, regardless of what $C$ is.

Apply the initial condition: $y(0)=Ce^0=C=5$. So the particular solution is $y=5e^{2x}$.

Practice 1

Verify that $y=x^2+3x+C$ solves the differential equation $y'=2x+3$.

Solution

$y' = 2x+3+0 = 2x+3$, matching the right-hand side of the equation for every $C$ (the constant vanishes under differentiation, which is exactly why it was free to be anything).

Practice 2

State the order of each equation: (a) $y'''-2y=x$   (b) $y'=xy$   (c) $y''+y'=\sin x$.

Solution

(a) Third order (highest derivative present is $y'''$). (b) First order. (c) Second order.

Practice 3

Given the general solution $y=Ce^{-2x}$ to some differential equation, find the particular solution satisfying $y(0)=7$.

Solution

$y(0)=Ce^0=C=7$, so the particular solution is $y=7e^{-2x}$.

Practice 4

Is $y=\sin x+C$ a solution to $y'=\cos(2x)$? Check directly rather than guessing.

Solution

No. Differentiating gives $y'=\cos x$, not $\cos(2x)$, these are different functions (for example, at $x=\pi/2$, $\cos x=0$ but $\cos(2x)=\cos\pi=-1$). Always verify a proposed solution by substituting it back in, rather than trusting a family resemblance between the answer and the equation.

Practice 5

Verify that $y=3+Ce^{-x}$ solves $y'=3-y$ for every value of $C$.

Solution

$y' = -Ce^{-x}$. And $3-y = 3-(3+Ce^{-x}) = -Ce^{-x}$. These match for every $C$, so the family does solve the equation.

Practice 6

Using Practice 5's general solution $y=3+Ce^{-x}$, find the particular solution with $y(0)=1$, and state what happens to $y$ as $x\to\infty$.

Solution

$y(0)=3+C=1 \Rightarrow C=-2$, so $y=3-2e^{-x}$.

As $x\to\infty$, $e^{-x}\to0$, so $y\to3$: the solution approaches the constant value $3$ but never quite reaches it for any finite $x$.

Practice 7

Explain, in your own words, the difference between a general solution and a particular solution, using $y'=2y$ as your example.

Solution

The general solution $y=Ce^{2x}$ describes an entire infinite family of curves, one for every possible value of $C$, all of which satisfy $y'=2y$. A particular solution is a single member of that family, for instance $y=5e^{2x}$ (taking $C=5$), pinned down by requiring the curve to pass through one specific point, such as $y(0)=5$. Without an initial condition, there is no way to prefer one value of $C$ over another, the differential equation alone is satisfied equally well by all of them.

2 Direction fields

A first order equation $y'=f(x,y)$ tells you the slope of the solution curve at every single point in the plane, even before you have solved the equation at all. Plotting a short line segment with that slope at a grid of points produces a direction field (or slope field), a picture that lets you sketch, or at least visualise, the shape of solution curves without doing any integration.

Key idea

At the point $(x_0,y_0)$, the slope of the solution curve passing through that point is simply $f(x_0,y_0)$, the right-hand side of the equation evaluated there. A solution curve, once you sketch one, must be tangent to the little slope segment at every point it passes through, since that segment is the derivative at that point, by definition.

Worked example

Compute the slope of the solution to $y'=x-y$ at the points $(0,0)$, $(1,0)$, $(0,1)$, $(1,1)$, and sketch a small direction field.

At $(0,0)$: slope $=0-0=0$. At $(1,0)$: slope $=1-0=1$. At $(0,1)$: slope $=0-1=-1$. At $(1,1)$: slope $=1-1=0$.

Each short segment shows the exact slope a solution curve must have if it passes through that point, no integration involved.

Practice 8

For $y'=xy$, compute the slope at $(1,1)$, $(2,1)$, $(1,-1)$, and $(0,5)$.

Solution

$(1,1)$: $1\times1=1$. $\ (2,1)$: $2\times1=2$. $\ (1,-1)$: $1\times(-1)=-1$. $\ (0,5)$: $0\times5=0$.

Practice 9

For $y'=y$, describe (without solving the equation) what the direction field looks like along the line $y=0$, and what that suggests about the constant function $y=0$.

Solution

Along $y=0$, the slope is $f(x,0)=0$ everywhere, horizontal segments all the way across. This means the constant function $y=0$ has a horizontal tangent at every point along its own graph, exactly what it needs to be a valid solution curve, since $y=0$ is indeed a solution to $y'=y$ (check: $y'=0$ and $y=0$ match). Direction fields often reveal these constant, "equilibrium" solutions just by looking at where the slope is uniformly zero.

Practice 10

For the equation $y'=x/y$ (for $y\neq0$), find the set of points where the slope equals a fixed value $k$ (called an isocline), and describe its shape.

Solution

Setting $x/y=k$ gives $x=ky$, or equivalently $y=x/k$ (for $k\neq0$): a straight line through the origin. So every isocline of $y'=x/y$ is a line through the origin, with different lines (different slopes through the origin) corresponding to different constant values $k$ of the solution's slope there.

Practice 11

For $y'=-x/y$, compute the slope at $(3,4)$ and at $(4,3)$, and notice that $3^2+4^2=4^2+3^2=25$. What does this suggest about the shape of the solution curve passing through both nearby regions of the circle $x^2+y^2=25$?

Solution

At $(3,4)$: slope $=-3/4$. At $(4,3)$: slope $=-4/3$.

The slope $-x/y$ at any point on the circle $x^2+y^2=25$ is exactly the negative reciprocal of $y/x$, the slope of the radius to that point, meaning the direction field is everywhere perpendicular to the radius. A curve that stays perpendicular to every radius of a circle is, geometrically, the circle itself. This suggests the solution curves of $y'=-x/y$ are concentric circles centred at the origin, which is indeed correct (they satisfy $x^2+y^2=C$ for various $C$).

Practice 12

For $y'=2$, describe the direction field and the shape of every solution curve, without solving anything.

Solution

The slope is $2$ at every single point in the plane, regardless of $x$ or $y$, so every little segment in the direction field points the same way. Solution curves must therefore be straight lines of slope $2$, and since there is one such line through every point, the family of solutions is $y=2x+C$ for every constant $C$, exactly what direct integration of $y'=2$ would give (Section 3), reached here purely by reasoning about the picture.

3 Solving $y'=f(x)$

The simplest possible first order equation is one where the right-hand side depends only on $x$, not on $y$ at all. This is not really a new technique, it is exactly the antiderivative-finding you already know from the integration lessons, just written using $y$ and $y'$ notation instead of $\int\cdots dx$.

Key idea

If $y'=f(x)$, then $y = \displaystyle\int f(x)\,dx$. Every technique from the integration lessons (substitution, parts, partial fractions, and so on) is fair game here, the differential equation is just asking you to undo a derivative.

Worked example

Solve the IVP $y'=4x^3$, $y(1)=2$.

Integrate: $y = \displaystyle\int4x^3\,dx = x^4+C$.

Apply the initial condition: $y(1)=1^4+C=2 \Rightarrow C=1$.

$$y = x^4+1.$$

Practice 13

Solve $y'=\cos x$, $y(0)=1$.

Solution

$y=\sin x+C$. $\ y(0)=0+C=1 \Rightarrow C=1$. $\ y=\sin x+1$.

Practice 14

Solve $y'=\dfrac1x$ (for $x>0$), $y(1)=0$.

Solution

$y=\ln x+C$ (no absolute value needed since $x>0$). $\ y(1)=\ln1+C=C=0$. $\ y=\ln x$.

Practice 15

Solve $y'=6x^2-2x+1$, $y(0)=3$.

Solution

$y=2x^3-x^2+x+C$. $\ y(0)=C=3$. $\ y=2x^3-x^2+x+3$.

Practice 16

Solve $y'=e^{-x}$, $y(0)=0$.

Solution

$y=-e^{-x}+C$. $\ y(0)=-1+C=0 \Rightarrow C=1$. $\ y=1-e^{-x}$.

Practice 17

Solve $y'=\sin(2x)$, $y(0)=2$.

Solution

$y=-\dfrac{\cos(2x)}2+C$. $\ y(0)=-\dfrac12+C=2 \Rightarrow C=\dfrac52$. $\ y=\dfrac52-\dfrac{\cos(2x)}2$.

Practice 18

Solve $y'=\dfrac3{x+2}$, $y(0)=3\ln2$.

Solution

$y=3\ln|x+2|+C$. $\ y(0)=3\ln2+C=3\ln2 \Rightarrow C=0$. $\ y=3\ln|x+2|$.

Practice 19

Solve $y'=xe^{x^2}$ (needs a substitution first), $y(0)=1$.

Solution

Let $w=x^2$, $dw=2x\,dx$: $\displaystyle\int xe^{x^2}\,dx = \frac12\int e^w\,dw = \frac12e^{x^2}+C$.

$y(0)=\dfrac12+C=1 \Rightarrow C=\dfrac12$. $\ y=\dfrac12e^{x^2}+\dfrac12$.

4 Solving $y'=g(y)$

Now suppose the right-hand side depends only on $y$, not $x$. Direct integration with respect to $x$ no longer works, since the right side isn't a function of $x$ to integrate. But you can still separate the variables: treat $y'=\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ as an honest ratio (it isn't quite one, but the algebra behaves exactly as if it were), and rearrange so all the $y$'s sit on one side and all the $x$'s (here, just $dx$) sit on the other.

Key idea

$$y'=g(y) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \frac{dy}{dx}=g(y) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \frac{dy}{g(y)} = dx \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \int\frac{dy}{g(y)} = \int dx = x+C.$$

This is called a separable equation. Once both sides are integrated, you have an equation relating $y$ and $x$ (possibly needing rearranging to isolate $y$ explicitly).

Why separating variables is legitimate

The rigorous justification isn't quite "cross-multiply the derivative like a fraction," it comes from the chain rule. Since $\dfrac1{g(y)}\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 1$ (dividing both sides of $y'=g(y)$ by $g(y)$), integrate both sides with respect to $x$: $\displaystyle\int\frac1{g(y)}\frac{dy}{dx}\,dx = \int1\,dx$. The left side is exactly a substitution integral in disguise (with $u=y(x)$, $du=y'\,dx$), so it simplifies to $\displaystyle\int\frac{dy}{g(y)}$, recovering the informal "separate and integrate" recipe from genuine substitution, not from treating $dy/dx$ as literal division.

Worked example

Solve $y'=y^2$, $y(0)=1$, and describe what happens to the solution as $t$ increases (writing the independent variable as $t$, common when $y$ represents a quantity changing over time).

Separate: $\displaystyle\frac{dy}{y^2}=dt \Rightarrow \int y^{-2}\,dy = \int dt \Rightarrow -\frac1y = t+C$.

Apply the initial condition: $-\dfrac11 = 0+C \Rightarrow C=-1$. So $-\dfrac1y = t-1$, giving $y=\dfrac1{1-t}$.

As $t\to1^-$, the denominator $1-t\to0^+$, so $y\to+\infty$: the solution "blows up" and ceases to exist at the finite time $t=1$, even though the differential equation itself has no obvious warning sign of trouble at $t=1$. This is a genuinely important phenomenon in nonlinear differential equations, unlike linear equations, solutions to nonlinear ones can fail to exist for all time.

Practice 20

Solve $y'=5y$, $y(0)=2$.

Solution

$\displaystyle\int\frac{dy}y = \int5\,dt \Rightarrow \ln|y|=5t+C_1 \Rightarrow y=Ce^{5t}$.

$y(0)=C=2$. $\ y=2e^{5t}$.

Practice 21

Solve $y'=-2y$, $y(0)=10$.

Solution

Same method as Practice 20: $y=Ce^{-2t}$. $\ y(0)=C=10$. $\ y=10e^{-2t}$.

Practice 22

Solve $y'=\sqrt y$, $y(0)=4$.

Solution

$\displaystyle\int y^{-1/2}\,dy = \int dt \Rightarrow 2\sqrt y = t+C$.

$y(0)=2\sqrt4=4=C$. $\ 2\sqrt y=t+4 \Rightarrow \sqrt y = \dfrac{t+4}2 \Rightarrow y=\dfrac{(t+4)^2}4$.

Practice 23

Solve $y'=1+y^2$ (leave the answer in terms of $\arctan$ inverted, i.e.\ solve for $y$).

Solution

$\displaystyle\int\frac{dy}{1+y^2} = \int dt \Rightarrow \arctan y = t+C \Rightarrow y=\tan(t+C)$.

Practice 24

Solve $y'=\dfrac{y}2$, $y(0)=6$, and compare the shape of the solution to Practice 20's.

Solution

$y=Ce^{t/2}$. $\ y(0)=C=6$. $\ y=6e^{t/2}$.

Same exponential shape as Practice 20, still growth (positive exponent), just at a slower rate, since $\frac12<5$.

Practice 25

Without fully solving it, explain why $y'=y^3$, $y(0)=1$ should be expected to blow up in finite time as well, by comparing it to the worked example's $y'=y^2$.

Solution

Whenever $y>1$, $y^3>y^2$, so the growth rate for $y'=y^3$ is even more aggressive than for $y'=y^2$ once the solution exceeds $1$. Since $y'=y^2$ already blows up at a finite time starting from $y(0)=1$, and $y'=y^3$ grows at least as fast from the same starting point, it is reasonable to expect $y'=y^3$ blows up even sooner. (Solving it directly confirms this: separating gives $-\frac1{2y^2}=t+C$, with $C=-\frac12$ from $y(0)=1$, giving blow-up at $t=\frac12$, earlier than the worked example's $t=1$.)

5 Solving $y'=f(x)g(y)$

Sections 3 and 4 are really both special cases of one more general technique: whenever the right-hand side of $y'=\cdots$ can be factored into a piece depending only on $x$ times a piece depending only on $y$, the same separation idea from Section 4 still works, you just end up with a nontrivial integral on both sides instead of a trivial $\int dx$ on the right.

Key idea

$$y'=f(x)g(y) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \frac{dy}{g(y)} = f(x)\,dx \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \int\frac{dy}{g(y)} = \int f(x)\,dx.$$

Section 3's technique is the special case $g(y)=1$; Section 4's is the special case $f(x)=1$. The general method is always: get every $y$ (including $dy$) on one side, every $x$ (including $dx$) on the other, then integrate each side separately.

Worked example

Solve $y'=xy$.

Separate: $\displaystyle\frac{dy}y = x\,dx$.

Integrate both sides: $\ln|y| = \dfrac{x^2}2+C_1$.

Exponentiate: $|y|=e^{x^2/2+C_1} = e^{C_1}e^{x^2/2}$. Since $e^{C_1}$ is just some positive constant, and allowing the sign to be absorbed, write $y=Ce^{x^2/2}$ for an arbitrary constant $C$ (this step, replacing $\pm e^{C_1}$ with a single unrestricted constant $C$, is standard and worth doing automatically once you've seen it).

Practice 26

Solve $y'=\dfrac{x}{y}$.

Solution

$y\,dy = x\,dx \Rightarrow \displaystyle\int y\,dy = \int x\,dx \Rightarrow \frac{y^2}2 = \frac{x^2}2+C_1 \Rightarrow y^2=x^2+C$ (absorbing the $2$ into a renamed constant).

Practice 27

Solve $y'=x^2y^3$.

Solution

$\dfrac{dy}{y^3}=x^2\,dx \Rightarrow \displaystyle\int y^{-3}\,dy = \int x^2\,dx \Rightarrow -\frac1{2y^2} = \frac{x^3}3+C.$

Practice 28

Solve $y'=e^{x+y}$ (hint: $e^{x+y}=e^x\cdot e^y$, so this is already in $f(x)g(y)$ form).

Solution

$\dfrac{dy}{e^y} = e^x\,dx \Rightarrow e^{-y}\,dy = e^x\,dx \Rightarrow \displaystyle\int e^{-y}\,dy = \int e^x\,dx \Rightarrow -e^{-y} = e^x+C$.

Practice 29

Solve $y'=xy^2$, $y(0)=1$.

Solution

$\dfrac{dy}{y^2}=x\,dx \Rightarrow -\dfrac1y = \dfrac{x^2}2+C$.

$y(0)=1$: $-1=0+C \Rightarrow C=-1$. So $-\dfrac1y = \dfrac{x^2}2-1$, giving $\dfrac1y = 1-\dfrac{x^2}2 = \dfrac{2-x^2}2$, so $y=\dfrac2{2-x^2}$.

Practice 30

Solve $y'=\dfrac{x+1}y$, $y(0)=2$.

Solution

$y\,dy=(x+1)\,dx \Rightarrow \dfrac{y^2}2 = \dfrac{x^2}2+x+C$.

$y(0)=2$: $\dfrac42=0+0+C \Rightarrow C=2$. So $y^2=x^2+2x+4$, and since $y(0)=2>0$, take the positive root: $y=\sqrt{x^2+2x+4}$.

Practice 31

Solve $y'=\dfrac{\cos x}{y}$, $y(0)=3$.

Solution

$y\,dy=\cos x\,dx \Rightarrow \dfrac{y^2}2=\sin x+C$.

$y(0)=3$: $\dfrac92=0+C \Rightarrow C=\dfrac92$. So $y^2=2\sin x+9$, and taking the positive root (since $y(0)=3>0$): $y=\sqrt{2\sin x+9}$.

Practice 32

Explain why $y'=x+y$ is not separable using the methods of this section (i.e., why it cannot be split into $f(x)$ times $g(y)$).

Solution

The right-hand side is a sum, $x+y$, not a product, of a function of $x$ alone and a function of $y$ alone. There is no way to factor $x+y$ as $f(x)g(y)$: any such factoring would have to work for all $x,y$ simultaneously, but setting $y=0$ gives $f(x)g(0)=x$, forcing $f(x)$ to be proportional to $x$, while setting $x=0$ gives $f(0)g(y)=y$, forcing $g(y)$ to be proportional to $y$; multiplying those together gives something proportional to $xy$, not $x+y$. Equations like this need an entirely different technique (an "integrating factor," beyond this lesson's scope), not separation of variables.

6 Modelling: first order

The real payoff of everything in this lesson is being able to translate a word problem about a changing quantity directly into a differential equation, almost always separable, solve it using Sections 4 or 5's techniques, and then answer a concrete numerical question.

Key idea: two workhorse models

Exponential growth/decay: whenever a quantity's rate of change is proportional to its current amount, $\dfrac{dP}{dt}=kP$, the solution is always $P=P_0e^{kt}$, with $k>0$ for growth (population, continuous compound interest) and $k<0$ for decay (radioactivity).

Newton's law of cooling: an object's temperature changes at a rate proportional to the difference between its temperature and the surrounding (ambient) temperature $T_s$: $\dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-T_s)$, with solution $T=T_s+(T_0-T_s)e^{-kt}$.

Where the cooling formula comes from

$\dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-T_s)$ is separable: let $u=T-T_s$ (so $du=dT$, since $T_s$ is constant). The equation becomes $\dfrac{du}{dt}=-ku$, exactly the exponential model from the key idea, with solution $u=u_0e^{-kt}$, i.e.\ $T-T_s = (T_0-T_s)e^{-kt}$, giving $T=T_s+(T_0-T_s)e^{-kt}$. So Newton's law of cooling isn't really a separate technique to memorise, it is exponential decay in disguise, applied to the difference between the object's temperature and its surroundings rather than to the temperature itself.

Worked example

A population starts at $100$ and doubles every $5$ hours. Find the population after $8$ hours.

Model: $P=P_0e^{kt}$ with $P_0=100$. Use the doubling information to find $k$: $P(5)=200$, so $100e^{5k}=200 \Rightarrow e^{5k}=2 \Rightarrow k=\dfrac{\ln2}5$.

$$P(t) = 100e^{(\ln2/5)t}.$$

$P(8) = 100e^{(\ln2/5)(8)} = 100e^{1.6\ln2} \approx 303.1$.

Practice 33

A sample of a radioactive isotope has a half-life of $10$ days. Starting from $50$g, how much remains after $30$ days?

Solution

$N=N_0e^{kt}$, $N_0=50$. Half-life gives $N(10)=25$: $50e^{10k}=25 \Rightarrow e^{10k}=\dfrac12 \Rightarrow k=\dfrac{\ln(1/2)}{10} = -\dfrac{\ln2}{10}$.

$N(30) = 50e^{-(\ln2/10)(30)} = 50e^{-3\ln2} = 50\cdot2^{-3} = 50\cdot\dfrac18 = 6.25\text{g}$.

(This also makes sense directly: $30$ days is exactly $3$ half-lives, so the amount is halved three times: $50\to25\to12.5\to6.25$.)

Practice 34

A cup of coffee starts at $90^\circ$C in a room at $20^\circ$C, and cools to $60^\circ$C after $10$ minutes. Find the temperature after $20$ minutes.

Solution

$T=20+(90-20)e^{-kt} = 20+70e^{-kt}$. Use $T(10)=60$: $60=20+70e^{-10k} \Rightarrow 40=70e^{-10k} \Rightarrow e^{-10k}=\dfrac47$.

$T(20) = 20+70e^{-20k} = 20+70(e^{-10k})^2 = 20+70\left(\dfrac47\right)^2 = 20+70\cdot\dfrac{16}{49} = 20+\dfrac{1120}{49} \approx 42.9^\circ\text{C}.$

Notice you never needed to solve for $k$ explicitly, squaring $e^{-10k}$ gives $e^{-20k}$ directly, since doubling the time squares the decay factor.

Practice 35

$\$1000$ is invested at a continuous interest rate of $5\%$ per year, modelled by $\dfrac{dA}{dt}=rA$. Find the amount after $10$ years.

Solution

$A=A_0e^{rt} = 1000e^{0.05t}$. $\ A(10) = 1000e^{0.5} \approx \$1648.72$.

Practice 36

A tank contains $100$L of water with $5$kg of dissolved salt. Brine with concentration $0.1$kg/L flows in at $2$L/min, and the well-mixed solution flows out at the same rate (so the volume stays constant at $100$L). The salt amount $S(t)$ satisfies $\dfrac{dS}{dt} = \dfrac{10-S}{50}$. Solve for $S(t)$ with $S(0)=5$, and find the amount after $50$ minutes.

Solution

Separate: $\dfrac{dS}{10-S} = \dfrac{dt}{50} \Rightarrow -\ln|10-S| = \dfrac{t}{50}+C_1$.

$S(0)=5$: $-\ln5 = C_1$. So $-\ln|10-S| = \dfrac{t}{50}-\ln5 \Rightarrow \ln|10-S| = \ln5-\dfrac{t}{50} \Rightarrow 10-S = 5e^{-t/50}$.

$$S(t) = 10-5e^{-t/50}.$$

$S(50) = 10-5e^{-1} \approx 10-1.84 \approx 8.16$kg.

Notice $S\to10$ as $t\to\infty$: the tank's salt content approaches, but never exceeds, the amount corresponding to the inflow concentration ($0.1$kg/L $\times$ $100$L $=10$kg), exactly as you would expect physically.

Practice 37

A population grows according to $\dfrac{dP}{dt}=kP$ and reaches $3$ times its initial size after $4$ years. Find $k$, and find how long it takes to reach $9$ times its initial size.

Solution

$P=P_0e^{kt}$. $\ P(4)=3P_0$: $e^{4k}=3 \Rightarrow k=\dfrac{\ln3}4$.

Reaching $9P_0=3^2P_0$: since $9=3^2$, and each factor of $3$ takes $4$ years, reaching $9$ times takes $2\times4=8$ years. (Directly: $e^{kt}=9=3^2 \Rightarrow kt=2\ln3 \Rightarrow t = \dfrac{2\ln3}k = \dfrac{2\ln3}{\ln3/4}=8$.)

Practice 38

A hot metal bar at $200^\circ$C is placed in a room at $25^\circ$C. After $5$ minutes it has cooled to $150^\circ$C. Find the cooling constant $k$, and predict the temperature after $15$ minutes.

Solution

$T=25+175e^{-kt}$. $\ T(5)=150$: $125=175e^{-5k} \Rightarrow e^{-5k}=\dfrac{125}{175}=\dfrac57 \Rightarrow k = -\dfrac15\ln\left(\dfrac57\right) = \dfrac15\ln\left(\dfrac75\right)$.

$T(15) = 25+175e^{-15k} = 25+175(e^{-5k})^3 = 25+175\left(\dfrac57\right)^3 = 25+175\cdot\dfrac{125}{343} \approx 25+63.8 \approx 88.8^\circ\text{C}$.

Again, $e^{-15k}=(e^{-5k})^3$ sidesteps ever needing a decimal value for $k$ itself.
