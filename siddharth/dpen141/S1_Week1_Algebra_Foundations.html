0 Sets, Intervals, and Absolute Value Inequalities

Before any calculus happens, you need a precise language for describing which numbers you're talking about. Set notation, interval notation, and absolute value are that language: three different ways of saying the same kind of thing, "the numbers between here and there."

Absolute value in particular deserves care because it shows up everywhere in engineering: tolerances, error bounds, and specifications are almost always written as $|x - \text{target}| \le \text{tolerance}$.

Key idea

$|A - a| \le \delta$ means $A$ is within $\delta$ of $a$ on either side. It unpacks to the double inequality $a - \delta \le A \le a + \delta$, which in interval notation is $A \in [a-\delta,\ a+\delta]$.

Union $\cup$ means "or" (combine the sets); intersection $\cap$ means "and" (only what's in both).

Worked example

A resistor is specified as $R = 220\,\Omega$ with a tolerance of $5\%$. Write the acceptable range as an absolute value inequality and as an interval.

$5\%$ of $220$ is $11$, so the condition is $|R - 220| \le 11$.

Unpacking: $220 - 11 \le R \le 220 + 11$, i.e. $209 \le R \le 231$.

In interval notation: $R \in [209, 231]$.

Practice 1

A manufacturer requires a bolt diameter $d$ (mm) to satisfy $|d - 12| \le 0.05$. Write this as an interval, and state the maximum and minimum acceptable diameters.

Solution

$d \in [11.95, 12.05]$. Maximum diameter $12.05$ mm, minimum $11.95$ mm.

Practice 2

Solve $|2x - 5| \le 7$ for $x$, giving your answer in interval notation.

Solution

$-7 \le 2x-5\le 7 \ \Rightarrow\ -2\le 2x\le 12 \ \Rightarrow\ -1\le x\le 6$. So $x\in[-1,6]$.

Practice 3

A component's mass $m$ (grams) must satisfy $|m - 50| > 2$. Describe this set in words, and write it as a union of two intervals.

Solution

The mass must be more than $2$ g away from $50$ g on either side — i.e. either too light or too heavy. As intervals: $m \in (-\infty, 48) \cup (52, \infty)$.

Practice 4

If $A = \{x : x^2 < 9\}$ and $B = \{x : x \ge 0\}$, find $A \cap B$ and $A \cup B$ in interval notation.

Solution

$A = (-3,3)$. $A\cap B = [0,3)$ (both conditions at once). $A\cup B = (-3,\infty)$ (either condition).

Solution

Practice 6

A square solar panel must have area $A$ satisfying $|A - 6.0| \le 0.15\ \text{m}^2$. To what tolerance (in cm) must the side length $x$ be manufactured? (Use $\sqrt{6}\approx 2.449$ m and approximate the tolerance using $dA \approx 2x\,dx$.)

Solution

$A=x^2$, so $x=\sqrt{6}\approx 2.449$ m. Differentiating loosely, $\Delta A \approx 2x\,\Delta x$, so $\Delta x \approx \dfrac{0.15}{2(2.449)} \approx 0.0306$ m $\approx 3.06$ cm. The side length must be held to within about $3.06$ cm of $2.449$ m.

1 Laws of Indices

Every index law you'll ever need reduces to one idea: an exponent counts how many times a factor is multiplied. From that single idea, every rule below can be re-derived on the spot if you forget it.

Key idea — the index laws

$$a^m\cdot a^n = a^{m+n}, \qquad \frac{a^m}{a^n}=a^{m-n}, \qquad (a^m)^n = a^{mn}, \qquad a^0=1\ (a\neq0), \qquad a^{-n}=\frac{1}{a^n}, \qquad a^{1/n}=\sqrt[n]{a}.$$

These hold for any real exponents (not just positive integers), which is what lets you simplify expressions with fractional or negative powers using exactly the same rules.

Worked example

Simplify $\dfrac{2^{5x}\times 8^x}{4^{2x}}$.

Rewrite every base as a power of $2$: $8^x = (2^3)^x = 2^{3x}$, and $4^{2x} = (2^2)^{2x} = 2^{4x}$.

$$\frac{2^{5x}\cdot 2^{3x}}{2^{4x}} = 2^{5x+3x-4x} = 2^{4x}.$$

Practice 7

Simplify $\dfrac{3^{4x}\times 9^{x}}{27^{x}}$, writing your answer as a single power of $3$.

Solution

$9^x=3^{2x}$, $27^x=3^{3x}$. $\dfrac{3^{4x}\cdot3^{2x}}{3^{3x}}=3^{4x+2x-3x}=3^{3x}$.

Practice 8

Simplify $\dfrac{(2x^3y^{-2})^3}{4x^{-1}y^4}$.

Solution

Numerator: $8x^9y^{-6}$. So $\dfrac{8x^9y^{-6}}{4x^{-1}y^4}=2x^{9-(-1)}y^{-6-4}=2x^{10}y^{-10}=\dfrac{2x^{10}}{y^{10}}$.

Practice 9

Solve $9^x + 2(3^x) - 15 = 0$ for $x$.

Solution

Let $u=3^x$. Then $u^2+2u-15=0 \Rightarrow (u+5)(u-3)=0 \Rightarrow u=-5$ or $u=3$. Since $u=3^x>0$, reject $u=-5$. So $3^x=3 \Rightarrow x=1$.

Practice 10

Solve for $x$: $4^{x+1} = 8^{x-1}$.

Solution

$2^{2(x+1)}=2^{3(x-1)} \Rightarrow 2x+2=3x-3 \Rightarrow x=5$.

Practice 11

Simplify $\left(\dfrac{a^{2/3}b^{-1/2}}{a^{-1/3}b^{3/2}}\right)^{6}$.

Solution

Inside the bracket: $a^{2/3-(-1/3)}b^{-1/2-3/2}=a^{1}b^{-2}$. Raising to the 6th power: $a^{6}b^{-12}=\dfrac{a^6}{b^{12}}$.

Practice 12

A radioactive sample decays according to $N(t)=N_0\left(\tfrac12\right)^{t/h}$ where $h$ is the half-life. If $N_0=800$ and the sample decays to $100$ after $30$ minutes, find $h$.

Solution

$100=800(1/2)^{30/h} \Rightarrow \tfrac18 = (1/2)^{30/h} \Rightarrow (1/2)^3=(1/2)^{30/h} \Rightarrow 3=30/h \Rightarrow h=10$ minutes.

Practice 13

Without a calculator, evaluate $\dfrac{12!}{6!\,6!}$.

Solution

$\dfrac{12!}{6!6!} = \binom{12}{6}$. Computing directly: $\dfrac{12\cdot11\cdot10\cdot9\cdot8\cdot7}{6!} = \dfrac{665280}{720}=924$.

2 Surds

A surd is just an index law in disguise: $\sqrt{a}=a^{1/2}$. Everything you do with surds — simplifying, combining, rationalising — is the same index-law manipulation from Section 1, applied to a fractional exponent.

Key idea

$\sqrt{a}\sqrt{b}=\sqrt{ab}$ and $\dfrac{\sqrt{a}}{\sqrt{b}}=\sqrt{\dfrac{a}{b}}$. To rationalise a denominator containing $\sqrt{b}$, multiply top and bottom by $\sqrt{b}$; if the denominator is $a+\sqrt{b}$, multiply top and bottom by the conjugate $a-\sqrt{b}$, since $(a+\sqrt b)(a-\sqrt b)=a^2-b$ removes the surd.

Worked example

Simplify $3\sqrt{32}+2\sqrt{50}-8\sqrt{18}$.

$\sqrt{32}=\sqrt{16\cdot2}=4\sqrt2$, $\ \sqrt{50}=\sqrt{25\cdot2}=5\sqrt2$, $\ \sqrt{18}=\sqrt{9\cdot2}=3\sqrt2$.

$3(4\sqrt2)+2(5\sqrt2)-8(3\sqrt2) = 12\sqrt2+10\sqrt2-24\sqrt2 = -2\sqrt2$.

Practice 14

Simplify $2\sqrt{10}\times 4\sqrt{15}$.

Solution

$2\times4\times\sqrt{10\times15}=8\sqrt{150}=8\sqrt{25\cdot6}=8\cdot5\sqrt6=40\sqrt6$.

Practice 15

Simplify $\sqrt{75}+\sqrt{48}-\sqrt{27}$.

Solution

$\sqrt{75}=5\sqrt3$, $\sqrt{48}=4\sqrt3$, $\sqrt{27}=3\sqrt3$. Sum: $5\sqrt3+4\sqrt3-3\sqrt3=6\sqrt3$.

Practice 16

Rationalise the denominator of $\dfrac{5}{\sqrt7}$.

Solution

$\dfrac{5}{\sqrt7}\times\dfrac{\sqrt7}{\sqrt7}=\dfrac{5\sqrt7}{7}$.

Practice 17

Rationalise the denominator of $\dfrac{3}{2+\sqrt5}$.

Solution

Multiply by the conjugate: $\dfrac{3}{2+\sqrt5}\times\dfrac{2-\sqrt5}{2-\sqrt5}=\dfrac{3(2-\sqrt5)}{4-5}=\dfrac{3(2-\sqrt5)}{-1}=3\sqrt5-6$.

Practice 18

Simplify $\dfrac{\sqrt{18}-\sqrt2}{\sqrt2}$ fully.

Solution

$\sqrt{18}=3\sqrt2$, so numerator is $3\sqrt2-\sqrt2=2\sqrt2$. Then $\dfrac{2\sqrt2}{\sqrt2}=2$.

Practice 19

A right triangle has legs of length $\sqrt{18}$ cm and $\sqrt{32}$ cm. Find the exact length of the hypotenuse in simplest surd form.

Solution

$h=\sqrt{18+32}=\sqrt{50}=5\sqrt2$ cm. (Note: this only works because you add the squares, i.e. the areas $18$ and $32$, not the surds directly.)

Practice 20

Rationalise and simplify $\dfrac{4-\sqrt3}{4+\sqrt3}$.

Solution

Multiply by conjugate $\dfrac{4-\sqrt3}{4-\sqrt3}$: numerator $(4-\sqrt3)^2=16-8\sqrt3+3=19-8\sqrt3$; denominator $16-3=13$. Result: $\dfrac{19-8\sqrt3}{13}$.

3 Expansion and Special Products

Expanding brackets is systematic multiplication, and a handful of "special products" come up so often that memorising their shape saves real time on longer problems, including the binomial expansion of $(a+b)^n$ used throughout this course.

Key patterns

$(a+b)^2=a^2+2ab+b^2$, $\quad (a-b)^2=a^2-2ab+b^2$, $\quad (a+b)(a-b)=a^2-b^2$.

Binomial expansion: $(a+b)^n = \displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{n}\binom{n}{k}a^{n-k}b^k$, where $\binom{n}{k}=\dfrac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$.

Worked example

Write down the complete binomial expansion of $(2-x)^5$.

Using $a=2,\ b=-x,\ n=5$: coefficients $\binom{5}{0},\dots,\binom{5}{5} = 1,5,10,10,5,1$.

$$(2-x)^5 = 32 - 80x + 80x^2 - 40x^3 + 10x^4 - x^5.$$

Each term's sign alternates because $b=-x$ is negative, and the power of $2$ decreases from $2^5$ to $2^0$ while the power of $x$ increases from $0$ to $5$.

Practice 21

Expand and simplify $(3x+4y)^2-(2x-y)^2$.

Solution

$(3x+4y)^2=9x^2+24xy+16y^2$. $(2x-y)^2=4x^2-4xy+y^2$. Difference: $5x^2+28xy+15y^2$.

Practice 22

Expand $(2x-3)(x^2-3x+1)$.

Solution

$2x(x^2-3x+1)-3(x^2-3x+1) = 2x^3-6x^2+2x-3x^2+9x-3 = 2x^3-9x^2+11x-3$.

Practice 23

Write the complete binomial expansion of $(x+2)^4$.

Solution

Coefficients $1,4,6,4,1$: $(x+2)^4 = x^4+8x^3+24x^2+32x+16$.

Practice 24

Find the term containing $x^3$ in the expansion of $(2x-1)^6$.

Solution

General term: $\binom{6}{k}(2x)^{6-k}(-1)^k$. Need $6-k=3\Rightarrow k=3$: $\binom{6}{3}(2x)^3(-1)^3 = 20\cdot8x^3\cdot(-1)=-160x^3$.

Practice 25

Expand $(a-2b+c)^2$ fully.

Solution

Using $(a-2b+c)^2=a^2+4b^2+c^2+2[a(-2b)+a(c)+(-2b)(c)]= a^2+4b^2+c^2-4ab+2ac-4bc$.

Practice 26

A rectangular duct has cross-section $(x+3)$ by $(x-1)$ cm. Expand to find its cross-sectional area as a polynomial in $x$, then evaluate it at $x=5$.

Solution

Area $=(x+3)(x-1)=x^2+2x-3$. At $x=5$: $25+10-3=32\ \text{cm}^2$.

4 Factorisation

Factorising is expansion run in reverse, and it's the single most-used algebraic skill in this course: solving equations, simplifying algebraic fractions, and finding limits all lean on being able to spot a common factor, a quadratic pattern, or a difference of squares quickly.

Strategies, roughly in the order to try them

1) Take out the highest common factor first, always. 2) Look for a difference of squares $a^2-b^2=(a+b)(a-b)$. 3) For a trinomial $ax^2+bx+c$, find two numbers multiplying to $ac$ and adding to $b$, then split the middle term. 4) For four terms, try grouping in pairs.

Worked example

Factorise $6x^2+7x-3$.

Need two numbers multiplying to $6\times(-3)=-18$ and adding to $7$: these are $9$ and $-2$.

Split the middle term: $6x^2+9x-2x-3$.

Group: $(6x^2+9x)+(-2x-3) = 3x(2x+3)-1(2x+3) = (2x+3)(3x-1)$.

Check by expanding: $(2x+3)(3x-1)=6x^2-2x+9x-3=6x^2+7x-3$. ✓

Test-before-you-hunt

For $ax^2+bx+c$, before hunting for integer factor pairs, check the discriminant $b^2-4ac$. If it's a perfect square, integer factors exist and the split-the-middle-term method will find them quickly. If it isn't a perfect square, stop hunting — the quadratic doesn't factor over the rationals, and its roots (found via the quadratic formula) are irrational. For example, $6x^2-5x-7$ has discriminant $(-5)^2-4(6)(-7)=25+168=193$, not a perfect square, so it does not factor over the integers — it's already in simplest form.

Practice 27

Factorise $5x^2-26x+24$.

Solution

Need factors of $5\times24=120$ summing to $-26$: $-6,-20$. Split: $5x^2-6x-20x+24=x(5x-6)-4(5x-6)=(5x-6)(x-4)$.

Practice 28

Factorise $x^2+3x+2$, then hence solve $x^2+3x+2<0$.

Solution

$x^2+3x+2=(x+1)(x+2)$. Roots at $x=-1,-2$; parabola opens up, so negative between roots: $-2

Practice 29

Factorise fully $2x^3-8x$.

Solution

Common factor $2x$: $2x(x^2-4)=2x(x-2)(x+2)$.

Practice 30

Factorise $9x^2-24x+16$.

Solution

Recognise a perfect square: $9x^2-24x+16=(3x-4)^2$ since $(3x)^2=9x^2$, $2(3x)(4)=24x$, $4^2=16$.

Practice 31

Factorise by grouping: $x^3-3x^2+2x-6$.

Solution

Group: $(x^3-3x^2)+(2x-6)=x^2(x-3)+2(x-3)=(x-3)(x^2+2)$.

Practice 32

Solve $x^2+3x+2<0$ using a sign diagram, confirming your Practice 21 factorisation. State the answer in interval notation.

Solution

Roots at $x=-2,-1$ from $(x+1)(x+2)$; testing a point between them (e.g. $x=-1.5$: $(-0.5)(0.5)=-0.25<0$) confirms negative there. Solution: $x\in(-2,-1)$.

Practice 33

Factorise $16x^4-1$ completely (over the reals).

Solution

Difference of squares twice: $16x^4-1=(4x^2-1)(4x^2+1)=(2x-1)(2x+1)(4x^2+1)$. The last factor has no real linear factors.

5 Algebraic Fractions and Rational Equations

Algebraic fractions follow the exact same rules as numerical fractions — you just factorise first to find common factors to cancel, and find a common denominator before adding or subtracting. Solving equations that contain them always starts the same way: clear every denominator, then solve the polynomial that's left, and finally check no solution makes an original denominator zero.

Key idea

Before cancelling, always factorise numerator and denominator fully — you can only cancel common factors, never terms that happen to look similar inside a sum.

When solving $\dfrac{P(x)}{Q(x)}=\dfrac{R(x)}{S(x)}$-style equations, multiply every term by the lowest common denominator, solve the resulting polynomial equation, then reject any solution that makes an original denominator $0$ (an extraneous solution).

Worked example

Solve $\dfrac{4}{x^2-4}-\dfrac{1}{x-2}=\dfrac{1}{x+2}$.

Factorise: $x^2-4=(x-2)(x+2)$. The LCD is $(x-2)(x+2)$.

Multiply every term by $(x-2)(x+2)$: $4 - (x+2) = (x-2)$.

$4-x-2=x-2 \ \Rightarrow\ 2-x=x-2 \ \Rightarrow\ 4=2x \ \Rightarrow\ x=2$.

But $x=2$ makes the original denominators $x-2$ and $x^2-4$ both zero — it's extraneous. So this equation has no solution.

Practice 34

Simplify $\dfrac{x^2-9}{x^2+x-12}$.

Solution

Factorise both: $\dfrac{(x-3)(x+3)}{(x+4)(x-3)}$. Cancel $(x-3)$: $\dfrac{x+3}{x+4}$ (with $x\ne3$).

Practice 35

Simplify $\dfrac{2x}{x-1}-\dfrac{3}{x+2}$ into a single fraction.

Solution

LCD $(x-1)(x+2)$: $\dfrac{2x(x+2)-3(x-1)}{(x-1)(x+2)}=\dfrac{2x^2+4x-3x+3}{(x-1)(x+2)}=\dfrac{2x^2+x+3}{(x-1)(x+2)}$.

Practice 36

Solve for $x$: $\dfrac{3x}{x^2-4}=\dfrac{2}{x+2}$.

Solution

Multiply by $(x-2)(x+2)$: $3x=2(x-2) \Rightarrow 3x=2x-4 \Rightarrow x=-4$. Check: doesn't make any denominator zero, so $x=-4$ is valid.

Practice 37

Simplify $\dfrac{x^2-5x+6}{x^2-4}\times\dfrac{x+2}{x-3}$.

Solution

Factorise: $\dfrac{(x-2)(x-3)}{(x-2)(x+2)}\times\dfrac{x+2}{x-3}$. Cancel $(x-2)$, $(x-3)$, $(x+2)$: result is $1$ (for $x\ne2,-2,3$).

Practice 38

Solve $\dfrac{1}{x-1}+\dfrac{1}{x+1}=\dfrac{4}{x^2-1}$.

Solution

LCD $(x-1)(x+1)$: $(x+1)+(x-1)=4 \Rightarrow 2x=4 \Rightarrow x=2$. Valid, since $x=2$ doesn't zero any denominator.

Practice 39

A flow-rate formula gives total resistance $R$ for two parallel pipes as $\dfrac{1}{R}=\dfrac{1}{R_1}+\dfrac{1}{R_2}$. Combine the right side into a single fraction and hence write $R$ explicitly in terms of $R_1$ and $R_2$.

Solution

$\dfrac{1}{R_1}+\dfrac{1}{R_2}=\dfrac{R_2+R_1}{R_1R_2}$, so $\dfrac{1}{R}=\dfrac{R_1+R_2}{R_1R_2}$, giving $R=\dfrac{R_1R_2}{R_1+R_2}$.

Practice 40

Solve $\dfrac{x+1}{x-3}-\dfrac{2}{x}=1$, checking for extraneous solutions.

Solution

LCD $x(x-3)$: $x(x+1)-2(x-3)=x(x-3) \Rightarrow x^2+x-2x+6=x^2-3x \Rightarrow x^2-x+6=x^2-3x \Rightarrow 2x=-6 \Rightarrow x=-3$. Check: $x=-3$ doesn't zero either original denominator, so it's valid.

Practice 41

A sensor calibration model gives \(R(x)=\dfrac{x^2-8x+15}{x-3}\). Simplify and state excluded \(x\)-values, then evaluate \(R(5)\).

Solution

Factor numerator: \(x^2-8x+15=(x-3)(x-5)\).

Simplified model: \(R(x)=x-5\), with restriction \(x\ne 3\).

\(R(5)=5-5=0\).

Practice 42

A bracket cross-section is modeled by \(A(x)=x^2-7x+12\). Find the roots and interpret where area collapses to zero.

Solution

Factor: \(A(x)=(x-3)(x-4)\).

Roots are \(x=3,4\). At these settings the model predicts zero area (degenerate section).

Practice 43

Solve \(\dfrac{2}{x-1}+\dfrac{3}{x+2}=1\), including domain restrictions and validity checks.

Solution

Domain: \(x\ne1,-2\).

Multiply by \((x-1)(x+2)\): \(2(x+2)+3(x-1)=x^2+x-2\).

So \(5x+1=x^2+x-2\Rightarrow x^2-4x-3=0\).

\(x=2\pm\sqrt7\), both satisfy domain restrictions.

Practice 44

Given \(f(x)=\dfrac{x^2-9}{x-3}\), find \(f(3+h)\) and use it to compute \(\lim_{h\to0} f(3+h)\).

Solution

\(f(x)=\dfrac{(x-3)(x+3)}{x-3}=x+3\) for \(x\ne3\).

Hence \(f(3+h)=6+h\) for \(h\ne0\).

\(\lim_{h\to0}f(3+h)=6\).

Practice 45

A resistance relation is \(\dfrac{1}{R}=\dfrac{1}{R_1}+\dfrac{1}{R_2}\). Derive explicit \(R\) and compute for \(R_1=12\,\Omega, R_2=18\,\Omega\).

Solution

Combine fractions: \(\dfrac{1}{R}=\dfrac{R_1+R_2}{R_1R_2}\Rightarrow R=\dfrac{R_1R_2}{R_1+R_2}\).

Substitute: \(R=\dfrac{12\cdot18}{12+18}=\dfrac{216}{30}=7.2\,\Omega\).
