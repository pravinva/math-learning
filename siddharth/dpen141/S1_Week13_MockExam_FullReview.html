Practice 1 EXAM-STYLE

Simplify $\dfrac{2^{3x}\times4^x}{8^{x}}$.

Solution

$4^x=2^{2x}$, $8^x=2^{3x}$. $\dfrac{2^{3x}\cdot2^{2x}}{2^{3x}}=2^{2x}$.

Practice 2 EXAM-STYLE

Solve $|3x-2|\le7$ for $x$, in interval notation.

Solution

$-7\le3x-2\le7 \Rightarrow -5\le3x\le9 \Rightarrow -\tfrac53\le x\le3$. $x\in\left[-\tfrac53,3\right]$.

Practice 3 EXAM-STYLE

Factorise $2x^2-x-15$.

Solution

Need factors of $-30$ summing to $-1$: $-6,5$. $(2x-... )$ split: $2x^2-6x+5x-15=2x(x-3)+5(x-3)=(2x+5)(x-3)$.

Practice 4 EXAM-STYLE

Evaluate $z_1\cdot z_2$ for $z_1=2-j$, $z_2=1+3j$.

Solution

$(2)(1)+(2)(3j)+(-j)(1)+(-j)(3j)=2+6j-j-3j^2=2+5j+3=5+5j$.

Practice 5 EXAM-STYLE

Find the polar form of $z=-\sqrt3+j$.

Solution

$r=\sqrt{3+1}=2$. Q2, reference angle $\dfrac{\pi}{6}$: $\theta=\pi-\dfrac{\pi}{6}=\dfrac{5\pi}{6}$. $z=2\left(\cos\tfrac{5\pi}{6}+j\sin\tfrac{5\pi}{6}\right)$.

Practice 6 EXAM-STYLE

Find $f^{-1}(x)$ for $f(x)=4x-9$.

Solution

$x=4y-9 \Rightarrow y=\dfrac{x+9}{4}$.

Practice 7 EXAM-STYLE

Differentiate $y=(5x-3)^4$.

Solution

$y'=4(5x-3)^3\times5=20(5x-3)^3$.

Practice 8 EXAM-STYLE

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ for $x^2+xy=10$ (implicit differentiation).

Solution

$2x+y+x\dfrac{dy}{dx}=0 \Rightarrow \dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{-2x-y}{x}$.

Practice 9 EXAM-STYLE

Differentiate $y=\ln(\sec x)$.

Solution

$y'=\dfrac{\sec x\tan x}{\sec x}=\tan x$.

Practice 10 EXAM-STYLE

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^2(3x^2-4x+1)\,dx$.

Solution

$\left[x^3-2x^2+x\right]_0^2=(8-8+2)-0=2$.

Practice 11 EXAM-STYLE

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int\frac{x\,dx}{x^2+4}$.

Solution

$\dfrac12\ln(x^2+4)+C$.

Practice 12 EXAM-STYLE

Find the critical point(s) of $f(x)=x^2-6x+1$.

Solution

$f'(x)=2x-6=0 \Rightarrow x=3$.

Practice 13 EXAM-STYLE

Find $\sinh(0)$ and $\cosh(0)$.

Solution

$\sinh(0)=\dfrac{1-1}{2}=0$. $\cosh(0)=\dfrac{1+1}{2}=1$.

Practice 14 EXAM-STYLE

Eliminate the parameter from $x=t+1,\ y=t^2$.

Solution

$t=x-1 \Rightarrow y=(x-1)^2$.

Practice 15 EXAM-STYLE

State the value of $\displaystyle\lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{\sin(3h)}{h}$ (recognise the standard limit pattern).

Solution

Using $\lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{\sin(kh)}{h}=k$: the limit is $3$.

Practice 16 EXAM-STYLE

Find the area under $y=e^x$ between $x=0$ and $x=\ln2$.

Solution

$\left[e^x\right]_0^{\ln2}=2-1=1$.

Practice 17 EXAM-STYLE

Question 1. Let $f(x) = \dfrac{2x-1}{x+1}$. (a) Find the domain of $f$. (b) Show that $f$ is one-to-one on its domain. (c) Find $f^{-1}(x)$, and state its domain. (d) Verify that $f(f^{-1}(2))=2$.

Solution

(a) Excluded value $x=-1$: domain $(-\infty,-1)\cup(-1,\infty)$.

(b) Suppose $f(a)=f(b)$: $\dfrac{2a-1}{a+1}=\dfrac{2b-1}{b+1} \Rightarrow (2a-1)(b+1)=(2b-1)(a+1) \Rightarrow 2ab+2a-b-1=2ab+2b-a-1 \Rightarrow 2a-b=2b-a \Rightarrow 3a=3b \Rightarrow a=b$. One-to-one.

(c) $x=\dfrac{2y-1}{y+1} \Rightarrow x(y+1)=2y-1 \Rightarrow xy+x=2y-1 \Rightarrow xy-2y=-x-1 \Rightarrow y(x-2)=-x-1 \Rightarrow y=\dfrac{-x-1}{x-2}=\dfrac{x+1}{2-x}$. Domain of $f^{-1}$: excludes $x=2$ (the range boundary corresponding to the horizontal asymptote of $f$), so domain is $x\ne2$.

(d) $f^{-1}(2)=\dfrac{2+1}{2-2}$ — undefined! This signals $x=2$ is exactly excluded from $f^{-1}$'s domain (as noted in (c)), so this check cannot be performed at $x=2$; instead verify at another value, e.g. $f^{-1}(1)=\dfrac{1+1}{2-1}=2$, then $f(2)=\dfrac{4-1}{3}=1$. ✓ (consistent, confirming the inverse relationship at a valid point).

Practice 18 EXAM-STYLE

Question 2. (a) Use first principles to find the derivative of $f(x)=x^2+2x$. (b) Hence, or otherwise, find the equation of the tangent line to $y=f(x)$ at $x=1$. (c) Find the equation of the normal line at the same point.

Solution

(a) $f(x+h)-f(x)=(x+h)^2+2(x+h)-x^2-2x = 2xh+h^2+2h = h(2x+h+2)$. Dividing by $h$ and letting $h\to0$: $f'(x)=2x+2$.

(b) At $x=1$: $f(1)=1+2=3$, $f'(1)=4$. Tangent: $y-3=4(x-1) \Rightarrow y=4x-1$.

(c) Normal slope $=-\dfrac14$ (negative reciprocal). $y-3=-\dfrac14(x-1) \Rightarrow y=-\dfrac14x+\dfrac{13}{4}$.

Practice 19 EXAM-STYLE

Question 3. A cylindrical tank (radius fixed at $3$ m) is being filled with water at $5\ \text{m}^3/\text{min}$. (a) Write the volume $V$ in terms of the water height $h$. (b) Find $\dfrac{dh}{dt}$. (c) Find how long it takes to fill the tank from empty to a height of $4$ m.

Solution

(a) $V=\pi r^2h=9\pi h$.

(b) $\dfrac{dV}{dt}=9\pi\dfrac{dh}{dt} \Rightarrow 5=9\pi\dfrac{dh}{dt} \Rightarrow \dfrac{dh}{dt}=\dfrac{5}{9\pi}$ m/min (constant, since the tank has uniform cross-section).

(c) Since the rate is constant, time $=\dfrac{\text{height}}{\text{rate}}=\dfrac{4}{5/(9\pi)}=\dfrac{36\pi}{5}\approx22.6$ minutes.

Practice 20 EXAM-STYLE

Question 4. Let $f(x)=x^4-4x^3$. (a) Find $f'(x)$ and $f''(x)$. (b) Find and classify all critical points. (c) Find any inflection point(s). (d) Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^4 f(x)\,dx$.

Solution

(a) $f'(x)=4x^3-12x^2$. $f''(x)=12x^2-24x$.

(b) $f'(x)=4x^2(x-3)$. Critical points $x=0,3$. $f''(0)=0$ (inconclusive by second derivative test; checking sign of $f'$ either side of $0$: both slightly negative and slightly positive $x$ give $f'<0$ since $x^2\ge0$ and $x-3<0$ near $0$ — no sign change, so $x=0$ is a stationary inflection, neither max nor min). $f''(3)=108-72=36>0$: local min at $x=3$.

(c) $f''(x)=12x(x-2)=0 \Rightarrow x=0,2$. At $x=0$: this coincides with the stationary point found above — check sign change of $f''$: for $x$ slightly negative, $f''>0$ (concave up); for $x$ slightly positive (but less than 2), $f''<0$ (concave down) — genuine inflection at $x=0$ too. At $x=2$: sign changes from negative to positive — genuine inflection.

(d) $\displaystyle\int_0^4(x^4-4x^3)\,dx=\left[\dfrac{x^5}{5}-x^4\right]_0^4=\left(\dfrac{1024}{5}-256\right)-0=\dfrac{1024-1280}{5}=-\dfrac{256}{5}$.

Practice 21 EXAM-STYLE

Question 5. (a) Find the points of intersection of $y=x^2-1$ and $y=3-x^2$. (b) Find the area enclosed between the two curves. (c) A solid is formed by rotating the region bounded by $y=x^2-1$ and the $x$-axis (between its real roots) about the $x$-axis. Set up (but do not evaluate) the integral for its volume.

Solution

(a) $x^2-1=3-x^2 \Rightarrow 2x^2=4 \Rightarrow x^2=2 \Rightarrow x=\pm\sqrt2$.

(b) At $x=0$: first curve gives $-1$, second gives $3$: second is on top. Area $=\displaystyle\int_{-\sqrt2}^{\sqrt2}\big[(3-x^2)-(x^2-1)\big]dx=\int_{-\sqrt2}^{\sqrt2}(4-2x^2)\,dx=\left[4x-\dfrac{2x^3}{3}\right]_{-\sqrt2}^{\sqrt2}$. At $x=\sqrt2$: $4\sqrt2-\dfrac{2(2\sqrt2)}{3}=4\sqrt2-\dfrac{4\sqrt2}{3}=\dfrac{8\sqrt2}{3}$. By symmetry (odd function of $x$ within the bracket), the value at $-\sqrt2$ is the negative: area $=\dfrac{8\sqrt2}{3}-\left(-\dfrac{8\sqrt2}{3}\right)=\dfrac{16\sqrt2}{3}$.

(c) Roots of $x^2-1=0$: $x=\pm1$. $V=\pi\displaystyle\int_{-1}^{1}(x^2-1)^2\,dx$.
