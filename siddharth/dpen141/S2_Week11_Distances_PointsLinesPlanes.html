0 Distance Formulas: A Complete Toolkit

This lesson pulls together every distance calculation from the whole vector geometry sequence into one place, filling in the two combinations not yet covered: the distance between two parallel lines, and the distance between two parallel planes.

Key formulas

Point to point: $|\overrightarrow{AB}|$. Point to line: $\dfrac{|\overrightarrow{AP}\times\mathbf{d}|}{|\mathbf{d}|}$. Point to plane: $\dfrac{|n_1x_0+n_2y_0+n_3z_0-d|}{|\mathbf{n}|}$.

Parallel planes $n_1x+n_2y+n_3z=d_1$ and $n_1x+n_2y+n_3z=d_2$ (same normal): $$\text{dist} = \frac{|d_1-d_2|}{|\mathbf{n}|}.$$

Worked example

Find the distance between the parallel planes $2x-y+2z=5$ and $2x-y+2z=-4$.

Same normal $\mathbf{n}=(2,-1,2)$, $|\mathbf{n}|=\sqrt{4+1+4}=3$.

$$\text{dist} = \frac{|5-(-4)|}{3} = \frac{9}{3}=3.$$

Practice 1

Find the distance between the parallel planes $x+2y-2z=1$ and $x+2y-2z=7$.

Solution

$|\mathbf{n}|=\sqrt{1+4+4}=3$. Distance $=\dfrac{|1-7|}{3}=\dfrac63=2$.

Practice 2

Find the distance between the parallel planes $2x-2y+z=4$ and $4x-4y+2z=6$ (note the second isn't in the same normalised form).

Solution

Rewrite the second by dividing by $2$: $2x-2y+z=3$. Same normal as the first, $\mathbf{n}=(2,-2,1)$, $|\mathbf{n}|=3$. Distance $=\dfrac{|4-3|}{3}=\dfrac13$.

Practice 3

Find the distance from the point $(3,-1,2)$ to the plane $x+y+z=0$.

Solution

$|\mathbf{n}|=\sqrt3$. Distance $=\dfrac{|3-1+2-0|}{\sqrt3}=\dfrac{4}{\sqrt3}=\dfrac{4\sqrt3}{3}$.

Practice 4

Find the distance from the point $(0,0,0)$ to the line through $(2,3,1)$ with direction $(1,0,0)$.

Solution

$\overrightarrow{AP}=(0,0,0)-(2,3,1)=(-2,-3,-1)$. Cross with $(1,0,0)$: $\mathbf{i}$: $(-3)(0)-(-1)(0)=0$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(-2)(0)-(-1)(1)]=-1$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(-2)(0)-(-3)(1)=3$. Result $(0,-1,3)$, magnitude $\sqrt{10}$. $|\mathbf{d}|=1$. Distance $=\sqrt{10}$.

Practice 5

Two parallel power lines run along $\mathbf{r}_1=(0,0,0)+t(1,1,0)$ and $\mathbf{r}_2=(0,3,0)+t(1,1,0)$ (in metres). Find the distance between them.

Solution

Since the lines are parallel, pick any point on $\mathcal{L}_2$, e.g. $(0,3,0)$, and find its distance to $\mathcal{L}_1$. $\overrightarrow{AP}=(0,3,0)-(0,0,0)=(0,3,0)$. Cross with $(1,1,0)$: $\mathbf{i}$: $(3)(0)-(0)(1)=0$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(0)(0)-(0)(1)]=0$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(0)(1)-(3)(1)=-3$. Result $(0,0,-3)$, magnitude $3$. $|\mathbf{d}|=\sqrt2$. Distance $=\dfrac{3}{\sqrt2}=\dfrac{3\sqrt2}{2}$ m.

1 Distance Between Skew Lines

Two skew lines (non-parallel, non-intersecting) are still separated by a well-defined shortest distance — measured along the one direction perpendicular to both lines simultaneously. That perpendicular direction is exactly the cross product of the two direction vectors.

Key formula

For lines through $A_1$ (direction $\mathbf{d}_1$) and $A_2$ (direction $\mathbf{d}_2$): $$\text{dist} = \frac{\big|\overrightarrow{A_1A_2}\cdot(\mathbf{d}_1\times\mathbf{d}_2)\big|}{|\mathbf{d}_1\times\mathbf{d}_2|}.$$ This is exactly the scalar triple product volume, divided by the base parallelogram area — the same "volume over base = height" idea from the point-to-line formula, generalised to two skew lines.

Worked example

Find the distance between $\mathcal{L}_1: \mathbf{r}=(0,1,0)+s(1,1,0)$ and $\mathcal{L}_2: \mathbf{r}=(0,1,1)+t(1,0,1)$ (shown to be skew in Strand 2, Lesson 8).

$A_1=(0,1,0)$, $A_2=(0,1,1)$. $\overrightarrow{A_1A_2}=(0,0,1)$.

$\mathbf{d}_1\times\mathbf{d}_2 = (1,1,0)\times(1,0,1)$: $\mathbf{i}$: $(1)(1)-(0)(0)=1$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(1)(1)-(0)(1)]=-1$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(1)(0)-(1)(1)=-1$. Result $(1,-1,-1)$.

$\overrightarrow{A_1A_2}\cdot(1,-1,-1) = 0(1)+0(-1)+1(-1)=-1$. $|(1,-1,-1)|=\sqrt3$.

$$\text{dist} = \frac{|-1|}{\sqrt3}=\frac{1}{\sqrt3}=\frac{\sqrt3}{3}.$$

Practice 6

Find the distance between the skew lines $\mathcal{L}_1: \mathbf{r}=(0,0,0)+s(1,0,0)$ and $\mathcal{L}_2: \mathbf{r}=(0,1,1)+t(0,1,0)$.

Solution

$\overrightarrow{A_1A_2}=(0,1,1)$. $\mathbf{d}_1\times\mathbf{d}_2=(1,0,0)\times(0,1,0)$: this is $\mathbf{i}\times\mathbf{j}=\mathbf{k}=(0,0,1)$. Dot product: $(0,1,1)\cdot(0,0,1)=1$. $|(0,0,1)|=1$. Distance $=\dfrac{1}{1}=1$.

Practice 7

Find the distance between the skew lines $\mathcal{L}_1: \mathbf{r}=(1,0,0)+s(0,1,1)$ and $\mathcal{L}_2: \mathbf{r}=(0,0,2)+t(1,0,1)$.

Solution

$\overrightarrow{A_1A_2}=(0,0,2)-(1,0,0)=(-1,0,2)$. $\mathbf{d}_1\times\mathbf{d}_2=(0,1,1)\times(1,0,1)$: $\mathbf{i}$: $(1)(1)-(1)(0)=1$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(0)(1)-(1)(1)]=1$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(0)(0)-(1)(1)=-1$. Result $(1,1,-1)$. Dot: $(-1)(1)+0(1)+2(-1)=-1-2=-3$. $|(1,1,-1)|=\sqrt3$. Distance $=\dfrac{3}{\sqrt3}=\sqrt3$.

Practice 8

Explain why the distance-between-skew-lines formula reduces to $0$ if the lines actually intersect, without doing any calculation.

Solution

If the lines intersect, $\overrightarrow{A_1A_2}$ (from a point on one line to a point on the other, taken as their actual intersection point coinciding) can be written as a combination of $\mathbf{d}_1$ and $\mathbf{d}_2$ (it lies in the plane they span), making the scalar triple product $\overrightarrow{A_1A_2}\cdot(\mathbf{d}_1\times\mathbf{d}_2)$ equal to $0$ — consistent with zero distance.

Practice 9 EXAM-STYLE

[Exam-style, multi-part] Consider the line $\mathcal{L}: \mathbf{r}=(1,2,0)+t(2,-1,2)$ and the plane $\mathcal{P}: x+y+z=6$. (a) Find the point where $\mathcal{L}$ meets $\mathcal{P}$ (substitute the parametric coordinates into the plane equation and solve for $t$). (b) Find the distance from the point $(1,2,0)$ to the plane $\mathcal{P}$. (c) Find the angle between the line's direction and the plane's normal.

Solution

(a) $x=1+2t,\ y=2-t,\ z=2t$. Substitute: $(1+2t)+(2-t)+2t=6 \Rightarrow 3+3t=6 \Rightarrow t=1$. Point: $(3,1,2)$.

(b) $|\mathbf{n}|=\sqrt3$. Distance $=\dfrac{|1+2+0-6|}{\sqrt3}=\dfrac{3}{\sqrt3}=\sqrt3$.

(c) $\mathbf{d}=(2,-1,2)$, $\mathbf{n}=(1,1,1)$. $\mathbf{d}\cdot\mathbf{n}=2-1+2=3$. $|\mathbf{d}|=3$, $|\mathbf{n}|=\sqrt3$. $\cos\theta=\dfrac{3}{3\sqrt3}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt3} \Rightarrow \theta\approx54.7^\circ$.

Practice 10 EXAM-STYLE

[Exam-style, multi-part] Let $A=(1,0,0)$, $B=(0,1,0)$, $C=(0,0,2)$. (a) Find the equation of the plane through $A,B,C$. (b) Find the area of triangle $ABC$ using $\dfrac12|\overrightarrow{AB}\times\overrightarrow{AC}|$. (c) Find the distance from the origin to this plane.

Solution

(a) $\overrightarrow{AB}=(-1,1,0)$, $\overrightarrow{AC}=(-1,0,2)$. Normal: $\mathbf{i}$: $(1)(2)-(0)(0)=2$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(-1)(2)-(0)(-1)]=2$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(-1)(0)-(1)(-1)=1$. $\mathbf{n}=(2,2,1)$. Equation: $2(x-1)+2y+z=0 \Rightarrow 2x+2y+z=2$.

(b) $|\mathbf{n}|=\sqrt{4+4+1}=3$. Area $=\dfrac{3}{2}$.

(c) Distance from $(0,0,0)$: $\dfrac{|0+0+0-2|}{3}=\dfrac23$.

Practice 11 EXAM-STYLE

[Exam-style, multi-part] Two lines are given by $\mathcal{L}_1: \mathbf{r}=(2,0,1)+s(1,1,0)$ and $\mathcal{L}_2: \mathbf{r}=(0,2,0)+t(1,-1,1)$. (a) Show that $\mathcal{L}_1$ and $\mathcal{L}_2$ are skew. (b) Find the distance between them.

Solution

(a) Directions $(1,1,0)$ and $(1,-1,1)$ aren't parallel. Equate: $2+s=t$ (from $x$), $s=2-t$ (from $y$), $1=t$ (from $z$). From $z$: $t=1$. Then from $y$: $s=2-1=1$. Check $x$: $2+1=3$, but $t=1$ gives $3\ne1$ — inconsistent, so no intersection: the lines are skew.

(b) $\overrightarrow{A_1A_2}=(0,2,0)-(2,0,1)=(-2,2,-1)$. $\mathbf{d}_1\times\mathbf{d}_2=(1,1,0)\times(1,-1,1)$: $\mathbf{i}$: $(1)(1)-(0)(-1)=1$. $\mathbf{j}$: $-[(1)(1)-(0)(1)]=-1$. $\mathbf{k}$: $(1)(-1)-(1)(1)=-2$. Result $(1,-1,-2)$. Dot: $(-2)(1)+2(-1)+(-1)(-2)=-2-2+2=-2$. $|(1,-1,-2)|=\sqrt6$. Distance $=\dfrac{2}{\sqrt6}=\dfrac{\sqrt6}{3}$.
