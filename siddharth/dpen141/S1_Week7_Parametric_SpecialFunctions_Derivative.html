0 Parametric Representation of Curves

Instead of writing $y$ directly in terms of $x$, a parametric curve describes both $x$ and $y$ as functions of a third variable (usually $t$, often representing time). This is often more natural for motion problems, and it easily describes curves (like circles) that fail the vertical line test as ordinary functions.

Key idea

Given $x=x(t),\ y=y(t)$, you can often eliminate the parameter $t$ to recover a Cartesian equation relating $x$ and $y$ directly, by solving one equation for $t$ and substituting into the other (or using an identity like $\sin^2t+\cos^2t=1$).

Worked example

If $x=t+\dfrac1t$ and $y=t-\dfrac1t$ for $t>0$, describe the path of the motion.

Compute $x^2-y^2 = \left(t+\dfrac1t\right)^2-\left(t-\dfrac1t\right)^2$.

Expanding: $\left(t^2+2+\dfrac{1}{t^2}\right)-\left(t^2-2+\dfrac{1}{t^2}\right) = 4$.

So $x^2-y^2=4$: the path lies on a hyperbola. Since $t>0$, by AM-GM $x=t+\tfrac1t\ge2$ always, so only the right branch ($x\ge2$) is traced.

Practice 1

Eliminate the parameter from $x=2t,\ y=t^2$ to find the Cartesian equation of the curve.

Solution

From the first, $t=\dfrac{x}{2}$. Substitute: $y=\left(\dfrac{x}{2}\right)^2=\dfrac{x^2}{4}$, a parabola.

Practice 2

Eliminate the parameter from $x=3\cos t,\ y=3\sin t$ ($0\le t\le2\pi$) to identify the curve.

Solution

$\dfrac{x}{3}=\cos t$, $\ \dfrac{y}{3}=\sin t$. Using $\sin^2t+\cos^2t=1$: $\left(\dfrac{x}{3}\right)^2+\left(\dfrac{y}{3}\right)^2=1 \Rightarrow x^2+y^2=9$, a circle of radius $3$.

Practice 3

Eliminate the parameter from $x=2\cos t,\ y=5\sin t$ to identify the curve.

Solution

$\dfrac{x}{2}=\cos t$, $\dfrac{y}{5}=\sin t$. Pythagorean identity: $\left(\dfrac{x}{2}\right)^2+\left(\dfrac{y}{5}\right)^2=1$, an ellipse with semi-axes $2$ and $5$.

Practice 4

Eliminate the parameter from $x=t^2,\ y=t^3$ to find a relationship between $x$ and $y$ (not solved explicitly for $y$).

Solution

$t=x^{1/2}$ (for $t\ge0$), so $y=t^3=(x^{1/2})^3=x^{3/2}$, i.e. $y^2=x^3$.

Practice 5

A particle's position is $x(t)=4t-1,\ y(t)=2t+3$. Show the path is a straight line, and find its Cartesian equation.

Solution

From the first, $t=\dfrac{x+1}{4}$. Substitute: $y=2\left(\dfrac{x+1}{4}\right)+3=\dfrac{x+1}{2}+3=\dfrac{x}{2}+\dfrac72$, i.e. $y=\dfrac12x+\dfrac72$ — a straight line.

1 Special Functions: Piecewise, Absolute Value, and Signum

Beyond the standard polynomial/trig/exponential families, a handful of "special" functions come up often enough in engineering (switching, rectification, thresholding) to deserve their own attention: the absolute value function, the signum function, and general piecewise definitions.

Key definitions

$$|x| = \begin{cases}x & x\ge0\\-x & x<0\end{cases}, \qquad \text{sgn}(x) = \begin{cases}1&x>0\\0&x=0\\-1&x<0\end{cases}.$$

A useful identity: $|x| = x\cdot\text{sgn}(x)$ for $x\ne0$.

Worked example

A half-wave rectifier passes a signal only when it's positive: $f(t) = \begin{cases}\sin t & \sin t\ge0\\0 & \sin t<0\end{cases}$. Evaluate $f\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{4}\right)$, $f\!\left(\dfrac{5\pi}{4}\right)$, and describe $f$ over one period $[0,2\pi]$.

$\sin\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{4}\right)=\dfrac{\sqrt2}{2}>0$, so $f\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{4}\right)=\dfrac{\sqrt2}{2}$.

$\sin\!\left(\dfrac{5\pi}{4}\right)=-\dfrac{\sqrt2}{2}<0$, so $f\!\left(\dfrac{5\pi}{4}\right)=0$.

Over $[0,2\pi]$: $f$ matches $\sin t$ on $[0,\pi]$ (where sine is non-negative), and is identically $0$ on $(\pi,2\pi)$ (where sine would be negative).

Practice 6

Evaluate $|{-7}|+|3|-|-2|$.

Solution

$7+3-2=8$.

Practice 7

Sketch (describe) $f(x)=|x-3|$: state its minimum value and where it occurs.

Solution

V-shape with vertex at $(3,0)$; minimum value $0$ at $x=3$.

Practice 8

Evaluate $\text{sgn}(-5)+\text{sgn}(0)+\text{sgn}(8)$.

Solution

$-1+0+1=0$.

Practice 9

A function is defined as $f(x)=\begin{cases}2x & x<1\\x^2+1 & x\ge1\end{cases}$. Evaluate $f(-2), f(1), f(3)$, and determine whether $f$ is continuous at $x=1$.

Solution

$f(-2)=2(-2)=-4$. $f(1)=1^2+1=2$ (using the second rule, since $1\ge1$). $f(3)=9+1=10$. Continuity at $x=1$: the left-hand limit is $\lim_{x\to1^-}2x=2$, matching $f(1)=2$. Continuous.

Practice 10

Write $|2x+3|$ as a piecewise function, identifying the break point.

Solution

Break point where $2x+3=0 \Rightarrow x=-\tfrac32$. $$|2x+3|=\begin{cases}2x+3 & x\ge-\tfrac32\\-(2x+3) & x<-\tfrac32\end{cases}$$

2 The Definition of the Derivative (First Principles)

Before any differentiation rules, the derivative is defined directly as a limit: the slope of the secant line through two points on a curve, as those points move infinitely close together. Every differentiation rule you'll use for the rest of the course is ultimately a shortcut derived from this one definition.

Key definition

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}.$$

This is the instantaneous rate of change of $f$ at $x$ — the slope of the tangent line, obtained as the limiting slope of secant lines as the second point approaches the first.

Worked example

Use first principles to find the derivative of $f(x)=x^2+3x$.

$f(x+h) = (x+h)^2+3(x+h) = x^2+2xh+h^2+3x+3h$.

$f(x+h)-f(x) = (x^2+2xh+h^2+3x+3h)-(x^2+3x) = 2xh+h^2+3h = h(2x+h+3)$.

$$\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} = 2x+h+3.$$

Taking $h\to0$: $f'(x) = 2x+3$.

Practice 11

Use first principles to find the derivative of $f(x)=5x-2$.

Solution

$f(x+h)-f(x)=5(x+h)-2-(5x-2)=5h$. $\dfrac{5h}{h}=5$. $f'(x)=5$ (constant, as expected for a line).

Practice 12

Use first principles to find the derivative of $f(x)=x^2$.

Solution

$f(x+h)-f(x)=(x+h)^2-x^2=2xh+h^2=h(2x+h)$. $\dfrac{h(2x+h)}{h}=2x+h$. As $h\to0$: $f'(x)=2x$.

Practice 13

Use first principles to find the derivative of $f(x)=x^3$.

Solution

$(x+h)^3-x^3 = 3x^2h+3xh^2+h^3=h(3x^2+3xh+h^2)$. Dividing by $h$ and letting $h\to0$: $f'(x)=3x^2$.

Practice 14

Use first principles to find the derivative of $f(x)=\dfrac1x$ (for $x\ne0$).

Solution

$f(x+h)-f(x)=\dfrac{1}{x+h}-\dfrac1x = \dfrac{x-(x+h)}{x(x+h)}=\dfrac{-h}{x(x+h)}$. Dividing by $h$: $\dfrac{-1}{x(x+h)}$. As $h\to0$: $f'(x)=-\dfrac{1}{x^2}$.

Practice 15

Use first principles to find the derivative of $f(x)=\sqrt{x}$ (for $x>0$), rationalising the numerator to evaluate the limit.

Solution

$\dfrac{\sqrt{x+h}-\sqrt{x}}{h}\times\dfrac{\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}}{\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}} = \dfrac{(x+h)-x}{h(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x})}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}}$. As $h\to0$: $f'(x)=\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}$.
