Topic 1: Calculus Fundamentals

Indices, surds, partial fractions, completing square, sigma notation, logs and transformations.

Why this topic matters (simple view)

If DPEN141 was a building, this topic is the concrete slab. Weak students often struggle later not because derivatives are impossible, but because algebra manipulation is shaky. This section fixes that by slowing down each move.

Motivation: before you can "do calculus", you must be able to reshape expressions quickly and safely.

Intuition Ladder (build step by step)

  • Step 1: indices are just repeated multiplication rules written compactly.
  • Step 2: surds are roots; rationalising is just rewriting the same number in cleaner form.
  • Step 3: partial fractions break a hard fraction into easy fractions for integration.
  • Step 4: completing the square turns "messy parabola" into "clear shape and location".
  • Step 5: sigma notation is a smart shortcut for long sums you cannot write manually.

Lesson Flow

  • Algebra fluency (indices, surds) powers every later calculus step.
  • Partial fractions and completing square are integration preparation tools.
  • Sigma notation builds summation fluency used in series and matrix operations.
  • Log laws and graph transforms connect algebra to functions and calculus behavior.
$$a^m a^n = a^{m+n},\quad \frac{a^m}{a^n}=a^{m-n},\quad (a^m)^n=a^{mn},\quad a^{-n}=\frac1{a^n}$$
$$\sum_{k=1}^n k=\frac{n(n+1)}2,\qquad \sum_{k=1}^n k^2=\frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}6$$
$$\log(AB)=\log A+\log B,\quad \log\left(\frac AB\right)=\log A-\log B,\quad \log(A^n)=n\log A$$
Visual Intuition: Completing the Square Moves a Parabola
Blue: \(y=x^2\). Orange: transformed \(y=3(x-2)^2-5\). Same basic shape family, new location and stretch.
Solved Example 1

Simplify \((8x^6)^{2/3}\).

\(8^{2/3}=4,\ x^{6\cdot2/3}=x^4\Rightarrow \boxed{4x^4}\).

Solved Example 2

Rationalise \( \frac{4}{3-\sqrt{5}} \).

Multiply by conjugate: \(\frac{4(3+\sqrt5)}{9-5}=3+\sqrt5\).

Solved Example 3

Decompose \(\frac{2x+3}{(x-1)(x+2)}\).

\(\frac{A}{x-1}+\frac{B}{x+2}\Rightarrow A=\frac53,\ B=\frac13\).

Solved Example 4

Complete square for \(3x^2-12x+7\).

\(3(x^2-4x)+7=3[(x-2)^2-4]+7=3(x-2)^2-5\).

Walkthrough Example (beginner pace)

Question: Solve \(\log_3(x)+\log_3(x-2)=2\).

Think first: logarithms need positive inputs, so \(x>0\) and \(x-2>0\Rightarrow x>2\). Keep this domain condition before solving.

Combine logs: \(\log_3(x(x-2))=2\Rightarrow x(x-2)=3^2=9\).

So \(x^2-2x-9=0\Rightarrow x=1\pm\sqrt{10}\). Only \(1+\sqrt{10}\) satisfies \(x>2\).

Lesson: algebra may give two roots, but domain keeps only physically/legally valid ones.

Insight: if you can convert expressions into cleaner algebraic forms quickly, differentiation and integration become pattern matching instead of brute-force manipulation.

Practice Set (6 problems)

  1. Simplify \( \left(27x^9\right)^{1/3} \).
    Show answer
    \(27^{1/3}=3,\ x^{9/3}=x^3\Rightarrow \boxed{3x^3}\).
  2. Rationalise \( \frac{5}{2+\sqrt3} \).
    Show answer
    \(\frac{5(2-\sqrt3)}{4-3}= \boxed{10-5\sqrt3}\).
  3. Decompose \( \frac{7x+1}{(x-2)(x+1)} \).
    Show answer
    \(\frac{A}{x-2}+\frac{B}{x+1}\Rightarrow A=5,\ B=2.\)
  4. Complete square: \(2x^2+8x+1\).
    Show answer
    \(2(x^2+4x)+1=2[(x+2)^2-4]+1= \boxed{2(x+2)^2-7}\).
  5. Evaluate \( \sum_{k=1}^{8} (2k-1) \).
    Show answer
    \(2\sum k-\sum 1=2(36)-8= \boxed{64}\).
  6. Solve \( \log_3(x)+\log_3(x-2)=2 \).
    Show answer
    \(\log_3(x(x-2))=2\Rightarrow x^2-2x=9\Rightarrow x^2-2x-9=0\). Valid root \(x=1+\sqrt{10}\) (other root invalid for domain).

Topic 2: Differentiation

Hyperbolic functions, inverse trig/hyperbolic derivatives, L'Hopital, logarithmic differentiation, parametric and related rates.

Why this topic matters

Differentiation answers one practical engineering question: "how fast is this changing right now?" In circuits, motion, signal slopes, and optimization, this is central.

Weak students often memorize derivative formulas without knowing when to use each one. The goal here is method selection, not formula dumping.

Method selection map

  • Looks like \(\arctan(f(x))\), \(\arcsin(f(x))\), \(\sinh(f(x))\)? -> derivative rule + chain rule.
  • Limit gives \(0/0\) or \(\infty/\infty\)? -> L'Hopital is allowed.
  • Variable in exponent and base (like \(x^x\))? -> logarithmic differentiation.
  • x and y both in parameter \(t\)? -> use \(\frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}\), not standard \(dy/dx\) directly.
$$\sinh x=\frac{e^x-e^{-x}}2,\quad \cosh x=\frac{e^x+e^{-x}}2,\quad \cosh^2x-\sinh^2x=1$$
$$\frac{d}{dx}\arctan x=\frac{1}{1+x^2},\quad \frac{d}{dx}\arcsin x=\frac1{\sqrt{1-x^2}}$$
$$\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}\quad \text{(parametric)}$$
Visual Intuition: Slope as "instantaneous tilt"
x-position
Curve is \(f(x)=x^2\). Moving slider changes tangent location. You can see "average slope" become "instant slope".
Solved Example 1

\(\frac{d}{dx}\arctan(3x^2)=\frac{6x}{1+9x^4}\).

Solved Example 2

\(\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\sin 3x}{x}\xrightarrow{\text{L'Hopital}} \lim_{x\to0}3\cos3x=3\).

Solved Example 3

Log-diff of \(y=x^x\): \(\ln y=x\ln x\Rightarrow y'=x^x(\ln x+1)\).

Solved Example 4

For \(x=t^2+1,\ y=t^3-3t\): \(\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{3t^2-3}{2t}\).

Walkthrough Example (related rates)

A circle grows with \(dr/dt=0.3\) m/s. At \(r=4\), find \(dA/dt\) where \(A=\pi r^2\).

Step 1: Write relationship first: \(A=\pi r^2\).

Step 2: Differentiate with respect to time: \(\frac{dA}{dt}=2\pi r\frac{dr}{dt}\).

Step 3: Only now substitute numbers: \(2\pi(4)(0.3)=2.4\pi\).

Key habit: differentiate symbolically first, substitute later.

Recall: L'Hopital works only for \(0/0\) or \(\infty/\infty\). Convert other indeterminate forms first.

Practice Set (6 problems)

  1. Differentiate \( \sinh(4x^2) \).
    Show answer
    \(\frac{d}{dx}\sinh(4x^2)=8x\cosh(4x^2)\).
  2. Differentiate \( \arcsin(2x-1) \).
    Show answer
    \(\frac{2}{\sqrt{1-(2x-1)^2}}\).
  3. Evaluate \( \lim_{x\to0}\frac{e^{2x}-1}{x} \).
    Show answer
    L'Hopital: \(\lim \frac{2e^{2x}}1=2\).
  4. Use log differentiation: \(y=(x^2+1)^x\).
    Show answer
    \(\ln y=x\ln(x^2+1)\Rightarrow \frac{y'}y=\ln(x^2+1)+\frac{2x^2}{x^2+1}\). So \(y'=(x^2+1)^x\left[\ln(x^2+1)+\frac{2x^2}{x^2+1}\right]\).
  5. For \(x=\cos t,\ y=\sin t\), find \(dy/dx\).
    Show answer
    \(\frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}=\frac{\cos t}{-\sin t}=-\cot t\).
  6. A circle radius grows at \(dr/dt=0.3\) m/s when \(r=4\). Find \(dA/dt\), \(A=\pi r^2\).
    Show answer
    \(\frac{dA}{dt}=2\pi r\frac{dr}{dt}=2\pi(4)(0.3)=2.4\pi\ \text{m}^2/\text{s}\).

Topic 3: Polar Coordinates & Integration

Polar conversion, area in polar form, integration by parts, trig substitution, improper integrals.

Why this topic matters

Many engineering shapes are circular, rotational, or radial. Cartesian \((x,y)\) coordinates can be awkward there. Polar coordinates describe such shapes more naturally using distance + angle.

Integration techniques then become your toolbox: each method is a different screwdriver for a different screw type.

Integration method chooser (simple)

  • Product of two unlike functions? -> try integration by parts.
  • Square roots like \(\sqrt{a^2-x^2}\), \(\sqrt{a^2+x^2}\), \(\sqrt{x^2-a^2}\)? -> trig substitution.
  • Infinite limit or vertical blow-up? -> improper integral with limits.
  • Polar area? -> \(\frac12\int r^2\,d\theta\), not \(\int y\,dx\).

What we investigate in this topic (exam + engineering lens)

  • Representation choice: is the geometry radial (polar) or rectangular (Cartesian)?
  • Area/accumulation: can we set up the correct integral with correct bounds?
  • Method selection: parts vs substitution vs improper-limit handling.
  • Physical meaning: radial area, flux-like accumulations, and decays over time/distance.
$$x=r\cos\theta,\quad y=r\sin\theta,\quad r^2=x^2+y^2$$
$$A=\frac12\int_{\theta_1}^{\theta_2}r^2\,d\theta$$
$$\int u\,dv=uv-\int v\,du$$

Why polar area uses \(\frac12 r^2 d\theta\) (derivation intuition)

Take a tiny angle slice \(d\theta\). That slice is approximately a thin sector with radius \(r\).

Sector area formula is \(\frac12 r^2\theta\), so tiny area is \(dA=\frac12 r^2 d\theta\).

Add all tiny sectors from \(\theta_1\) to \(\theta_2\):

$$A=\int_{\theta_1}^{\theta_2} dA=\frac12\int_{\theta_1}^{\theta_2} r(\theta)^2\,d\theta$$

This is why the polar-area formula is not memorized magic; it is accumulated tiny sectors.

Solved Example 1

Convert \((-1,\sqrt3)\) to polar: \(r=2\), \(\theta=2\pi/3\).

Solved Example 2

One petal area of \(r=2\cos(3\theta)\): \(A=\pi/3\).

Solved Example 3

\(\int xe^{2x}\,dx=\frac{e^{2x}(2x-1)}4+C\) via parts.

Solved Example 4

\(\int_0^\infty xe^{-x}dx=1\) (convergent improper integral).

Solved Example 5 (method choice)

Evaluate \(\int x\cos x\,dx\).

Product of algebraic and trig functions -> integration by parts.

Let \(u=x,\ dv=\cos x\,dx\Rightarrow du=dx,\ v=\sin x\).

\(\int x\cos x\,dx=x\sin x-\int\sin x\,dx=x\sin x+\cos x+C\).

Solved Example 6 (when trig substitution appears)

Evaluate \(\int \frac{dx}{\sqrt{9-x^2}}\).

Use \(x=3\sin\theta\), so \(dx=3\cos\theta\,d\theta\), \(\sqrt{9-x^2}=3\cos\theta\).

Integral becomes \(\int d\theta=\theta+C=\arcsin(x/3)+C\).

Walkthrough Example (polar conversion)

Convert \((x,y)=(-\sqrt3,1)\) to polar with \(0\le\theta<2\pi\).

Distance first: \(r=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}=\sqrt{3+1}=2\).

Angle idea: \(\tan\theta=y/x=-1/\sqrt3\). Reference angle is \(\pi/6\). Since \((x,y)\) is in Quadrant II, \(\theta=\pi-\pi/6=5\pi/6\).

Final: \((r,\theta)=(2,5\pi/6)\).

Connection: Polar-area and trig-sub both rely on geometry identities; visualising triangles/circles often simplifies integral setup faster than algebra alone.

Practice Set (6 problems)

  1. Convert Cartesian point \((-\sqrt3,1)\) to polar with \(0\le\theta<2\pi\).
    Show answer
    \(r=2,\ \theta=5\pi/6\).
  2. Find area enclosed by \(r=1+\cos\theta\) over \(0\to2\pi\).
    Show answer
    \(\frac12\int_0^{2\pi}(1+\cos\theta)^2d\theta=\frac{3\pi}{2}\).
  3. Evaluate \(\int x\ln x\,dx\).
    Show answer
    Use parts: \(\frac{x^2}{2}\ln x-\frac{x^2}{4}+C\).
  4. Evaluate \(\int \sqrt{4-x^2}\,dx\).
    Show answer
    \(\frac{x}{2}\sqrt{4-x^2}+2\arcsin\left(\frac x2\right)+C\).
  5. Determine convergence of \(\int_1^\infty \frac{1}{x^{3/2}}dx\).
    Show answer
    Converges (\(p=3/2>1\)); value \(=2\).
  6. Evaluate \(\int_0^\infty e^{-2x}dx\).
    Show answer
    \(\left[-\frac{1}{2}e^{-2x}\right]_0^\infty=\frac12\).

Topic 4: Matrices & Determinants

Matrix arithmetic, elimination/system types, determinant/inverse, eigenvalues/eigenvectors.

Why this topic matters

Matrices are engineering language for many variables at once: circuits with many nodes, structural systems, state-space control, machine learning models. If calculus answers "how change happens", matrices answer "how many equations interact".

Intuition map

  • Matrix multiplication = "row meets column" interaction.
  • Gaussian elimination = systematic cleaning of equations.
  • Determinant tells if transformation crushes space (det=0 means collapsed, no inverse).
  • Eigenvectors are "special directions" not rotated by the matrix.

Why determinant and transpose matter (not just definitions)

  • Determinant: tells if a system is uniquely solvable and if a transformation collapses dimension.
  • Determinant sign: tells orientation flip (+ no flip, - mirror flip).
  • Transpose: swaps row-view and column-view of data; critical for projections, least squares, covariance, and symmetry checks.
  • Engineering use: control/state models, sensor calibration, regression fits, network equations.
$$\det\begin{bmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{bmatrix}=ad-bc,\qquad A^{-1}\ \text{exists iff}\ \det(A)\neq0$$
$$A^T_{ij}=A_{ji},\qquad (AB)^T=B^TA^T,\qquad (A^T)^T=A$$

What we investigate with matrices in practice

  • System type: one solution / no solution / infinitely many?
  • Sensitivity: determinant near zero means unstable/ill-conditioned behavior.
  • Geometry: scaling, shear, rotation, reflection from matrix action.
  • Data structure: transpose helps align equations and data dimensions correctly.

Solved Example 3

Solved Example 4

For \(A=\begin{bmatrix}3&1\\0&2\end{bmatrix}\), eigenvalues are \(3,2\), eigenvectors \([1,0]^T\), \([-1,1]^T\).

Solved Example 5 (why determinant = solvability test)

System matrix \(A=\begin{bmatrix}2&4\\1&2\end{bmatrix}\) has \(\det(A)=0\).

Rows are dependent (\(R_1=2R_2\)), so equations cannot produce a unique intersection point.

This is why \(\det(A)=0\Rightarrow\) no inverse and no unique solution.

Solved Example 6 (transpose in action)

\(A=\begin{bmatrix}1&2&3\\4&5&6\end{bmatrix}\Rightarrow A^T=\begin{bmatrix}1&4\\2&5\\3&6\end{bmatrix}\).

Interpretation: rows-as-observations become columns-as-features. This is exactly what we need in many data-fitting formulas.

Solved Example 7 (quick least-squares connection)

Overdetermined model \(Ax\approx b\) cannot be solved exactly. We solve normal equations:

$$A^TAx=A^Tb$$

Transpose appears because it converts residual minimization into a square solvable system.

Walkthrough Example (solution type check)

System: \(x+y=3,\ 2x+2y=6\).

Second equation is exactly \(2\times\) first. So there is no new information.

Geometrically, both equations are the same line, so infinitely many intersection points.

Set \(y=t\), then \(x=3-t\): whole family of solutions.

Practice Set (6 problems)

  1. Compute \(AB\): \(A=\begin{bmatrix}2&0\\1&3\end{bmatrix},\ B=\begin{bmatrix}1&4\\2&5\end{bmatrix}\).
    Show answer
    \(AB=\begin{bmatrix}2&8\\7&19\end{bmatrix}\).
  2. Solve: \(x+y=3,\ 2x+2y=6\).
    Show answer
    Dependent equations \(\Rightarrow\) infinitely many solutions \(x=3-y\).
  3. Find \(\det\begin{bmatrix}4&1\\2&3\end{bmatrix}\).
    Show answer
    \(4\cdot3-1\cdot2=10\).
  4. Check invertibility: \(A=\begin{bmatrix}2&4\\1&2\end{bmatrix}\).
    Show answer
    \(\det A=4-4=0\Rightarrow\) not invertible.
  5. Find eigenvalues of \(A=\begin{bmatrix}1&0\\0&-2\end{bmatrix}\).
    Show answer
    Diagonal entries: \(\lambda_1=1,\ \lambda_2=-2\).
  6. If a row swap occurs during determinant evaluation, how does determinant change?
    Show answer
    Determinant changes sign (multiplied by \(-1\)).

Topic 5: Vector Geometry (3D)

Magnitude/unit vectors, dot/cross products, lines in 3D, planes and distances.

Why this topic matters

Engineers work in 3D: forces in space, robot paths, drone motion, normals to surfaces, and shortest distances. Vector geometry translates pictures into precise calculations.

Mental model for weak learners

  • Vector magnitude: "how long".
  • Dot product: "how aligned".
  • Cross product: "which perpendicular direction + what area".
  • Line: starting point + travel direction.
  • Plane: one point + normal vector.
$$|\mathbf v|=\sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2},\quad \mathbf a\cdot\mathbf b=|\mathbf a||\mathbf b|\cos\theta,\quad \text{proj}_{\mathbf a}\mathbf b=\frac{\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf b}{|\mathbf a|^2}\mathbf a$$
$$\mathbf a\times\mathbf b= \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf i&\mathbf j&\mathbf k\\ a_1&a_2&a_3\\ b_1&b_2&b_3 \end{vmatrix},\quad |\mathbf a\times\mathbf b|=|\mathbf a||\mathbf b|\sin\theta$$

Why dot product? Why cross product?

  • Dot product: measures alignment. Used for work \(W=\mathbf F\cdot\mathbf s\), projections, angle checks, orthogonality tests.
  • Cross product: creates perpendicular direction and area magnitude. Used for torque \(\boldsymbol\tau=\mathbf r\times\mathbf F\), surface normals, rotational direction.
  • Rule of thumb: "How much along?" -> dot. "How much turning/perpendicular?" -> cross.

What we investigate with vectors

  • Are two directions close, opposite, or perpendicular?
  • What component of a force actually does useful work?
  • What normal vector defines a plane/surface orientation?
  • How to classify lines/planes as intersecting, parallel, or skew?

Solved Example 4

Solved Example 5 (dot product practical use)

Force \(\mathbf F=(12,5,0)\) N moves object by \(\mathbf s=(3,0,0)\) m.

Work \(W=\mathbf F\cdot\mathbf s=36\) J. Only x-component contributes because motion is along x.

This is why dot product filters "effective component".

Solved Example 6 (cross product practical use)

\(\mathbf r=(0.2,0,0)\) m, \(\mathbf F=(0,50,0)\) N.

\(\boldsymbol\tau=\mathbf r\times\mathbf F=(0,0,10)\) N·m.

Direction is out of page (right-hand rule), magnitude is rotational strength.

Solved Example 7 (plane normal from cross product)

Given \(P(0,0,0),Q(1,0,0),R(0,2,0)\), vectors in plane are \(\overrightarrow{PQ}=(1,0,0)\), \(\overrightarrow{PR}=(0,2,0)\).

Normal \(\mathbf n=\overrightarrow{PQ}\times\overrightarrow{PR}=(0,0,2)\sim(0,0,1)\), so plane is \(z=0\).

Walkthrough Example (distance to plane)

Find distance from \(P(1,1,1)\) to plane \(2x-2y+z=4\).

Template: \(d=\frac{|ax_0+by_0+cz_0-d|}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2}}\).

Substitute \(a=2,b=-2,c=1,d=4\): numerator \(=|2-2+1-4|=3\), denominator \(=\sqrt{9}=3\).

Distance \(=1\). Keep absolute value; distance cannot be negative.

    Yes; second is \(2\times\) first.

Extra Topics (High-Value Extensions)

Added for stronger exam readiness and bridging into 2nd-year engineering math subjects.

How to use these extras

Do not start here if core topics feel weak. First finish Topics 1-5. Then come back to these extras to build confidence for harder university subjects. Think of them as "next-level connectors".

Extra Topic A: Taylor & Maclaurin Approximations

Used in numerical methods, control, and engineering modeling. Big idea: complicated functions can be approximated by simple polynomials near a point.

$$e^x\approx 1+x+\frac{x^2}{2!}+\frac{x^3}{3!},\quad \sin x\approx x-\frac{x^3}{3!}$$

Extra Topic B: Complex Numbers in Polar Form

Important for AC phasors and signals. Multiplication becomes easier in polar form because magnitudes multiply and angles add.

$$z=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)=re^{i\theta},\quad r=\sqrt{a^2+b^2},\ \theta=\arg(z)$$

Extra Topic C: First-Order Differential Equations (Separable)

Builds directly on growth/decay and appears in circuits and mechanics. It is one of the most physically meaningful equation types in engineering.

$$\frac{dy}{dt}=ky\Rightarrow y=Ce^{kt}$$
