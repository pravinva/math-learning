0 Why polar coordinates?

Cartesian coordinates, $(x,y)$, describe a point by how far to walk right and how far to walk up. That is a natural fit for grids and rectangles, but it is a clumsy fit for anything built around a centre and a sweeping angle, a satellite orbiting the earth, a radar beam sweeping the sky, a robotic arm reaching out from a fixed joint, or the rotating phasors used to describe AC circuits.

Polar coordinates describe a point differently: instead of "how far right, how far up," you specify "how far out, and in which direction," a distance $r$ from a fixed centre point, and an angle $\theta$ measured from a fixed reference direction. For anything with circular or radial symmetry, a shape that would need a genuinely awkward equation in $x,y$ often becomes almost trivial in $r,\theta$: a circle centred at the origin, which is $x^2+y^2=a^2$ in Cartesian form, is simply $r=a$ in polar form.

This lesson builds the whole toolkit: plotting a point given $(r,\theta)$, converting freely between the two coordinate systems (both for individual points and for whole equations), recognising the handful of curve shapes that show up constantly (circles, cardioids, roses, spirals), and, since it is directly on MATH141's own formula sheet, the polar form of a complex number, which turns out to use exactly the same conversion formulas as an ordinary point.

1 The polar coordinate system

A point in the polar system is written $(r,\theta)$. The fixed centre point is called the pole (it plays the role the origin plays in Cartesian coordinates), and the fixed reference direction, usually drawn pointing right, is called the polar axis (it plays the role of the positive $x$-axis).

$r$ is the distance from the pole to the point. $\theta$ is the angle, measured counter-clockwise from the polar axis, that you rotate through before walking outward a distance $r$. Angles can be given in degrees or radians; MATH141 mostly works in radians, so that is the default here unless a question states degrees explicitly.

Key idea: polar coordinates are not unique

Unlike Cartesian coordinates, where every point has exactly one $(x,y)$ pair, a single point has infinitely many valid polar representations:

$$(r,\theta) = (r,\ \theta+2k\pi) \quad \text{for any integer } k$$

(a full extra revolution lands you back on the same point), and also

$$(r,\theta) = (-r,\ \theta+\pi)$$

(walking a negative distance $r$ in direction $\theta$ is the same as walking the positive distance $|r|$ in the opposite direction, $\theta+\pi$). The pole itself is the special case $(0,\theta)$ for any angle $\theta$ at all, since a distance of $0$ never actually goes anywhere.

Worked example

Give three different polar representations of the point $\left(2,\dfrac{\pi}3\right)$.

Add a full revolution: $\left(2,\ \dfrac{\pi}3+2\pi\right) = \left(2,\ \dfrac{7\pi}3\right)$.

Subtract a full revolution: $\left(2,\ \dfrac{\pi}3-2\pi\right) = \left(2,\ -\dfrac{5\pi}3\right)$.

Flip the sign of $r$ and add $\pi$ to the angle: $\left(-2,\ \dfrac{\pi}3+\pi\right) = \left(-2,\ \dfrac{4\pi}3\right)$.

All four pairs, the original plus these three, land on exactly the same physical point.

Practice 1

Give another representation of $\left(5,\dfrac{\pi}6\right)$ using $r>0$ and a different angle.

Solution

Add a full revolution: $\left(5,\ \dfrac{\pi}6+2\pi\right) = \left(5,\ \dfrac{13\pi}6\right)$.

Practice 2

Give a representation of $\left(5,\dfrac{\pi}6\right)$ using a negative value of $r$.

Solution

$\left(-5,\ \dfrac{\pi}6+\pi\right) = \left(-5,\ \dfrac{7\pi}6\right)$.

Practice 3

Is $\left(3,\dfrac{\pi}2\right)$ the same point as $\left(3,\dfrac{5\pi}2\right)$? Justify.

Solution

Yes. $\dfrac{5\pi}2 - \dfrac{\pi}2 = 2\pi$, exactly one full revolution, so both angles point in the same direction with the same distance $r=3$.

Practice 4

Is $\left(3,\dfrac{\pi}2\right)$ the same point as $\left(-3,\dfrac{3\pi}2\right)$? Justify.

Solution

Yes. Using $(r,\theta)=(-r,\theta+\pi)$: $\left(3,\dfrac{\pi}2\right) = \left(-3,\ \dfrac{\pi}2+\pi\right) = \left(-3,\dfrac{3\pi}2\right)$, exactly the pair given.

Practice 5

What are all the valid polar representations of the pole (the origin)?

Solution

$(0,\theta)$ for any angle $\theta$ at all. Since $r=0$, the point never actually moves away from the pole no matter what direction you rotate through first, so every angle describes the identical point.

2 Converting points: Cartesian → polar

Given a point as $(x,y)$, finding $r$ is just the distance formula from the origin: Pythagoras' theorem. Finding $\theta$ needs a little more care, since $\tan\theta=y/x$ alone only ever returns an angle between $-90^\circ$ and $90^\circ$, it has no way to tell you whether the point was actually in the left half of the plane. You have to look at the signs of $x$ and $y$ to place $\theta$ in the correct quadrant.

Key idea

$$r = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}$$

For $\theta$, first find the reference angle $\alpha=\tan^{-1}\left|\dfrac{y}{x}\right|$ (always taken as positive, between $0$ and $\dfrac{\pi}2$), then place it using the quadrant of $(x,y)$:

Quadrant I ($x>0,y>0$): $\theta=\alpha$.   Quadrant II ($x<0,y>0$): $\theta=\pi-\alpha$.

Quadrant III ($x<0,y<0$): $\theta=-(\pi-\alpha)$.   Quadrant IV ($x>0,y<0$): $\theta=-\alpha$.

On an axis ($x=0$ or $y=0$), skip the formula and read the angle straight off: positive $x$-axis is $\theta=0$, positive $y$-axis is $\theta=\dfrac{\pi}2$, negative $x$-axis is $\theta=\pi$, negative $y$-axis is $\theta=-\dfrac{\pi}2$.

Worked example

Convert $(3,3)$ and $(-1,\sqrt3)$ to polar form.

$(3,3)$: $r=\sqrt{9+9}=\sqrt{18}=3\sqrt2$. Quadrant I, reference angle $\alpha=\tan^{-1}(1)=\dfrac{\pi}4$, so $\theta=\dfrac{\pi}4$.

$(-1,\sqrt3)$: $r=\sqrt{1+3}=2$. Quadrant II ($x<0,y>0$), reference angle $\alpha=\tan^{-1}(\sqrt3)=\dfrac{\pi}3$, so $\theta=\pi-\dfrac{\pi}3=\dfrac{2\pi}3$.

Practice 6

Convert $(5,5)$ to polar form.

Solution

$r=\sqrt{25+25}=5\sqrt2$. Quadrant I: $\theta=\tan^{-1}(1)=\dfrac{\pi}4$. $\ \left(5\sqrt2,\dfrac{\pi}4\right)$.

Practice 7

Convert $(-2,2)$ to polar form.

Solution

$r=\sqrt{4+4}=2\sqrt2$. Quadrant II: reference angle $\tan^{-1}(1)=\dfrac{\pi}4$, so $\theta=\pi-\dfrac{\pi}4=\dfrac{3\pi}4$. $\ \left(2\sqrt2,\dfrac{3\pi}4\right)$.

Practice 8

Convert $(-3,-3)$ to polar form.

Solution

$r=\sqrt{9+9}=3\sqrt2$. Quadrant III: reference angle $\tan^{-1}(1)=\dfrac{\pi}4$, so $\theta=-\left(\pi-\dfrac{\pi}4\right)=-\dfrac{3\pi}4$. $\ \left(3\sqrt2,-\dfrac{3\pi}4\right)$ (equivalently $\dfrac{5\pi}4$).

Practice 9

Convert $(1,-\sqrt3)$ to polar form.

Solution

$r=\sqrt{1+3}=2$. Quadrant IV: reference angle $\tan^{-1}(\sqrt3)=\dfrac{\pi}3$, so $\theta=-\dfrac{\pi}3$. $\ \left(2,-\dfrac{\pi}3\right)$.

Practice 10

Convert $(0,-5)$ to polar form.

Solution

On the negative $y$-axis: $r=5$, $\theta=-\dfrac{\pi}2$.

Practice 11

Convert $(-6,0)$ to polar form.

Solution

On the negative $x$-axis: $r=6$, $\theta=\pi$.

3 Converting points: polar → Cartesian

This direction is the easy one: no quadrant-checking needed at all, just ordinary right-triangle trigonometry, exactly the reasoning the rotation-matrix worked example used in the Transformations lesson.

Key idea

$$x = r\cos\theta, \qquad y = r\sin\theta.$$

These formulas work for any $r$, including negative values, no special cases needed: a negative $r$ simply lands the point on the opposite side of the pole, and the formulas handle that automatically through the sign of cosine and sine.

Worked example

Convert $\left(4,\dfrac{\pi}3\right)$ and $\left(2,-\dfrac{\pi}4\right)$ to Cartesian form.

$\left(4,\dfrac{\pi}3\right)$: $x=4\cos60^\circ=4\left(\dfrac12\right)=2$. $\ y=4\sin60^\circ=4\left(\dfrac{\sqrt3}2\right)=2\sqrt3$. Point: $(2,2\sqrt3)$.

$\left(2,-\dfrac{\pi}4\right)$: $x=2\cos(-45^\circ)=2\left(\dfrac{\sqrt2}2\right)=\sqrt2$. $\ y=2\sin(-45^\circ)=-\sqrt2$. Point: $(\sqrt2,-\sqrt2)$.

Practice 12

Convert $\left(6,\dfrac{\pi}6\right)$ to Cartesian form.

Solution

$x=6\cos30^\circ=6\left(\dfrac{\sqrt3}2\right)=3\sqrt3$. $\ y=6\sin30^\circ=6\left(\dfrac12\right)=3$. $\ (3\sqrt3,\ 3)$.

Practice 13

Convert $\left(4,\dfrac{2\pi}3\right)$ to Cartesian form.

Solution

$x=4\cos120^\circ=4\left(-\dfrac12\right)=-2$. $\ y=4\sin120^\circ=4\left(\dfrac{\sqrt3}2\right)=2\sqrt3$. $\ (-2,\ 2\sqrt3)$.

Practice 14

Convert $\left(5,-\dfrac{\pi}4\right)$ to Cartesian form.

Solution

$x=5\cos(-45^\circ)=\dfrac{5\sqrt2}2$. $\ y=5\sin(-45^\circ)=-\dfrac{5\sqrt2}2$. $\ \left(\dfrac{5\sqrt2}2,\ -\dfrac{5\sqrt2}2\right)$.

Practice 15

Convert $\left(-4,\dfrac{\pi}3\right)$ to Cartesian form (note the negative $r$).

Solution

$x=-4\cos60^\circ=-4\left(\dfrac12\right)=-2$. $\ y=-4\sin60^\circ=-4\left(\dfrac{\sqrt3}2\right)=-2\sqrt3$. $\ (-2,\ -2\sqrt3)$. The formulas needed no special handling for the negative $r$.

Practice 16

Convert $(3,180^\circ)$ to Cartesian form.

Solution

$x=3\cos180^\circ=-3$. $\ y=3\sin180^\circ=0$. $\ (-3,\ 0)$.

Practice 17

Convert $\left(2,\dfrac{5\pi}6\right)$ to Cartesian form.

Solution

$x=2\cos150^\circ=2\left(-\dfrac{\sqrt3}2\right)=-\sqrt3$. $\ y=2\sin150^\circ=2\left(\dfrac12\right)=1$. $\ (-\sqrt3,\ 1)$.

4 Converting equations

Converting a single point is Sections 2–3. Converting a whole equation, an entire curve's worth of points at once, uses the same two substitutions, $x=r\cos\theta$ and $y=r\sin\theta$, plus one more identity worth keeping on hand: $x^2+y^2=r^2$ (immediate from $r=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$).

Key idea

Cartesian → polar: substitute $x=r\cos\theta$, $y=r\sin\theta$, and $x^2+y^2=r^2$ wherever they fit, then simplify. A factor of $r$ can often be cancelled from both sides (this is legal everywhere except at the pole itself, which is usually still included on the curve anyway).

Polar → Cartesian: the same three substitutions run in reverse. If the polar equation has a lone $r$ (not $r^2$) multiplied by $\cos\theta$ or $\sin\theta$, multiply both sides by $r$ first, so that $r\cos\theta$ and $r\sin\theta$ appear and can become $x$ and $y$, and $r^2$ appears and can become $x^2+y^2$.

Worked example

Convert the Cartesian circle $x^2+y^2=4x$ to polar form, and convert the polar equation $r=6\sin\theta$ back to Cartesian form, identifying the curve.

$x^2+y^2=4x$: substitute $r^2$ for the left side and $r\cos\theta$ for $x$ on the right: $r^2 = 4r\cos\theta$. Cancel a factor of $r$: $r=4\cos\theta$.

$r=6\sin\theta$: multiply both sides by $r$ first, so a substitutable pattern appears: $r^2=6r\sin\theta$, i.e.\ $x^2+y^2=6y$.

Complete the square on $y$: $x^2+(y^2-6y+9)=9$, i.e.\ $x^2+(y-3)^2=9$. This is a circle of radius $3$ centred at $(0,3)$, which passes through the pole (check: $(0,0)$ satisfies $x^2+(y-3)^2=0+9=9$ ✓).

Practice 18

Convert $x^2+y^2=25$ to polar form.

Solution

$r^2=25 \Rightarrow r=5$ (a circle of radius $5$ centred at the pole).

Practice 19

Convert $y=\sqrt3\,x$ to polar form.

Solution

$r\sin\theta = \sqrt3\,r\cos\theta \Rightarrow \tan\theta=\sqrt3 \Rightarrow \theta=\dfrac{\pi}3$ (a straight line through the pole at a fixed angle — polar equations of lines through the pole are just "$\theta=$ constant").

Practice 20

Convert $x^2+y^2=6y$ to polar form.

Solution

$r^2 = 6r\sin\theta \Rightarrow r=6\sin\theta$.

Practice 21

Convert $x^2+y^2=-8x$ to polar form.

Solution

$r^2 = -8r\cos\theta \Rightarrow r=-8\cos\theta$.

Practice 22

Convert $r=4\cos\theta$ to Cartesian form, and identify the curve.

Solution

Multiply by $r$: $r^2=4r\cos\theta \Rightarrow x^2+y^2=4x$. Complete the square: $(x-2)^2+y^2=4$: a circle of radius $2$ centred at $(2,0)$.

Practice 23

Convert $r\cos\theta=5$ to Cartesian form, and identify the curve.

Solution

$r\cos\theta=x$, directly: $x=5$, a vertical line.

5 The standard polar curves

A handful of equation shapes come up constantly, and it is worth being able to recognise each family on sight, straight from the equation, without needing to plot a single point.

Key idea

Circles: $r=a$ (centred at the pole); $r=2a\cos\theta$ (through the pole, centred on the polar axis); $r=2a\sin\theta$ (through the pole, centred on the $\theta=\frac{\pi}2$ axis).

Lines: $\theta=k$ (a straight line through the pole at fixed angle $k$); $r\cos\theta=k$ (vertical line $x=k$); $r\sin\theta=k$ (horizontal line $y=k$).

Cardioids and limaçons: $r=a\pm b\cos\theta$ or $r=a\pm b\sin\theta$. If $a=b$: a cardioid (heart-shaped, passes through the pole). If $a>b$: a limaçon without an inner loop. If $alimaçon with an inner loop.

Roses: $r=a\cos(n\theta)$ or $r=a\sin(n\theta)$. If $n$ is odd, the rose has $n$ petals; if $n$ is even, it has $2n$ petals.

Spirals: $r=a\theta$ (an Archimedean spiral, $r$ grows steadily as $\theta$ increases without bound).

Worked example

Identify $r=2+5\cos\theta$ and $r=4\cos(3\theta)$.

$r=2+5\cos\theta$: here $a=2$, $b=5$, and $b>a$, so this is a limaçon with an inner loop.

$r=4\cos(3\theta)$: this is a rose with $n=3$, which is odd, so it has exactly 3 petals.

Practice 24

Identify $r=3+3\cos\theta$.

Solution

$a=b=3$: a cardioid.

Practice 25

Identify $r=6+2\sin\theta$.

Solution

$a=6$, $b=2$, $a>b$: a limaçon without an inner loop, symmetric about the $\theta=\frac{\pi}2$ axis (since it's written in terms of $\sin\theta$).

Practice 26

Identify $r=5\sin(2\theta)$.

Solution

$n=2$, even: a rose with $2n=4$ petals.

Practice 27

Identify $r=2\theta$ for $\theta\geq0$.

Solution

An Archimedean spiral: $r$ grows steadily and without bound as $\theta$ increases.

Practice 28

Identify $r=4\sin\theta$ (compare against the circle rules in the key idea).

Solution

Matches $r=2a\sin\theta$ with $2a=4 \Rightarrow a=2$: a circle of radius $2$, passing through the pole, centred on the $\theta=\frac{\pi}2$ axis.

Practice 29

For the cardioid $r=1+\cos\theta$, build a table of $r$ at $\theta=0,\ \frac{\pi}2,\ \pi,\ \frac{3\pi}2$, and state which point sits farthest from the pole and which one touches it.

Solution

$\theta=0$: $r=1+1=2$. $\ \theta=\frac{\pi}2$: $r=1+0=1$. $\ \theta=\pi$: $r=1+(-1)=0$. $\ \theta=\frac{3\pi}2$: $r=1+0=1$.

Farthest point: $r=2$ at $\theta=0$ (along the polar axis). The curve actually touches the pole at $\theta=\pi$ ($r=0$), which is exactly the cardioid's characteristic "dimple."

6 Symmetry and sketching strategy

Before plotting a table of points by hand, it is worth checking for symmetry first, it can cut the amount of plotting you need to do roughly in half, and it is a quick, purely algebraic check.

Key idea: three symmetry tests

Symmetric about the polar axis (the $x$-axis) if replacing $\theta\to-\theta$ leaves the equation unchanged.

Symmetric about the $\theta=\frac{\pi}2$ axis (the $y$-axis) if replacing $\theta\to\pi-\theta$ leaves the equation unchanged.

Symmetric about the pole if replacing $r\to-r$ (equivalently $\theta\to\theta+\pi$) leaves the equation unchanged.

These are sufficient conditions, not strictly necessary ones (a curve can sometimes be symmetric without passing one of these particular algebraic tests, because of the multiple-representation issue from Section 1), but when a test does pass, the symmetry is guaranteed.

Worked example

Test the cardioid $r=2+2\cos\theta$ for symmetry about the polar axis.

Replace $\theta$ with $-\theta$: $\cos(-\theta)=\cos\theta$ (cosine is an even function), so the equation becomes $r=2+2\cos\theta$, completely unchanged.

Symmetric about the polar axis, matching the shape you'd expect: a cardioid built from $\cos\theta$ points its "nose" along the polar axis, with the two halves mirroring each other above and below it.

Practice 30

Test $r=2\sin\theta$ for symmetry about the $\theta=\frac{\pi}2$ axis.

Solution

Replace $\theta\to\pi-\theta$: $\sin(\pi-\theta)=\sin\theta$, so the equation is unchanged. Symmetric about the $\theta=\frac{\pi}2$ axis.

Practice 31

Test $r^2=4\cos(2\theta)$ for symmetry about the pole.

Solution

Replace $r\to-r$: $(-r)^2=r^2$, so the left side is unchanged and the equation still holds. Symmetric about the pole.

Practice 32

Test the rose $r=4\cos(3\theta)$ for symmetry about the polar axis.

Solution

Replace $\theta\to-\theta$: $\cos(-3\theta)=\cos(3\theta)$, unchanged. Symmetric about the polar axis.

Practice 33

A curve is known to satisfy $r(\theta) = r(\pi-\theta)$ for every $\theta$. Which symmetry does this indicate, and why?

Solution

This is precisely the $\theta\to\pi-\theta$ substitution test stated directly: it indicates symmetry about the $\theta=\frac{\pi}2$ axis.

Practice 34

Using the symmetry test, explain why the limaçon $r=3+5\sin\theta$ is symmetric about the $\theta=\frac{\pi}2$ axis but there is no guarantee it is symmetric about the polar axis.

Solution

$\theta\to\pi-\theta$: $\sin(\pi-\theta)=\sin\theta$, unchanged, so the $\theta=\frac{\pi}2$-axis test passes. But $\theta\to-\theta$: $\sin(-\theta)=-\sin\theta$, giving $r=3-5\sin\theta$, a genuinely different equation, so the polar-axis test fails, there is no guarantee of that symmetry (and, geometrically, a sine-based limaçon's "nose" points along the $y$-axis, not the $x$-axis, so this is exactly what you'd expect).

7 Polar form of complex numbers

Every complex number $z=a+bj$ (engineering notation uses $j$ instead of $i$, to avoid clashing with current $i$ in circuit analysis) can be plotted as a point at $(a,b)$ on the complex plane, and that point has a polar form exactly like any other: a distance from the origin, called the modulus, and an angle, called the argument. The conversion formulas are identical to Section 2, just with the labels renamed.

Key idea

For $z=a+bj$: modulus $r=|z|=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$, argument $\theta=\arg z$ found by the exact same quadrant rules as Section 2 (with $a$ playing the role of $x$, $b$ playing the role of $y$).

Polar form: $z = r(\cos\theta+j\sin\theta)$. Euler's form (the same statement, written more compactly): $z=re^{j\theta}$.

Multiplication and division become simple in polar form: $|z_1z_2|=|z_1|\,|z_2|$ and $\arg(z_1z_2)=\arg z_1+\arg z_2$, moduli multiply, arguments add. This is the main reason engineers reach for polar form at all, it turns rotation-and-scaling problems (like AC phasors) into simple arithmetic.

Worked example

Convert $z=3+3j$ to polar form, and convert $z=4(\cos120^\circ+j\sin120^\circ)$ back to $a+bj$ form.

$z=3+3j$: $r=\sqrt{9+9}=3\sqrt2$. Quadrant I: $\theta=\tan^{-1}(1)=\dfrac{\pi}4$ ($45^\circ$).

$$z = 3\sqrt2(\cos45^\circ+j\sin45^\circ) = 3\sqrt2\,e^{j\pi/4}.$$

$z=4(\cos120^\circ+j\sin120^\circ)$: $a=4\cos120^\circ=4\left(-\dfrac12\right)=-2$. $\ b=4\sin120^\circ=4\left(\dfrac{\sqrt3}2\right)=2\sqrt3$.

$$z = -2+2\sqrt3\,j.$$

Practice 35

Convert $z=4+4j$ to polar form.

Solution

$r=\sqrt{16+16}=4\sqrt2$. Quadrant I: $\theta=45^\circ=\dfrac{\pi}4$.

$$z = 4\sqrt2(\cos45^\circ+j\sin45^\circ) = 4\sqrt2\,e^{j\pi/4}.$$

Practice 36

Convert $z=-3+3j$ to polar form.

Solution

$r=\sqrt{9+9}=3\sqrt2$. Quadrant II: reference angle $45^\circ$, so $\theta=180^\circ-45^\circ=135^\circ$.

$$z = 3\sqrt2(\cos135^\circ+j\sin135^\circ).$$

Practice 37

Convert $z=-2-2j$ to polar form.

Solution

$r=\sqrt{4+4}=2\sqrt2$. Quadrant III: reference angle $45^\circ$, so $\theta=-(180^\circ-45^\circ)=-135^\circ$.

$$z = 2\sqrt2(\cos(-135^\circ)+j\sin(-135^\circ)).$$

Practice 38

Convert $z=6(\cos60^\circ+j\sin60^\circ)$ to $a+bj$ form.

Solution

$a=6\cos60^\circ=6\left(\dfrac12\right)=3$. $\ b=6\sin60^\circ=6\left(\dfrac{\sqrt3}2\right)=3\sqrt3$.

$$z = 3+3\sqrt3\,j.$$

Practice 39

Given $z_1=2(\cos30^\circ+j\sin30^\circ)$ and $z_2=3(\cos40^\circ+j\sin40^\circ)$, find $z_1z_2$ in polar form.

Solution

Moduli multiply: $|z_1z_2| = 2\times3=6$. Arguments add: $\arg(z_1z_2) = 30^\circ+40^\circ=70^\circ$.

$$z_1z_2 = 6(\cos70^\circ+j\sin70^\circ).$$

Practice 40

Convert $z=5(\cos270^\circ+j\sin270^\circ)$ to $a+bj$ form, and simplify.

Solution

$a=5\cos270^\circ=5(0)=0$. $\ b=5\sin270^\circ=5(-1)=-5$.

$$z = 0-5j = -5j.$$

Where to go from here. That's the full 3-hour Polar Coordinates unit: plotting, converting points, converting equations, recognising the standard curves, symmetry, and the polar form of complex numbers. It stands alone, nothing here depends on the matrices material, and nothing later in MATH141 builds on it, so it can be revised independently at any point in the session. The one place it does connect outward is complex numbers: if you go on to any further work with phasors or AC analysis, the $r(\cos\theta+j\sin\theta)$ and $re^{j\theta}$ forms from Section 7 are exactly the tools that make that work manageable.
