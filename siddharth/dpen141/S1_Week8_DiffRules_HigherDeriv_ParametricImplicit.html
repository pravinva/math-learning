0 Power, Product, and Quotient Rules

First-principles differentiation is powerful but slow. These three rules let you differentiate almost any combination of standard functions directly, without ever returning to the limit definition.

Key rules

Power rule: $\dfrac{d}{dx}(x^n)=nx^{n-1}$. Product rule: $(uv)'=u'v+uv'$. Quotient rule: $\left(\dfrac{u}{v}\right)'=\dfrac{u'v-uv'}{v^2}$.

Worked example

Differentiate $f(x)=\dfrac{x^2+1}{2x-3}$.

Let $u=x^2+1,\ v=2x-3$. Then $u'=2x,\ v'=2$.

$$f'(x) = \frac{u'v-uv'}{v^2} = \frac{2x(2x-3)-(x^2+1)(2)}{(2x-3)^2}.$$

Expand the numerator: $4x^2-6x-2x^2-2 = 2x^2-6x-2$. So $f'(x)=\dfrac{2x^2-6x-2}{(2x-3)^2}$.

Practice 1

Differentiate $f(x)=5x^4-3x^2+7x-1$.

Solution

$f'(x)=20x^3-6x+7$.

Practice 2

Differentiate $f(x)=(3x+1)(x^2-2)$ using the product rule.

Solution

$u=3x+1,\ v=x^2-2$; $u'=3,\ v'=2x$. $f'(x)=3(x^2-2)+(3x+1)(2x)=3x^2-6+6x^2+2x=9x^2+2x-6$.

Practice 3

Differentiate $f(x)=\dfrac{3x-1}{x+2}$ using the quotient rule.

Solution

$u=3x-1,v=x+2$; $u'=3,v'=1$. $f'(x)=\dfrac{3(x+2)-(3x-1)(1)}{(x+2)^2}=\dfrac{3x+6-3x+1}{(x+2)^2}=\dfrac{7}{(x+2)^2}$.

Practice 4

Differentiate $f(x)=x^3\sqrt{x}$ by first simplifying using index laws, then applying the power rule.

Solution

$f(x)=x^3\cdot x^{1/2}=x^{7/2}$. $f'(x)=\dfrac72x^{5/2}$.

Practice 5

Differentiate $f(x)=\dfrac{x^2}{\sqrt{x}+1}$ using the quotient rule.

Solution

$u=x^2,\ v=\sqrt{x}+1$; $u'=2x$, $v'=\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}$. $f'(x)=\dfrac{2x(\sqrt{x}+1)-x^2\cdot\tfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}}{(\sqrt{x}+1)^2}$.

1 The Chain Rule

The chain rule handles composite functions: differentiate the "outer" function first (treating the inner function as a single block), then multiply by the derivative of the "inner" function. It's the single most-used differentiation rule in the entire course.

Key formula

$$\frac{d}{dx}\big[f(g(x))\big] = f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x).$$

In Leibniz notation, with $y=f(u)$ and $u=g(x)$: $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{dy}{du}\cdot\dfrac{du}{dx}$.

Worked example

Differentiate $y=(3x^2-1)^5$.

Outer function: $u^5$ (derivative $5u^4$); inner function: $u=3x^2-1$ (derivative $6x$).

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = 5(3x^2-1)^4\times6x = 30x(3x^2-1)^4.$$

Practice 6

Differentiate $y=(2x+5)^4$.

Solution

$y'=4(2x+5)^3\times2=8(2x+5)^3$.

Practice 7

Differentiate $y=\sqrt{x^2+9}$.

Solution

$y=(x^2+9)^{1/2}$. $y'=\dfrac12(x^2+9)^{-1/2}\times2x=\dfrac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+9}}$.

Practice 8

Differentiate $y=\dfrac{1}{(3x-2)^3}$.

Solution

$y=(3x-2)^{-3}$. $y'=-3(3x-2)^{-4}\times3=\dfrac{-9}{(3x-2)^4}$.

Practice 9

Differentiate $y=\sin(x^2)$ and $y=\sin^2(x)$, and explain the difference in method.

Solution

$\dfrac{d}{dx}\sin(x^2)=\cos(x^2)\times2x=2x\cos(x^2)$ (chain rule with outer $\sin u$, inner $u=x^2$). $\dfrac{d}{dx}\sin^2x=\dfrac{d}{dx}(\sin x)^2=2\sin x\times\cos x=\sin(2x)$ (chain rule with outer $u^2$, inner $u=\sin x$). Different inner/outer split gives genuinely different derivatives.

Practice 10

Differentiate $y=e^{3x^2-x}$.

Solution

$y'=e^{3x^2-x}\times(6x-1)=(6x-1)e^{3x^2-x}$.

Practice 11

Differentiate $y=\left(\dfrac{2x+1}{x-3}\right)^4$ using the chain rule combined with the quotient rule.

Solution

Outer: $u^4$, derivative $4u^3$. Inner $u=\dfrac{2x+1}{x-3}$, derivative (quotient rule): $\dfrac{2(x-3)-(2x+1)(1)}{(x-3)^2}=\dfrac{2x-6-2x-1}{(x-3)^2}=\dfrac{-7}{(x-3)^2}$. Combined: $y'=4\left(\dfrac{2x+1}{x-3}\right)^3\times\dfrac{-7}{(x-3)^2}=\dfrac{-28(2x+1)^3}{(x-3)^5}$.

2 Higher Derivatives

Differentiating a derivative gives the second derivative, and so on — each one measuring the rate of change of the previous one. Physically, if $x(t)$ is position, $x'(t)$ is velocity and $x''(t)$ is acceleration.

Notation

$f''(x)$, $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$, and $f^{(n)}(x)$ (the $n$th derivative) are all standard notations for repeated differentiation.

Worked example

Find the sixth derivative of $f(x)=\sin(2x)+x^5+x^2+3$.

Polynomial terms die out after enough derivatives: $x^5$ needs $5$ derivatives to reach a constant, then vanishes on the $6$th; $x^2$ vanishes after $2$ derivatives (definitely gone by the $6$th); the constant $3$ vanishes immediately. So only the $\sin(2x)$ term survives to the $6$th derivative.

Each derivative of $\sin(2x)$ multiplies by $2$ and cycles through $\sin\to\cos\to-\sin\to-\cos\to\sin\dots$ For the $6$th derivative: factor is $2^6=64$, and the cycle position after $6$ steps (period $4$) is the same as after $2$ steps: $\sin\to\cos\to-\sin$.

$$f^{(6)}(x) = -64\sin(2x).$$

Practice 12

Find $f''(x)$ for $f(x)=x^4-3x^3+2x$.

Solution

$f'(x)=4x^3-9x^2+2$. $f''(x)=12x^2-18x$.

Practice 13

Find the third derivative of $f(x)=\sin x$.

Solution

$f'=\cos x$, $f''=-\sin x$, $f'''=-\cos x$.

Practice 14

Find $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ for $y=e^{3x}$.

Solution

$y'=3e^{3x}$, $y''=9e^{3x}$.

Practice 15

A particle's position is $x(t)=4t^3-6t^2+2t$. Find its velocity and acceleration functions, and evaluate the acceleration at $t=1$.

Solution

$v(t)=x'(t)=12t^2-12t+2$. $a(t)=v'(t)=24t-12$. At $t=1$: $a(1)=24-12=12$.

Practice 16

Find the fourth derivative of $f(x)=\cos(3x)$.

Solution

$f'=-3\sin3x$, $f''=-9\cos3x$, $f'''=27\sin3x$, $f^{(4)}=81\cos3x$.

3 Parametric and Implicit Differentiation

Not every curve is given as $y=f(x)$. When $x$ and $y$ are both functions of a parameter $t$, or when $y$ is tangled up with $x$ in a single equation, you need different (but related) strategies to find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$.

Key formulas

Parametric: if $x=x(t),\ y=y(t)$, then $$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}.$$

Implicit: differentiate both sides of an equation with respect to $x$, treating $y$ as a function of $x$ (so every $\dfrac{d}{dx}(y^n)$ picks up a factor of $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ via the chain rule), then solve algebraically for $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$.

Worked example

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ for the parametric curve $x=t^2+\dfrac1t,\ y=t^2-\dfrac1t$.

$\dfrac{dx}{dt}=2t-\dfrac{1}{t^2}$, $\quad \dfrac{dy}{dt}=2t+\dfrac{1}{t^2}$.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2t+\tfrac{1}{t^2}}{2t-\tfrac{1}{t^2}} = \frac{2t^3+1}{2t^3-1}$$ (multiplying top and bottom by $t^2$).

Practice 17

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ for the parametric curve $x=3t^2,\ y=2t^3$.

Solution

$\dfrac{dx}{dt}=6t$, $\dfrac{dy}{dt}=6t^2$. $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{6t^2}{6t}=t$.

Practice 18

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ for the parametric curve $x=\cos t,\ y=\sin t$, and interpret the result geometrically at $t=\dfrac{\pi}{4}$.

Solution

$\dfrac{dx}{dt}=-\sin t$, $\dfrac{dy}{dt}=\cos t$. $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-\dfrac{\cos t}{\sin t}=-\cot t$. At $t=\tfrac{\pi}{4}$: slope $=-1$, matching the tangent to the unit circle at $\left(\tfrac{\sqrt2}{2},\tfrac{\sqrt2}{2}\right)$, which is indeed perpendicular to the radius there.

Practice 19

Use implicit differentiation to find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ for $x^2+y^2=25$.

Solution

$2x+2y\dfrac{dy}{dx}=0 \Rightarrow \dfrac{dy}{dx}=-\dfrac{x}{y}$.

Practice 20

Use implicit differentiation to find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ for $x^2y+y^3=10$.

Solution

Differentiate term by term: $\dfrac{d}{dx}(x^2y)=2xy+x^2\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ (product rule). $\dfrac{d}{dx}(y^3)=3y^2\dfrac{dy}{dx}$. So $2xy+x^2\dfrac{dy}{dx}+3y^2\dfrac{dy}{dx}=0 \Rightarrow \dfrac{dy}{dx}(x^2+3y^2)=-2xy \Rightarrow \dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{-2xy}{x^2+3y^2}$.

Practice 21

Find the slope of the graph $2\sin x\cos y = 1$ at the point $\left(\dfrac{\pi}{4},\dfrac{\pi}{4}\right)$.

Solution

Differentiate implicitly: $2\cos x\cos y + 2\sin x(-\sin y)\dfrac{dy}{dx}=0 \Rightarrow \dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{\cos x\cos y}{\sin x\sin y}$. At $x=y=\tfrac{\pi}{4}$: $\cos\tfrac{\pi}{4}=\sin\tfrac{\pi}{4}=\tfrac{\sqrt2}{2}$, so $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{(\tfrac{\sqrt2}{2})^2}{(\tfrac{\sqrt2}{2})^2}=1$.

Practice 22

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ for the parametric curve $x=t^2+\dfrac1t,\ y=t^2-\dfrac1t$ (as in the worked example) evaluated at $t=1$, and hence find the equation of the tangent line at that point.

Solution

At $t=1$: $x=1+1=2$, $y=1-1=0$. From the worked example, $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{2t^3+1}{2t^3-1}$; at $t=1$: $\dfrac{2+1}{2-1}=3$. Tangent line: $y-0=3(x-2) \Rightarrow y=3x-6$.
