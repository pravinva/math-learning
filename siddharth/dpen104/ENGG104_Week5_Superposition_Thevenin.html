Superposition & Thévenin's Theorem

Two of the most powerful circuit-simplification tools: Superposition handles circuits with multiple sources one at a time, and Thévenin's Theorem collapses an entire complicated network down to just two numbers.

1 Superposition Theorem

In a linear circuit with multiple sources, it's often easier to handle one source at a time than to solve everything at once.

Key idea

For each source in turn: "kill" every other independent source (voltage source → short circuit, current source → open circuit), find the desired current or voltage due to that one source alone, then algebraically add all the individual contributions together.

Worked example

Two sources, \(E_1=20\text{ V}\) (in series with \(R_1=4\ \Omega\)) and \(E_2=8\text{ V}\) (in series with \(R_2=2\ \Omega\)), both connect in parallel across a shared load \(R_3=6\ \Omega\). Find the current through \(R_3\).

Kill \(E_2\) (short it): \(E_1\) now drives \(R_1\) in series with \((R_2\|R_3)\). \(R_2\|R_3=(2)(6)/8=1.5\ \Omega\). Total \(=4+1.5=5.5\ \Omega\). \(I_{total}=20/5.5=3.636\text{ A}\) (through \(R_1\)).

This splits between \(R_2,R_3\) (current divider): \(I_{R3,\ due\ to\ E_1}=3.636\times\dfrac{2}{8}=0.909\text{ A}\).

Kill \(E_1\) (short it): \(E_2\) drives \(R_2\) in series with \((R_1\|R_3)\). \(R_1\|R_3=(4)(6)/10=2.4\ \Omega\). Total \(=2+2.4=4.4\ \Omega\). \(I_{total}=8/4.4=1.818\text{ A}\).

\(I_{R3,\ due\ to\ E_2}=1.818\times\dfrac{4}{10}=0.727\text{ A}\).

Sum: \(I_{R3}=0.909+0.727=1.636\text{ A}\).

Practice 1

State the two steps of superposition in your own words.

Solution

1. Analyse the circuit with only one source active at a time, replacing every other source with its "killed" equivalent, and find the desired current/voltage from that source alone. 2. Repeat for every other source, then add all the individual contributions together (respecting direction/sign) to get the true total.

Practice 2

When "killing" a voltage source, what do you replace it with, and why?

Solution

A short circuit (0 V). An ideal voltage source maintains a fixed voltage regardless of current, so "turning it off" means setting its voltage to zero, exactly what a short circuit represents.

Practice 3

When "killing" a current source, what do you replace it with, and why?

Solution

An open circuit. An ideal current source maintains a fixed current regardless of voltage, so "turning it off" means setting that current to zero, exactly what an open circuit (no path at all) represents.

Practice 4

A resistor carries 3 A due to source A alone and 2 A due to source B alone, both in the SAME direction. Find the total current.

Solution

\(3+2=5\text{ A}\).

Practice 5

If the two contributions from Practice 4 instead flowed in OPPOSITE directions, find the net current.

Solution

\(3-2=1\text{ A}\), in the direction of the larger contribution.

2 Thévenin's Theorem

Any linear two-terminal network, however complicated, behaves exactly like a single voltage source in series with a single resistor, as far as anything connected to those two terminals is concerned.

Key idea

\(V_{th}\): the open-circuit voltage at the two terminals, with the load removed. \(R_{th}\): the resistance seen looking back into the network from those terminals, with all independent sources killed.

Worked example

A source \(E=12\text{ V}\) connects through \(R_1=4\ \Omega\) to node A; from A, \(R_2=8\ \Omega\) goes to ground (node B). The load, removed for this analysis, would connect across A–B. Find \(V_{th}\) and \(R_{th}\).

With the load removed, this is just a voltage divider: \(V_{th}=E\times\dfrac{R_2}{R_1+R_2}=12\times\dfrac{8}{12}=8\text{ V}\).

Killing the source (shorting it) puts \(R_1\) and \(R_2\) in parallel as seen from A–B: \(R_{th}=R_1\|R_2=\dfrac{4(8)}{12}=2.667\ \Omega\).

Worked example 2

Using that Thévenin equivalent (\(V_{th}=8\text{ V}, R_{th}=2.667\ \Omega\)), a load \(R_L=5\ \Omega\) is now connected. Find the load current and voltage.

\(I_L=\dfrac{V_{th}}{R_{th}+R_L}=\dfrac{8}{2.667+5}=\dfrac{8}{7.667}=1.043\text{ A}\).

\(V_L=I_LR_L=1.043(5)=5.217\text{ V}\).

Practice 6

A source \(E=15\text{ V}\), \(R_1=3\ \Omega\), \(R_2=6\ \Omega\) (same voltage-divider topology, load removed at the \(R_2\) tap). Find \(V_{th}\) and \(R_{th}\).

Solution

\(V_{th}=15\times6/9=10\text{ V}\). \(R_{th}=R_1\|R_2=(3)(6)/9=2\ \Omega\).

Practice 7

For Practice 6, a load \(R_L=4\ \Omega\) is connected. Find the load current and voltage.

Solution

\(I_L=10/(2+4)=1.667\text{ A}\). \(V_L=1.667(4)=6.667\text{ V}\).

Practice 8

A network has \(E=20\text{ V}\), \(R_1=5\ \Omega\), \(R_2=10\ \Omega\) (same topology). Find \(V_{th}\) and \(R_{th}\).

Solution

\(V_{th}=20\times10/15=13.33\text{ V}\). \(R_{th}=(5)(10)/15=3.33\ \Omega\).

Practice 9

Explain, step by step, how to find \(R_{th}\) for a network of only resistors and independent sources.

Solution

"Kill" every independent source (voltage sources → short, current sources → open), then compute the equivalent resistance of the remaining resistor network as seen from the two open terminals, using ordinary series/parallel combination rules.

Practice 10

Explain why finding \(V_{th}\) specifically requires the load to be removed (open-circuit).

Solution

\(V_{th}\) is defined as the open-circuit voltage. With the load still connected, current would flow through it and change the terminal voltage, so it wouldn't capture the network's true, load-independent characteristic, removing the load isolates the pure source-and-network behaviour.

Practice 11

Once \(V_{th}\) and \(R_{th}\) are known, explain why they let you quickly find the current for ANY load resistor without re-analysing the whole original circuit each time.

Solution

The entire original network's behaviour at those two terminals is captured in just two numbers, so the load simply sees a simple series circuit: \(I_L=V_{th}/(R_{th}+R_L)\) works directly for any \(R_L\), without repeating the full original analysis every time the load changes.

Practice 12

A Thevenin network has Vth=16 V and Rth=4 ohm. For RL=8 ohm, find IL and VL.

Solution

IL=16/(4+8)=1.333 A. VL=IL*RL=10.667 V.

Practice 13

For Vth=20 V and Rth=5 ohm, find RL for maximum power transfer and Pmax.

Solution

RL=Rth=5 ohm. Pmax=Vth^2/(4Rth)=20.000 W.

Practice 14

Given Voc=12 V and Isc=2.00 A, determine Vth and Rth.

Solution

Vth=Voc=12 V. Rth=Voc/Isc=6.000 ohm.

Practice 15

Convert a 18 V source in series with 3 ohm to Norton equivalent.

Solution

IN=V/R=18/3=6.000 A and RN=3 ohm.

Practice 16

For Vth=24 V and Rth=6 ohm, compare IL for RL=3 ohm and RL=12 ohm.

Solution

IL1=24/(6+3)=2.667 A, IL2=24/(6+12)=1.333 A.

Practice 17

Method choice: explain when superposition is faster and when Thevenin is faster in exam workflows.

Solution

Superposition is fastest for one branch response with multiple independent sources; Thevenin is fastest when load values vary and repeated load calculations are needed.

Practice 18

A Thevenin network has Vth=18 V and Rth=5 ohm. For RL=9 ohm, find IL and VL.

Solution

IL=18/(5+9)=1.286 A. VL=IL*RL=11.571 V.

Practice 19

For Vth=22 V and Rth=6 ohm, find RL for maximum power transfer and Pmax.

Solution

RL=Rth=6 ohm. Pmax=Vth^2/(4Rth)=20.167 W.

Practice 20

Given Voc=14 V and Isc=2.50 A, determine Vth and Rth.

Solution

Vth=Voc=14 V. Rth=Voc/Isc=5.600 ohm.

Practice 21

Convert a 20 V source in series with 4 ohm to Norton equivalent.

Solution

IN=V/R=20/4=5.000 A and RN=4 ohm.

Practice 22

For Vth=26 V and Rth=7 ohm, compare IL for RL=3 ohm and RL=14 ohm.

Solution

IL1=26/(7+3)=2.600 A, IL2=26/(7+14)=1.238 A.
