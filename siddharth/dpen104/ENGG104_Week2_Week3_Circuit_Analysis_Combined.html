1 Ohm's Law

The relationship every other formula in this lesson builds on: voltage, current, and resistance are locked together.

Key idea

$$V=IR, \qquad I=\frac{V}{R}, \qquad R=\frac{V}{I}.$$

Worked example 1

A 12 V supply pushes current through a 3.3 kΩ resistor. Find the current.

\(I=\dfrac{V}{R}=\dfrac{12}{3300}=3.636\text{ mA}\).

Worked example 2

A resistor has 5 V across it and 2 mA flowing through it. Find its resistance.

\(R=\dfrac{V}{I}=\dfrac{5}{0.002}=2500\ \Omega=2.5\text{ k}\Omega\).

Practice 1

A 9 V battery is connected to a 1.2 kΩ resistor. Find the current.

Solution

\(I=9/1200=7.5\text{ mA}\).

Practice 2

A resistor carries 15 mA with 45 V across it. Find its resistance.

Solution

\(R=45/0.015=3000\ \Omega=3\text{ k}\Omega\).

Practice 3

A 2.2 kΩ resistor carries 3 mA. Find the voltage across it.

Solution

\(V=IR=0.003(2200)=6.6\text{ V}\).

Practice 4

A 10 kΩ resistor has 12 V across it. Find the current, in mA.

Solution

\(I=12/10000=1.2\text{ mA}\).

Practice 5

If the voltage across a fixed resistor doubles, what happens to the current? Explain.

Solution

The current also doubles. Since \(I=V/R\) and \(R\) is fixed, current is directly proportional to voltage.

Practice 6

A component draws 50 mA from a 5 V supply. Find its resistance.

Solution

\(R=5/0.05=100\ \Omega\).

2 Series circuits & total resistance

In series, resistors sit one after another along a single path, so there's only one route for current, meaning the same current flows through every one of them.

Key idea

$$R_t=R_1+R_2+\cdots+R_n.$$

Current is identical through every series component; voltage divides between them.

Worked example 1

Three resistors in series: \(R_1=3.3\text{ k}\Omega\), \(R_2=10\text{ k}\Omega\), \(R_3=2.2\text{ k}\Omega\) (the exact values from Experiment 3's Circuit 1). Find \(R_t\).

\(R_t=3300+10000+2200=15{,}500\ \Omega=15.5\text{ k}\Omega\).

Worked example 2

For that same circuit with \(E=12\text{ V}\), find the total (and only) current.

\(I=\dfrac{E}{R_t}=\dfrac{12}{15{,}500}=0.7742\text{ mA}=774.2\ \mu\text{A}\).

Practice 7

Resistors 700 Ω, 700 Ω, and 1 kΩ in series. Find \(R_t\).

Solution

\(R_t=700+700+1000=2400\ \Omega=2.4\text{ k}\Omega\).

Practice 8

A series circuit: \(R_1=1.2\text{ k}\Omega, R_2=2.2\text{ k}\Omega, R_3=3.3\text{ k}\Omega\), supply 15 V. Find \(R_t\) and the current.

Solution

\(R_t=1200+2200+3300=6700\ \Omega\). \(I=15/6700=2.239\text{ mA}\).

Practice 9

If a fourth resistor is added to a series circuit, does total resistance increase or decrease? Explain.

Solution

Increases. Series resistances simply add, so any additional resistor increases \(R_t\).

Practice 10

A series circuit has \(R_t=8\text{ k}\Omega\) and carries 1.5 mA. Find the supply voltage.

Solution

\(E=IR_t=0.0015(8000)=12\text{ V}\).

Practice 11

Explain why current is the same through every component in a series circuit.

Solution

There's only one path for current to flow, no branches exist. Whatever current enters one end must be the exact same current that exits the other; current can't "pile up" or disappear anywhere along a single path.

Practice 12

Three equal 1 kΩ resistors are in series, supply 9 V. Find the voltage across each, reasoning from symmetry (before the formal rule in Section 5).

Solution

Equal resistors share the voltage equally: \(9/3=3\text{ V}\) each.

3 Parallel circuits & total resistance

In parallel, resistors sit side by side, each getting its own direct connection across the same two nodes, so every parallel branch sees the identical voltage.

Key idea

$$\frac1{R_t}=\frac1{R_1}+\frac1{R_2}+\cdots+\frac1{R_n}.$$

Voltage is identical across every parallel branch; current divides between them.

Worked example 1

Two resistors in parallel: \(2.2\text{ k}\Omega\) and \(3.3\text{ k}\Omega\) (matching Experiment 3's Circuit 2, R2 and R3). Find \(R_t\).

\(\dfrac1{R_t}=\dfrac1{2200}+\dfrac1{3300}=0.0004545+0.0003030=0.0007576\).

\(R_t=1/0.0007576=1320\ \Omega=1.32\text{ k}\Omega\).

Worked example 2

Add the series \(R_1=1.2\text{ k}\Omega\) from Circuit 2 in front of that parallel pair, supply 15 V. Find the total current.

\(R_{total}=1200+1320=2520\ \Omega\). \(I_t=15/2520=5.952\text{ mA}\), matching Experiment 3's Table 3 exactly.

Practice 13

Two resistors, \(4.7\text{ k}\Omega\) and \(5.6\text{ k}\Omega\), in parallel. Find \(R_t\).

Solution

\(1/R_t=1/4700+1/5600=0.0003913\). \(R_t=2555\ \Omega=2.555\text{ k}\Omega\).

Practice 14

Three equal 6 kΩ resistors in parallel. Find \(R_t\) (shortcut: \(R/n\) for \(n\) equal resistors).

Solution

\(R_t=6000/3=2000\ \Omega=2\text{ k}\Omega\).

Practice 15

A 12 V supply connects across two parallel branches: 3 kΩ and 6 kΩ. Find the current in each branch.

Solution

Same voltage (12 V) across both: \(I_{3k}=12/3000=4\text{ mA}\). \(I_{6k}=12/6000=2\text{ mA}\).

Practice 16

For Practice 15, find the total current supplied, and confirm it equals the sum of the branch currents.

Solution

\(I_t=4+2=6\text{ mA}\) — this is exactly Kirchhoff's Current Law, covered formally next section.

Practice 17

Explain why the total resistance of a parallel combination is always LESS than the smallest individual resistor.

Solution

Adding a parallel path gives current more routes to flow, so overall resistance to current flow only ever decreases when a new branch is added, \(R_t\) must be smaller than even the smallest single resistor.

Practice 18

Two equal resistors, each \(R\), are placed in parallel. Show that \(R_t=R/2\).

Solution

\(\dfrac1{R_t}=\dfrac1R+\dfrac1R=\dfrac2R \Rightarrow R_t=\dfrac{R}{2}\).

4 Kirchhoff's Current & Voltage Laws

Two conservation statements, one for charge at a junction, one for energy around a loop, that must hold in every circuit, no matter how complex.

Key idea

KCL (Current Law): the sum of currents flowing into a node equals the sum flowing out.

KVL (Voltage Law): around any complete loop, the sum of voltage drops equals the source voltage.

Worked example 1 (KCL)

At a node, 8 mA flows in, splitting into two branches, one carrying 5 mA. Find the other branch's current.

\(8=5+I_2 \Rightarrow I_2=3\text{ mA}\).

Worked example 2 (KVL)

A series circuit has a 12 V supply and two resistor drops, \(V_1=7\text{ V}\) and \(V_2=?\). Find \(V_2\).

\(12=7+V_2 \Rightarrow V_2=5\text{ V}\).

Practice 19

At a node, two currents enter: 4 mA and 6 mA. One current leaves. Find it.

Solution

\(I_{leaving}=4+6=10\text{ mA}\).

Practice 20

A series loop has a 15 V supply and drops \(V_1=3\text{ V}, V_2=8\text{ V}, V_3=?\). Find \(V_3\).

Solution

\(15=3+8+V_3 \Rightarrow V_3=4\text{ V}\).

Practice 21

At a node, 10 mA flows in, splitting into three branches: 4 mA, 3 mA, and \(I_3\). Find \(I_3\).

Solution

\(I_3=10-4-3=3\text{ mA}\).

Practice 22

Explain KVL in your own words, connecting it to energy conservation.

Solution

Travelling all the way around a closed loop and back to the starting point, the total "push" (voltage) supplied must exactly equal the total "drop" used up by components. Energy can't be created or destroyed, so whatever electrical energy per unit charge goes in around the loop must come back out as drops.

Practice 23

A circuit has a 24 V supply and four series resistors. Three of the drops are 5 V, 9 V, and 6 V. Find the fourth.

Solution

\(24=5+9+6+V_4 \Rightarrow V_4=4\text{ V}\).

Practice 24

Explain why KCL must be true, using the idea that charge cannot accumulate at a node.

Solution

Charge is conserved and doesn't pile up anywhere in a standard circuit node, so whatever current flows into a junction per unit time must flow back out; otherwise charge would be mysteriously appearing or vanishing.

5 Voltage Divider Rule

A direct shortcut for series circuits: find any resistor's share of the total voltage without computing the current first.

Key idea

$$V_n = E\times\frac{R_n}{\Sigma R}$$

where \(\Sigma R\) is the total series resistance.

Worked example

Using Experiment 3's exact Circuit 1 (\(R_1=3.3\text{ k}\Omega, R_2=10\text{ k}\Omega, R_3=2.2\text{ k}\Omega, E=12\text{ V}\)), find \(V_1, V_2, V_3\).

\(\Sigma R = 15{,}500\ \Omega\).

\(V_1=12\times\dfrac{3300}{15500}=2.555\text{ V}\). \(\quad V_2=12\times\dfrac{10000}{15500}=7.742\text{ V}\). \(\quad V_3=12\times\dfrac{2200}{15500}=1.703\text{ V}\).

Check (KVL): \(2.555+7.742+1.703=12.0\text{ V}\) ✓. These are the exact calculated values from Experiment 3's Table 2.

Practice 25

Series circuit: \(R_1=1\text{ k}\Omega, R_2=4\text{ k}\Omega, E=10\text{ V}\). Find \(V_1\) and \(V_2\).

Solution

\(\Sigma R=5000\). \(V_1=10(1000/5000)=2\text{ V}\). \(V_2=10(4000/5000)=8\text{ V}\).

Practice 26

Series circuit: \(R_1=2.2\text{ k}\Omega, R_2=3.3\text{ k}\Omega, R_3=1.2\text{ k}\Omega, E=9\text{ V}\). Find \(V_2\).

Solution

\(\Sigma R=6700\). \(V_2=9(3300/6700)=4.428\text{ V}\).

Practice 27

Three equal \(5\text{ k}\Omega\) resistors in series, \(E=15\text{ V}\). Find the voltage across each.

Solution

\(\Sigma R=15{,}000\). Each: \(15(5000/15000)=5\text{ V}\), confirming the equal-split expectation.

Practice 28

For Practice 26's circuit, find \(V_1\) and \(V_3\), then verify all three sum to \(E\).

Solution

\(V_1=9(2200/6700)=2.955\text{ V}\). \(V_3=9(1200/6700)=1.612\text{ V}\). Sum: \(2.955+4.428+1.612\approx9\text{ V}\) ✓.

Practice 29

Explain why a LARGER resistor in a series circuit gets a LARGER share of the voltage.

Solution

Since current is the same through every series resistor, \(V=IR\) means a bigger resistance with the same current produces a bigger voltage drop, the \(R_n/\Sigma R\) ratio directly reflects that a resistor's share of total resistance equals its share of total voltage.

Practice 30

A series circuit has \(R_1=6\text{ k}\Omega\) getting 8 V, out of a 20 V total supply. Find \(\Sigma R\), without knowing the other resistors.

Solution

\(8=20(6000/\Sigma R) \Rightarrow \Sigma R = 20(6000)/8=15{,}000\ \Omega=15\text{ k}\Omega\).

6 Current Divider Rule

The parallel-circuit counterpart to the Voltage Divider Rule: find one branch's share of the total current.

Key idea (two resistors in parallel)

$$I_1 = I_s\times\frac{R_2}{R_1+R_2}, \qquad I_2 = I_s\times\frac{R_1}{R_1+R_2}.$$

Notice each branch's current uses the other resistor in the numerator.

Worked example

Using Experiment 3's exact Circuit 2 (\(R_1=1.2\text{ k}\Omega\) in series feeding a parallel pair \(R_2=2.2\text{ k}\Omega, R_3=3.3\text{ k}\Omega\), supply 15 V), find \(I_1\) and \(I_2\) (the currents through \(R_2\) and \(R_3\)).

From Section 3: \(R_{2}\|R_{3}=1320\ \Omega\), \(R_{total}=2520\ \Omega\), \(I_s=15/2520=5.952\text{ mA}\).

\(I_{R2}=I_s\times\dfrac{R_3}{R_2+R_3}=5.952\times\dfrac{3300}{5500}=5.952(0.6)=3.571\text{ mA}\).

\(I_{R3}=I_s\times\dfrac{R_2}{R_2+R_3}=5.952\times\dfrac{2200}{5500}=5.952(0.4)=2.381\text{ mA}\).

Check: \(3.571+2.381=5.952\text{ mA}=I_s\) ✓. These match Experiment 3's Table 3 calculated values exactly.

Practice 31

Two resistors in parallel, \(R_1=4\text{ k}\Omega, R_2=6\text{ k}\Omega\), total current \(I_s=10\text{ mA}\). Find \(I_1\) and \(I_2\).

Solution

\(I_1=10(6000/10000)=6\text{ mA}\). \(I_2=10(4000/10000)=4\text{ mA}\). Check: \(6+4=10\) ✓.

Practice 32

Two resistors in parallel, \(R_1=1\text{ k}\Omega, R_2=9\text{ k}\Omega\), \(I_s=20\text{ mA}\). Find \(I_1\) and \(I_2\). Which carries more, and why?

Solution

\(I_1=20(9000/10000)=18\text{ mA}\). \(I_2=20(1000/10000)=2\text{ mA}\). \(R_1\) (the smaller resistor) carries more, current prefers the path of least resistance.

Practice 33

Two equal resistors in parallel share total current \(I_s=8\text{ mA}\). Find the current through each.

Solution

By symmetry, \(4\text{ mA}\) each. Formula check: \(I_1=I_s\times R/(R+R)=I_s(0.5)=4\text{ mA}\) ✓.

Practice 34

A parallel pair has \(R_1=2\text{ k}\Omega\) carrying 6 mA, \(R_2=4\text{ k}\Omega\). Find the total current \(I_s\).

Solution

\(6=I_s(4000/6000)=I_s(0.6667) \Rightarrow I_s=6/0.6667=9\text{ mA}\).

Practice 35

Explain why the Current Divider Rule uses the OPPOSITE resistor in its formula, and how this connects to smaller resistors carrying more current.

Solution

Since voltage is identical across both branches, \(I_1=V/R_1\) and \(I_2=V/R_2\): a bigger \(R_1\) gives a smaller \(I_1\) for the same \(V\). Combining \(V=I_1R_1=I_2R_2\) with \(I_s=I_1+I_2\) algebraically produces the "opposite resistor" formula, and it naturally makes the smaller resistor pull the larger share of current.

7 Advanced: series-parallel combination circuits

Real circuits, like Experiment 3's Part 1, mix series and parallel sections together. The strategy: reduce the parallel sub-groups to single equivalent resistors first, then treat what's left as a straightforward series chain.

Worked example

\(R_1=700\ \Omega\) in series with (\(R_2=700\ \Omega \parallel R_3=1\text{ k}\Omega\)), that combination in series with (\(R_4=2\text{ k}\Omega \parallel R_5=2.2\text{ k}\Omega\)), supply 12 V. Find \(R_t\) and the total current.

\(R_2\|R_3 = \dfrac{700\times1000}{700+1000}=411.76\ \Omega\).

\(R_4\|R_5 = \dfrac{2000\times2200}{2000+2200}=1047.62\ \Omega\).

\(R_t=700+411.76+1047.62=2159.38\ \Omega\). \(I_t=12/2159.38=5.557\text{ mA}\).

Practice 36

A circuit has \(R_1=1\text{ k}\Omega\) in series with (\(R_2=2\text{ k}\Omega \parallel R_3=3\text{ k}\Omega\)), supply 9 V. Find \(R_t\) and \(I_t\).

Solution

\(R_2\|R_3=(2000\times3000)/5000=1200\ \Omega\). \(R_t=1000+1200=2200\ \Omega\). \(I_t=9/2200=4.09\text{ mA}\).

Practice 37

For Practice 36, find the voltage across the parallel block, then the individual branch currents through \(R_2\) and \(R_3\).

Solution

Using VDR: \(V_{parallel}=9(1200/2200)=4.909\text{ V}\).

\(I_{R2}=4.909/2000=2.455\text{ mA}\). \(I_{R3}=4.909/3000=1.636\text{ mA}\). Check: \(2.455+1.636=4.09\text{ mA}\) ✓.

Practice 38

A circuit has (\(R_1=500\ \Omega \parallel R_2=1500\ \Omega\)) in series with \(R_3=2\text{ k}\Omega\), supply 20 V. Find \(R_t\) and \(I_t\).

Solution

\(R_1\|R_2=(500\times1500)/2000=375\ \Omega\). \(R_t=375+2000=2375\ \Omega\). \(I_t=20/2375=8.421\text{ mA}\).

Practice 39

Outline, step by step, the general strategy for solving any series-parallel combination circuit, without doing the arithmetic.

Solution

1. Identify which resistors are purely series and which are purely parallel within sub-groups. 2. Reduce each parallel sub-group to a single equivalent resistance. 3. Add the now-simplified series chain to find total \(R_t\). 4. Use Ohm's Law to find the total current from the source. 5. Work backward: find the voltage across each reduced parallel block (VDR, or Ohm's Law with the total current), then apply the Current Divider Rule within each block for the individual branch currents.
