1 Sine wave basics

An AC voltage or current constantly changes, sinusoidally, described by three linked quantities: how big it gets, how long one cycle takes, and how many cycles happen per second.

Key idea

$$v(t) = V_m\sin(\omega t)$$

\(V_m\) = peak (amplitude), \(T\) = period (seconds), \(f=1/T\) (Hz), \(\omega=2\pi f\) (rad/s).

Worked example 1

A sine wave has frequency 50 Hz. Find its period and angular frequency.

\(T=1/50=0.02\text{ s}=20\text{ ms}\). \(\omega=2\pi(50)=314.16\text{ rad/s}\).

Worked example 2

A voltage waveform has period 4 ms. Find its frequency.

\(f=1/0.004=250\text{ Hz}\).

Practice 1

\(f=60\text{ Hz}\). Find \(T\).

Solution

\(T=1/60=16.67\text{ ms}\).

Practice 2

\(T=25\text{ ms}\). Find \(f\).

Solution

\(f=1/0.025=40\text{ Hz}\).

Practice 3

\(f=100\text{ Hz}\). Find \(\omega\).

Solution

\(\omega=2\pi(100)=628.3\text{ rad/s}\).

Practice 4

\(\omega=377\text{ rad/s}\). Find \(f\).

Solution

\(f=377/(2\pi)=60\text{ Hz}\).

Practice 5

Explain the relationship between period and frequency in words.

Solution

They're reciprocals of each other, frequency is how many complete cycles occur per second, period is how long one single cycle takes: \(f=1/T\) and \(T=1/f\).

Practice 6

A generator produces a 50 Hz sine wave. How many complete cycles occur in 0.5 s?

Solution

Cycles \(=f\times t=50(0.5)=25\) cycles.

2 Instantaneous value & angular frequency

At any specific moment in time, a sine wave has one particular value, found by plugging that time into \(v(t)=V_m\sin(\omega t)\).

Key idea

$$v(t) = V_m\sin(\omega t+\theta)$$ Since \(\omega\) is in rad/s, \(\omega t\) is already in radians, no separate conversion needed.

Worked example 1

\(V_m=100\text{ V}\), \(f=50\text{ Hz}\). Find \(v(t)\) at \(t=2\text{ ms}\) (\(\theta=0\)).

\(\omega=314.16\text{ rad/s}\). \(\omega t=314.16(0.002)=0.6283\text{ rad}\).

\(v=100\sin(0.6283)=100(0.5878)=58.78\text{ V}\).

Worked example 2

Same wave, find \(v(t)\) at \(t=5\text{ ms}\).

\(\omega t=314.16(0.005)=1.5708\text{ rad}=90^\circ\). \(v=100\sin(90^\circ)=100\text{ V}\), exactly the peak, as expected at \(t=T/4\).

Practice 7

\(V_m=50\text{ V}\), \(f=60\text{ Hz}\). Find \(v(t)\) at \(t=1\text{ ms}\).

Solution

\(\omega=376.99\text{ rad/s}\). \(\omega t=0.377\text{ rad}\). \(v=50\sin(0.377)=50(0.368)=18.4\text{ V}\).

Practice 8

\(V_m=20\text{ V}\), \(\omega=200\text{ rad/s}\). Find \(v(t)\) at \(t=0.01\text{ s}\).

Solution

\(\omega t=2\text{ rad}\). \(v=20\sin(2)=20(0.909)=18.18\text{ V}\).

Practice 9

Explain why radians (not degrees) are used inside \(\sin(\omega t)\) when \(\omega\) is in rad/s.

Solution

Since \(\omega\) itself has units of radians per second, multiplying by \(t\) (seconds) directly gives an angle already in radians, keeping units consistent without needing a conversion step.

Practice 10

A sine wave reaches its peak at \(t=T/4\). Explain why, using \(\omega t=\pi/2\) at that instant.

Solution

At \(t=T/4\): \(\omega t=(2\pi/T)(T/4)=\pi/2=90^\circ\), and \(\sin(90^\circ)=1\), the maximum possible value, so the wave is exactly at its peak.

Practice 11

\(V_m=15\text{ V}\), \(f=50\text{ Hz}\). Find \(v(t)\) at \(t=T/2\), and explain the result physically.

Solution

At \(t=T/2\): \(\omega t=\pi=180^\circ\), \(\sin(180^\circ)=0\), so \(v=0\), the wave has completed half a cycle and crossed back through zero, about to go negative.

3 RMS & average values

The peak value alone doesn't tell you how much real work an AC wave can do. RMS gives the DC-equivalent value for power purposes.

Key idea

$$V_{rms}=\frac{V_m}{\sqrt2}\approx0.707V_m, \qquad V_{avg}\ (\text{half-cycle})=\frac{2V_m}{\pi}\approx0.637V_m.$$

Worked example 1

A sine wave has \(V_m=170\text{ V}\) (a typical mains peak). Find \(V_{rms}\).

\(V_{rms}=170/1.414=120.2\text{ V}\), matching the standard 120 V RMS mains supply.

Worked example 2

Find the average value (half-cycle) of the same wave.

\(V_{avg}=2(170)/\pi=340/3.1416=108.2\text{ V}\).

Practice 12

\(V_m=100\text{ V}\). Find \(V_{rms}\).

Solution

\(V_{rms}=100/1.414=70.7\text{ V}\).

Practice 13

\(V_{rms}=230\text{ V}\) (standard mains). Find \(V_m\).

Solution

\(V_m=230(1.414)=325.3\text{ V}\).

Practice 14

\(V_m=50\text{ V}\). Find the average value (half-cycle).

Solution

\(V_{avg}=2(50)/\pi=31.83\text{ V}\).

Practice 15

Explain why RMS value is used for power calculations rather than peak or average.

Solution

RMS is defined as the equivalent DC value that delivers the SAME average power to a resistive load. It accounts for power depending on voltage squared, so a simple average (which is even zero over a full cycle) doesn't correctly represent the wave's power-delivering capability.

Practice 16

Why is the average value of a FULL sine wave cycle always zero?

Solution

The positive half of the cycle is perfectly mirrored by an equal negative half, they cancel out exactly over a full cycle, giving zero net average, which is why average value is conventionally quoted over a half-cycle instead.

4 Phase relationships

Two sine waves at the same frequency can still differ in WHEN they reach their peak, that offset is the phase angle.

Key idea

$$v(t)=V_m\sin(\omega t+\theta)$$

A larger (more positive) \(\theta\) means the wave reaches its peak earlier (it "leads"). A smaller (more negative) \(\theta\) means it "lags."

Worked example 1

\(v_1=V_m\sin(\omega t+30^\circ)\), \(v_2=V_m\sin(\omega t-20^\circ)\). Find the phase difference and state which leads.

Difference \(=30-(-20)=50^\circ\). \(v_1\) leads \(v_2\) by 50°.

Worked example 2

A current \(i=I_m\sin(\omega t-40^\circ)\) and voltage \(v=V_m\sin(\omega t+10^\circ)\). Find the phase angle between them.

Difference \(=10-(-40)=50^\circ\). Current lags voltage by 50°.

Practice 17

\(v_1=V_m\sin(\omega t+60^\circ)\), \(v_2=V_m\sin(\omega t+20^\circ)\). Find the phase difference and which leads.

Solution

Difference \(=60-20=40^\circ\). \(v_1\) leads \(v_2\) by 40°.

Practice 18

\(i=I_m\sin(\omega t-15^\circ)\), \(v=V_m\sin(\omega t+25^\circ)\). Find the phase angle and describe the relationship.

Solution

Difference \(=25-(-15)=40^\circ\). Current lags voltage by 40°.

Practice 19

Two waves are "in phase." What does this mean about their phase angles?

Solution

Their phase angles are equal (\(\theta_1=\theta_2\)), so they reach their peaks, zero-crossings, and troughs at exactly the same instants.

Practice 20

Two waves are 180° out of phase. Describe their relationship at any given instant.

Solution

They are always exact opposites, when one is at its positive peak, the other is at its negative peak, and vice versa.

Practice 21

Explain the difference between a wave that "leads" and one that "lags."

Solution

A leading wave reaches its peak (and every other point in its cycle) EARLIER in time than the lagging wave; the leading wave has the larger (more positive) phase angle \(\theta\).

Practice 22

In an inductive AC circuit, current lags voltage by 90°. Write \(i(t)\) if \(v(t)=V_m\sin(\omega t)\).

Solution

\(i(t) = I_m\sin(\omega t - 90^\circ)\).
