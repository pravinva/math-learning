1 Impedance & phasor representation

Impedance folds resistance and reactance into one number that carries both magnitude and phase.

Key idea

$$Z=R+jX, \qquad |Z|=\sqrt{R^2+X^2}, \qquad \theta=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{X}{R}\right).$$

For series RL: \(X=+X_L\) (\(\theta\) positive, voltage leads). For series RC: \(X=-X_C\) (\(\theta\) negative, current leads).

Worked example 1

A series RL circuit: \(R=30\ \Omega\), \(X_L=40\ \Omega\). Find \(|Z|\) and \(\theta\).

\(|Z|=\sqrt{30^2+40^2}=\sqrt{2500}=50\ \Omega\). \(\theta=\tan^{-1}(40/30)=53.13^\circ\).

Worked example 2

For that circuit, \(V_{rms}=100\text{ V}\). Find \(I_{rms}\).

\(I_{rms}=100/50=2\text{ A}\).

Practice 1

Series RL: \(R=6\ \Omega, X_L=8\ \Omega\). Find \(|Z|\) and \(\theta\).

Solution

\(|Z|=\sqrt{36+64}=10\ \Omega\). \(\theta=\tan^{-1}(8/6)=53.13^\circ\).

Practice 2

Series RC: \(R=12\ \Omega, X_C=5\ \Omega\). Find \(|Z|\) and \(\theta\).

Solution

\(|Z|=\sqrt{144+25}=13\ \Omega\). \(\theta=\tan^{-1}(-5/12)=-22.62^\circ\) (current leads).

Practice 3

For Practice 1, \(V_{rms}=50\text{ V}\). Find \(I_{rms}\).

Solution

\(I_{rms}=50/10=5\text{ A}\).

Practice 4

Explain why a series RL circuit's \(\theta\) is positive, while a series RC circuit's is negative.

Solution

In RL, inductive reactance makes current lag voltage (voltage leads), giving a positive \(\theta\) by convention. In RC, capacitive reactance makes current lead voltage, giving a negative \(\theta\). The sign of \(\theta\) directly tells you whether the circuit is net inductive or net capacitive.

Practice 5

A circuit has \(|Z|=25\ \Omega\), \(\theta=0^\circ\). What does this tell you about its composition?

Solution

\(\theta=0^\circ\) means voltage and current are exactly in phase, indicating the circuit is purely resistive, no net reactance (\(X_L\) and \(X_C\) either don't exist or perfectly cancel).

Practice 6

Explain why impedance combines \(R\) and \(X\) using \(\sqrt{R^2+X^2}\) rather than simple addition.

Solution

Resistance and reactance affect the circuit 90° out of phase with each other, so they can't simply add like ordinary numbers. Combining them requires treating them like perpendicular components of a right triangle, exactly like finding a vector's magnitude from its \(x\) and \(y\) components, hence the Pythagorean-style combination.

2 Frequency response summary

Because \(X_L\) and \(X_C\) respond to frequency in opposite directions, RL and RC circuits behave very differently as frequency changes.

Worked example

A series RC circuit, \(R=100\ \Omega\), \(C=10\ \mu\text{F}\). Find \(X_C\) and \(|Z|\) at \(f=100\text{ Hz}\) and \(f=10{,}000\text{ Hz}\), and compare.

\(f=100\text{ Hz}\): \(X_C=1/(2\pi\times100\times10^{-5})=159.2\ \Omega\). \(|Z|=\sqrt{100^2+159.2^2}=188.0\ \Omega\).

\(f=10{,}000\text{ Hz}\): \(X_C=1/(2\pi\times10{,}000\times10^{-5})=1.592\ \Omega\). \(|Z|=\sqrt{100^2+1.592^2}\approx100.01\ \Omega\).

At high frequency, \(X_C\) becomes negligible and \(|Z|\approx R\) (the capacitor acts almost like a wire); at low frequency, \(X_C\) dominates and \(|Z|\) is much larger (the capacitor blocks more).

Practice 7

For the same RC circuit, find \(X_C\) and \(|Z|\) at \(f=1000\text{ Hz}\).

Solution

\(X_C=1/(2\pi\times1000\times10^{-5})=15.92\ \Omega\). \(|Z|=\sqrt{100^2+15.92^2}=101.26\ \Omega\).

Practice 8

Based on the pattern observed, does a series RC circuit pass high or low frequencies more easily? Explain.

Solution

High frequencies pass more easily, as frequency increases, \(X_C\) decreases, so total impedance decreases toward just \(R\), offering less opposition. This makes an RC circuit behave like a "high-pass" filter.

Practice 9

For a series RL circuit, would you expect LOW or HIGH frequencies to pass more easily?

Solution

Low frequencies pass more easily, since \(X_L=2\pi fL\) increases with frequency, higher frequencies see more opposition (larger \(|Z|\)), so an RL circuit tends to block high frequencies more, behaving like a "low-pass" filter.

Practice 10

Summarise, in one sentence each, how \(X_L\) and \(X_C\) individually respond to increasing frequency.

Solution

\(X_L\) increases with frequency; \(X_C\) decreases with frequency, exactly opposite trends.

3 Average power & power factor

Only the resistive part of a circuit actually dissipates power. Power factor tells you what fraction of the apparent VI product is real, useful power.

Key idea

$$P=V_{rms}I_{rms}\cos\theta, \qquad PF=\cos\theta.$$

\(PF=1\) (\(\theta=0^\circ\)): all power is real (purely resistive). \(PF=0\) (\(\theta=90^\circ\)): no real power delivered (purely reactive).

Worked example 1

\(V_{rms}=100\text{ V}\), \(I_{rms}=5\text{ A}\), \(\theta=30^\circ\). Find the average power and power factor.

\(PF=\cos(30^\circ)=0.866\). \(P=100(5)(0.866)=433\text{ W}\).

Worked example 2

For the series RL circuit from Section 1 (\(R=30\ \Omega, X_L=40\ \Omega, |Z|=50\ \Omega, \theta=53.13^\circ, V_{rms}=100\text{ V}, I_{rms}=2\text{ A}\)), find the average power.

\(PF=\cos(53.13^\circ)=0.6\). \(P=100(2)(0.6)=120\text{ W}\).

Check: \(P=I^2R=2^2(30)=120\text{ W}\) ✓, confirming real power in a resistive-reactive circuit always equals \(I^2R\), the reactive part contributes zero net power.

Practice 11

\(V_{rms}=120\text{ V}\), \(I_{rms}=3\text{ A}\), \(\theta=45^\circ\). Find \(PF\) and average power.

Solution

\(PF=\cos45^\circ=0.707\). \(P=120(3)(0.707)=254.5\text{ W}\).

Practice 12

A purely resistive circuit has \(\theta=0^\circ\). Find its power factor.

Solution

\(PF=\cos(0)=1\) (unity power factor).

Practice 13

A purely reactive circuit has \(\theta=90^\circ\). Find its power factor and average power delivered.

Solution

\(PF=\cos(90^\circ)=0\), so average power \(=0\), no real power delivered, purely reactive.

Practice 14

Series RC: \(R=12\ \Omega, X_C=5\ \Omega, |Z|=13\ \Omega\), \(V_{rms}=26\text{ V}\). Find \(I_{rms}\), \(PF\), and average power.

Solution

\(I_{rms}=26/13=2\text{ A}\). \(\theta=22.62^\circ\), \(PF=\cos(22.62^\circ)=0.923\). \(P=26(2)(0.923)=48\text{ W}\). Check: \(I^2R=2^2(12)=48\text{ W}\) ✓.

Practice 15

Explain physically why a purely reactive circuit delivers ZERO average power, even though voltage and current are both nonzero.

Solution

Because voltage and current are 90° out of phase, energy flows INTO the component during part of the cycle and flows entirely back OUT during another part. Over a full cycle, the net energy transferred averages to exactly zero, the component only stores and releases energy, it never consumes any.

Practice 16

Explain why power companies care about power factor, using \(I=P/(V\cdot PF)\) for a fixed useful power \(P\).

Solution

For a fixed amount of useful power \(P\), a lower power factor means MORE current must flow to deliver that same real power, since some current is "wasted" going back and forth without doing useful work. This extra current still causes resistive losses in the supply wiring, so utilities penalise low power factor to discourage this inefficiency.
