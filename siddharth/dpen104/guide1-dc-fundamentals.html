Topic 1 · Week 1
Charge, Current & Voltage
The three quantities that describe what electricity is and what makes it move
You cannot see electricity, so engineers describe it with three measurable quantities. Charge is the "stuff", current is how fast it flows, and voltage is the pressure that pushes it. Get these three rock-solid now — every later topic (Ohm's law, power, AC, motors) is just a rearrangement of how they relate.
DefinitionElectric charge▶

Charge is a fundamental property of matter. Electrons carry negative charge, protons positive. The SI unit is the coulomb (C).

\[ 1\ \text{coulomb} = 6.242\times10^{18}\ \text{electrons} \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad q_{e}=1.602\times10^{-19}\ \text{C} \]

So the charge on one electron is \(-1.602\times10^{-19}\,\text{C}\). To find total charge from a number of electrons \(n\):

\[ Q = n\,q_e \]
Charge is conserved: it is never created or destroyed in a circuit, only moved. This is the deep reason Kirchhoff's Current Law (Topic 5) works.
DefinitionCurrent — the flow of charge▶

Current is the rate of flow of charge past a point. One ampere is one coulomb per second.

\[ I = \frac{Q}{t}\qquad\text{(steady)} \qquad\qquad i = \frac{dq}{dt}\qquad\text{(instantaneous)} \]

Rearranged: \(Q = I\,t\). If 2 A flows for 5 s, then \(Q = 2\times5 = 10\,\text{C}\) passes by.

Conventional vs electron flow: conventional current flows from + to − (the direction a positive charge would move). Electrons actually drift the opposite way. All circuit analysis in this course uses conventional current — stick to it.
DefinitionVoltage — potential difference▶

Voltage (potential difference) is the energy given to or taken from each coulomb of charge as it moves between two points.

\[ V = \frac{W}{Q} \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad 1\,\text{V} = 1\,\text{J/C} \]

A 9 V battery gives 9 joules of energy to every coulomb that passes through it. Voltage is always measured between two points — there is no such thing as voltage "at" a single point without a reference.

Analogy: think of a water system. Charge = water, current = flow rate (L/s), voltage = pump pressure. No pressure → no flow. This analogy is used throughout the guide.
PatternVoltage sources — ideal vs real▶

A voltage source maintains a fixed voltage across its terminals. Two kinds:

1
Ideal: terminal voltage stays at \(E\) no matter how much current is drawn.
2
Real (practical): has an internal resistance \(R_{int}\) in series, so terminal voltage drops as current rises (covered fully in Topic 4).

DC vs AC sources: a DC source (battery, cell) has constant polarity; an AC source (mains, generator) reverses periodically. Guide 1-3 are DC; Guide 4 is AC.

ExampleFour worked examples▶
Example 1 — Charge from electron count

How much charge is carried by \(3.121\times10^{19}\) electrons?

\[ Q = n\,q_e = 3.121\times10^{19}\times1.602\times10^{-19}=5.0\ \text{C} \]
ANSWER \(Q = 5\ \text{C}\)
Example 2 — Current from charge and time

A charge of 60 C passes a point in 2 minutes. Find the current.

1
Convert time: \(t = 2\times60 = 120\,\text{s}\).
2
\(I = Q/t = 60/120 = 0.5\,\text{A}\).
ANSWER \(I = 0.5\ \text{A} = 500\ \text{mA}\)
Example 3 — Charge delivered over time

A device draws 250 mA. How much charge flows in 1 hour?

\[ Q = I\,t = 0.25\times3600 = 900\ \text{C} \]
ANSWER \(Q = 900\ \text{C}\)
Example 4 — Voltage from energy

A battery transfers 48 J of energy to move 4 C of charge through a lamp. What is the voltage across the lamp?

\[ V = \frac{W}{Q} = \frac{48}{4} = 12\ \text{V} \]
ANSWER \(V = 12\ \text{V}\)
NoteCommon beginner traps▶
Watch out: current is measured in amperes, not "amps per second". An ampere already is "coulombs per second". Saying "amps per second" is wrong.
Watch out: always convert prefixes before plugging into formulas. 250 mA is 0.25 A, not 250 A. A huge fraction of exam errors come from prefix slips.

Topic 2 · Week 1
Resistance & Ohm's Law
The single most-used equation in all of electrical engineering
Resistance is the circuit's "friction" — it opposes current. Ohm's Law, \(V=IR\), is the relationship you will use in literally every circuit problem for the rest of the subject. Master rearranging it three ways and you can find any one of V, I, R from the other two.
DefinitionResistance, resistivity & conductance▶

Resistance \(R\) (ohms, \(\Omega\)) measures opposition to current. It depends on the material and geometry of the conductor:

\[ R = \frac{\rho\,\ell}{A} \]

where \(\rho\) = resistivity (\(\Omega\!\cdot\!\text{m}\)), \(\ell\) = length, \(A\) = cross-sectional area. Longer wire → more resistance; thicker wire → less resistance.

Conductance \(G\) is the reciprocal — how easily current flows:

\[ G = \frac{1}{R}\qquad\text{(unit: siemens, S)} \]
DefinitionOhm's Law — the three forms▶

For a resistor, voltage is directly proportional to current; the constant of proportionality is the resistance:

\[ V = IR \qquad\qquad I = \frac{V}{R} \qquad\qquad R = \frac{V}{I} \]

Cover the quantity you want in the V–I–R triangle to read off the formula:

You want…You know…Use
Voltage \(V\)\(I\) and \(R\)\(V=IR\)
Current \(I\)\(V\) and \(R\)\(I=V/R\)
Resistance \(R\)\(V\) and \(I\)\(R=V/I\)
A graph of \(V\) (y) against \(I\) (x) for an ohmic resistor is a straight line through the origin whose gradient equals \(R\).
ExampleFive worked examples▶
Example 1 — Find current

A 12 V supply is connected across a \(2\,\text{k}\Omega\) resistor. Find the current.

\[ I = \frac{V}{R} = \frac{12}{2000} = 6\times10^{-3}\,\text{A} = 6\ \text{mA} \]
ANSWER \(I = 6\ \text{mA}\)
Example 2 — Find resistance

A current of 50 mA flows when 5 V is applied. Find \(R\).

\[ R = \frac{V}{I} = \frac{5}{0.05} = 100\ \Omega \]
ANSWER \(R = 100\ \Omega\)
Example 3 — Find voltage

What voltage drives 0.25 A through a \(330\,\Omega\) resistor?

\[ V = IR = 0.25\times330 = 82.5\ \text{V} \]
ANSWER \(V = 82.5\ \text{V}\)
Example 4 — Resistance from geometry

A copper wire (\(\rho=1.68\times10^{-8}\,\Omega\!\cdot\!\text{m}\)) is 50 m long with cross-section \(1\times10^{-6}\,\text{m}^2\). Find \(R\).

\[ R=\frac{\rho\ell}{A}=\frac{1.68\times10^{-8}\times50}{1\times10^{-6}} = 0.84\ \Omega \]
ANSWER \(R = 0.84\ \Omega\)
Example 5 — Conductance

A resistor has \(R = 250\,\Omega\). Find its conductance, and the current at 10 V.

1
\(G = 1/R = 1/250 = 0.004\,\text{S} = 4\,\text{mS}\)
2
\(I = GV = 0.004\times10 = 0.04\,\text{A} = 40\,\text{mA}\) (same as \(V/R\)).
ANSWER \(G = 4\,\text{mS}\), \(I = 40\,\text{mA}\)
NoteWhen Ohm's Law does NOT apply▶
Watch out: Ohm's Law applies to ohmic (linear) devices only. Diodes, lamps (hot filament) and semiconductors are non-ohmic — their V–I graph is curved, so \(R\) is not constant. We meet diodes in Guide 4.
Watch out: keep units consistent. If you use volts and ohms you get amperes; if you use volts and kΩ you get mA. Mixing these is the #1 source of factor-of-1000 errors.

Topic 3 · Week 2
Energy & Power
How fast a circuit converts energy — and what it costs
Power is what we actually pay for and what makes components heat up, glow, or fail. The relation \(P=VI\) (and its Ohm's-law variants) tells you the rating a component needs and the energy a system consumes. Choosing a resistor that can't handle its power dissipation is a classic — and smoky — beginner mistake.
DefinitionEnergy, work and power▶

Power is the rate of doing work (transferring energy). One watt is one joule per second.

\[ P = \frac{W}{t}\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad W = P\,t \]

Combining \(P=W/t\), \(V=W/Q\) and \(I=Q/t\) gives the central electrical power formula:

\[ \boxed{P = VI} \]
PatternThe three power formulas (use Ohm's Law to swap)▶

Substitute \(V=IR\) or \(I=V/R\) into \(P=VI\) to get three equivalent forms. Pick whichever uses the two quantities you already know:

\[ P = VI \qquad P = I^2 R \qquad P = \frac{V^2}{R} \]
You know…Use
\(V\) and \(I\)\(P=VI\)
\(I\) and \(R\)\(P=I^2R\)
\(V\) and \(R\)\(P=V^2/R\)
Efficiency: \(\eta = \dfrac{P_{out}}{P_{in}}\times100\%\). No real machine is 100% efficient — the lost power becomes heat.
ExampleFive worked examples▶
Example 1 — Power from V and I

A lamp runs at 12 V and draws 2 A. Find the power.

\[ P = VI = 12\times2 = 24\ \text{W} \]
ANSWER \(P = 24\ \text{W}\)
Example 2 — Resistor power rating (P = I²R)

A \(100\,\Omega\) resistor carries 0.3 A. What power does it dissipate, and is a \(\tfrac14\)-W resistor safe?

\[ P = I^2R = (0.3)^2\times100 = 9\ \text{W} \]
ANSWER \(P = 9\ \text{W}\) — a ¼-W resistor would burn out; you need ≥10 W rating.
Example 3 — Power from V and R

How much power does a \(60\,\Omega\) heater element take from 240 V?

\[ P = \frac{V^2}{R} = \frac{240^2}{60} = \frac{57600}{60} = 960\ \text{W} \]
ANSWER \(P = 960\ \text{W}\)
Example 4 — Energy and cost (kWh)

A 2 kW heater runs for 3 hours. Energy used, and cost at $0.30/kWh?

1
\(W = Pt = 2\,\text{kW}\times3\,\text{h} = 6\ \text{kWh}\)
2
Cost \(= 6\times\$0.30 = \$1.80\)
ANSWER 6 kWh, costing $1.80
Example 5 — Efficiency

A motor takes 500 W electrical input and delivers 425 W of mechanical output. Find efficiency.

\[ \eta = \frac{425}{500}\times100\% = 85\% \]
ANSWER \(\eta = 85\%\) (75 W lost as heat)
NoteJoules vs kilowatt-hours▶
The kWh is just a big joule. \(1\,\text{kWh} = 1000\,\text{W}\times3600\,\text{s} = 3.6\times10^6\,\text{J} = 3.6\,\text{MJ}\). Energy bills use kWh because joules give awkwardly large numbers.
Watch out: in \(P=I^2R\), square the current first, then multiply by R. \((0.3)^2=0.09\), not 0.3. Forgetting the square is extremely common.

Topic 4 · Week 2
Series Circuits & Kirchhoff's Voltage Law
One path for current, voltages that add up
In a series circuit there is exactly one loop, so the same current flows everywhere. Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL) — the voltages around any loop sum to zero — plus the voltage divider rule are the workhorses for analysing them. Real batteries and the "loaded source voltage drop" problem are pure series analysis.
DefinitionSeries resistance & the same-current rule▶

Resistors are in series when they are connected end-to-end so the same current passes through each. The total (equivalent) resistance is the sum:

\[ R_T = R_1 + R_2 + \dots + R_n \]

Key series facts:

1
Current is the same through every element: \(I = E/R_T\).
2
Voltages add to the supply: \(E = V_1 + V_2 + \dots\)
3
The largest resistor drops the most voltage.
DefinitionKirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)▶

KVL states: the algebraic sum of all voltages around any closed loop is zero.

\[ \sum_{\text{loop}} V = 0 \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad \sum E_{\text{rises}} = \sum V_{\text{drops}} \]

It is energy conservation in disguise: a charge gains energy at the source and gives it all back to the components in one trip around the loop.

Sign convention: going around the loop, count a voltage rise (− to +) as positive and a drop (+ to −) as negative — or vice versa. Be consistent for the whole loop.
PatternVoltage Divider Rule (VDR)▶

Because series current is shared, each resistor takes a fraction of the supply proportional to its resistance. No need to find the current first:

\[ V_x = E\cdot\frac{R_x}{R_T} \]

The voltage across \(R_x\) is the supply times "its resistance over the total". This is one of the most used shortcuts in the subject.

PatternReal (practical) voltage source▶

A real source = ideal EMF \(E\) in series with internal resistance \(R_{int}\). When a load \(R_L\) is connected:

\[ I = \frac{E}{R_{int}+R_L} \qquad V_{terminal} = E - I\,R_{int} = E\cdot\frac{R_L}{R_{int}+R_L} \]

The terminal voltage drops below \(E\) as the load draws more current — this is why a car's headlights dim when the starter motor cranks.

ExampleFive worked examples▶
Example 1 — Series total & current

A 24 V supply drives \(R_1=2\,\Omega\), \(R_2=4\,\Omega\), \(R_3=6\,\Omega\) in series. Find \(R_T\) and \(I\).

1
\(R_T = 2+4+6 = 12\,\Omega\)
2
\(I = E/R_T = 24/12 = 2\,\text{A}\)
ANSWER \(R_T = 12\,\Omega\), \(I = 2\,\text{A}\)
Example 2 — Voltage across each resistor (KVL check)

Using Example 1, find \(V_1, V_2, V_3\) and verify KVL.

1
\(V_1 = IR_1 = 2\times2 = 4\,\text{V}\)
2
\(V_2 = 2\times4 = 8\,\text{V}\); \(V_3 = 2\times6 = 12\,\text{V}\)
3
KVL: \(4+8+12 = 24\,\text{V} = E\) ✓
ANSWER 4 V, 8 V, 12 V — they sum to the 24 V supply.
Example 3 — Voltage Divider Rule

Across a 15 V supply, \(R_1=3\,\text{k}\Omega\) and \(R_2=2\,\text{k}\Omega\) are in series. Find \(V_2\) directly with VDR.

\[ V_2 = 15\cdot\frac{2\text{k}}{3\text{k}+2\text{k}} = 15\cdot\frac{2}{5} = 6\ \text{V} \]
ANSWER \(V_2 = 6\ \text{V}\)
Example 4 — Find an unknown resistor with KVL

A 20 V supply has \(R_1=5\,\Omega\) (drops 5 V) and unknown \(R_2\) in series. Find \(V_2\), \(I\), and \(R_2\).

1
KVL: \(V_2 = 20 - 5 = 15\,\text{V}\)
2
\(I = V_1/R_1 = 5/5 = 1\,\text{A}\) (same current series)
3
\(R_2 = V_2/I = 15/1 = 15\,\Omega\)
ANSWER \(V_2=15\,\text{V}\), \(I=1\,\text{A}\), \(R_2=15\,\Omega\)
Example 5 — Real source terminal voltage

A battery has \(E=12\,\text{V}\), \(R_{int}=0.5\,\Omega\), and supplies a \(5.5\,\Omega\) load. Find \(I\) and terminal voltage.

1
\(I = \dfrac{12}{0.5+5.5} = \dfrac{12}{6} = 2\,\text{A}\)
2
\(V_T = E - IR_{int} = 12 - 2(0.5) = 11\,\text{V}\)
ANSWER \(I=2\,\text{A}\), terminal voltage \(=11\,\text{V}\) (1 V lost internally)
NoteSeries safety facts & traps▶
Watch out: if one element in a series circuit opens (breaks), the current everywhere becomes zero — old Christmas lights failed this way. Series = "all or nothing".
VDR only works for resistors carrying the same current (i.e. genuinely in series). Don't apply it across resistors that have a branch between them.

Topic 5 · Week 3
Parallel Circuits & Kirchhoff's Current Law
Many paths, shared voltage, currents that split
Parallel is how your house is wired: every appliance gets the same voltage and works independently. Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL) — current in equals current out at any node — and the current divider rule let you track how current splits between branches. This completes the toolkit needed before tackling mixed series–parallel networks in Guide 2.
DefinitionParallel resistance & the same-voltage rule▶

Resistors are in parallel when both ends connect to the same two nodes, so they share the same voltage. Total resistance is found from reciprocals:

\[ \frac{1}{R_T} = \frac{1}{R_1}+\frac{1}{R_2}+\dots+\frac{1}{R_n} \]

Two-resistor shortcut ("product over sum"):

\[ R_T = \frac{R_1 R_2}{R_1+R_2} \]

\(n\) equal resistors of value \(R\): \(R_T = R/n\).

Key fact: the total parallel resistance is always smaller than the smallest branch resistor — adding paths makes it easier for current to flow.
DefinitionKirchhoff's Current Law (KCL)▶

KCL states: the total current entering a node equals the total current leaving it (sum of currents at a node = 0).

\[ \sum I_{\text{in}} = \sum I_{\text{out}} \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad \sum_{\text{node}} I = 0 \]

It is charge conservation: charge cannot pile up at a junction. For parallel branches: \(I_T = I_1 + I_2 + \dots\)

PatternCurrent Divider Rule (CDR)▶

Current splits between parallel branches in inverse proportion to resistance — more current takes the easier (lower-R) path. General form:

\[ I_x = I_T\cdot\frac{R_T}{R_x} \qquad(\text{using parallel } R_T) \]

For exactly two branches, the clean form (note the opposite resistor on top):

\[ I_1 = I_T\cdot\frac{R_2}{R_1+R_2} \qquad I_2 = I_T\cdot\frac{R_1}{R_1+R_2} \]
ExampleFive worked examples▶
Example 1 — Two-resistor parallel total

Find \(R_T\) for \(6\,\Omega \parallel 3\,\Omega\).

\[ R_T = \frac{6\times3}{6+3} = \frac{18}{9} = 2\ \Omega \]
ANSWER \(R_T = 2\,\Omega\) (smaller than both ✓)
Example 2 — Three resistors by reciprocals

\(R_1=4\,\Omega\), \(R_2=4\,\Omega\), \(R_3=2\,\Omega\) in parallel.

\[ \frac{1}{R_T} = \frac14+\frac14+\frac12 = \frac{1+1+2}{4} = 1 \Rightarrow R_T = 1\ \Omega \]
ANSWER \(R_T = 1\,\Omega\)
Example 3 — Branch currents & KCL

A 12 V supply feeds \(6\,\Omega \parallel 4\,\Omega\). Find each branch current and the total (check KCL).

1
Same voltage 12 V across each. \(I_1 = 12/6 = 2\,\text{A}\)
2
\(I_2 = 12/4 = 3\,\text{A}\)
3
KCL: \(I_T = I_1+I_2 = 5\,\text{A}\)
ANSWER \(I_1=2\,\text{A}\), \(I_2=3\,\text{A}\), \(I_T=5\,\text{A}\)
Example 4 — Current Divider Rule

A total of 9 A enters the junction of \(R_1=2\,\Omega \parallel R_2=4\,\Omega\). Find \(I_1\).

\[ I_1 = 9\cdot\frac{R_2}{R_1+R_2} = 9\cdot\frac{4}{6} = 6\ \text{A} \]
ANSWER \(I_1=6\,\text{A}\) (and \(I_2=3\,\text{A}\); the smaller resistor takes the larger current)
Example 5 — Finding an unknown branch with KCL

At a node, 8 A and 3 A enter; 6 A and \(I_x\) leave. Find \(I_x\).

\[ \sum I_{in}=\sum I_{out}:\quad 8+3 = 6 + I_x \Rightarrow I_x = 5\ \text{A} \]
ANSWER \(I_x = 5\,\text{A}\) (leaving)
