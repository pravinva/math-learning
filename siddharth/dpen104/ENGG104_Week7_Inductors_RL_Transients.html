1 Self-inductance & induced voltage

An inductor doesn't oppose current, it opposes change in current. A steady DC current, once established, flows through an ideal inductor as if it were a plain wire.

Key idea

$$V_L = L\frac{di}{dt}$$ where \(L\) is inductance in henries (H). The induced voltage opposes the change (Lenz's Law).

Worked example 1

An inductor \(L=0.5\text{ H}\) has current changing at 4 A/s. Find the induced voltage.

\(V=L(di/dt)=0.5(4)=2\text{ V}\).

Worked example 2

A 200 mH inductor has 6 V induced across it. Find the rate of current change.

\(di/dt=V/L=6/0.2=30\text{ A/s}\).

Practice 1

An inductor \(L=2\text{ H}\), current changes at 3 A/s. Find the induced voltage.

Solution

\(V=2(3)=6\text{ V}\).

Practice 2

A 50 mH inductor has 4 V across it. Find the rate of current change.

Solution

\(di/dt=4/0.05=80\text{ A/s}\).

Practice 3

Explain why an inductor opposes CHANGES in current, but not a steady current itself.

Solution

\(V_L=L(di/dt)\); if current is steady (constant), \(di/dt=0\), so \(V_L=0\). The inductor only generates an opposing voltage when current is actually changing, at steady-state it behaves like a plain wire.

Practice 4

If current through an inductor is INCREASING, in which direction does the induced voltage act?

Solution

It opposes the increase (Lenz's Law), effectively "pushing back" against the current trying to grow.

Practice 5

An inductor has zero voltage across it. What does this tell you about the current through it?

Solution

The current must be constant (steady-state) at that instant, since \(V_L=0\) only when \(di/dt=0\).

2 Inductors in series & parallel

Unlike capacitors, inductors combine exactly like resistors.

Key idea

Series: $$L_t=L_1+L_2+\cdots+L_n$$

Parallel: $$\frac1{L_t}=\frac1{L_1}+\frac1{L_2}+\cdots+\frac1{L_n}$$

Worked example 1

Three inductors in series: 2 H, 3 H, 5 H. Find \(L_t\).

\(L_t=2+3+5=10\text{ H}\).

Worked example 2

Two inductors in parallel: 4 H, 6 H. Find \(L_t\).

\(1/L_t=1/4+1/6=0.4167\). \(L_t=2.4\text{ H}\).

Practice 6

Two inductors in series: 1.5 H, 2.5 H. Find \(L_t\).

Solution

\(L_t=1.5+2.5=4\text{ H}\).

Practice 7

Two inductors in parallel: 3 H, 3 H. Find \(L_t\).

Solution

\(L_t=3/2=1.5\text{ H}\).

Practice 8

Three equal 6 H inductors in parallel. Find \(L_t\).

Solution

\(L_t=6/3=2\text{ H}\).

Practice 9

Explain why inductors combine the SAME way as resistors, unlike capacitors (which do the opposite).

Solution

Inductance represents opposition to changing current, structurally similar to how resistance opposes steady current. More series inductors mean more total opposition to current change (adding, like resistors); parallel inductors give current more paths, reducing overall opposition (like resistors in parallel), exactly mirroring resistor rules rather than the reciprocal capacitor rules.

Practice 10

A single 8 H inductor is replaced by two 16 H inductors in parallel. Show these give the same total inductance.

Solution

\(1/L_t=1/16+1/16=2/16=1/8 \Rightarrow L_t=8\text{ H}\), matching the original ✓.

3 RL transients & time constants

Just as a capacitor's voltage can't jump instantly, an inductor's current can't jump instantly, the RL time constant governs how fast it ramps up.

Key idea

$$\tau=\frac{L}{R} \text{ (seconds)}, \qquad i(t)=I_{max}\left(1-e^{-t/\tau}\right), \qquad I_{max}=\frac{E}{R}.$$

After \(5\tau\), current is essentially at steady-state.

Worked example 1

A circuit has \(L=2\text{ H}\), \(R=4\ \Omega\). Find the time constant.

\(\tau=L/R=2/4=0.5\text{ s}\).

Worked example 2

For that circuit with \(E=12\text{ V}\), find \(I_{max}\) and the current at \(t=\tau\).

\(I_{max}=12/4=3\text{ A}\).

\(i(\tau)=3(1-e^{-1})=3(0.632)=1.897\text{ A}\).

Practice 11

\(L=1\text{ H}, R=5\ \Omega\). Find \(\tau\).

Solution

\(\tau=1/5=0.2\text{ s}\).

Practice 12

For Practice 11, \(E=10\text{ V}\). Find \(I_{max}\).

Solution

\(I_{max}=10/5=2\text{ A}\).

Practice 13

For Practices 11–12's circuit, find \(i(t)\) at \(t=\tau\).

Solution

\(i=2(1-e^{-1})=2(0.632)=1.264\text{ A}\).

Practice 14

How long, in terms of \(\tau\), is current generally considered to have reached steady-state?

Solution

\(5\tau\).

Practice 15

Explain why current through an inductor cannot change instantaneously.

Solution

Since \(V_L=L(di/dt)\), an instantaneous change would require \(di/dt\to\infty\), demanding infinite voltage, which is physically impossible. The induced voltage inherently limits how quickly current can change, forcing it to ramp gradually rather than jump.

Practice 16

Compare \(\tau=L/R\) (RL) to \(\tau=RC\) (RC, Week 6). Why is one a ratio and the other a product?

Solution

Both must give units of seconds. Resistance (Ω) times capacitance (F) gives seconds directly, so RC is a product. For inductance (H) and resistance (Ω), the combination that yields seconds is L divided by R (H/Ω = s), so RL's time constant is a ratio, the difference comes from how each unit is dimensionally defined relative to Ω and seconds.
