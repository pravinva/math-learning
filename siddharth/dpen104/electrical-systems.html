Topic 1 · Core language
Charge, Current, Voltage, Energy
Everything in electrical engineering starts from these four quantities and their physical meaning.

Concept build-up

Charge is the underlying quantity. Current is charge flow per second. Voltage is energy difference per unit charge. These are not independent facts; they are connected through energy transfer and device behavior.

Foundational definitions
$$I=\frac{Q}{t},\qquad V=\frac{W}{Q},\qquad W=VQ$$
Recall: if units don't match, the formula setup is wrong. Always check C, A, V, J dimensions before solving.

Solved examples

Example 1

Battery moves 540 C in 3 min. $I=540/180=3.0$ A.

Example 2

12 V source moves 80 C. Energy delivered: $W=VQ=960$ J.

Example 3

Load consumes 1800 J in 30 s at 5 A. Find voltage: $P=1800/30=60$ W, so $V=P/I=12$ V.

Exam-style practice
  1. Find current when 250 C flows in 40 s.
    Answer
    $I=6.25$ A.
  2. A source provides 24 V and transfers 120 C. Find energy in kJ.
    Answer
    $W=2880$ J = 2.88 kJ.
  3. Device uses 3.6 MJ over 2 h. Find average power in W.
    Answer
    $P=3.6\times10^6/7200=500$ W.
  4. Concept + math: if charge transfer doubles but energy fixed, what happens to voltage?
    Answer
    $V=W/Q$, so doubling $Q$ halves $V$.
  5. Given $V=9$ V and $I=0.75$ A, find charge moved in 4 min.
    Answer
    $Q=It=0.75\times240=180$ C.
Topic 2 · Material response
Resistance, Ohm's Law, Power and Energy
From definitions to practical design: current, heat, rating, and safety limits.

Core relations

Design equations
$$V=IR,\quad P=VI=I^2R=\frac{V^2}{R},\quad E=Pt$$
Connection insight: a circuit can satisfy Ohm's law numerically and still fail physically due to thermal overload. Electrical + thermal reasoning must be combined.

Voltage 12 V
Resistance 24 Ω

Solved examples

Example 1

230 V kettle rated 1840 W: $I=P/V=8$ A and $R=V/I=28.75\,\Omega$.

Example 2

12 V on 6 Ω gives $I=2$ A and $P=24$ W. In 10 min, energy is $24\times600=14.4$ kJ.

Example 3

Max 0.25 W resistor on 5 V requires $R\ge V^2/P=100\,\Omega$.

Exam-style practice
  1. Find current and power for 18 V across 9 Ω.
    Answer
    $I=2$ A, $P=36$ W.
  2. Resistor dissipates 50 W at 5 A. Find resistance and voltage.
    Answer
    $R=P/I^2=2\,\Omega$, $V=IR=10$ V.
  3. Heater draws 4 A from 240 V for 30 min. Find energy in MJ.
    Answer
    $P=960$ W, $E=960\times1800=1.728$ MJ.
  4. Concept + math: if supply voltage doubles and R fixed, how does power change?
    Answer
    $P=V^2/R$, so power increases by factor 4.
  5. A 12 V LED branch must be limited to 20 mA. Choose resistor value.
    Answer
    $R=V/I=12/0.02=600\,\Omega$ (choose nearest standard, usually 620 Ω).
Topic 3 · Conservation laws
Kirchhoff Laws and Series/Parallel Circuits
KCL and KVL turn circuit diagrams into solvable systems of equations.
Kirchhoff framework
$$\sum I_{in}=\sum I_{out},\qquad \sum V=0 \text{ (closed loop)}$$
ConfigurationCurrentVoltageEquivalent R
SeriesSameDivides$R_{eq}=\sum R$
ParallelDividesSame$1/R_{eq}=\sum 1/R$

Solved examples

Example 1

8 V source with 2 Ω and 6 Ω series: $I=1$ A; drops are 2 V and 6 V.

Example 2

Parallel 3 Ω and 6 Ω on 12 V: branch currents 4 A and 2 A, total 6 A.

Example 3

Node: 5 A enters, 1.4 A and 2.1 A leave. Third leaving current = 1.5 A.

Exam-style practice
  1. 24 V source with resistors 5 Ω and 7 Ω in series. Find current and each drop.
    Answer
    $I=2$ A, drops 10 V and 14 V.
  2. Parallel branches 4 Ω, 8 Ω, 16 Ω on 32 V. Find each branch current and total.
    Answer
    $I_1=8$ A, $I_2=4$ A, $I_3=2$ A, total 14 A.
  3. KCL node: 3 A and 2.4 A enter, 1.1 A leaves plus unknown $I_x$. Find $I_x$.
    Answer
    $I_x=4.3$ A leaving.
  4. Concept + math: one branch resistance in parallel is halved. What happens to total source current?
    Answer
    $R_{eq}$ decreases, so total current increases at fixed voltage.
  5. A loop has source 15 V and resistor drops 4 V, 3 V, and $V_x$. Find $V_x$.
    Answer
    $V_x=8$ V by KVL.
Topic 4 · Simplify complex networks
Superposition, Thevenin, Norton
These theorems reduce multi-source networks to solvable single equivalents.
Thevenin/Norton conversion
$$V_{th}=I_N R_{th},\qquad I_N=\frac{V_{th}}{R_{th}}$$

Superposition: evaluate one independent source at a time (others deactivated), then add responses.

Solved examples

Example 1

$V_{th}=12$ V, $R_{th}=4\Omega$, load 8 Ω. Load current $=12/(4+8)=1$ A.

Example 2

Equivalent Norton current for above: $I_N=12/4=3$ A.

Example 3

For max load power in DC resistive network: $R_L=R_{th}$.

Exam-style practice
  1. A network has $V_{th}=18$ V and $R_{th}=6\Omega$. Find Norton equivalent.
    Answer
    $I_N=3$ A, $R_N=6\Omega$.
  2. With same network and load 6 Ω, find load current and power.
    Answer
    $I_L=18/(6+6)=1.5$ A; $P_L=I_L^2R_L=13.5$ W.
  3. Concept + math: if $R_L$ increases far above $R_{th}$, what happens to load current?
    Answer
    It decreases toward zero since $I=V_{th}/(R_{th}+R_L)$.
  4. Two source contributions to a branch current are +1.8 A and -0.5 A by superposition. Net current?
    Answer
    1.3 A.
  5. Given $I_N=5$ A and $R_N=2\Omega$, find open-circuit voltage.
    Answer
    $V_{th}=I_N R_N=10$ V.
Topic 5 · Energy storage elements
Capacitance and Inductance
Capacitors store electric field energy; inductors store magnetic field energy.
Storage equations
$$Q=CV,\quad U_C=\frac12CV^2,\quad V_L=L\frac{di}{dt},\quad U_L=\frac12LI^2$$
Connection: capacitors resist sudden voltage change; inductors resist sudden current change.

Solved examples

Example 1

10 µF capacitor at 20 V stores $Q=200$ µC and $U=2.0$ mJ.

Example 2

2 mH inductor carrying 3 A stores $U=\frac12(0.002)(9)=9$ mJ.

Example 3

If inductor current rises from 1 A to 4 A in 2 ms and $L=5$ mH, $V=L\Delta i/\Delta t=7.5$ V.

Exam-style practice
  1. Find charge on 47 µF capacitor at 12 V.
    Answer
    $Q=564$ µC.
  2. Find capacitor energy for 22 µF at 15 V.
    Answer
    $U=2.48$ mJ.
  3. 1 H inductor current changes from 0.2 A to 0.8 A in 0.05 s. Find inductor voltage.
    Answer
    $V=L\Delta i/\Delta t=12$ V.
  4. Concept + math: if capacitor voltage doubles, by what factor does stored energy change?
    Answer
    By factor 4 ($U\propto V^2$).
  5. For fixed current, if inductance triples, what happens to stored magnetic energy?
    Answer
    Triples ($U_L\propto L$).
Topic 6 · Time-domain behavior
RC and RL Transients
Transient response explains startup and switching behavior in real electrical systems.
First-order transients
$$v_C(t)=V_s\left(1-e^{-t/RC}\right),\qquad i_L(t)=I_f\left(1-e^{-t/\tau}\right),\ \tau=\frac{L}{R}$$
Interactive Simulator · RC Charging Curve
Vs 12 V
R (kΩ) 10 kΩ
C (µF) 47 µF

Solved examples

Example 1

For $R=10$ kΩ and $C=100$ µF, $\tau=1$ s. At $t=\tau$, capacitor voltage reaches 63.2% of final.

Example 2

RL with $L=20$ mH and $R=4\Omega$: $\tau=L/R=5$ ms.

Example 3

At $t=3\tau$, first-order response is about 95% settled; at $5\tau$, >99%.

Exam-style practice
  1. For $R=2$ kΩ and $C=50$ µF, find time constant.
    Answer
    $\tau=RC=0.1$ s.
  2. 12 V RC charging with $\tau=0.2$ s. Find $v_C$ at $t=0.2$ s.
    Answer
    $v_C=12(1-e^{-1})=7.58$ V.
  3. RL circuit final current 3 A, $\tau=10$ ms. Find current at 20 ms.
    Answer
    $i=3(1-e^{-2})=2.59$ A.
  4. Concept + math: after how many time constants can RC be treated as fully charged for most engineering calculations?
    Answer
    About 5 time constants (99.3%).
  5. If R doubles and C halves, what happens to $\tau$?
    Answer
    No change: $\tau=RC$ remains same.
Topic 7 · Frequency domain
AC Sinusoids, Phasors, and Impedance
AC analysis becomes efficient when differential equations are replaced by phasor algebra.
AC impedance
$$X_L=\omega L,\quad X_C=\frac{1}{\omega C},\quad Z=R+j(X_L-X_C),\quad |Z|=\sqrt{R^2+(X_L-X_C)^2}$$
Interactive Simulator · RLC Impedance Explorer
R 10 Ω
XL 25 Ω
XC 10 Ω

Solved examples

Example 1

$R=8\Omega$, $X_L=6\Omega$, $X_C=2\Omega$. Net reactance = 4 Ω, $|Z|=\sqrt{80}=8.94\Omega$.

Example 2

At resonance, $X_L=X_C$ so impedance is purely resistive ($Z=R$).

Example 3

For 120 V rms and $|Z|=20\Omega$, current is 6 A rms.

Exam-style practice
  1. Given $R=12\Omega$, $X_L=16\Omega$, $X_C=4\Omega$, find $|Z|$.
    Answer
    Net X = 12 Ω, so $|Z|=\sqrt{12^2+12^2}=16.97\Omega$.
  2. For 230 V rms source and $|Z|=46\Omega$, find current.
    Answer
    $I=5$ A rms.
  3. If frequency doubles, what happens to $X_L$ and $X_C$?
    Answer
    $X_L$ doubles, $X_C$ halves.
  4. Concept + math: in an inductive circuit ($X_L>X_C$), does current lead or lag voltage?
    Answer
    Current lags voltage.
  5. Find phase angle for $R=10\Omega$, net reactance $=10\Omega$.
    Answer
    $\phi=\tan^{-1}(10/10)=45^\circ$.
Topic 8 · Efficiency and grid quality
AC Power, Reactive Power, Power Factor
Real systems are rated not only by watts, but by how effectively apparent power is converted to useful work.
Power triangle
$$P=VI\cos\phi,\quad Q=VI\sin\phi,\quad S=VI,\quad \text{pf}=\cos\phi=\frac{P}{S}$$
Interactive Simulator · Power Factor Triangle
P (kW) 8
pf 0.80

Solved examples

Example 1

Single-phase 240 V, 10 A, pf 0.8: $P=1920$ W, $S=2400$ VA, $Q=1440$ var.

Example 2

Improving pf from 0.7 to 0.95 at same real power reduces line current significantly, cutting losses.

Example 3

If $P=5$ kW and pf=0.6, then $S=P/pf=8.33$ kVA.

Exam-style practice
  1. 240 V load draws 15 A at pf 0.9. Find real power.
    Answer
    $P=240\times15\times0.9=3240$ W.
  2. Given $P=4$ kW and pf 0.8, find apparent and reactive power.
    Answer
    $S=5$ kVA, $Q=\sqrt{S^2-P^2}=3$ kvar.
  3. Concept + math: for fixed P and V, raising pf from 0.7 to 1.0 changes line current by what factor?
    Answer
    $I\propto 1/\text{pf}$, so current becomes 0.7 of original (30% reduction).
  4. A 10 kVA load operates at pf 0.6. Find real power.
    Answer
    $P=6$ kW.
  5. Why is low power factor costly in distribution systems?
    Answer
    Higher current for same real power means larger losses ($I^2R$), voltage drop, and equipment oversizing.
Topic 9 · Engineering applications
Motors, Generators, and Practical Electrical Systems
DPEN104 links circuit fundamentals to electromechanical energy conversion in real devices.

Application bridge

Motors convert electrical to mechanical power. Generators do the reverse. Both rely on electromagnetic induction and magnetic force principles from earlier topics.

Power conversion
$$\eta=\frac{P_{out}}{P_{in}},\qquad P_{mech}=\tau\omega$$
Connection insight: circuits are not separate from machines; they are the electrical boundary conditions that control torque, speed, and losses.

Solved examples

Example 1

Motor input 1.2 kW, output 900 W: efficiency = 75%.

Example 2

Generator delivers 20 A at 230 V. Electrical output = 4.6 kW.

Example 3

Mechanical output with torque 12 N·m at 150 rad/s: $P=1800$ W.

Exam-style practice
  1. A motor draws 10 A from 240 V, pf 0.85. Input real power?
    Answer
    $P_{in}=2040$ W.
  2. If same motor has 82% efficiency, find shaft output power.
    Answer
    $P_{out}=0.82\times2040=1673$ W.
  3. Generator outputs 3.0 kW at 90% efficiency. Required mechanical input?
    Answer
    $P_{in}=3.33$ kW.
  4. Concept + math: if copper losses double while input stays fixed, what happens to efficiency?
    Answer
    Output power decreases, so efficiency decreases.
  5. Motor produces 25 N·m at 120 rad/s. Find mechanical power.
    Answer
    $P=3000$ W.
Topic 10 · Lab to exam performance
Measurement, Simulation, and High-Yield Recall
DPEN104 is not only solving equations; it includes measurement accuracy, interpretation, and reporting.

Measurement workflow

  • Set expected range before connecting multimeter or scope.
  • Record measured value + uncertainty + setup condition.
  • Compare theory/simulation/measurement using percent error.
  • Discuss likely causes: tolerance, loading, wiring, instrument bandwidth.
Useful reporting formulas
$$\%\text{error}=\frac{|x_{meas}-x_{theory}|}{x_{theory}}\times100\%,\qquad \%\text{difference}=\frac{|x_1-x_2|}{(x_1+x_2)/2}\times100\%$$
Things to recall fast in exam: KCL/KVL sign consistency, unit conversions (mA, µF, mH), time constants, AC power triangle, and when to use RMS values.

Solved examples

Example 1

Theory current 2.00 A, measured 1.90 A: error = 5%.

Example 2

RMS to peak conversion: 120 V rms sinusoid has $V_{peak}=120\sqrt2=169.7$ V.

Example 3

Capacitor marked 100 µF ±10% can be anywhere from 90 to 110 µF.

Exam-style practice
  1. Theory voltage 15.0 V, measured 14.4 V. Find percent error.
    Answer
    4.0%.
  2. Sinusoidal current is 5 A rms. Find peak and peak-to-peak.
    Answer
    $I_{pk}=7.07$ A, $I_{pp}=14.14$ A.
  3. Concept + math: resistor tolerance ±5%, nominal 220 Ω. Min and max values?
    Answer
    209 Ω to 231 Ω.
  4. An RC lab gives theory $\tau=0.22$ s and measured $\tau=0.24$ s. Percent error?
    Answer
    $|0.24-0.22|/0.22\times100=9.09\%$.
  5. In one sentence, explain why simulation and experiment may differ even with correct equations.
    Answer
    Real components and instruments are non-ideal (tolerance, parasitics, loading), while base simulations often assume ideal values.
