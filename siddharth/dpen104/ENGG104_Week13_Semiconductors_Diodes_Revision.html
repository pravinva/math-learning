1 Semiconductor basics

Semiconductors sit between conductors and insulators, and crucially, their conductivity can be deliberately controlled through doping.

Key idea

Doping adds impurity atoms to a semiconductor (silicon or germanium) to create N-type material (excess free electrons, from pentavalent dopants like phosphorus) or P-type material (excess "holes," from trivalent dopants like boron).

Practice 1

What is the main difference between N-type and P-type material, in terms of majority charge carriers?

Solution

N-type has an excess of free electrons (negative charge carriers) as majority carriers; P-type has an excess of "holes" (effectively positive charge carriers) as majority carriers.

Practice 2

Silicon (Group 4) is doped with Phosphorus (Group 5). What type results, and why?

Solution

N-type. Phosphorus has 5 valence electrons but silicon's lattice only needs 4 for bonding, leaving one extra free electron per Phosphorus atom.

Practice 3

Silicon is doped with Boron (Group 3). What type results, and why?

Solution

P-type. Boron has only 3 valence electrons, one short of what's needed for a complete bond, creating a "hole" per Boron atom, an effective positive charge carrier.

Practice 4

Why are semiconductors more useful for building electronic devices than pure conductors or pure insulators?

Solution

Their conductivity can be precisely controlled, and even switched on/off, via doping, applied voltage, or temperature, unlike a conductor (always highly conductive) or an insulator (never conductive). This tunability is what makes transistors, diodes, and all modern electronics possible.

Practice 5

Explain, conceptually, what a "hole" represents in P-type material (it isn't a literal particle).

Solution

A hole represents the ABSENCE of an electron in a bond where one would normally be. It behaves like a positive charge carrier because when a nearby electron moves to fill that gap, the "hole" effectively moves in the opposite direction, mimicking the motion of a positive charge.

2 PN junctions

Joining P-type and N-type material creates the basic building block of every diode and transistor.

Key idea

At the junction, electrons and holes combine, creating a depletion region with no free carriers, and a built-in barrier voltage (≈0.7 V for silicon, ≈0.3 V for germanium) that must be exceeded before current flows.

Practice 6

What is the "depletion region," and how does it form?

Solution

A narrow zone around the junction with no free charge carriers. It forms because electrons from the N-side diffuse across and combine with holes on the P-side (and vice versa) near the boundary, leaving behind fixed charged ions and depleting that region of free carriers.

Practice 7

What is the typical barrier voltage for a silicon PN junction?

Solution

Approximately 0.7 V.

Practice 8

What is the typical barrier voltage for a germanium PN junction?

Solution

Approximately 0.3 V.

Practice 9

Explain, in terms of the depletion region, why a PN junction doesn't conduct until the barrier voltage is exceeded (forward bias).

Solution

Below the barrier voltage, the applied forward voltage isn't strong enough to overcome the built-in electric field across the depletion region. Once it exceeds the barrier voltage, the depletion region narrows enough that charge carriers can cross the junction freely, and current flows.

Practice 10

What happens to the depletion region's width under reverse bias, versus forward bias?

Solution

The depletion region widens under reverse bias (blocking current more strongly), whereas forward bias narrows it, eventually allowing conduction once the barrier voltage is overcome.

3 The ideal diode model

For basic circuit analysis, a diode is simplified to a perfect one-way switch.

Key idea

Forward biased: zero resistance (acts as a short/wire). Reverse biased: infinite resistance (acts as an open circuit). No voltage drop, no barrier voltage, in this idealised model.

Worked example 1

A 10 V source, 100 Ω resistor, and ideal diode in series, forward biased. Find the current.

The diode acts as a short (0 V drop): \(I=10/100=0.1\text{ A}=100\text{ mA}\).

Worked example 2

Same circuit, but the diode is now reverse biased. Find the current.

The diode acts as an open circuit: \(I=0\text{ A}\) (no current flows at all).

Practice 11

A 5 V source, 50 Ω resistor, ideal diode (forward biased) in series. Find the current.

Solution

\(I=5/50=0.1\text{ A}=100\text{ mA}\).

Practice 12

Same circuit, but the diode is reverse biased. Find the current.

Solution

\(I=0\text{ A}\).

Practice 13

Two ideal diodes in series with a resistor, both forward biased, source \(=9\text{ V}\), \(R=45\ \Omega\). Find the current.

Solution

Both diodes conduct as ideal shorts: \(I=9/45=0.2\text{ A}=200\text{ mA}\).

Practice 14

An ideal diode is in PARALLEL with \(R=100\ \Omega\), both across a 12 V source, diode forward biased. Find the voltage across the resistor.

Solution

\(0\text{ V}\). Since the ideal diode acts as a short (0 V) when forward biased, and it's in parallel with \(R\), it forces the voltage across BOTH to be 0 V, the diode shorts out the resistor entirely.

Practice 15

Explain the key difference between the "ideal diode" model and a real diode's actual behaviour.

Solution

The ideal model assumes zero voltage drop when conducting and infinite resistance when blocking (a perfect switch). A real diode has a small forward voltage drop (≈0.7 V for silicon) when conducting, and a very small leakage current when reverse biased. The ideal model is a simplification useful for basic circuit analysis.

Practice 16

Why is the ideal diode model a useful starting point before studying real diodes, rectifiers, and PN junctions in depth?

Solution

It captures the diode's most important practical behaviour, current flows freely in one direction and is blocked in the other, without the added complexity of exact voltage drops or nonlinear characteristics, letting you analyse basic circuits (like simple rectifiers) quickly and build intuition before adding real-world detail.

Practice 17Wk 2–3

Two resistors, 100 Ω and 200 Ω, in parallel, fed by 12 V. Find the total current.

Solution

\(R_t=(100\times200)/300=66.67\ \Omega\). \(I=12/66.67=0.18\text{ A}=180\text{ mA}\).

Practice 18Wk 6

A 47 µF and 100 µF capacitor in series. Find \(C_t\).

Solution

\(1/C_t=1/47+1/100=0.03128\). \(C_t=31.98\ \mu\text{F}\).

Practice 19Wk 8

A sine wave has \(V_m=340\text{ V}\) (a typical mains peak). Find \(V_{rms}\).

Solution

\(V_{rms}=340/1.414=240.4\text{ V}\), matching a standard 240 V mains supply.

Practice 20Wk 10

A series RC circuit has \(R=40\ \Omega\), \(X_C=30\ \Omega\). Find \(|Z|\), \(\theta\), and \(PF\).

Solution

\(|Z|=\sqrt{1600+900}=50\ \Omega\). \(\theta=\tan^{-1}(-30/40)=-36.87^\circ\). \(PF=\cos(36.87^\circ)=0.8\).

Practice 21Wk 13

An ideal diode and 200 Ω resistor are in series with a 10 V source (forward biased). Find the current and power dissipated in the resistor.

Solution

\(I=10/200=0.05\text{ A}=50\text{ mA}\). \(P=I^2R=0.05^2(200)=0.5\text{ W}\).

Practice 22Capstone

Name ONE concept from DC circuits (Weeks 1–7) that reappears, in modified form, in AC circuit analysis (Weeks 8–12). Explain the connection.

Solution

Ohm's Law (\(V=IR\)) reappears as \(V=IZ\) in AC analysis. Resistance \(R\) is generalised to impedance \(Z\), which incorporates reactance and phase, but the fundamental relationship, voltage equals current times opposition, is exactly the same underlying idea, just extended to handle the phase-shifting behaviour of capacitors and inductors.

Thirteen weeks, from a single electron's charge through to the full AC power-and-phase picture of a semiconductor rectifier circuit, all built on the same handful of ideas: Ohm's Law, Kirchhoff's Laws, and the series/parallel reduction workflow, reapplied over and over to increasingly rich components.
