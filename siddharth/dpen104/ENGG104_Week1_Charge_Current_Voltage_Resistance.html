0 Foundations refresher: work, energy, potential, power

Before charge/current/voltage formulas, lock in the physical meanings and units. These are the quantities that appear repeatedly in ENGG104 and explain why electrical equations look the way they do.

Core meanings

Work is energy transferred when a force causes displacement. In mechanics: \(W=F d\) (for constant force along motion).

Energy is the capacity to do work (stored or available). Batteries store chemical energy and deliver electrical energy.

Potential difference (voltage) is energy transfer per unit charge: \(V=\dfrac{W}{Q}\). It tells you how much energy each coulomb gains/loses between two points.

Power is the rate of energy transfer: \(P=\dfrac{W}{t}\). In circuits this becomes \(P=VI\).

Units and dimensions (SI)

Work / Energy: joule (J). \(1\ \text{J}=1\ \text{N}\cdot\text{m}=1\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2\cdot\text{s}^{-2}\).

Charge: coulomb (C). \(1\ \text{C}=1\ \text{A}\cdot\text{s}\).

Potential difference: volt (V). \(1\ \text{V}=1\ \text{J}/\text{C}\).

Power: watt (W). \(1\ \text{W}=1\ \text{J}/\text{s}=1\ \text{V}\cdot\text{A}\).

Why are work and energy both in joules?

Because they are the same physical quantity viewed from two angles:

- Energy is what a system has (stored amount).

- Work is energy in transfer (process of moving that amount).

If a battery gives \(24\ \text{J}\) to a load, the load receives \(24\ \text{J}\) of energy and the source has done \(24\ \text{J}\) of work on charges. Same unit, same quantity, different viewpoint.

Power and its relation to work (quick check)

If \(120\ \text{J}\) is transferred in \(10\ \text{s}\), then \(P=W/t=120/10=12\ \text{W}\).

In electrical form, \(P=VI\). For example, \(6\ \text{V}\) across a device with \(2\ \text{A}\) gives \(P=12\ \text{W}\), consistent with the same energy-rate idea.

1 Electric charge & current

Current is nothing more than charge in motion, how much charge passes a point per second.

Key idea

$$I=\frac{Q}{t}$$ where \(Q\) is charge in coulombs (C), \(t\) is time in seconds, and \(I\) is current in amperes (A). One amp is one coulomb per second.

Worked example 1

12 coulombs of charge pass a point in 4 seconds. Find the current.

\(I=Q/t=12/4=3\text{ A}\).

Worked example 2

A current of 2 A flows for 30 seconds. Find the total charge that passed.

\(Q=It=2(30)=60\text{ C}\).

Practice 1

20 C pass a point in 5 s. Find the current.

Solution

\(I=20/5=4\text{ A}\).

Practice 2

A current of 0.5 A flows for 10 s. Find the charge.

Solution

\(Q=0.5(10)=5\text{ C}\).

Practice 3

100 mC pass a point in 0.02 s. Find the current in amps.

Solution

\(I=0.1/0.02=5\text{ A}\).

Practice 4

Explain what a "steady" (DC) current of 3 A physically means, using \(I=Q/t\).

Solution

3 coulombs of charge pass a given point in the circuit every single second, consistently, without changing over time.

Practice 5

A charge of 5 C moves through a wire in 2.5 s. Find the current in mA.

Solution

\(I=5/2.5=2\text{ A}=2000\text{ mA}\).

2 Voltage & EMF

Voltage is energy per unit charge, how much "push" is available to move each coulomb through the circuit. EMF is the source's full theoretical push; terminal voltage is what's actually available once current is flowing.

Key idea

$$V=\frac{W}{Q}$$ (volts = joules/coulomb). For a real source: $$V_{terminal} = EMF - I r_{internal}$$

Worked example 1

A source does 24 J of work moving 4 C of charge. Find its voltage.

\(V=W/Q=24/4=6\text{ V}\).

Worked example 2

A 9 V battery has internal resistance 0.5 Ω. Under a load drawing 0.4 A, find the terminal voltage.

\(V_{terminal}=9-0.4(0.5)=9-0.2=8.8\text{ V}\).

Practice 6

A source does 50 J of work moving 10 C. Find its voltage.

Solution

\(V=50/10=5\text{ V}\).

Practice 7

A 12 V battery, internal resistance 0.2 Ω, supplies 2 A. Find the terminal voltage.

Solution

\(V=12-2(0.2)=11.6\text{ V}\).

Practice 8

Explain the difference between EMF and terminal voltage.

Solution

EMF is the source's full theoretical voltage with no losses. Terminal voltage is what's actually measurable at its terminals once current flows, reduced by the drop across the source's own internal resistance.

Practice 9

A 1.5 V battery (internal resistance 0.1 Ω) is short-circuited. Estimate the current.

Solution

\(I=EMF/r=1.5/0.1=15\text{ A}\), very high, characteristic of a short circuit.

Practice 10

If a source has ZERO internal resistance (ideal source), how does terminal voltage compare to EMF?

Solution

They're always equal. With \(r=0\), there's no internal voltage drop regardless of current drawn, so terminal voltage equals EMF for an ideal source, no matter the load.

3 Voltage sources: ideal vs real

An ideal source maintains constant voltage no matter what current is drawn. A real source is exactly that ideal source plus a small series (internal) resistance, so voltage sags as more current is drawn.

Worked example 1

A real 6 V battery, \(r=0.3\ \Omega\). Find the terminal voltage at \(I=0,\ 1,\ 2\text{ A}\).

\(I=0\): \(V=6-0=6\text{ V}\) (open-circuit voltage equals full EMF).

\(I=1\): \(V=6-1(0.3)=5.7\text{ V}\). \(\quad I=2\): \(V=6-2(0.3)=5.4\text{ V}\).

Worked example 2

The same battery is connected to a 5.7 Ω load. Find the current and terminal voltage.

\(I=\dfrac{EMF}{r+R_{load}}=\dfrac{6}{0.3+5.7}=\dfrac{6}{6}=1\text{ A}\).

\(V_{terminal}=IR_{load}=1(5.7)=5.7\text{ V}\), matching the \(I=1\) case above.

Practice 11

A 12 V source, \(r=0.5\ \Omega\), load \(=5.5\ \Omega\). Find the current and terminal voltage.

Solution

\(I=12/(0.5+5.5)=12/6=2\text{ A}\). \(V=2(5.5)=11\text{ V}\).

Practice 12

A source shows 8 V open-circuit and 7.2 V when supplying 4 A. Find its internal resistance.

Solution

\(r=(8-7.2)/4=0.2\ \Omega\).

Practice 13

Explain why a car battery's voltage "sags" noticeably when the starter motor draws a very large current.

Solution

The starter motor draws a very large current, and \(V_{terminal}=EMF-Ir\) drops significantly at high \(I\), even a small internal resistance \(r\) causes a large voltage sag once the current is large enough.

Practice 14

For Practice 11's circuit, if the load is disconnected entirely, what does a voltmeter across the source read?

Solution

The full EMF, 12 V, since no current flows and there's no internal voltage drop.

Practice 15

A source has \(EMF=10\text{ V}\), \(r=2\ \Omega\). At what current does the terminal voltage drop to exactly half the EMF?

Solution

\(5=10-I(2) \Rightarrow I=2.5\text{ A}\).

4 Resistance & conductance

Resistance opposes current flow; conductance is simply its reciprocal, how easily current flows.

Key idea

$$G=\frac1R \text{ (siemens, S)}, \qquad R=\frac{\rho L}{A}$$

where \(\rho\) is resistivity (a material property), \(L\) is length, and \(A\) is cross-sectional area.

Worked example 1

A copper wire (\(\rho=1.72\times10^{-8}\ \Omega\cdot\text{m}\)), length 10 m, area \(2\times10^{-6}\text{ m}^2\). Find its resistance.

\(R=\dfrac{\rho L}{A}=\dfrac{1.72\times10^{-8}(10)}{2\times10^{-6}}=0.086\ \Omega\).

Worked example 2

A resistor has \(R=250\ \Omega\). Find its conductance.

\(G=1/250=0.004\text{ S}=4\text{ mS}\).

Practice 16

A wire, \(\rho=2.5\times10^{-8}\ \Omega\cdot\text{m}\), length 5 m, area \(1\times10^{-6}\text{ m}^2\). Find \(R\).

Solution

\(R=\dfrac{2.5\times10^{-8}(5)}{1\times10^{-6}}=0.125\ \Omega\).

Practice 17

A resistor has \(G=0.02\text{ S}\). Find \(R\).

Solution

\(R=1/0.02=50\ \Omega\).

Practice 18

If a wire's length doubles (same material, same area), what happens to its resistance?

Solution

Resistance doubles — \(R\) is directly proportional to length.

Practice 19

If a wire's cross-sectional area doubles (same length, same material), what happens to resistance?

Solution

Resistance halves — \(R\) is inversely proportional to area (more area means more room for current to flow, effectively).

Practice 20

A resistor's conductance is 0.005 S. Find the current through it when 20 V is applied, using \(I=VG\).

Solution

\(I=VG=20(0.005)=0.1\text{ A}=100\text{ mA}\).
