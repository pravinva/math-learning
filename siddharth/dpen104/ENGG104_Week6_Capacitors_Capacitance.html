Capacitors & Capacitance

A capacitor stores energy in an electric field rather than dissipating it like a resistor. This week covers the basic C=Q/V relationship, how capacitors combine in series and parallel (opposite to resistors!), and the RC time constant governing how fast they charge.

1 Capacitance basics

Capacitance measures how much charge a component can store for a given voltage.

Key idea

$$C=\frac{Q}{V}$$ (farads, F = coulombs/volt). Most practical capacitors are measured in microfarads (µF) or picofarads (pF).

Worked example 1

A capacitor stores 500 µC when 10 V is applied. Find its capacitance.

\(C=Q/V=500\times10^{-6}/10=50\ \mu\text{F}\).

Worked example 2

A 100 µF capacitor has 12 V applied. Find the charge stored.

\(Q=CV=100\times10^{-6}(12)=1200\ \mu\text{C}=1.2\text{ mC}\).

Practice 1

A capacitor stores 200 µC at 5 V. Find \(C\).

Solution

\(C=200/5=40\ \mu\text{F}\).

Practice 2

A 47 µF capacitor has 9 V across it. Find the charge.

Solution

\(Q=47(9)=423\ \mu\text{C}\).

Practice 3

If the voltage across a capacitor doubles (fixed \(C\)), what happens to the stored charge?

Solution

The charge also doubles, since \(Q=CV\) with \(C\) fixed, \(Q\) is directly proportional to \(V\).

Practice 4

A capacitor is rated 220 µF, max voltage 25 V. Find the maximum charge it can safely store.

Solution

\(Q_{max}=220(25)=5500\ \mu\text{C}=5.5\text{ mC}\).

Practice 5

Explain, conceptually, what physically happens inside a capacitor as it charges.

Solution

Charge accumulates on two separated conductive plates, positive on one plate and equal negative on the other, separated by an insulator (the dielectric), creating an electric field between them that stores energy.

2 Capacitors in series & parallel

Here's the twist: capacitors combine the opposite way to resistors. Parallel capacitors add directly; series capacitors combine reciprocally.

Key idea

Parallel: $$C_t=C_1+C_2+\cdots+C_n$$

Series: $$\frac1{C_t}=\frac1{C_1}+\frac1{C_2}+\cdots+\frac1{C_n}$$

Worked example 1

Three capacitors in parallel: 10 µF, 22 µF, 47 µF. Find \(C_t\).

\(C_t=10+22+47=79\ \mu\text{F}\).

Worked example 2

Two capacitors in series: 20 µF and 30 µF. Find \(C_t\).

\(1/C_t=1/20+1/30=0.05+0.0333=0.0833\). \(C_t=12\ \mu\text{F}\).

Practice 6

Two capacitors in parallel: 15 µF, 25 µF. Find \(C_t\).

Solution

\(C_t=15+25=40\ \mu\text{F}\).

Practice 7

Two capacitors in series: 10 µF, 15 µF. Find \(C_t\).

Solution

\(1/C_t=1/10+1/15=0.1667\). \(C_t=6\ \mu\text{F}\).

Practice 8

Three equal 12 µF capacitors in series. Find \(C_t\).

Solution

\(C_t=12/3=4\ \mu\text{F}\).

Practice 9

Explain why capacitors in parallel ADD, unlike resistors, using the idea of "more plate area."

Solution

Parallel capacitors effectively increase the total plate area available to store charge at the same voltage, so the combined capacitance is larger, a direct sum, exactly opposite to resistors, where parallel paths reduce resistance.

Practice 10

Explain why series capacitors combine like resistors in parallel (reciprocal sum), using "effectively increasing plate separation."

Solution

Series capacitors share the same charge but each contributes its own voltage drop; stacking them effectively increases the total separation between the outermost plates for a given charge, reducing overall capacitance, mirroring how parallel resistors reduce resistance by adding more current paths.

Practice 11

A 100 µF capacitor is placed in series with another 100 µF capacitor. Find \(C_t\), and compare it to a single capacitor's value.

Solution

\(C_t=100/2=50\ \mu\text{F}\), half of a single capacitor's value. Series always reduces total capacitance, the opposite of resistors in series.

3 Charging, time constants & energy storage

A capacitor doesn't charge instantly, the RC time constant governs how quickly it approaches its final voltage.

Key idea

$$\tau=RC \text{ (seconds)}, \qquad V_C(t)=E\left(1-e^{-t/\tau}\right), \qquad E_{stored}=\frac12CV^2.$$

After \(5\tau\), a capacitor is considered fully charged (over 99%).

Worked example 1

A 100 µF capacitor charges through a 10 kΩ resistor. Find the time constant.

\(\tau=RC=10{,}000(100\times10^{-6})=1\text{ s}\).

Worked example 2

For that circuit with \(E=12\text{ V}\), find the capacitor voltage after one time constant, and the energy stored once fully charged.

\(V_C(\tau)=12(1-e^{-1})=12(0.632)=7.58\text{ V}\).

Fully charged (\(V_C=12\text{ V}\)): \(E_{stored}=\dfrac12(100\times10^{-6})(144)=0.0072\text{ J}=7.2\text{ mJ}\).

Practice 12

A 50 µF capacitor charges through 20 kΩ. Find \(\tau\).

Solution

\(\tau=20{,}000(50\times10^{-6})=1\text{ s}\).

Practice 13

For Practice 12's circuit, \(E=9\text{ V}\), find \(V_C\) at \(t=\tau\).

Solution

\(V_C=9(1-e^{-1})=9(0.632)=5.69\text{ V}\).

Practice 14

Find the energy stored in a 200 µF capacitor charged to 15 V.

Solution

\(E=\dfrac12(200\times10^{-6})(225)=0.0225\text{ J}=22.5\text{ mJ}\).

Practice 15

How long, in terms of \(\tau\), is a capacitor generally considered "fully charged"?

Solution

\(5\tau\) (about 99.3% of full voltage).

Practice 16

A 10 µF capacitor charges through a 1 kΩ resistor. Find \(\tau\) in ms, and estimate the time to full charge.

Solution

\(\tau=1000(10\times10^{-6})=0.01\text{ s}=10\text{ ms}\). Full charge \(\approx5\tau=50\text{ ms}\).

Practice 17

Explain why energy stored is proportional to \(V^2\), connecting this to why doubling the voltage quadruples the stored energy.

Solution

Since \(E=\dfrac12CV^2\), energy depends on the square of voltage. Doubling \(V\) makes \(V^2\) four times larger, so stored energy quadruples, not just doubles.

Where to go from here. Week 7 introduces the inductor, the "opposite" of a capacitor in many ways: it stores energy in a magnetic field, and its combination rules (series adds, parallel reduces) mirror resistors rather than capacitors.
