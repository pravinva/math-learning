AC Circuits & Devices
The mains supply, every motor, every transformer and every radio runs on alternating current — voltages and currents that vary sinusoidally with time. This guide gives you the language of AC (waveforms and phasors), the AC version of resistance (impedance), how power really works when current and voltage are out of step, and finishes with the semiconductor diode that converts AC back to DC.

Topic 1 · Week 8 · Boylestad Ch 13
Sinusoidal Waveforms
The shape that nature and generators love — and the numbers that describe it.
Why sine waves? Because a rotating generator coil naturally produces one, and because a sinusoid is the only waveform whose shape is unchanged by capacitors and inductors (their derivatives and integrals are still sinusoids). Master the anatomy of one sine wave — amplitude, frequency, phase, RMS — and you can describe every AC voltage and current in the course.
DefinitionThe general sinusoid▶
\[ v(t) = V_m\sin(\omega t + \theta) \]

\(V_m\) = peak amplitude; \(\omega = 2\pi f\) = angular frequency (rad/s); \(\theta\) = phase angle (rad or degrees).

\[ \omega = 2\pi f = \frac{2\pi}{T} \qquad f = \frac{1}{T} \]

\(T\) = period (seconds per cycle); \(f\) = frequency (hertz, cycles per second). Australian mains: \(f=50\,\text{Hz}\), so \(T=20\,\text{ms}\).

PatternPeak, peak-to-peak, average & RMS▶
MeasureFormula (sinusoid)Meaning
Peak\(V_m\)Maximum height
Peak-to-peak\(2V_m\)Top to bottom
Average (full cycle)\(0\)Symmetric → cancels
Average (half cycle)\(0.637\,V_m\)\(=2V_m/\pi\)
RMS\(0.707\,V_m = V_m/\sqrt2\)Equivalent DC heating value
RMS is the value that matters. "230 V mains" is the RMS — the peak is actually \(230\times\sqrt2 \approx 325\,\text{V}\). Meters and power calculations all use RMS.
Worked Example 1 — reading a sinusoid

A voltage is \(v(t) = 170\sin(377t)\,\text{V}\). Find the peak, RMS, frequency and period.

1Peak: \(V_m = 170\,\text{V}\).
2RMS: \(V_{rms} = 170/\sqrt2 = 120\,\text{V}\) (US mains).
3\(\omega=377\Rightarrow f=\omega/2\pi = 377/6.283 = 60\,\text{Hz}\).
4\(T = 1/f = 1/60 = 16.7\,\text{ms}\).
ANSWER \(V_m=170\,\text{V}\), \(V_{rms}=120\,\text{V}\), \(f=60\,\text{Hz}\), \(T=16.7\,\text{ms}\).
Worked Example 2 — phase relationship

\(v_1 = 10\sin(\omega t)\) and \(v_2 = 8\sin(\omega t - 30^\circ)\). Describe their phase relationship.

\(v_2\) reaches its peak \(30^\circ\) after \(v_1\), so \(v_2\) lags \(v_1\) by 30° (equivalently, \(v_1\) leads \(v_2\) by 30°).

ANSWER \(v_2\) lags \(v_1\) by \(30^\circ\).
Quick PracticeTap to reveal worked answers
1A 50 Hz supply — find its period and angular frequency.▶
\(T=20\,\text{ms}\), \(\omega=314\,\text{rad/s}\). \(T=1/50=0.02\,\text{s}\); \(\omega=2\pi\times50=314\).
2Australian mains is 230 V RMS. What is the peak voltage?▶
≈ 325 V. \(V_m=V_{rms}\sqrt2=230\times1.414=325\,\text{V}\).
3Why is the full-cycle average of a sine wave zero?▶
The positive and negative half-cycles are equal and opposite, so they cancel exactly over one period.
4A 12 V peak sine — what RMS voltage does a multimeter read?▶
≈ 8.49 V. \(12/\sqrt2=8.49\,\text{V}\).
Topic 2 · Week 9 · Boylestad Ch 14
Phasors & Reactance
Turn the calculus of AC into simple vector algebra — and meet the AC "resistance" of L and C.
Doing AC analysis with \(\sin\) and \(\cos\) and their derivatives is exhausting. The breakthrough is the phasor: represent each sinusoid as a rotating vector (a complex number) carrying its amplitude and phase. Then adding sinusoids becomes adding vectors, and differentiating becomes multiplying by \(j\omega\). With phasors, capacitors and inductors get an Ohm's-Law-style "resistance" called reactance — and AC circuits become as easy as DC.
DefinitionPhasor representation▶

A phasor encodes a sinusoid's amplitude and phase (the common \(\omega\) is implicit):

\[ v(t)=V_m\sin(\omega t+\theta) \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \mathbf{V} = V_{rms}\angle\theta \]

Drawn as an arrow of length \(V\) at angle \(\theta\). Phasors that share \(\omega\) can be added like vectors and converted between polar \(V\angle\theta\) and rectangular \(a+jb\) form.

PatternReactance of L and C▶

Reactance is the opposition of an inductor or capacitor to AC, measured in ohms but frequency-dependent:

\[ X_L = \omega L = 2\pi f L \qquad\qquad X_C = \frac{1}{\omega C} = \frac{1}{2\pi f C} \]
LInductor reactance rises with frequency — blocks high frequencies.
CCapacitor reactance falls with frequency — passes high frequencies.
ELI the ICE man — the classic memory aid. In an inductor (L), voltage E leads current I → ELI. In a capacitor (C), current I leads voltage E → ICE. Each by 90° for a pure element.
Worked Example 3 — reactances at 50 Hz

Find \(X_L\) of a 0.1 H inductor and \(X_C\) of a \(10\,\mu\text{F}\) capacitor at 50 Hz.

1\(X_L = 2\pi f L = 2\pi(50)(0.1) = 31.4\,\Omega\).
2\(X_C = \dfrac{1}{2\pi f C} = \dfrac{1}{2\pi(50)(10\times10^{-6})} = \dfrac{1}{3.14\times10^{-3}} = 318\,\Omega\).
ANSWER \(X_L = 31.4\,\Omega\), \(X_C = 318\,\Omega\).
Worked Example 4 — frequency dependence

For the same components, what happens to each reactance if frequency rises to 5 kHz (×100)?

1\(X_L\) scales with \(f\): \(31.4 \times 100 = 3140\,\Omega\) (much higher).
2\(X_C\) scales with \(1/f\): \(318/100 = 3.18\,\Omega\) (much lower).
ANSWER \(X_L\to3140\,\Omega\); \(X_C\to3.18\,\Omega\). This is the basis of all filters.
Quick PracticeTap to reveal worked answers
1\(X_L\) of a 0.2 H inductor at 100 Hz?▶
≈ 126 Ω. \(2\pi(100)(0.2)=125.7\,\Omega\).
2In a pure capacitor, does current lead or lag voltage?▶
Current leads voltage by 90° — the "ICE" of ELI the ICE man.
3As frequency increases, what happens to \(X_C\)?▶
It decreases (\(X_C=1/\omega C\)). A capacitor approaches a short at high frequency.
4What is the phasor for \(v=100\sin(\omega t+45^\circ)\) in RMS polar form?▶
\(70.7\angle45^\circ\,\text{V}\). \(V_{rms}=100/\sqrt2=70.7\,\text{V}\) at 45°.
Topic 3 · Weeks 10-12 · Boylestad Ch 15-16
Impedance & RLC Circuits
The AC generalisation of resistance — combining R, L and C into one complex number.
Impedance \(Z\) is the single most important quantity in AC analysis: it is "resistance for AC", but it is a complex number that captures both magnitude (how much it opposes) and phase (how much it shifts current relative to voltage). Once you have \(Z\), AC Ohm's Law \(\mathbf{V}=\mathbf{I}\,\mathbf{Z}\) works exactly like DC. This is where series RL, RC and RLC circuits — and resonance — all come together.
DefinitionImpedance & the impedance triangle▶

Impedance combines resistance \(R\) (real) and reactance \(X\) (imaginary):

\[ \mathbf{Z} = R + jX \qquad |Z| = \sqrt{R^2 + X^2} \qquad \theta = \tan^{-1}\!\frac{X}{R} \]

where \(X = X_L - X_C\) for a series circuit. The impedance triangle has \(R\) along the base, \(X\) vertical, \(|Z|\) as the hypotenuse, and \(\theta\) the phase angle between voltage and current.

\[ \mathbf{V} = \mathbf{I}\,\mathbf{Z} \qquad(\text{AC Ohm's Law}) \]
PatternSeries RL, RC and RLC▶
CircuitImpedance \(Z\)Phase \(\theta\)
Series R-L\(\sqrt{R^2+X_L^2}\)\(+\tan^{-1}(X_L/R)\) — V leads I
Series R-C\(\sqrt{R^2+X_C^2}\)\(-\tan^{-1}(X_C/R)\) — I leads V
Series R-L-C\(\sqrt{R^2+(X_L-X_C)^2}\)\(\tan^{-1}\!\frac{X_L-X_C}{R}\)
Resonance: when \(X_L=X_C\), they cancel, \(Z=R\) (minimum, purely resistive), and current is maximum. Resonant frequency: \(f_0 = \dfrac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}}\). This is how radios select a station.
Worked Example 5 — series RL circuit

A series circuit has \(R=30\,\Omega\) and \(X_L=40\,\Omega\), driven by 100 V RMS. Find \(Z\), the current, and the phase angle.

1\(|Z| = \sqrt{30^2+40^2} = \sqrt{900+1600} = \sqrt{2500} = 50\,\Omega\).
2\(I = V/|Z| = 100/50 = 2\,\text{A}\).
3\(\theta = \tan^{-1}(40/30) = 53.1^\circ\) — voltage leads current by 53.1°.
ANSWER \(Z=50\,\Omega\), \(I=2\,\text{A}\), \(\theta=53.1^\circ\) (V leads I).
Worked Example 6 — series RLC & resonance

A series RLC has \(R=10\,\Omega\), \(X_L=50\,\Omega\), \(X_C=30\,\Omega\), at 100 V RMS. Find \(Z\) and \(I\). Then find the resonant condition.

1Net reactance: \(X = X_L - X_C = 50-30 = 20\,\Omega\) (inductive).
2\(|Z| = \sqrt{10^2+20^2} = \sqrt{500} = 22.4\,\Omega\).
3\(I = 100/22.4 = 4.47\,\text{A}\); \(\theta=\tan^{-1}(20/10)=63.4^\circ\).
4At resonance \(X_L=X_C\), so \(Z=R=10\,\Omega\) and \(I_{max}=100/10=10\,\text{A}\).
ANSWER \(Z=22.4\,\Omega\), \(I=4.47\,\text{A}\); at resonance \(I_{max}=10\,\text{A}\).
Quick PracticeTap to reveal worked answers
1\(R=60\,\Omega\), \(X_C=80\,\Omega\). Find \(|Z|\).▶
100 Ω. \(\sqrt{60^2+80^2}=\sqrt{10000}=100\,\Omega\).
2At resonance in a series RLC, what is the impedance?▶
\(Z=R\) (purely resistive, minimum). \(X_L\) and \(X_C\) cancel.
3Find resonant frequency for \(L=10\,\text{mH}\), \(C=1\,\mu\text{F}\).▶
≈ 1592 Hz. \(f_0=1/(2\pi\sqrt{10^{-2}\cdot10^{-6}})=1/(2\pi\times10^{-4})=1592\,\text{Hz}\).
4In a series RC circuit, does the current lead or lag the supply voltage?▶
Current leads (capacitive circuit, ICE). \(\theta\) is negative.
Topic 4 · Week 10 · Boylestad Ch 14.7-14.9
AC Power & Power Factor
When voltage and current are out of phase, "power" splits into three kinds.
In DC, power is simply \(P=VI\). In AC, because current and voltage can be out of phase, only part of the product does useful work — the rest sloshes back and forth into reactive elements. Understanding real, reactive and apparent power, and the power factor that links them, is essential: it's why factories get charged for poor power factor and why the grid cares so much about it.
DefinitionReal, reactive & apparent power▶
PowerSymbol & unitFormula
Real (active)\(P\) — watts (W)\(P = VI\cos\theta\)
Reactive\(Q\) — VAR\(Q = VI\sin\theta\)
Apparent\(S\) — VA\(S = VI = \sqrt{P^2+Q^2}\)

These form the power triangle: \(P\) horizontal, \(Q\) vertical, \(S\) the hypotenuse, with angle \(\theta\) (the same impedance phase angle).

DefinitionPower factor▶
\[ \text{Power factor} = \cos\theta = \frac{P}{S} \]

It is the fraction of apparent power that becomes real, useful power. PF = 1 (purely resistive) is ideal; PF < 1 means current is being drawn that does no work.

Lagging vs leading: inductive loads (motors) → lagging PF; capacitive loads → leading PF. Power factor correction adds capacitors to cancel an inductive lag and bring PF back toward 1.
Worked Example 7 — the power triangle

A load draws 5 A RMS from a 230 V supply at a phase angle of 40° (lagging). Find P, Q, S and the power factor.

1\(S = VI = 230 \times 5 = 1150\,\text{VA}\).
2\(P = VI\cos40^\circ = 1150 \times 0.766 = 881\,\text{W}\).
3\(Q = VI\sin40^\circ = 1150 \times 0.643 = 739\,\text{VAR}\).
4\(\text{PF} = \cos40^\circ = 0.766\) lagging.
ANSWER \(S=1150\,\text{VA}\), \(P=881\,\text{W}\), \(Q=739\,\text{VAR}\), PF = 0.766 lag.
Worked Example 8 — why PF matters

A 10 kW motor runs at PF = 0.5. Compare the supply current with the same 10 kW at PF = 1, on 230 V.

1At PF=1: \(I = P/V = 10000/230 = 43.5\,\text{A}\).
2At PF=0.5: \(I = P/(V\cos\theta) = 10000/(230\times0.5) = 87\,\text{A}\) — double the current for the same real power!
ANSWER Poor PF doubles current → bigger cables & more \(I^2R\) losses. Hence PF correction.
Quick PracticeTap to reveal worked answers
1\(V=120\,\text{V}\), \(I=10\,\text{A}\), \(\theta=0\). Find P and PF.▶
\(P=1200\,\text{W}\), PF=1. Purely resistive: all apparent power is real.
2What are the units of real, reactive and apparent power?▶
W (watts), VAR, VA respectively.
3A load has \(P=800\,\text{W}\), \(S=1000\,\text{VA}\). Find PF and Q.▶
PF=0.8; Q=600 VAR. \(\cos\theta=P/S=0.8\); \(Q=\sqrt{1000^2-800^2}=600\).
4How do you correct a lagging (inductive) power factor?▶
Add capacitors in parallel. Their leading reactive power cancels the inductive lag, raising PF toward 1.

Semiconductors & Diodes
The PN junction — the one-way valve that turns AC into DC and underpins all modern electronics.
Everything so far used passive components. The semiconductor diode is the gateway to active electronics. A diode conducts in one direction only, so it can rectify AC into DC — the first job in nearly every power supply. Understanding the PN junction also sets you up for transistors, LEDs, solar cells and every chip you'll ever use. This closes the loop: AC in, DC out.
DefinitionSemiconductors & the PN junction▶

A semiconductor (silicon) conducts between a conductor and an insulator. Doping creates two types:

NN-type: doped to have extra free electrons (negative carriers).
PP-type: doped to have "holes" — missing electrons that act as positive carriers.

Join them and you get a PN junction. At the boundary a depletion region forms with a built-in barrier voltage (~0.7 V for silicon, ~0.3 V for germanium).

DefinitionDiode operation & biasing▶
BiasConnectionBehaviour
Forward+ to P (anode), − to N (cathode)Conducts once V > ~0.7 V; low resistance
Reverse+ to N, − to PBlocks (tiny leakage); very high resistance
Ideal diode model: a perfect one-way switch — zero drop when forward, open when reverse. Practical model: add a constant 0.7 V forward drop for silicon.
PatternRectification▶

Using diodes to convert AC to DC:

½Half-wave: one diode passes only the positive half-cycles. \(V_{DC(avg)} = V_m/\pi = 0.318\,V_m\).
FFull-wave (bridge of 4 diodes): both half-cycles become positive. \(V_{DC(avg)} = 2V_m/\pi = 0.637\,V_m\).

A smoothing capacitor across the output then fills the gaps to give near-constant DC.

Worked Example 9 — forward voltage

A silicon diode in series with a \(1\,\text{k}\Omega\) resistor across a 5 V source. Find the current (practical model, 0.7 V drop).

\[ I = \frac{V_{source} - V_D}{R} = \frac{5 - 0.7}{1000} = \frac{4.3}{1000} = 4.3\,\text{mA} \]
ANSWER \(I = 4.3\,\text{mA}\).
Worked Example 10 — rectifier output

A transformer gives 12 V RMS to a full-wave bridge rectifier. Find the peak and the average (DC) output voltage (ideal diodes).

1Peak: \(V_m = 12\sqrt2 = 17\,\text{V}\).
2Full-wave average: \(V_{DC} = 0.637\,V_m = 0.637\times17 = 10.8\,\text{V}\).
ANSWER \(V_m=17\,\text{V}\), \(V_{DC}\approx10.8\,\text{V}\) (before smoothing).
Quick PracticeTap to reveal worked answers
1What is the typical forward voltage drop of a silicon diode?▶
≈ 0.7 V. (Germanium ≈ 0.3 V.)
2How many diodes in a full-wave bridge rectifier?▶
Four. Arranged so both half-cycles drive the load the same direction.
3A half-wave rectifier on \(V_m=20\,\text{V}\). DC average output?▶
≈ 6.37 V. \(V_{DC}=V_m/\pi=20/3.1416=6.37\,\text{V}\).
4Why does a PN junction conduct in only one direction?▶
Forward bias shrinks the depletion region so carriers cross easily; reverse bias widens it, blocking flow. The junction acts as a one-way valve.
