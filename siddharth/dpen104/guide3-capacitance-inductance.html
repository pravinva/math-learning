Capacitance & Inductance
Resistors dissipate energy. Two other elements store it: the capacitor stores energy in an electric field, the inductor in a magnetic field. Both introduce time into circuits — voltages and currents no longer jump instantly but rise and fall along exponential curves governed by a time constant. This is the bridge from DC to AC.

Topic 1 · Week 6 · Boylestad Ch 10.1-10.6
Capacitors & Capacitance
A device that stores charge — and therefore energy — in the electric field between two conductors.
A capacitor is just two conductive plates separated by an insulator (the dielectric). Connect a voltage and charge piles up on the plates — it stores energy without dissipating it. Capacitors are everywhere: they smooth power supplies, time the flash on a camera, tune radios, and form the memory cell of every DRAM chip. The single relationship \(Q = CV\) unlocks all of it.
DefinitionCapacitance▶

Capacitance is the ratio of stored charge to the voltage across the plates — a measure of how much charge a capacitor holds per volt.

\[ C = \frac{Q}{V} \qquad\text{unit: farad (F)} = \frac{\text{coulomb}}{\text{volt}} \]

One farad is enormous; real capacitors are µF, nF or pF. Rearranged: \(Q = CV\).

The farad is so large because storing one coulomb at one volt requires huge plates. A typical electrolytic might be \(1000\,\mu\text{F}\); a ceramic, \(100\,\text{nF}\).
PatternThe parallel-plate formula▶

Capacitance is set entirely by geometry and the dielectric — not by the applied voltage:

\[ C = \frac{\varepsilon A}{d} = \frac{\varepsilon_0 \varepsilon_r A}{d} \]

where \(A\) = plate area, \(d\) = plate separation, \(\varepsilon_0 = 8.85\times10^{-12}\,\text{F/m}\) (permittivity of free space), and \(\varepsilon_r\) = relative permittivity (dielectric constant) of the insulator.

↑Bigger plates (\(A\)) → more capacitance.
↓Wider gap (\(d\)) → less capacitance.
×Better dielectric (\(\varepsilon_r\)) → more capacitance (mica, ceramic ≫ air).
DefinitionThe i-v relationship▶

Current through a capacitor is proportional to the rate of change of its voltage:

\[ i_C = C\,\frac{dv_C}{dt} \]

Two crucial consequences:

Capacitor voltage cannot change instantly — that would need infinite current. At the instant a switch closes, \(v_C\) is whatever it was just before.
At DC steady state \(dv/dt=0\), so \(i_C=0\): a fully-charged capacitor behaves as an open circuit.
Worked Example 1 — charge stored

A \(100\,\mu\text{F}\) capacitor is charged to 12 V. How much charge is stored?

\[ Q = CV = (100\times10^{-6})(12) = 1.2\times10^{-3}\,\text{C} = 1.2\,\text{mC} \]
ANSWER \(Q = 1.2\,\text{mC}\).
Worked Example 2 — designing capacitance

Two parallel plates of area \(0.02\,\text{m}^2\) are separated by 1 mm of air (\(\varepsilon_r=1\)). Find C.

\[ C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 \varepsilon_r A}{d} = \frac{(8.85\times10^{-12})(1)(0.02)}{0.001} = 1.77\times10^{-10}\,\text{F} = 177\,\text{pF} \]
ANSWER \(C \approx 177\,\text{pF}\). Filling the gap with mica (\(\varepsilon_r\approx5\)) would give ≈ 885 pF.
Quick PracticeTap to reveal worked answers
1A 47 µF capacitor holds 2.35 mC. What is its voltage?▶
50 V. \(V=Q/C=2.35\times10^{-3}/47\times10^{-6}=50\,\text{V}\).
2How does doubling the plate separation affect capacitance?▶
Halves it. \(C \propto 1/d\).
3What does a fully-charged capacitor look like to a DC circuit?▶
An open circuit. No further current flows once \(v_C\) stops changing.
4Why can't the voltage across a capacitor jump instantly?▶
Because \(i_C=C\,dv/dt\); an instantaneous voltage jump means \(dv/dt\to\infty\), demanding infinite current.
Topic 2 · Week 6 · Boylestad Ch 10.7-10.10
RC Transients & Time Constants
When you switch a capacitor onto a source through a resistor, voltage and current follow exponential curves.
Switch a resistor and capacitor onto a battery and nothing happens instantly. The capacitor charges up along a smooth exponential curve, fast at first then slowing as it fills. The single number that sets the speed is the time constant \(\tau = RC\). This one idea explains camera-flash delays, the timing in a 555 timer, debounce circuits, and the rise-time limits of every digital signal. The course formula sheet states these equations exactly — learn them cold.
DefinitionThe time constant τ = RC▶

The time constant \(\tau\) (tau) is the product of resistance and capacitance:

\[ \tau = RC \qquad [\text{seconds}] \]

It is the time for the capacitor to reach 63.2% of its final voltage when charging (or fall to 36.8% when discharging). After 5τ the capacitor is considered fully charged/discharged (>99%).

Time% of final (charging)% remaining (discharge)
1τ63.2%36.8%
2τ86.5%13.5%
3τ95.0%5.0%
5τ99.3% (≈ full)0.7% (≈ zero)
PatternCharging equations▶

Capacitor initially uncharged, switched onto source \(E\) through \(R\):

\[ v_C(t) = E\left(1 - e^{-t/RC}\right) \]
\[ i_C(t) = \frac{E}{R}\,e^{-t/RC} \]

The voltage rises toward \(E\); the current starts at its maximum \(E/R\) and decays to zero. (This matches the course formula sheet exactly.)

PatternDischarging equations▶

Capacitor charged to \(V_0\), then the source is removed and it discharges through \(R\):

\[ v_C(t) = V_0\,e^{-t/RC} \qquad i_C(t) = \frac{V_0}{R}\,e^{-t/RC} \]

Both decay exponentially to zero with the same \(\tau = RC\).

Worked Example 3 — charging

A \(10\,\mu\text{F}\) capacitor charges from a 20 V source through a \(50\,\text{k}\Omega\) resistor. Find τ, the voltage after 0.5 s, and the time to reach ≈ full charge.

1\(\tau = RC = (50\times10^{3})(10\times10^{-6}) = 0.5\,\text{s}\).
2At \(t=0.5\,\text{s} = 1\tau\): \(v_C = 20(1-e^{-1}) = 20(0.632) = 12.6\,\text{V}\).
3Fully charged after \(5\tau = 2.5\,\text{s}\).
ANSWER \(\tau=0.5\,\text{s}\), \(v_C(0.5\,\text{s})=12.6\,\text{V}\), full at ≈ 2.5 s.
Worked Example 4 — initial current & discharge

The same capacitor, now charged to 20 V, discharges through a \(2\,\text{k}\Omega\) resistor. Find the initial current and the voltage after one time constant.

1Initial current: \(i_0 = V_0/R = 20/2000 = 10\,\text{mA}\).
2\(\tau = (2\times10^3)(10\times10^{-6}) = 0.02\,\text{s} = 20\,\text{ms}\).
3At \(t=1\tau\): \(v_C = 20\,e^{-1} = 20(0.368) = 7.36\,\text{V}\).
ANSWER \(i_0 = 10\,\text{mA}\), \(\tau = 20\,\text{ms}\), \(v_C(20\,\text{ms}) = 7.36\,\text{V}\).

Inductance & Induced Voltage
A coil that opposes changes in current by storing energy in a magnetic field.
An inductor is simply a coil of wire. When current flows it creates a magnetic field; when that current changes, the collapsing/growing field induces a voltage that opposes the change (Lenz's law). This is the exact magnetic mirror image of the capacitor. Inductors are the heart of transformers, motors, switch-mode power supplies and radio tuning — and the reason a spark jumps when you unplug an inductive load.
DefinitionSelf-inductance▶

Inductance \(L\) measures how much voltage a coil induces per unit rate-of-change of current. Unit: the henry (H).

\[ v_L = L\,\frac{di_L}{dt} \qquad 1\,\text{H} = 1\,\frac{\text{V}\cdot\text{s}}{\text{A}} \]

For a solenoid: \( L = \dfrac{\mu N^2 A}{\ell}\) — more turns \(N\), bigger core area \(A\), higher core permeability \(\mu\), all increase \(L\).

DefinitionFaraday's & Lenz's laws▶

Faraday's law: a changing magnetic flux \(\Phi\) through a coil of \(N\) turns induces a voltage:

\[ e = N\,\frac{d\Phi}{dt} \]

Lenz's law sets the sign: the induced voltage always opposes the change that created it (hence the minus sign in \(e=-N\,d\Phi/dt\)). This is conservation of energy in action.

Inductor current cannot change instantly — that would need infinite voltage. This is the dual of the capacitor's voltage rule.
At DC steady state \(di/dt=0\), so \(v_L=0\): an inductor behaves as a short circuit (just its wire resistance).
Worked Example 5 — induced voltage

The current through a 0.5 H inductor increases steadily from 0 to 4 A in 2 ms. Find the induced voltage.

\[ v_L = L\,\frac{di}{dt} = 0.5 \times \frac{4-0}{2\times10^{-3}} = 0.5 \times 2000 = 1000\,\text{V} \]
ANSWER \(v_L = 1000\,\text{V}\) — a huge spike! This is why switching off inductors arcs.
Worked Example 6 — Faraday's law

A 200-turn coil experiences a flux change of 0.01 Wb in 0.05 s. Find the induced EMF.

\[ e = N\,\frac{d\Phi}{dt} = 200 \times \frac{0.01}{0.05} = 200 \times 0.2 = 40\,\text{V} \]
ANSWER \(e = 40\,\text{V}\).
Quick PracticeTap to reveal worked answers
1Current in a 2 H inductor changes at 3 A/s. Find \(v_L\).▶
6 V. \(v_L=L\,di/dt=2\times3=6\,\text{V}\).
2What does an inductor look like to a DC circuit at steady state?▶
A short circuit (only its small wire resistance remains).
3State Lenz's law in one sentence.▶
The induced voltage/current always opposes the change in flux that produced it.
4Why does a spark form when you disconnect an inductive load?▶
Breaking the circuit forces \(di/dt\) to be very large, so \(v_L=L\,di/dt\) spikes high enough to ionise the air gap and arc.
Topic 4 · Week 7 · Boylestad Ch 12
RL Transients
Current build-up and decay in an inductor — the magnetic dual of the RC transient.
Just as a capacitor's voltage eases in along an exponential, an inductor's current eases in when you switch it onto a source — it cannot jump. The time constant is now \(\tau = L/R\). Everything you learned about RC charging maps directly across: swap voltage for current, swap \(RC\) for \(L/R\). This duality is one of the most satisfying patterns in circuit theory.
DefinitionRL time constant▶
\[ \tau = \frac{L}{R} \qquad [\text{seconds}] \]

Same 63.2% / 5τ rules as RC — but now they describe the current through the inductor, not the voltage.

PatternCurrent build-up and decay▶

Build-up (switch onto source \(E\), inductor initially at 0 A):

\[ i_L(t) = \frac{E}{R}\left(1 - e^{-tR/L}\right) \qquad v_L(t) = E\,e^{-tR/L} \]

Decay (source removed, current \(I_0\) decays through \(R\)):

\[ i_L(t) = I_0\,e^{-tR/L} \]

The current rises toward its final value \(E/R\); the voltage starts at \(E\) and decays to 0 — mirror image of the capacitor.

Worked Example 7 — current build-up

A 2 H inductor with \(R = 100\,\Omega\) is switched onto 50 V. Find τ, the final current, and the current after one time constant.

1\(\tau = L/R = 2/100 = 0.02\,\text{s} = 20\,\text{ms}\).
2Final current: \(I_{final} = E/R = 50/100 = 0.5\,\text{A}\).
3At \(t=1\tau\): \(i_L = 0.5(1-e^{-1}) = 0.5(0.632) = 0.316\,\text{A}\).
ANSWER \(\tau=20\,\text{ms}\), \(I_{final}=0.5\,\text{A}\), \(i_L(20\,\text{ms})=0.316\,\text{A}\).
Quick PracticeTap to reveal worked answers
1\(L=0.4\,\text{H}\), \(R=20\,\Omega\). Find τ.▶
0.02 s. \(\tau=L/R=0.4/20=0.02\,\text{s}\).
2In RL build-up, what is the inductor current at \(t=0^+\)?▶
0 A. Inductor current cannot jump; it starts at its prior value (here 0).
3After 5τ, what fraction of final current is reached?▶
≈ 99.3% — effectively the final (steady-state) current.
4Compare the RC and RL time constants. What's dual to what?▶
\(\tau_{RC}=RC\) governs capacitor voltage; \(\tau_{RL}=L/R\) governs inductor current. Swap \(v\leftrightarrow i\) and \(C\leftrightarrow L\).

Stored Energy & Combinations
How much energy each element stores, and how they combine in series and parallel.
Both capacitors and inductors store energy rather than dissipating it — that's what makes them so useful for smoothing, filtering and timing. Knowing the stored energy lets you size a flash capacitor, a UPS bank, or the snubber that protects a switch. And combining them in series/parallel follows rules that are the exact opposite of resistors for capacitors — a classic exam trap.
PatternStored energy▶
\[ W_C = \tfrac12 C V^2 \qquad\qquad W_L = \tfrac12 L I^2 \]

Energy in joules when \(C\) in farads, \(V\) in volts; \(L\) in henries, \(I\) in amperes. Note both depend on the square of the stored quantity — doubling the voltage on a capacitor quadruples its energy.

PatternSeries & parallel combinations▶
SeriesParallel
Capacitors\(\dfrac{1}{C_T}=\dfrac{1}{C_1}+\dfrac{1}{C_2}+\dots\)\(C_T = C_1 + C_2 + \dots\)
Inductors\(L_T = L_1 + L_2 + \dots\)\(\dfrac{1}{L_T}=\dfrac{1}{L_1}+\dfrac{1}{L_2}+\dots\)
Capacitors combine "backwards" from resistors: series capacitors add reciprocally (total is smaller), parallel capacitors add directly. Inductors combine like resistors.
Worked Example 8 — energy stored

Find the energy in (a) a \(470\,\mu\text{F}\) capacitor at 24 V, and (b) a 0.5 H inductor carrying 3 A.

a\(W_C = \tfrac12 C V^2 = \tfrac12(470\times10^{-6})(24^2) = \tfrac12(470\times10^{-6})(576) = 0.135\,\text{J}\).
b\(W_L = \tfrac12 L I^2 = \tfrac12(0.5)(3^2) = \tfrac12(0.5)(9) = 2.25\,\text{J}\).
ANSWER \(W_C = 0.135\,\text{J}\), \(W_L = 2.25\,\text{J}\).
Worked Example 9 — combinations

Find (a) \(6\,\mu\text{F}\) and \(3\,\mu\text{F}\) in series, (b) the same two in parallel, (c) 4 H and 12 H in parallel.

aSeries C: \(C_T = \dfrac{6\times3}{6+3} = \dfrac{18}{9} = 2\,\mu\text{F}\).
bParallel C: \(C_T = 6+3 = 9\,\mu\text{F}\).
cParallel L: \(L_T = \dfrac{4\times12}{4+12} = \dfrac{48}{16} = 3\,\text{H}\).
ANSWER (a) \(2\,\mu\text{F}\), (b) \(9\,\mu\text{F}\), (c) \(3\,\text{H}\).
Quick PracticeTap to reveal worked answers
1Energy in a 100 µF capacitor at 100 V?▶
0.5 J. \(\tfrac12(100\times10^{-6})(100^2)=\tfrac12(10^{-4})(10^4)=0.5\,\text{J}\).
2Two 10 µF capacitors in series. Total?▶
5 µF. Equal series caps halve: \(10/2=5\,\mu\text{F}\).
3Two 10 mH inductors in series. Total?▶
20 mH. Inductors in series add like resistors.
4If you double the voltage on a capacitor, how does stored energy change?▶
Quadruples. \(W\propto V^2\), so \(\times2\) voltage \(=\times4\) energy.
