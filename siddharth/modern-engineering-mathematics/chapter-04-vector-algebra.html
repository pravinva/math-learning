4.2 Vectors and Components

Scalars vs vectors, magnitude, unit vectors, addition, and component form.

Motivation first

A scalar gives only "how much". A vector gives "how much + which direction". In engineering, force, velocity, acceleration, and electric field are vectors. If direction is ignored, answers can be dangerously wrong.

$$\mathbf v=(a,b,c),\quad |\mathbf v|=\sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2},\quad \hat{\mathbf v}=\frac{\mathbf v}{|\mathbf v|}$$

Step-by-step Example

Given \(\mathbf v=(2,-1,3)\): magnitude \(=\sqrt{14}\). Unit vector is \(\left(\frac{2}{\sqrt{14}},-\frac{1}{\sqrt{14}},\frac{3}{\sqrt{14}}\right)\). Why divide by magnitude? To keep direction but force length to 1.

Recall: unit vectors are used to represent direction cleanly in force decomposition and in line equations.

4.2.8 Scalar (Dot) Product

Dot product tells you how much one vector points along another.
$$\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf b=a_1b_1+a_2b_2+a_3b_3=|\mathbf a||\mathbf b|\cos\theta$$

Intuition in simple words

If vectors point same way, dot product is large positive. If perpendicular, it is 0. If opposite, negative. So dot product is really an "alignment score".

Why \(\cos\theta\) appears (geometric reason)

Dot product measures how much of one vector lies along the direction of the other. That is a projection question. The projection length of \(\mathbf b\) on \(\mathbf a\) is \(|\mathbf b|\cos\theta\), so multiplying by \(|\mathbf a|\) gives \(|\mathbf a||\mathbf b|\cos\theta\).

$$\text{projection length of }\mathbf b\text{ on }\mathbf a=|\mathbf b|\cos\theta,\qquad \mathbf a\cdot\mathbf b=|\mathbf a|\big(|\mathbf b|\cos\theta\big)$$
Why-cos Quick Check

If \(|\mathbf a|=4\), \(|\mathbf b|=10\), and \(\theta=60^\circ\), then the part of \(\mathbf b\) along \(\mathbf a\) is \(10\cos60^\circ=5\). So \(\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf b=4\times5=20\).

Worked Example

\(\mathbf a=(1,2,-1),\ \mathbf b=(3,0,2)\Rightarrow \mathbf a\cdot\mathbf b=1\). Then \(\cos\theta=\frac{1}{\sqrt6\sqrt{13}}\), so \(\theta\approx83.5^\circ\). Nearly perpendicular, consistent with small dot product.

4.2.10 Vector (Cross) Product

Cross product gives a vector perpendicular to both inputs.
$$\mathbf a\times\mathbf b= \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf i&\mathbf j&\mathbf k\\ a_1&a_2&a_3\\ b_1&b_2&b_3 \end{vmatrix},\qquad |\mathbf a\times\mathbf b|=|\mathbf a||\mathbf b|\sin\theta$$

Why engineers care

Cross product appears in torque, rotational motion, and normals to surfaces. Magnitude gives area of the parallelogram spanned by two vectors.

Why \(\sin\theta\) appears (geometric reason)

Cross product magnitude is area. Area of a parallelogram is base \(\times\) perpendicular height. If base is \(|\mathbf a|\), then the height from \(\mathbf b\) is \(|\mathbf b|\sin\theta\). So \(|\mathbf a\times\mathbf b|=|\mathbf a||\mathbf b|\sin\theta\).

$$\text{height} = |\mathbf b|\sin\theta,\qquad \text{area} = |\mathbf a|\cdot(|\mathbf b|\sin\theta)=|\mathbf a\times\mathbf b|$$

Worked Example

\(\mathbf a=(1,2,-1), \mathbf b=(3,0,2)\Rightarrow \mathbf a\times\mathbf b=(4,-5,-6)\). Check anti-commutative rule: \(\mathbf b\times\mathbf a=-(4,-5,-6)\).

How to calculate cross product (step by step)

Let \(\mathbf a=(1,2,-1)\), \(\mathbf b=(3,0,2)\). Expand the determinant along the first row:

$$ \mathbf a\times\mathbf b= \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf i&\mathbf j&\mathbf k\\ 1&2&-1\\ 3&0&2 \end{vmatrix} =\mathbf i(2\cdot2-(-1)\cdot0)-\mathbf j(1\cdot2-(-1)\cdot3)+\mathbf k(1\cdot0-2\cdot3) $$
$$ =\mathbf i(4)-\mathbf j(5)+\mathbf k(-6)=(4,-5,-6) $$

Direction comes from the right-hand rule, and magnitude is \(\sqrt{4^2+(-5)^2+(-6)^2}=\sqrt{77}=|\mathbf a||\mathbf b|\sin\theta\).

4.3 Geometry of Lines and Planes

Vector equations for lines/planes and geometric classification (parallel, intersecting, skew).
$$\mathbf r=\mathbf r_0+t\mathbf v\quad\text{(line)},\qquad \mathbf n\cdot(\mathbf r-\mathbf r_0)=0\quad\text{(plane)}$$

Step-by-step mental model

  • Line = starting point + travel direction.
  • Plane = one point + normal direction.
  • For two lines: check direction multiples first (parallel test), then solve simultaneously for intersection.
Worked Example

Line 1 through \((1,0,-1)\), direction \((2,1,3)\): \((x,y,z)=(1,0,-1)+t(2,1,3)\).

Line 2 through \((3,1,0)\), direction \((1,-1,2)\): solve component equations; contradiction -> skew lines.
