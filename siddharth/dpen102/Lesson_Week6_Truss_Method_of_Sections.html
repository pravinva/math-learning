0 Assumptions & why method of sections

A truss is analysed under three assumptions: loads act only at joints, members are two-force members carrying purely axial force (no bending), and joints behave as ideal frictionless pins. Under these assumptions, method of joints (solving each joint's \(\Sigma F_x=0,\Sigma F_y=0\) one at a time) works, but is slow if you only need one member deep inside the truss. Method of sections cuts straight through up to three unknown members at once and solves for them directly, using the same equilibrium tools, applied to a whole cut portion of the truss instead of one joint.

1 Zero-force members

Some members carry no force at all under a given loading, and can be spotted by inspection, no calculation needed, which trims the analysis before it even starts.

Two rules

Rule 1: if only two non-collinear members meet at an unloaded joint, both are zero-force members.

Rule 2: if three members meet at an unloaded joint and two of them are collinear (in a straight line), the third (non-collinear) member is a zero-force member, and the two collinear members carry equal force.

Worked example

At joint A, members AB and AC meet at 90°, with no external load applied at A and nothing else connected there. State the force in each.

Rule 1 applies directly: two non-collinear members, unloaded joint. Both AB and AC are zero-force members.

Practice 1

At joint D, three members meet: DE and DF are collinear, DG is at an angle to them, and there is no load at D. Which member is zero-force?

Solution

DG, the non-collinear third member (Rule 2).

Practice 2

At joint H, only two members meet, HI and HJ, not collinear, and there's no external load at H. State the force in each.

Solution

Both are zero-force members (Rule 1).

Practice 3

At joint K, KL and KM are collinear, KN is at an angle, but there IS an external load applied at K. Does Rule 2 still apply? Explain.

Solution

No. Rule 2 only applies at unloaded joints. With an external load present, KN generally must carry a nonzero force to help balance it.

Practice 4

A truss has 8 members. By inspection you identify 2 zero-force members. How many members must actually be solved via joints/sections?

Solution

6. The 2 zero-force members are already known without calculation, leaving only the remaining 6 to analyse.

Practice 5

Zero-force members carry no force under this specific load case, so why do engineers still include them in the truss at all?

Solution

They provide bracing and stability, preventing buckling or shifting under other loading conditions (wind, construction loads, unbalanced live loads) not present in this particular idealised case. Zero-force here does not mean zero-force always.

2 Global support reactions for trusses

Before any cut can be made, the truss's overall support reactions must be known, exactly the same beam-style reaction-finding from Weeks 4–5, just applied to the truss as a whole rigid assembly.

Worked example

A truss spans 8 m, pin at A (left), roller at B (right), with a single 500 N downward load at a joint 3 m from A. Find the reactions.

\(\Sigma M_A\): \(R_B(8)=500(3)=1500 \Rightarrow R_B=187.5\text{ N}\).

\(\Sigma F_y\): \(R_A=500-187.5=312.5\text{ N}\).

Practice 6

Truss span 10 m, pin-roller, 800 N load at 4 m from A. Find the reactions.

Solution

\(R_B=800(4)/10=320\text{ N}\). \(R_A=480\text{ N}\).

Practice 7

Truss span 12 m, two loads: 400 N at 3 m, 600 N at 8 m from A. Find the reactions.

Solution

\(\Sigma M_A\): \(R_B(12)=400(3)+600(8)=6000 \Rightarrow R_B=500\text{ N}\). \(R_A=1000-500=500\text{ N}\).

Practice 8

Truss span 6 m, single 300 N load exactly at midspan. Find the reactions.

Solution

Symmetric: \(R_A=R_B=150\text{ N}\).

Practice 9

Explain why the global support reactions must be found before cutting a truss with the method of sections.

Solution

The cut portion's equilibrium equations include any support reaction acting on it. If those reactions are still unknown, the cut piece has too many unknowns (member forces plus unknown reactions) to solve with only three equilibrium equations.

3 The method of sections algorithm

Cut through no more than three unknown members, keep one side, and use moments cleverly to isolate one unknown at a time.

The 5-step algorithm

1. Find global support reactions. 2. Cut through at most 3 unknown members of interest. 3. Take moments about the point where two of the unknown members' lines of action intersect, this eliminates both from that equation, isolating the third. 4. Use \(\Sigma F_x,\Sigma F_y\) for the remaining unknowns. 5. Classify each result: positive (under a tension assumption) means tension, negative means compression.

Worked example

A truss: span 16 m, height 3 m, panel width 4 m (giving 3-4-5 diagonals). Bottom joints A(0,0), C(4,0), E(8,0), G(12,0), I(16,0); top joints B(4,3), D(8,3), F(12,3); pin at A, roller at I. A single 800 N load acts at D. By symmetry, \(R_A=R_I=400\text{ N}\).

Cut through panel 2 (top chord BD, diagonal EB, bottom chord CE). Keep the left portion (joints A, C, B, with \(R_A=400\text{ N}\), nothing else since the load at D sits to the right of this cut).

\(\Sigma M_E=0\) (both \(F_{CE}\) and \(F_{EB}\) pass through E, eliminating them): \(R_A(8)=F_{BD}(3) \Rightarrow F_{BD}=400(8)/3=1066.67\text{ N}\) (compression, typical for a top chord).

\(\Sigma M_B=0\) (both \(F_{BD}\) and \(F_{EB}\) pass through B): \(R_A(4)=F_{CE}(3) \Rightarrow F_{CE}=400(4)/3=533.33\text{ N}\) (tension, typical for a bottom chord).

\(\Sigma F_y=0\): \(R_A=F_{EB}\sin\theta\), with \(\sin\theta=3/5\) for this 3-4-5 diagonal: \(F_{EB}=400(5)/3=666.67\text{ N}\) (tension).

Practice 10

Using the same truss and loading as the worked example, cut through panel 3 (members: top chord DF, diagonal GD, bottom chord EG). By left–right symmetry with the worked example, state \(F_{DF}\).

Solution

By symmetry (load at midspan, symmetric supports): \(F_{DF}=F_{BD}=1066.67\text{ N}\) (compression).

Practice 11

For the same panel 3 cut, state \(F_{EG}\) (bottom chord) and \(F_{GD}\) (diagonal) by symmetry.

Solution

\(F_{EG}=F_{CE}=533.33\text{ N}\) (tension). \(F_{GD}=F_{EB}=666.67\text{ N}\) (tension).

Practice 12

Now the load is moved to joint F(12,3) instead of D, with magnitude 600 N. Find the new reactions \(R_A,R_I\).

Solution

\(\Sigma M_A\): \(R_I(16)=600(12)=7200 \Rightarrow R_I=450\text{ N}\). \(R_A=600-450=150\text{ N}\).

Practice 13

Using the load-at-F scenario from Practice 12, cut through panel 2 (top BD, diagonal EB, bottom CE) using the left portion (\(R_A=150\text{ N}\), no load between A and this cut). Find \(F_{BD}\), \(F_{CE}\), and \(F_{EB}\).

Solution

\(\Sigma M_E\): \(F_{BD}=150(8)/3=400\text{ N}\) (compression).

\(\Sigma M_B\): \(F_{CE}=150(4)/3=200\text{ N}\) (tension).

\(\Sigma F_y\): \(F_{EB}=150(5)/3=250\text{ N}\) (tension).

Practice 14

In the worked example, \(F_{BD}=1066.67\text{ N}\) but \(F_{CE}=533.33\text{ N}\), exactly half. Explain why, referencing the moment arms used in each equation.

Solution

\(F_{BD}\) came from \(R_A(8)=F_{BD}(3)\), using moment centre E, 8 m from A. \(F_{CE}\) came from \(R_A(4)=F_{CE}(3)\), using moment centre B, only 4 m from A. The numerator halves (8 vs 4) because E and B sit at different horizontal distances from the support, a direct geometric consequence of where each moment centre is located, not an error.

4 Advanced: tension/compression classification

Always assume tension as the default direction when setting up the equations. The sign of the result then tells you the physical answer directly.

Key idea

Positive result (under a tension assumption) → member is in tension. Negative result → the assumed direction was wrong, the member is actually in compression, with that magnitude.

Worked example

Solving a cut, an engineer assumes tension for a bottom-chord member and gets a result of \(-450\text{ N}\). What does this mean?

A negative result under a tension assumption means the member is actually in compression, with magnitude 450 N.

Practice 15

A diagonal member's force comes out as \(+320\text{ N}\) under a tension assumption. State its actual condition and magnitude.

Solution

Tension, 320 N (the positive sign confirms the assumed direction).

Practice 16

A top chord in a simply-supported, gravity-loaded truss is calculated to have a force of \(-890\text{ N}\) assuming tension. Is this consistent with the general expectation for top chords?

Solution

Yes. Top chords in simply-supported, gravity-loaded trusses are typically in compression, and the negative sign confirms exactly that (890 N compression).

Practice 17

A bottom chord's calculation gives \(+610\text{ N}\) under a tension assumption. Is this the expected sign for a bottom chord? Explain.

Solution

Yes. Bottom chords are typically in tension in this type of truss, and the positive sign confirms it.

Practice 18

Why is it good practice to always assume tension as the default direction when setting up method-of-sections equations, before you know the actual answer?

Solution

A consistent default convention means the sign of the final result directly tells you the physical state (positive = tension, negative = compression), without re-deriving the assumed direction case by case for every single member. It standardises the bookkeeping and avoids sign confusion across a larger truss with many members.
