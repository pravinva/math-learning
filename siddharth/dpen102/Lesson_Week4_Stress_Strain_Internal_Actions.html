Stress, Strain & Internal Actions

External equilibrium tells you a structure isn't moving. It says nothing about whether the material inside can actually survive the forces running through it. This lesson covers normal stress and strain, allowable-stress design with factor of safety, and cutting a member open to find the internal axial force, shear, and bending moment at any section.

0 Why external equilibrium isn't enough

A cable holding a 5-tonne load can be in perfect static equilibrium, \(\Sigma F=0\), right up until the instant it snaps. Equilibrium only checks whether a body as a whole is balanced; it says nothing about whether the material itself can withstand the forces passing through it. This week adds that missing layer: stress (how concentrated a force is over the material's cross-section) and internal actions (what forces and moments are actually running through any given point inside a loaded member).

1 Normal stress & strain

Stress is force spread over area, how concentrated the load is. Strain is how much the material actually deforms in response, as a fraction of its original length.

Key idea

$$\sigma = \frac{F}{A} \quad(\text{normal stress, units Pa} = \text{N/m}^2), \qquad \varepsilon = \frac{\Delta L}{L_0} \quad(\text{strain, dimensionless}).$$

Tension (stretching) is conventionally positive; compression is negative. Strain has no units since it's a ratio of two lengths.

Worked example

A steel rod of diameter 10 mm carries an axial tensile load of 15,000 N. Its original length is 2 m, and it stretches by 1.2 mm. Find the stress and the strain.

\(A=\pi r^2 = \pi(0.005)^2 = 7.854\times10^{-5}\text{ m}^2\).

\(\sigma = \dfrac{15000}{7.854\times10^{-5}} = 190.9\times10^6\text{ Pa} = 190.9\text{ MPa}\).

\(\varepsilon = \dfrac{0.0012}{2} = 6\times10^{-4}\) (0.06%).

Practice 1

A bar of cross-sectional area 500 mm² carries an axial load of 40 kN. Find the stress.

Solution

\(\sigma = \dfrac{40000}{500\times10^{-6}} = 80\times10^6\text{ Pa} = 80\text{ MPa}\).

Practice 2

A cable of diameter 8 mm carries 12 kN tension. Find the stress.

Solution

\(A=\pi(0.004)^2=5.027\times10^{-5}\text{ m}^2\). \(\sigma=\dfrac{12000}{5.027\times10^{-5}}=238.7\text{ MPa}\).

Practice 3

A 3 m long rod stretches 2.4 mm under load. Find the strain.

Solution

\(\varepsilon = \dfrac{0.0024}{3}=8\times10^{-4}\).

Practice 4

A column of cross-sectional area 0.02 m² carries a compressive load of 500 kN. Find the stress.

Solution

\(\sigma = \dfrac{500000}{0.02}=25\times10^6\text{ Pa}=25\text{ MPa}\) (compressive).

Practice 5

A wire of original length 1.5 m is stretched to 1.5036 m. Find the strain.

Solution

\(\Delta L=0.0036\text{ m}\). \(\varepsilon = \dfrac{0.0036}{1.5}=0.0024\).

Practice 6

Two rods carry the same force \(F\). Rod A has twice the cross-sectional area of rod B. Which has higher stress, and by what factor?

Solution

Since \(\sigma=F/A\) and \(F\) is the same for both, the smaller area carries higher stress. Rod B has twice the stress of rod A.

2 Allowable stress design & factor of safety

Materials don't get loaded right up to their yield point in practice, a margin is always built in. The factor of safety (FoS) is exactly that margin: how many times stronger the material is than it strictly needs to be.

Key idea

$$\sigma_{allow} = \frac{\sigma_y}{FoS}, \qquad \text{design requires } \sigma_{work} \le \sigma_{allow}, \qquad A_{req} = \frac{F}{\sigma_{allow}}.$$

Worked example

A steel bracket must carry 80 kN. \(\sigma_y=250\text{ MPa}\), required \(FoS=2.5\). Find the required cross-sectional area, and the minimum diameter if circular.

\(\sigma_{allow}=250/2.5=100\text{ MPa}\).

\(A_{req}=80000/(100\times10^6)=8\times10^{-4}\text{ m}^2 = 800\text{ mm}^2\).

\(d=\sqrt{4A/\pi}=\sqrt{4(8\times10^{-4})/\pi}=0.0319\text{ m}=31.9\text{ mm}\).

Practice 7

A tie rod must carry 60 kN. \(\sigma_y=300\text{ MPa}\), \(FoS=3\). Find \(\sigma_{allow}\) and \(A_{req}\).

Solution

\(\sigma_{allow}=100\text{ MPa}\). \(A_{req}=60000/(100\times10^6)=6\times10^{-4}\text{ m}^2=600\text{ mm}^2\).

Practice 8

For Practice 7, find the minimum diameter if a circular rod is used.

Solution

\(d=\sqrt{4(6\times10^{-4})/\pi}=\sqrt{7.639\times10^{-4}}=0.02764\text{ m}=27.64\text{ mm}\).

Practice 9

A column, \(\sigma_y=350\text{ MPa}\), needs \(FoS=2\) while carrying 700 kN. Find \(A_{req}\).

Solution

\(\sigma_{allow}=175\text{ MPa}\). \(A_{req}=700000/(175\times10^6)=4\times10^{-3}\text{ m}^2=4000\text{ mm}^2\).

Practice 10

A cable has \(\sigma_y=400\text{ MPa}\) and is currently working at 150 MPa. Find the actual factor of safety in use.

Solution

\(FoS=\sigma_y/\sigma_{work}=400/150=2.67\).

Practice 11

A bolt with \(A=113\text{ mm}^2\) carries 20 kN. \(\sigma_y=240\text{ MPa}\). Find the actual factor of safety.

Solution

\(\sigma_{work}=20000/(113\times10^{-6})=176.99\text{ MPa}\). \(FoS=240/176.99=1.36\).

Practice 12

Design A uses \(FoS=2\), Design B uses \(FoS=4\), same material and load. Which requires more material, and why?

Solution

Design B. A higher \(FoS\) means a lower \(\sigma_{allow}\), which forces a larger required area (\(A_{req}=F/\sigma_{allow}\)) to carry the same load.

3 Internal actions at a cut: N, V, M

Stress needs a force to act on, but what force is actually running through the middle of a loaded beam? To find out, imagine cutting the member at that point and looking at just one side. Whatever the removed half was doing to hold the cut piece in equilibrium becomes three internal actions: axial force \(N\) (along the member), shear force \(V\) (perpendicular to it), and bending moment \(M\).

Key idea

At any cut, isolate one side only, replace the removed portion with \(N\), \(V\), and \(M\), then solve using ordinary equilibrium: \(\Sigma F_x=0\) gives \(N\), \(\Sigma F_y=0\) gives \(V\), \(\Sigma M_{cut}=0\) gives \(M\).

Worked example

A simply-supported beam, 6 m long, carries a single 120 N downward load at its centre. Find \(N\), \(V\), and \(M\) just to the left of the load.

Reactions (symmetric load): \(R_A=R_B=60\text{ N}\).

Cut at \(x=3\text{ m}\) (just left of the load), keep the left segment: only \(R_A=60\text{ N}\) acts on it besides the cut itself.

\(\Sigma F_x=0\): \(N=0\) (no horizontal loads). \(\ \Sigma F_y=0\): \(V=R_A=60\text{ N}\).

\(\Sigma M_{cut}=0\): \(M=R_A\times3=60\times3=180\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}\).

Practice 13

A beam, span 4 m, symmetric central 200 N load (\(R_A=R_B=100\text{ N}\)). Find \(V\) and \(M\) just left of the load.

Solution

\(V=R_A=100\text{ N}\). \(M=R_A\times2=200\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}\).

Practice 14

A cantilever, fixed at A, 3 m long, with a 90 N load at the free end. Find \(N\), \(V\), \(M\) at a cut 1 m from the fixed end (using the free-end side, 2 m long).

Solution

\(N=0\). \(\Sigma F_y\): \(V=90\text{ N}\). \(\Sigma M_{cut}\): \(M=90\times2=180\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}\).

Practice 15

At a cut 2.5 m from the left support, the left segment's only external force is the 80 N reaction at the far end (nothing between). Find \(V\) and \(M\) at the cut.

Solution

\(V=80\text{ N}\). \(M=80\times2.5=200\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}\).

Practice 16

Explain why the internal axial force \(N=0\) everywhere along a beam that carries only vertical loads and has only vertical support reactions.

Solution

With no horizontal external forces anywhere (all loads and reactions are vertical), \(\Sigma F_x=0\) forces \(N=0\) at every section, there's simply nothing horizontal to balance.

Practice 17

Explain why cutting a member and analysing only one side's FBD (not both) is enough to find the internal actions at that section.

Solution

By Newton's third law, whatever internal force and moment one side exerts on the other at the cut, the other side exerts back equally and oppositely. Since both sides individually must be in equilibrium, solving either one alone gives exactly the same \(N,V,M\) at that section, one side is sufficient.

Practice 18

A simply-supported beam, span 8 m, has a single point load 6 m from A. Find the reactions, then \(V\) and \(M\) just left of the load.

Solution

Let the load be \(P\). \(\Sigma M_A\): \(R_B(8)=P(6) \Rightarrow R_B=0.75P\), \(R_A=0.25P\). Just left of the load: \(V=R_A=0.25P\), \(M=R_A(6)=1.5P\). (Numerically, for \(P=200\text{ N}\): \(V=50\text{ N}\), \(M=300\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}\).)

4 Advanced: sizing from internal actions

Once you know the internal axial force \(N\) at a critical section, from method-of-sections or a beam cut, you can size that section exactly like a simple axial member: apply \(\sigma=N/A\) and the allowable-stress method from Section 2 directly.

Worked example

A tie rod carries an internal axial tension of \(N=45\text{ kN}\) at its critical section. \(\sigma_y=280\text{ MPa}\), required \(FoS=2.8\). Find the minimum diameter.

\(\sigma_{allow}=280/2.8=100\text{ MPa}\).

\(A_{req}=45000/(100\times10^6)=4.5\times10^{-4}\text{ m}^2=450\text{ mm}^2\).

\(d=\sqrt{4(450)/\pi}=\sqrt{572.96}=23.9\text{ mm}\).

Practice 19

A truss member carries an internal axial tension \(N=32\text{ kN}\). \(\sigma_y=250\text{ MPa}\), \(FoS=2.5\). Find \(A_{req}\) and the minimum diameter.

Solution

\(\sigma_{allow}=100\text{ MPa}\). \(A_{req}=32000/(100\times10^6)=3.2\times10^{-4}\text{ m}^2=320\text{ mm}^2\). \(d=\sqrt{4(320)/\pi}=20.19\text{ mm}\).

Practice 20

A column's internal axial compressive force is \(N=180\text{ kN}\). \(\sigma_y=300\text{ MPa}\), \(FoS=3\). Find \(A_{req}\).

Solution

\(\sigma_{allow}=100\text{ MPa}\). \(A_{req}=180000/(100\times10^6)=1.8\times10^{-3}\text{ m}^2=1800\text{ mm}^2\).

Practice 21

A hanger rod carries \(N=25\text{ kN}\). The cross-section is rectangular, 20 mm wide, \(\sigma_{allow}=125\text{ MPa}\). Find the required height.

Solution

\(A_{req}=25000/(125\times10^6)=2\times10^{-4}\text{ m}^2=200\text{ mm}^2\). Height \(=200/20=10\text{ mm}\).

Practice 22

A beam's maximum internal bending moment \(M\) occurs at midspan, while its maximum shear \(V\) occurs at the supports. Explain why a designer checking bending-related failure should evaluate stress at midspan, not at the supports.

Solution

Bending-induced normal stress is directly proportional to the local bending moment \(M\) (via the flexure formula), so the section with the largest \(M\), here, midspan, is where bending stress and bending failure risk peak. Shear stress is a separate failure mode that peaks where \(V\) is largest, at the supports, so each mode must be checked at its own critical section, not at the same location.

Where to go from here. Section 3's internal-actions method, cutting a beam to find \(V\) and \(M\) at one point, is exactly what Week 5 extends into full shear force and bending moment diagrams, finding \(V(x)\) and \(M(x)\) at every point along the beam, not just one cut.
