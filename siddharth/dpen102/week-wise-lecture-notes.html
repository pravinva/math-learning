Week 1 - Newton Laws + Equilibrium Foundations

Engineering method, force concepts, Newton's laws, weight/normal/friction intro, and what statics means.

Lecture Focus (from source)

  • What ENG102/DPEN102 actually trains: engineering method + abstraction + diagrams.
  • Force systems, Newton's 1st/2nd/3rd law, static vs dynamic branches.
  • Weight \(W=mg\), normal reaction, and friction as tangential contact effect.
Net force: \(\Sigma \vec{F}=m\vec{a}\). Static equilibrium condition: \(\Sigma \vec{F}=0\).
Study method: Every problem starts with a clean physical statement: What is the body? What external forces exist? Is it static (\(a=0\)) or dynamic (\(a\neq0\))?

Mastery Check

  • Can you explain in one sentence why "balanced forces" means no acceleration?
  • Can you distinguish mass vs weight without mixing units?

Week 2 - Analysis Tools, Units, and FBD Discipline

Dimensions, SI conversions, significant figures, free-body diagram steps, and formal engineering-problem presentation.

Structured Workflow

  • Define dimensions and units first.
  • Convert all values into one consistent unit system (prefer SI).
  • Create FBD before equations.
  • Use the 6-step method: Define -> Data/Diagram -> Theory -> Estimate -> Solve -> Verify.
For moments in planar statics: \(\Sigma M_O = 0\), with sign convention fixed before calculation.

Week 3 - Scalars, Vectors, Components, and Equilibrium Setup (Expanded)

Deep mechanics vector week: component decomposition, unit vectors, force systems, and full FBD-to-equations workflow.

Core Learning Targets

  • Convert force vectors between magnitude-angle and component form.
  • Use \(i,j,k\) notation confidently: \(\vec{A}=A_x i + A_y j + A_z k\).
  • Build equilibrium equations from an FBD: \(\Sigma F_x=0,\ \Sigma F_y=0,\ \Sigma M=0\).
  • Recognize support reactions (roller, pin, fixed) and unknown counts.

Detailed Lesson Flow

  1. Sketch body and choose coordinate axes aligned with as many forces as possible.
  2. Resolve all angled forces into \(x,y\) components using sign-aware trig.
  3. Replace supports with reaction unknowns.
  4. Write independent equilibrium equations in clean order.
  5. Solve and sanity-check direction/magnitude.
\(|\vec{R}|=\sqrt{R_x^2+R_y^2}\), \(\theta=\tan^{-1}(R_y/R_x)\), \(\vec{R}=\sum \vec{F}\).
Worked BlockRod held by cable (equilibrium scaffold)

1) Draw rod as isolated body. 2) Apply weight at center. 3) Add cable tension along cable axis. 4) Add support reactions. 5) Sum moments at support first to reduce unknowns, then \(\Sigma F_x,\Sigma F_y\).

Expected trap: students solve components before fixing cable angle from geometry.

Week 4 - Structural Failure, Stress/Strain, and Internal Actions (Expanded)

Failure modes, factor of safety logic, and transitioning from external FBD to internal force/moment understanding.

What this week adds beyond equilibrium

  • External equilibrium does not guarantee safe design.
  • You must check induced stress/strain and compare against allowable values.
  • Internal actions at a cut section: axial \(N\), shear \(V\), bending \(M\).
Normal stress: \(\sigma = \frac{F}{A}\), strain: \(\varepsilon=\frac{\Delta L}{L_0}\), design check: \(\sigma_{work}\le \frac{\sigma_y}{FoS}\).
Worked BlockTurnbuckle-type sizing template

Given load \(F\), yield stress \(\sigma_y\), and \(FoS\): allowable stress \(=\sigma_y/FoS\). Required area \(A_{req}=F/\sigma_{allow}\). Convert area to diameter and compare against standard thread tensile area table (not nominal diameter area).

Week 5 - Shear Force & Bending Moment Equations/Diagrams (Expanded)

Derive piecewise \(V(x), M(x)\), handle load discontinuities, and read structural risk from diagrams.

High-value rules for exams

  • Point load -> jump in shear by load magnitude.
  • Distributed load \(w(x)\) -> slope of shear: \(dV/dx=w\).
  • Shear is slope of moment: \(dM/dx=V\).
  • Max/min moment usually where \(V=0\) (except point moments/discontinuities).
\(\frac{dV}{dx}=w(x)\), \(\frac{dM}{dx}=V(x)\), \(M(x)=\int V(x)\,dx\).
Worked BlockSimply supported central point load template

1) Reactions by symmetry: \(R_A=R_C=P/2\). 2) Section left of load gives constant \(V=+P/2\). 3) Section right gives \(V=-P/2\). 4) Integrate/piecewise moment functions. 5) Peak moment at center equals area under left shear block.

Mastery Check

  • Can you explain why shear graph slope equals distributed load?
  • Can you compute moment by area under shear without re-solving full equilibrium?

Week 6 - Truss Analysis (Method of Sections Priority) (Expanded)

Planar truss assumptions, zero-force members, section cuts, and tension/compression interpretation.

Assumptions you must state

  • Loads act at joints.
  • Members are two-force members (axial only).
  • Joints are ideal pins.

Method of Sections Algorithm

  1. Find global support reactions first.
  2. Cut through max 3 unknown member forces of interest.
  3. Take moments about intersection of two unknown force lines to isolate third unknown.
  4. Use \(\Sigma F_x,\Sigma F_y\) for remaining unknowns.
  5. Classify each result: tension (+) or compression (-).
Simple-truss relation: \(M=2J-3\) (quick stability/determinacy check for ideal planar simple truss).

Visual - Section Cut Strategy

Common Error Checks

  • Did you accidentally include forces from removed half twice?
  • Did you assume T/C direction and keep sign from equations?

Week 7 - Friction: Static, Impending, Kinetic + Wedges/Belts (Expanded)

Full friction-state reasoning, Coulomb model, angle of friction, and multi-contact systems (wedge/belt).

Three friction states (must identify before solving)

  • Static, not impending: \(F < F_s^{max}\), where \(F_s^{max}=\mu_sN\).
  • Impending: \(F=F_s^{max}=\mu_sN\).
  • Kinetic: \(F_k=\mu_kN\), usually \(\mu_k<\mu_s\).
\(F_s \le \mu_s N,\quad F_k=\mu_kN\).

Wedge problem structure

Build separate FBDs for wedge and lifted block. At each contact surface: normal perpendicular, friction tangent opposing relative motion tendency. Number unknowns and equations before arithmetic.

Belt friction key idea

Tension rises exponentially over wrap angle due to distributed friction interaction: \(T_2/T_1=e^{\mu\beta}\) for impending belt slip over drum.

Week 8 - Centroids, Center of Gravity, and Inertia (Expanded)

Resultant of distributed weight, composite area centroids, and second moment of area thinking for stiffness/design.

Core concepts

  • Center of gravity = line of action point of resultant weight.
  • For uniform material sections, centroid methods locate this point.
  • Second moment of area \(I\) controls bending stiffness and deflection behavior.
\(\bar{x}=\frac{\sum A_i x_i}{\sum A_i},\quad \bar{y}=\frac{\sum A_i y_i}{\sum A_i}\), and beam deflection sensitivity increases as \(I\) decreases.

Composite-section workflow

  1. Split section into simple shapes (add) and holes (subtract).
  2. Use consistent reference axes.
  3. Compute area moments \(\sum A_ix_i,\sum A_iy_i\).
  4. Find centroid and then use parallel-axis theorem if required for \(I\).

Visual - Composite Area Centroid

Mastery Check

  • Can you explain physically why larger \(I\) reduces deflection?
  • Can you keep sign convention for subtractive voids consistent?

Week 9 - Work, Potential/Kinetic Energy, and Conservation (Expanded)

Energy method for problems with varying forces and spring/gravity systems.

Week 9 conceptual spine

  • Energy = ability to do work.
  • Work-energy theorem links net work and kinetic energy change.
  • Conservative forces (gravity, ideal spring) allow potential-energy formulation.
\(W_{net}=\Delta K,\quad U_g=mgh,\quad U_s=\frac12kx^2,\quad K_1+U_1=K_2+U_2\) (for conservative-only cases).
Worked BlockSpring-block energy structure

At release: \(K_1=0,\ U_{s1}=\frac12kx_1^2\). At later point: include \(K_2\) and updated \(U_{s2}\), plus gravity term if height changes. Solve scalar equation directly (often faster than force-acceleration differential steps).

Exam strategy: Use energy when force varies with displacement (springs, gravity path changes) and when kinematics path is complicated but start/end states are clear.

  • Session B (2h): solve 6-10 problems using full format (Define -> FBD -> Equations -> Solve -> Verify).
