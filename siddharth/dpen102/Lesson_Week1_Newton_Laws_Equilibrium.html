1 Newton's three laws

Statics is the branch of mechanics dealing with bodies that don't accelerate, everything from here on is built directly on Newton's laws applied to that special case.

Key idea

1st law (inertia): an object at rest stays at rest, and an object moving at constant velocity keeps moving at that same velocity, unless acted on by a net force.

2nd law: \(\Sigma F=ma\).

3rd law: every action has an equal and opposite reaction.

Worked example 1

A 1000 kg car is parked on level ground. Using Newton's 1st law, explain why it doesn't move even though gravity acts on it.

Gravity (the car's weight) is exactly balanced by an equal and opposite normal force from the ground. Net force \(=0\), so by the 1st law the car's state (at rest) doesn't change.

Worked example 2

A book presses down on a table with 20 N. By Newton's 3rd law, what force does the table exert on the book?

\(20\text{ N}\) upward, the equal and opposite reaction.

Practice 1

A 5 kg object rests on a table. State the two forces acting on it and their relationship, per Newton's 1st law.

Solution

Weight \(=49\text{ N}\) down, Normal \(=49\text{ N}\) up, equal and opposite, net force \(=0\), so it stays at rest.

Practice 2

A hockey puck slides across frictionless ice at constant velocity. Which law explains why it doesn't slow down?

Solution

The 1st law: no net horizontal force acts on it, so there's no change in velocity.

Practice 3

A rocket expels gas downward to launch upward. Which law explains this, and how?

Solution

The 3rd law: the rocket pushes gas down (action), the gas pushes the rocket up (equal and opposite reaction), propelling it upward.

Practice 4

A 2 kg object has a net force of 10 N applied. Find its acceleration.

Solution

\(a=F/m=10/2=5\text{ m/s}^2\).

Practice 5

Is a car moving at a constant 100 km/h on a straight highway in equilibrium? Explain.

Solution

Yes. Constant velocity means zero acceleration, so by \(\Sigma F=ma=0\), the car is in equilibrium even though it's moving, equilibrium only requires zero net force, not being at rest.

Practice 6

Explain why "balanced forces" and "no acceleration" mean the same thing, using Newton's 2nd law.

Solution

Since \(\Sigma F=ma\), if \(\Sigma F=0\) (balanced forces) then \(a\) must also be \(0\) (for any nonzero mass), the two statements are mathematically the same thing, viewed through the 2nd law.

2 Weight, mass & normal force

Mass and weight get used interchangeably in casual speech, but they're not the same thing, and mixing them up is one of the most common early mistakes in mechanics.

Key idea

Mass \(m\) (kg): amount of matter, doesn't change with location. Weight \(W=mg\) (N): a force, depends on local gravity. Normal force \(N\): the contact reaction perpendicular to a surface, adjusts to whatever's needed for equilibrium, it is not always equal to weight.

Worked example 1

A 60 kg person stands on a scale on Earth (\(g=9.8\)). Find their weight.

\(W=60(9.8)=588\text{ N}\).

Worked example 2

The same person stands on a scale inside an elevator accelerating upward at 2 m/s². Find the scale reading.

\(N-W=ma \Rightarrow N=W+ma=588+60(2)=708\text{ N}\), the scale reads more than the person's actual weight while accelerating upward.

Practice 7

Find the weight of a 75 kg person on Earth.

Solution

\(W=75(9.8)=735\text{ N}\).

Practice 8

Find the weight of an 80 kg astronaut on the Moon (\(g_{moon}=1.62\text{ m/s}^2\)).

Solution

\(W=80(1.62)=129.6\text{ N}\).

Practice 9

Explain why mass stays the same on the Moon but weight changes.

Solution

Mass is the amount of matter (unchanged anywhere). Weight depends on local gravity via \(W=mg\), and \(g\) on the Moon (1.62) differs from Earth's (9.8), so \(W\) changes even though \(m\) doesn't.

Practice 10

A 50 kg person stands on a scale in an elevator accelerating DOWNWARD at 1.5 m/s². Find the scale reading.

Solution

Taking up as positive: \(N-W=m(-1.5)\). \(W=490\text{ N}\). \(N=490-50(1.5)=490-75=415\text{ N}\).

Practice 11

For Practice 10, what would the scale read if the elevator were in free fall (\(a=g\) downward)?

Solution

\(N=W-mg=W-W=0\), weightlessness, the scale reads zero.

Practice 12

A block rests on a table with weight 50 N, plus an additional 30 N pressed down on it. Find the normal force.

Solution

\(N=50+30=80\text{ N}\), the normal force adjusts to balance whatever is actually pressing down, not just the object's own weight.

3 Static equilibrium: ΣF=0

An object in static equilibrium has zero net force in every direction, not just one.

Key idea

$$\Sigma F_x=0, \qquad \Sigma F_y=0.$$

Worked example 1

A 40 N sign hangs from a single vertical rope. Find the rope tension.

\(\Sigma F_y=0\): \(T-W=0 \Rightarrow T=40\text{ N}\).

Worked example 2

A 25 kg crate on a frictionless surface is held by two horizontal ropes pulling in opposite directions, \(T_1=80\text{ N}\) left. Find \(T_2\) (right) for equilibrium.

\(\Sigma F_x=0\): \(T_2-T_1=0 \Rightarrow T_2=80\text{ N}\).

Practice 13

A 60 N lamp hangs from a single vertical cable. Find the tension.

Solution

\(T=60\text{ N}\).

Practice 14

A 15 kg object hangs from a vertical rope. Find the tension.

Solution

\(T=15(9.8)=147\text{ N}\).

Practice 15

Two horizontal forces act on a stationary block: 45 N left and \(F\) right. Find \(F\) for equilibrium.

Solution

\(F=45\text{ N}\).

Practice 16

A block in equilibrium has three horizontal forces: 20 N right, 15 N right, and \(F\) left. Find \(F\).

Solution

\(F=20+15=35\text{ N}\).

Practice 17

Explain why equilibrium requires BOTH \(\Sigma F_x=0\) AND \(\Sigma F_y=0\), not just one.

Solution

An object could have zero net force in one direction but a nonzero net force in the other, still causing acceleration in that other direction. True equilibrium (zero net force overall) needs both components to independently sum to zero.

Practice 18

A 90 N sign is held by two vertical ropes sharing the load equally. Find the tension in each.

Solution

Each rope \(=45\text{ N}\) (split equally by symmetry).

4 Newton's second law for dynamics

Statics (\(\Sigma F=0\)) is only the special case where nothing accelerates. When forces don't balance, the imbalance itself, the net force, tells you exactly how fast the object speeds up.

Key idea

$$\Sigma F=ma.$$

Worked example 1

A 10 kg cart is pushed with a net horizontal force of 25 N. Find its acceleration.

\(a=25/10=2.5\text{ m/s}^2\).

Worked example 2

A 5 kg block experiences 30 N forward and 12 N of friction backward. Find the net force and acceleration.

Net force \(=30-12=18\text{ N}\). \(a=18/5=3.6\text{ m/s}^2\).

Practice 19

An 8 kg object has a net force of 40 N. Find its acceleration.

Solution

\(a=40/8=5\text{ m/s}^2\).

Practice 20

A 12 kg cart has 50 N forward and 20 N of resistance backward. Find the net force and acceleration.

Solution

Net \(=50-20=30\text{ N}\). \(a=30/12=2.5\text{ m/s}^2\).

Practice 21

A 3 kg object accelerates at 4 m/s². Find the net force required.

Solution

\(F=3(4)=12\text{ N}\).

Practice 22

Explain the key difference between solving a "statics" problem (\(\Sigma F=0\)) and a "dynamics" problem (\(\Sigma F=ma\neq0\)).

Solution

In statics, unknown forces are solved so the equations balance to zero (nothing moves). In dynamics, the imbalance itself, the net force, is used directly to compute how fast the object accelerates. Statics finds unknown forces assuming no motion; dynamics finds the resulting motion from known force imbalances.

Practice 23

A 500 kg rocket has thrust 8000 N upward and weight 4900 N. Find its acceleration.

Solution

Net force \(=8000-4900=3100\text{ N}\). \(a=3100/500=6.2\text{ m/s}^2\) upward.

Where to go from here. Every equilibrium equation used from here on, \(\Sigma F_x=0\), \(\Sigma F_y=0\), and later \(\Sigma M=0\), is just Newton's laws applied systematically. Week 2 adds the bookkeeping discipline (units, significant figures, the 6-step method) and the FBD workflow that makes all of this reliable at scale.
