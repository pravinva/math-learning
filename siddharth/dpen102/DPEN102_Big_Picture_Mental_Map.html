The Big Picture, Before the Details

Thirteen weeks, forty-some topics, hundreds of practice problems. Before diving into any of it, here's the one question the whole course is actually answering, the four-stage arc it moves through to answer it, and how the pieces connect to each other.

The Big Goal
Given a real object under real loads — will it hold?
Every formula, every diagram, every practice problem in DPEN102 is one piece of answering that single question: does the structure stay in equilibrium, and can the material survive the forces running through it?

That's what "statics" actually means: analysing things that don't move under load, bridges, cranes, brackets, beams, the frame of a building, then, once you know how to do that, checking whether the material inside is strong enough not to fail. Everything from Week 1's Newton's laws to Week 13's final mock is building toward being able to answer that question quickly, correctly, and with a diagram you could hand to another engineer.

The Equilibrium Chain

Nearly every problem in this course, no matter which week it's from, walks through some version of the same eight-step chain. Learning the course is really learning to run this chain fluently, then applying it to increasingly complicated objects.

1
Describe the forcesWeeks 1–3: vectors, components, magnitude and angle. A force isn't just a number, it has a direction too.
2
Isolate the object & draw every force acting on itThe FBD lesson: the single most important habit in the entire course.
3
Balance the forces and momentsΣFx=0, ΣFy=0, ΣM=0. This is what "equilibrium" means, mathematically.
4
Find what's happening inside the objectWeek 4: cut it open, find the internal axial force, shear, and bending moment at that point.
5
Check whether the material survivesWeek 4 & 11: convert those internal forces into stress, compare against an allowable limit.
6
Apply it to real structural shapesWeeks 5–6: whole beams (SFD/BMD) and whole trusses (method of sections), not just one isolated cut.
7
Add real-world complicationsWeeks 7–9: friction resisting motion, geometry itself as a design variable (centroids, stiffness), and energy as an alternative lens entirely.
8
Do all eight steps together, fluently, under pressureWeeks 10–13: the same chain, but now on realistic, multi-part, timed problems.
The Four Arcs

Zoom out from individual weeks, and the course actually has just four movements. Each one answers a different question, and each one leans on everything before it.

ARC I

Foundations

Weeks 1–3 + FBD

Question it answers: "How do I correctly describe and add up forces?"

Newton's laws explain why balanced forces mean no motion. Units and the 6-step method build the discipline to not make careless mistakes. Vectors and components give the algebra for forces at angles. The FBD lesson ties all three together into the one practical skill every later week reuses: isolate an object, draw every force acting on it, nothing more, nothing less.

ARC II

Structures

Weeks 4–6

Question it answers: "What's happening inside the object, and does it survive?"

Week 4 cuts a member open to find the internal forces at one point, then checks the material against an allowable stress. Week 5 stretches that same cut into a full diagram along an entire beam. Week 6 applies the identical equilibrium tools, now to a whole network of pin-jointed members, a truss, solving for individual member forces with a single well-placed cut.

ARC III

Real-World Complications

Weeks 7–9

Question it answers: "What else acts on real objects, and are there other ways to solve for it?"

Friction adds a force that resists sliding, in disguises ranging from a block on a table to a rope wrapped around a capstan. Centroids and second moment of area turn geometry itself into a design tool, where a shape's material sits determines how stiff it is. Energy methods step outside the force-and-moment toolkit entirely, useful exactly when tracking forces directly gets too messy (springs, curved paths, varying accelerations).

ARC IV

Synthesis

Weeks 10–13

Question it answers: "Can you actually do all of this, fluently, like an engineer would?"

Week 10 combines pairs and triples of everything above into single multi-part problems. Week 11 turns the stress and geometry tools into real pass/fail design decisions. Week 12 runs timed mixed practice and tags recurring mistakes by type. Week 13 closes with a formula sheet, a readiness checklist, and one final comprehensive mock, no new material, just proof the whole chain runs smoothly under pressure.

The Mental Map

Visually, the course looks less like a straight line and more like a trunk with branches, a few foundational ideas (FBD, equilibrium) that nearly everything later reuses.

Follow the Thread: two ideas across the whole course

The clearest way to see how the weeks connect is to pick one concept and trace it start to finish.

Thread 1: the moment
Wk 2: M=F×d→ Wk 3: ΣM=0 in equilibrium→ Wk 4: internal bending moment M at a cut→ Wk 5: M(x) along a whole beam (BMD)→ Wk 11: σ=Mc/I, bending stress

A single simple idea, force times distance, starts as a way to balance a seesaw in Week 2, and by Week 11 is the exact quantity used to decide whether a real beam's material will crack under load. Same formula, same logic, applied to something enormously more complex by the end.

Thread 2: the free body diagram
Wk 1: which forces act here?→ FBD Lesson: isolate, draw, equilibrium→ Wk 4–6: FBD of a cut, a beam, a truss joint→ Wk 7: FBD + friction force→ Wk 10–13: FBD as step one of everything

Every single practice problem in this course, from the simplest block on a table to the final mock's twelve-part problem, starts with the exact same five-step FBD method built in Week 1–2. Nothing new is ever added to that method, later weeks just apply it to more complicated objects: a cut section, a whole beam, a truss joint, a wedge, a ladder against a wall.

Why this matters beyond the course

This isn't just an academic sequence, it's the actual workflow a structural or mechanical engineer runs on a real project: figure out the loads, draw the FBD, balance the forces, check the internal stresses, size the members, and verify the design has an adequate safety margin. DPEN102 teaches that workflow on progressively harder objects, a block, a beam, a truss, a bracket, so that by Week 13 it's second nature rather than something to look up.

Every later mechanics, structures, or machine-design subject in the Diploma and beyond assumes this chain is already automatic. That's the actual point of the thirteen weeks: not to memorise formulas, but to build the habit of thinking in FBDs and equilibrium until it's the default way of looking at anything that holds weight.
