0 Composite centroids: addition

The centroid of a composite shape is an area-weighted average of each piece's own centroid, larger pieces pull the average toward themselves, exactly like a weighted mean.

Key idea

$$\bar x = \frac{\sum A_ix_i}{\sum A_i}, \qquad \bar y = \frac{\sum A_iy_i}{\sum A_i}.$$

Worked example 1

Two rectangles: \(R_1\) area 8, centroid \((2,1)\); \(R_2\) area 6, centroid \((5,1.5)\). Find the composite centroid.

\(\bar x = \dfrac{8(2)+6(5)}{14}=\dfrac{46}{14}=3.286\).

\(\bar y = \dfrac{8(1)+6(1.5)}{14}=\dfrac{17}{14}=1.214\).

Worked example 2

An L-shaped section: base rectangle 6×2 (area 12, centroid \((3,1)\)), upright rectangle 2×4 sitting on top of its right end (area 8, centroid \((5,4)\)). Find the composite centroid.

\(\bar x = \dfrac{12(3)+8(5)}{20}=\dfrac{76}{20}=3.8\).

\(\bar y = \dfrac{12(1)+8(4)}{20}=\dfrac{44}{20}=2.2\).

Practice 1

Two rectangles: \(A_1=10\) at \((1,2)\), \(A_2=15\) at \((4,3)\). Find the composite centroid.

Solution

\(\bar x=\dfrac{10(1)+15(4)}{25}=\dfrac{70}{25}=2.8\). \(\bar y=\dfrac{10(2)+15(3)}{25}=\dfrac{65}{25}=2.6\).

Practice 2

Three equal squares, each area 4, centroids at \((1,1),(3,1),(5,1)\). Find the composite centroid.

Solution

\(\bar x=\dfrac{4(1)+4(3)+4(5)}{12}=\dfrac{36}{12}=3\). \(\bar y=1\) (all pieces share the same \(y\)).

Practice 3

A rectangle \(A_1=20\) at \((2,5)\) and a triangle \(A_2=6\) at \((6,2)\). Find the composite centroid.

Solution

\(\bar x=\dfrac{20(2)+6(6)}{26}=\dfrac{76}{26}=2.923\). \(\bar y=\dfrac{20(5)+6(2)}{26}=\dfrac{112}{26}=4.308\).

Practice 4

A T-shape: top rectangle \(A_1=12\) at \((3,5)\), stem \(A_2=8\) at \((3,2)\). Find the composite centroid.

Solution

\(\bar x=3\) (symmetric, both pieces share \(x=3\)). \(\bar y=\dfrac{12(5)+8(2)}{20}=\dfrac{76}{20}=3.8\).

Practice 5

State the centroid location (from the flat edge) of a semicircle of radius \(r=6\), using \(\bar y=\dfrac{4r}{3\pi}\).

Solution

\(\bar y=\dfrac{4(6)}{3\pi}=\dfrac{24}{9.4248}=2.546\).

Practice 6

Explain why an L-shaped composite section's centroid does NOT sit at the "visual middle" of the shape.

Solution

\(\bar x,\bar y\) are area-weighted averages, so the piece with more area pulls the composite centroid toward itself, exactly like a weighted average leans toward the larger weight. The "visual middle" only coincides with the centroid for symmetric shapes.

1 Composite areas with holes: subtraction

A hole is just negative area, plug it into the exact same formulas with a minus sign.

Worked example 1

A 10×8 rectangle (area 80, centroid \((5,4)\)) has a circular hole radius 2 (area \(=12.57\)) centered at \((7,4)\). Find the composite centroid.

\(\bar x=\dfrac{80(5)-12.57(7)}{80-12.57}=\dfrac{312.04}{67.43}=4.629\).

\(\bar y=\dfrac{80(4)-12.57(4)}{67.43}=4.0\) (unchanged, since the hole's \(y\) matches the gross centroid's \(y\)).

Worked example 2

A 12×6 rectangle (area 72, centroid \((6,3)\)) has two square holes: side 2 (area 4) at \((3,3)\), and side 1.5 (area 2.25) at \((9,4)\). Find the composite centroid.

Total area \(=72-4-2.25=65.75\).

\(\bar x=\dfrac{72(6)-4(3)-2.25(9)}{65.75}=\dfrac{399.75}{65.75}=6.080\).

\(\bar y=\dfrac{72(3)-4(3)-2.25(4)}{65.75}=\dfrac{195}{65.75}=2.966\).

Practice 7

An 8×6 rectangle (area 48, centroid \((4,3)\)) with a circular hole radius 1.5 (area 7.069) at \((5,3)\). Find \(\bar x,\bar y\).

Solution

Total \(=40.93\). \(\bar x=\dfrac{48(4)-7.069(5)}{40.93}=\dfrac{156.66}{40.93}=3.828\). \(\bar y=3.0\) (unchanged).

Practice 8

A 10×10 square (area 100, centroid \((5,5)\)) with a square hole side 3 (area 9) at \((2,2)\). Find \(\bar x,\bar y\).

Solution

Total \(=91\). \(\bar x=\bar y=\dfrac{100(5)-9(2)}{91}=\dfrac{482}{91}=5.297\).

Practice 9

A 6×4 rectangle (area 24, centroid \((3,2)\)) with two circular holes, each radius 1, at \((2,2)\) and \((4,2)\). Find \(\bar x,\bar y\).

Solution

Total \(=24-3.1416-3.1416=17.717\). \(\bar x=\dfrac{72-6.283-12.566}{17.717}=3.0\) (symmetric). \(\bar y=2\) (unchanged).

Practice 10

Explain why removing a hole located exactly AT the gross shape's centroid doesn't shift \(\bar x\) or \(\bar y\) at all.

Solution

The hole's coordinates equal the gross centroid's coordinates, so the amount subtracted from the numerator is exactly proportional to what's subtracted from the denominator, algebraically, removing area exactly at the current balance point can't shift that balance point.

Practice 11

A 12×8 rectangle (area 96, centroid \((6,4)\)) has a rectangular hole 4×2 (area 8) centered at \((9,6)\). Find \(\bar x,\bar y\).

Solution

Total \(=88\). \(\bar x=\dfrac{576-72}{88}=5.727\). \(\bar y=\dfrac{384-48}{88}=3.818\).

Practice 12

A washer is formed by removing a 2 cm-radius hole from the centre of a 6 cm-radius disk. Where is the composite centroid, and why?

Solution

Still at the exact centre. Since both the gross disk and the hole are concentric, sharing the same centroid, removing the hole doesn't move the balance point at all.

2 Second moment of area & parallel axis theorem

Second moment of area \(I\) measures how resistant a cross-section is to bending, larger \(I\) means a stiffer beam for the same material. The parallel axis theorem lets you shift \(I\) from a shape's own centroidal axis to any other parallel axis.

Key idea

Rectangle about its own centroidal axis: $$I=\frac{bh^3}{12}.$$ Parallel axis theorem: $$I_{other} = I_{centroidal} + Ad^2,$$ where \(d\) is the distance between the two parallel axes.

Worked example 1

A rectangular beam cross-section, \(b=100\text{ mm}\), \(h=200\text{ mm}\). Find \(I\) about its own centroidal axis.

\(I=\dfrac{100(200)^3}{12}=\dfrac{800{,}000{,}000}{12}=66{,}666{,}667\text{ mm}^4\).

Worked example 2

The same rectangle's centroid sits 150 mm from a reference axis. Find \(I\) about that reference axis.

\(A=100(200)=20{,}000\text{ mm}^2\).

\(I_{ref}=66{,}666{,}667+20{,}000(150)^2=66{,}666{,}667+450{,}000{,}000=516{,}666{,}667\text{ mm}^4\).

Practice 13

A rectangle, \(b=80\text{ mm}, h=150\text{ mm}\). Find its centroidal \(I\).

Solution

\(I=\dfrac{80(150)^3}{12}=\dfrac{270{,}000{,}000}{12}=22{,}500{,}000\text{ mm}^4\).

Practice 14

A rectangle, \(b=60\text{ mm}, h=250\text{ mm}\). Find its centroidal \(I\).

Solution

\(I=\dfrac{60(250)^3}{12}=\dfrac{937{,}500{,}000}{12}=78{,}125{,}000\text{ mm}^4\).

Practice 15

A rectangle, \(A=20{,}000\text{ mm}^2\), centroidal \(I=40{,}000{,}000\text{ mm}^4\), centroid 100 mm from a reference axis. Find \(I\) about that axis.

Solution

\(I_{ref}=40{,}000{,}000+20{,}000(100)^2=40{,}000{,}000+200{,}000{,}000=240{,}000{,}000\text{ mm}^4\).

Practice 16

A shape has \(A=5000\text{ mm}^2\), centroidal \(I=1{,}000{,}000\text{ mm}^4\), centroid 80 mm from a reference axis. Find \(I_{ref}\), and what fraction came from the transfer term.

Solution

Transfer term \(=5000(80)^2=32{,}000{,}000\text{ mm}^4\). \(I_{ref}=1{,}000{,}000+32{,}000{,}000=33{,}000{,}000\text{ mm}^4\). Fraction from transfer \(=32{,}000{,}000/33{,}000{,}000=97.0\%\).

Practice 17

Two identical rectangles, each centroidal \(I=10{,}000{,}000\text{ mm}^4\) and \(A=8000\text{ mm}^2\), placed with centroids 30 mm on either side of a reference axis. Find the total \(I\) about that axis.

Solution

Each: \(10{,}000{,}000+8000(30)^2=10{,}000{,}000+7{,}200{,}000=17{,}200{,}000\text{ mm}^4\). Total \(=2(17{,}200{,}000)=34{,}400{,}000\text{ mm}^4\).

Practice 18

Explain why the parallel-axis transfer term (\(Ad^2\)) makes I-beams put most of their material in the flanges, far from the centroid, rather than spreading it evenly.

Solution

Because the transfer term is \(Ad^2\), doubling the distance \(d\) quadruples that term's contribution. Material far from the axis contributes vastly more stiffness per unit area than material near it, so I-beams concentrate material at the flanges to maximise \(I\) for a given amount of material, rather than wasting it near the centroid where it barely helps.

3 Advanced: composite second moment of area

For a composite section: find the composite centroid first (Sections 0–1), then apply the parallel axis theorem to each piece individually, shifting its own \(I\) to the shared composite axis, then sum everything.

Worked example

A T-section: flange 100×20 mm (area 2000, centroid at \(y=190\text{ mm}\)), stem 20×180 mm (area 3600, centroid at \(y=90\text{ mm}\)). Find the composite centroidal \(I\).

Composite centroid: \(\bar y=\dfrac{2000(190)+3600(90)}{5600}=\dfrac{704{,}000}{5600}=125.71\text{ mm}\).

Flange's own \(I_1=\dfrac{100(20)^3}{12}=66{,}667\text{ mm}^4\). Stem's own \(I_2=\dfrac{20(180)^3}{12}=9{,}720{,}000\text{ mm}^4\).

Distances to composite centroid: \(d_1=|190-125.71|=64.29\text{ mm}\), \(d_2=|90-125.71|=35.71\text{ mm}\).

\(I_{1,total}=66{,}667+2000(64.29)^2=8{,}333{,}067\text{ mm}^4\). \(I_{2,total}=9{,}720{,}000+3600(35.71)^2=14{,}310{,}720\text{ mm}^4\).

Composite \(I=8{,}333{,}067+14{,}310{,}720=22{,}643{,}787\text{ mm}^4\).

Practice 19

For the T-section above, is the composite centroid closer to the stem's own centroid (90) or the flange's (190)? Why, given the areas?

Solution

Closer to the stem (distance 35.71 vs 64.29), since the stem has more area (3600 vs 2000), pulling the composite centroid toward itself.

Practice 20

A composite section: \(R_1\) area 1500, own \(I=50{,}000\text{ mm}^4\), centroid 40 mm from the composite axis; \(R_2\) area 2500, own \(I=120{,}000\text{ mm}^4\), centroid 25 mm away (opposite side). Find the total composite \(I\).

Solution

\(R_1\): \(50{,}000+1500(40)^2=2{,}450{,}000\text{ mm}^4\). \(R_2\): \(120{,}000+2500(25)^2=1{,}682{,}500\text{ mm}^4\). Total \(=4{,}132{,}500\text{ mm}^4\).

Practice 21

Explain why you must find the OVERALL composite centroid first, before applying the parallel axis theorem to each piece.

Solution

The parallel-axis theorem shifts a piece's own centroidal \(I\) to a different, chosen reference axis. If that reference were each piece's own local centroid, you'd just recover each piece's own \(I\) with no meaningful combination, the whole point is measuring every piece's contribution relative to the same shared axis, which must be the composite centroid.

Practice 22

If a piece's own centroid happens to sit exactly at the composite centroid (\(\bar y=125.71\text{ mm}\)), what is its transfer term, and why?

Solution

Zero. The transfer term is \(Ad^2\), and \(d=0\) when the piece's own centroid coincides with the composite centroid, its full contribution comes purely from its own centroidal \(I\), with no transfer needed.

Practice 23

Two composite sections have the same total area and height. Section A concentrates area far from the centroid (I-beam-like); Section B spreads it evenly (solid rectangle). Which has the larger \(I\), and why?

Solution

Section A. The transfer term \(Ad^2\) rewards area placed far from the axis quadratically, concentrating area at the extremes maximises \(I\) for a given total area, exactly why I-beams are shaped the way they are.

Where to go from here. Second moment of area is what turns Week 4's internal bending moment \(M\) into an actual bending stress, via \(\sigma=Mc/I\), where \(c\) is the distance from the composite centroid to the extreme fibre. Week 9 shifts gears entirely, from geometry and stress to energy methods.
