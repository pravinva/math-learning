1 SI units & conversions

Every calculation in DPEN102 should run in one consistent unit system, preferably SI, before any arithmetic happens.

Key idea

Base SI units: length (m), mass (kg), time (s). Force is derived: \(\text{N}=\text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2\). Common conversions: \(1\text{ kN}=1000\text{ N}\), \(1\text{ mm}=0.001\text{ m}\), \(1\text{ MPa}=10^6\text{ Pa}=1\text{ N/mm}^2\).

Worked example 1

Convert 2.5 kN to N.

\(2.5\times1000=2500\text{ N}\).

Worked example 2

A force of 450 N acts on an area of 15 mm². Find the stress in MPa.

\(\sigma = 450/(15\times10^{-6}\text{ m}^2) = 30\times10^6\text{ Pa} = 30\text{ MPa}\).

Shortcut: since \(1\text{ N/mm}^2=1\text{ MPa}\), you can skip straight to \(450\text{ N}/15\text{ mm}^2=30\text{ N/mm}^2=30\text{ MPa}\).

Practice 1

Convert 3.2 kN to N.

Solution

\(3200\text{ N}\).

Practice 2

Convert 850 mm to m.

Solution

\(0.85\text{ m}\).

Practice 3

Convert 0.06 m² to mm².

Solution

\(0.06\times10^6=60{,}000\text{ mm}^2\) (since \(1\text{ m}^2=10^6\text{ mm}^2\)).

Practice 4

A stress of 85 MPa acts over an area of 200 mm². Find the force in N.

Solution

\(F=\sigma\times A=85(200)=17{,}000\text{ N}=17\text{ kN}\) (using the MPa \(=\) N/mm² shortcut directly).

Practice 5

Convert 45,000 Pa to kPa and to MPa.

Solution

\(45\text{ kPa}\), \(0.045\text{ MPa}\).

Practice 6

Show why 1 N/mm² is numerically equal to 1 MPa.

Solution

\(1\text{ N/mm}^2 = 1\text{ N}/(10^{-3}\text{ m})^2 = 1\text{ N}/10^{-6}\text{ m}^2 = 10^6\text{ N/m}^2 = 10^6\text{ Pa} = 1\text{ MPa}\). The conversion factor works out to exactly 1, making the two units interchangeable for stress calculations.

2 Significant figures

Report answers to a precision consistent with your least precise given value, no more, no less.

Key idea

The final answer's significant figures are limited by the least precise input value. Don't round mid-calculation, only round the final reported answer.

Worked example 1

Multiply 4.2 (2 sig figs) by 1.75 (3 sig figs). How many sig figs should the answer have?

The least precise input has 2 sig figs, so the answer is limited to 2: \(4.2\times1.75=7.35 \to 7.4\).

Worked example 2

A calculated stress comes out to 83.276491 MPa. The input data had 3 significant figures. How should this be reported?

83.3 MPa (3 sig figs).

Practice 7

How many significant figures does 0.00450 have?

Solution

3 (the trailing zero after the 5 is explicitly written, so it counts; the leading zeros do not).

Practice 8

Multiply \(2.5\times3.14159\) and report to appropriate significant figures (2.5 has 2 sig figs).

Solution

\(2.5\times3.14159=7.853975 \to \mathbf{7.9}\) (2 sig figs).

Practice 9

A length is measured as 12.400 m. How many significant figures does it have?

Solution

5 (all digits, including trailing zeros after a decimal point, are significant).

Practice 10

Explain why reporting a result as "83.276491 MPa" when your input data only had 3 sig figs is misleading.

Solution

It implies a level of certainty the original measurements never had. Carrying that many digits falsely suggests you know the answer to within millionths of a MPa, when the input data was only reliable to 3 significant figures.

Practice 11

During a multi-step calculation, should you round after every step, or only at the very end? Explain why.

Solution

Only at the very end. Rounding after every intermediate step introduces small errors that can compound and produce a noticeably wrong final answer. Keep full precision through the calculation and round only the final reported result.

3 The 6-step engineering method

Define → Data/Diagram → Theory → Estimate → Solve → Verify. Always loop back if a units, sign, or physics check fails.

Worked example

Apply the 6-step method to: "Find the tension in a rope holding a 20 kg mass at rest."

Define: the rope tension. Data/Diagram: \(m=20\text{ kg}\), \(g=9.8\), FBD showing \(W\) down, \(T\) up.

Theory: \(\Sigma F_y=0\) (static equilibrium). Estimate: roughly \(20\times10=200\text{ N}\).

Solve: \(T=W=20(9.8)=196\text{ N}\). Verify: close to the 200 N estimate, units are N (force), sign makes sense, checks out.

Practice 12

Why does "Estimate" come BEFORE "Solve" in the 6-step method?

Solution

A rough estimate gives something to sanity-check the precise answer against. Having it in advance prevents unconsciously adjusting the estimate to match whatever answer you calculate, it keeps the check honest.

Practice 13

A calculated answer comes out in the wrong units (expected N, got N·m). Which step catches this, and what should you do?

Solution

Caught at Verify. Loop back through the calculation (likely to Theory or Solve) to find where an extra length term was accidentally introduced.

Practice 14

Why is drawing the FBD placed under "Data/Diagram" rather than "Theory"?

Solution

The FBD is a visual organisation of the given information (what forces exist and where), part of correctly capturing the data, not yet applying the physics laws (equilibrium equations) that come next under Theory.

Practice 15

Apply the 6-step method (briefly, one line per step) to: "Find the acceleration of a 4 kg block pushed by a net force of 20 N."

Solution

Define: find acceleration. Data: \(m=4\text{ kg}, F=20\text{ N}\). Theory: \(\Sigma F=ma\). Estimate: roughly \(5\text{ m/s}^2\). Solve: \(a=20/4=5\text{ m/s}^2\). Verify: matches the estimate, units correct ✓.

4 Moments: ΣM=0 basics

A moment is force's rotational cousin, how much a force tends to twist something around a pivot.

Key idea

$$M=F\times d$$ where \(d\) is the perpendicular distance from the pivot to the force's line of action. Fix a sign convention (commonly counter-clockwise positive) before calculating. For rigid-body equilibrium: \(\Sigma M_O=0\) about any chosen point \(O\).

Worked example 1

A 50 N force is applied 3 m from a pivot, perpendicular to the lever arm. Find the moment.

\(M=50(3)=150\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}\).

Worked example 2

A seesaw is balanced with a 40 kg child sitting 1.5 m from the pivot on one side. Where must a 30 kg child sit on the other side to balance it?

\(M_1=M_2\): \(40(9.8)(1.5)=30(9.8)(d)\), mass and \(g\) both cancel: \(40(1.5)=30d \Rightarrow d=2\text{ m}\).

Practice 16

A 100 N force acts 2 m from a pivot, perpendicular. Find the moment.

Solution

\(M=100(2)=200\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}\).

Practice 17

A wrench applies 80 N at the end of a 0.3 m handle, perpendicular. Find the moment on the bolt.

Solution

\(M=80(0.3)=24\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}\).

Practice 18

A 25 kg child sits 2 m from a seesaw's pivot. Find the distance for a 20 kg child on the other side to balance it.

Solution

\(25(2)=20d \Rightarrow d=2.5\text{ m}\).

Practice 19

Two moments act about a pivot: 60 N·m clockwise and 60 N·m counter-clockwise. Is the system in rotational equilibrium?

Solution

Yes. The two moments are equal and opposite, so \(\Sigma M=0\), satisfying rotational equilibrium.

Practice 20

Explain why the sign convention (clockwise vs counter-clockwise) must be fixed BEFORE calculating moments, not decided afterward.

Solution

Without a fixed convention, it's easy to accidentally add two moments that should actually be subtracted (or vice versa), since a moment's sign depends entirely on its rotational direction relative to the chosen convention. Deciding it first ensures every term in \(\Sigma M=0\) is combined correctly.

Where to go from here. Moments here are the direct foundation for Week 4's internal bending moment \(M\) and Week 5's full bending moment diagrams, exactly the same \(F\times d\) idea, just applied at every point along a beam instead of one pivot.
