0 The three friction states

Friction isn't one fixed value, it's whatever is needed for equilibrium, up to a ceiling. Below that ceiling, the object stays put; at the ceiling, it's about to slip; once actually sliding, a different (usually lower) coefficient applies.

Key idea

Static, not impending: \(F

Impending: \(F=F_s^{max}=\mu_sN\) (about to slip).

Kinetic: \(F_k=\mu_kN\), once actually sliding, usually with \(\mu_k<\mu_s\).

Worked example 1

A 20 kg crate sits on a floor, \(\mu_s=0.4\). A horizontal force of 60 N is applied. Does it move? Find the actual friction force.

\(W=196\text{ N}=N\). \(F_s^{max}=0.4(196)=78.4\text{ N}\).

\(60\text{ N} < 78.4\text{ N}\): the crate does not move. Friction simply matches the applied force: \(f=60\text{ N}\).

Worked example 2

Same crate, the applied force is increased to 90 N (\(\mu_k=0.3\)). Does it move now, and what's the actual friction while moving?

\(90\text{ N} > 78.4\text{ N}\) (the static maximum): the crate does slide.

Once sliding, kinetic friction applies: \(f_k=\mu_kN=0.3(196)=58.8\text{ N}\), leaving a net force of \(90-58.8=31.2\text{ N}\) to accelerate the crate.

Practice 1

A 15 kg box, \(\mu_s=0.35\), on a floor. Applied force 40 N. Does it move? Find the actual friction.

Solution

\(W=147\text{ N}\). \(F_s^{max}=0.35(147)=51.45\text{ N}\). \(40<51.45\): does not move; \(f=40\text{ N}\).

Practice 2

Same box, force increased to 55 N. Does it move?

Solution

\(55>51.45\): yes, it moves.

Practice 3

A 10 kg block, \(\mu_s=0.5\), applied force 55 N. Find \(F_s^{max}\) and determine if it moves.

Solution

\(W=98\text{ N}\). \(F_s^{max}=0.5(98)=49\text{ N}\). \(55>49\): it moves.

Practice 4

For Practice 3 (\(\mu_k=0.35\)), find the kinetic friction and the net accelerating force once moving.

Solution

\(f_k=0.35(98)=34.3\text{ N}\). Net force \(=55-34.3=20.7\text{ N}\).

Practice 5

A 25 kg crate, \(\mu_s=0.45\). What is the maximum horizontal force before it moves?

Solution

\(W=245\text{ N}\). \(F_s^{max}=0.45(245)=110.25\text{ N}\).

Practice 6

Explain why \(\mu_k\) is typically less than \(\mu_s\), and what this means for a crate that has just started sliding.

Solution

Once surfaces are sliding, microscopic contact points don't "lock in" as strongly as when stationary, reducing resistance. Practically, this means the force needed to keep a crate moving is less than what it took to start it moving, once it breaks free, the crate can suddenly accelerate as the resisting force drops from \(F_s^{max}\) to the lower \(F_k\).

1 Friction on inclines & angle of repose

On an incline, gravity's down-slope pull is \(W\sin\theta\), balanced by \(N=W\cos\theta\). At the exact angle where friction alone can no longer hold a block, \(\tan\theta=\mu_s\), the "angle of repose", the mass cancels out entirely.

Worked example 1

A block is on the verge of sliding on a 22° incline (friction alone, no rope). Find \(\mu_s\).

\(\mu_s=\tan22^\circ = 0.404\).

Worked example 2

A 30 kg crate sits on a 15° incline, \(\mu_s=0.3\). Does it stay in place? Find the actual friction force.

\(W=294\text{ N}\). \(N=294\cos15^\circ=284.0\text{ N}\). \(F_s^{max}=0.3(284)=85.2\text{ N}\).

Required friction \(=W\sin15^\circ=294(0.2588)=76.1\text{ N}\). Since \(76.1<85.2\): stays in place, actual friction \(=76.1\text{ N}\) (below maximum).

Practice 7

A block is on the verge of sliding on an 18° incline. Find \(\mu_s\).

Solution

\(\mu_s=\tan18^\circ=0.325\).

Practice 8

A 40 kg crate on a 20° incline, \(\mu_s=0.25\). Does it stay? Compare required vs maximum friction.

Solution

\(W=392\text{ N}\). \(N=392\cos20^\circ=368.4\text{ N}\). \(F_s^{max}=0.25(368.4)=92.1\text{ N}\). Required \(=392\sin20^\circ=134.1\text{ N}\). Since \(134.1>92.1\): it slides.

Practice 9

For Practice 8, once sliding (\(\mu_k=0.18\)), find the kinetic friction and the net force down the slope.

Solution

\(f_k=0.18(368.4)=66.3\text{ N}\). Net force \(=134.1-66.3=67.8\text{ N}\) down the slope.

Practice 10

Find the acceleration of the crate in Practice 9 (mass 40 kg).

Solution

\(a=67.8/40=1.695\text{ m/s}^2\) down the slope.

Practice 11

A 20 kg crate on a 25° incline, \(\mu_s=0.5\), no rope. Find the maximum additional downslope force before it slides.

Solution

\(W=196\text{ N}\). \(N=196\cos25^\circ=177.6\text{ N}\). \(F_s^{max}=0.5(177.6)=88.8\text{ N}\). Required (gravity alone) \(=196\sin25^\circ=82.8\text{ N}\). Margin \(=88.8-82.8=6.0\text{ N}\).

Practice 12

Explain, using \(\tan\theta=\mu_s\), why a steeper incline than the angle of repose guarantees sliding regardless of the block's weight.

Solution

The ratio "required friction / maximum available" \(=\tan\theta/\mu_s\) is entirely independent of \(W\), the mass cancels out completely. Whether a block slides depends only on the angle versus \(\mu_s\), never on how heavy the block is, so once the incline exceeds \(\arctan(\mu_s)\), sliding is guaranteed for any weight.

2 Wedges

A wedge problem needs a separate FBD at each contact surface. Using the "friction angle" \(\varphi=\arctan(\mu)\) simplifies things: instead of tracking a normal force and a separate friction force, treat their combined effect as a single resultant tilted \(\varphi\) away from the true normal, in the direction that opposes motion.

Key idea (single friction surface, frictionless floor)

For a wedge of angle \(\theta\) raising a vertically-guided block, with friction \(\mu\) only between wedge and block: $$P = W\tan(\theta+\varphi), \qquad \varphi=\arctan(\mu).$$

Worked example

A 15° wedge raises a 500 N block (vertically guided, frictionless wall), \(\mu=0.25\) between wedge and block, frictionless floor. Find the force \(P\) needed to just start raising the block.

\(\varphi=\arctan(0.25)=14.04^\circ\). \(\theta+\varphi=29.04^\circ\).

\(P=W\tan(\theta+\varphi)=500\tan(29.04^\circ)=500(0.5548)=277.4\text{ N}\).

Practice 13

Wedge \(\theta=10^\circ\), block 400 N, \(\mu=0.3\). Find \(\varphi\) and \(P\).

Solution

\(\varphi=\arctan(0.3)=16.70^\circ\). \(P=400\tan(26.70^\circ)=400(0.5029)=201.2\text{ N}\).

Practice 14

Wedge \(\theta=20^\circ\), block 800 N, \(\mu=0.2\). Find \(P\).

Solution

\(\varphi=\arctan(0.2)=11.31^\circ\). \(P=800\tan(31.31^\circ)=800(0.6086)=486.9\text{ N}\).

Practice 15

Wedge \(\theta=12^\circ\), block 350 N, \(\mu=0.35\). Find \(P\).

Solution

\(\varphi=\arctan(0.35)=19.29^\circ\). \(P=350\tan(31.29^\circ)=350(0.6082)=212.9\text{ N}\).

Practice 16

Explain why \(P=W\tan(\theta+\varphi)\) grows extremely quickly as \(\theta+\varphi\) approaches 90°.

Solution

As \((\theta+\varphi)\to90^\circ\), \(\tan(\theta+\varphi)\to\infty\). Physically, a steep, high-friction wedge becomes extremely hard to drive in, almost all the applied force fights friction and geometry rather than usefully lifting the load.

Practice 17

The formula assumed a frictionless floor. Qualitatively, how would adding floor friction change \(P\), and why?

Solution

\(P\) would increase: the wedge now must also overcome resistance from the floor as it's driven forward, an additional friction force enters the wedge's horizontal equilibrium, always opposing \(P\).

3 Belt friction

Wrapping a rope or belt around a drum lets friction accumulate over the entire contact arc, producing an exponential, not linear, relationship between the two side tensions.

Key idea

$$\frac{T_2}{T_1}=e^{\mu\beta}$$ where \(T_2\) is the tension on the side about to slip toward (the "tight" side), \(T_1\) the slack side, and \(\beta\) the wrap angle in radians.

Worked example 1

A belt wraps 180° (\(\pi\) rad) around a drum, \(\mu=0.3\). Tight-side tension is 500 N. Find the maximum slack-side tension before slipping.

\(T_2/T_1=e^{\mu\beta}=e^{0.3\pi}=e^{0.9425}=2.566\).

\(T_1=500/2.566=194.9\text{ N}\).

Worked example 2

A rope is wrapped 2.5 times (\(5\pi\) rad) around a capstan, \(\mu=0.25\). Find the maximum load \(W\) a person can hold by pulling with just 100 N.

\(\mu\beta=0.25(5\pi)=3.927\). \(e^{3.927}=50.75\).

\(W=T_2=100(50.75)=5075\text{ N}\), over 5 kN held by a 100 N pull.

Practice 18

Belt wraps 90° (\(\pi/2\) rad), \(\mu=0.25\). \(T_2=300\text{ N}\). Find \(T_1\).

Solution

\(\mu\beta=0.25(1.5708)=0.3927\). \(e^{0.3927}=1.481\). \(T_1=300/1.481=202.6\text{ N}\).

Practice 19

Belt wraps 270° (\(3\pi/2\) rad), \(\mu=0.35\). \(T_2=800\text{ N}\). Find \(T_1\).

Solution

\(\mu\beta=0.35(4.712)=1.649\). \(e^{1.649}=5.203\). \(T_1=800/5.203=153.8\text{ N}\).

Practice 20

Belt wraps \(\pi\) rad, \(\mu=0.2\). \(T_1=100\text{ N}\). Find the max \(T_2\) before slipping.

Solution

\(\mu\beta=0.2\pi=0.6283\). \(e^{0.6283}=1.874\). \(T_2=100(1.874)=187.4\text{ N}\).

Practice 21

Belt wraps \(2\pi\) rad (full wrap), \(\mu=0.3\). \(T_1=50\text{ N}\). Find \(T_2\).

Solution

\(\mu\beta=0.3(2\pi)=1.885\). \(e^{1.885}=6.585\). \(T_2=50(6.585)=329.3\text{ N}\).

Practice 22

Belt wraps 150° (\(5\pi/6\) rad), \(\mu=0.28\). \(T_1=250\text{ N}\). Find \(T_2\).

Solution

\(\beta=2.618\). \(\mu\beta=0.28(2.618)=0.733\). \(e^{0.733}=2.081\). \(T_2=250(2.081)=520.3\text{ N}\).

Practice 23

Explain why increasing the wrap angle \(\beta\) increases the sustainable tension ratio so dramatically, and why engineers wrap a rope multiple times around a capstan to hold huge loads.

Solution

Since \(T_2/T_1=e^{\mu\beta}\) grows exponentially with \(\beta\), even a modest increase in wrap angle dramatically boosts the sustainable ratio. Wrapping a rope multiple times adds several full turns (each \(2\pi\)) to \(\beta\), making the ratio astronomically large, exactly why a small hand-held tension can resist an enormous load without slipping, the working principle of every capstan and belay device.
