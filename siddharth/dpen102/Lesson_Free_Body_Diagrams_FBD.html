0 What is an FBD, and the 5-step method

A Free Body Diagram is a simplified sketch of a single object, isolated from everything it touches, showing every external force acting on it, and nothing else. It is not a drawing of the physical scene, it is a drawing of forces. Get the FBD right and the equations (\(\Sigma F_x=0\), \(\Sigma F_y=0\), and for rigid bodies \(\Sigma M=0\)) almost write themselves. Get it wrong, and no amount of careful algebra afterward will save you.

The 5-step method

1. Isolate. Mentally "cut" the object free from everything touching it, the ground, ropes, other objects, walls.

2. List every external force. Weight (always, acting at the centre, straight down, \(W=mg\)); contact forces from surfaces (normal force, always perpendicular to the surface); friction (parallel to the surface, opposing relative sliding or its tendency); applied forces (pushes, pulls); rope/cable tension (always pulls, along the rope, away from the body); support reactions (see below).

3. Draw the body as a simple shape, or for problems where rotation doesn't matter, just a point.

4. Draw each force as an arrow from its point of application, in its real or assumed direction, labelled with a symbol.

5. Leave out any force the body exerts on something else (only forces acting ON this body belong), and leave out internal forces (they vanish once you've cut the body free).

Support reactions, at a glance

Roller: 1 reaction, perpendicular to the rolling surface only (it would simply roll away under any other force direction).

Pin: 2 reactions (horizontal and vertical), since a pin resists motion in any direction but still allows free rotation.

Fixed support: 2 reactions plus a reaction moment, since it resists both movement and rotation.

Weight (W)
Normal force (N)
Applied force / Tension (P, T)
Friction (f)

This colour code is used in every diagram from here on.

Worked example

A book rests on a table, nothing else touching it. Draw its FBD.

Isolate the book. The only things touching it are gravity (always) and the table surface.

Two forces: weight \(W=mg\) acting down at the book's centre, and the normal force \(N\) from the table acting straight up.

Since the book isn't moving, \(\Sigma F_y=0 \Rightarrow N=W\).

Practice 1

A hanging picture frame is supported by a single nail. List which of these belong in the frame's FBD: (a) weight of frame, (b) force the frame exerts on the nail, (c) force the wall exerts on the frame, (d) force the nail exerts on the frame.

Solution

(a) and (d) belong. (b) is a force the frame exerts on something else (the nail), never the frame itself, so it's excluded. (c) is excluded unless the frame is stated to actually be touching the wall, since a hanging frame usually only contacts the nail.

Practice 2

True or false: an FBD should include forces the object exerts on its surroundings.

Solution

False. An FBD includes only forces acting on the isolated object, never forces it exerts elsewhere.

Practice 3

For a ball resting on the ground with nothing else touching it, list the two forces in its FBD and their directions.

Solution

Weight \(W\), straight down. Normal force \(N\), straight up from the ground.

1 Beginner: block on a horizontal surface

The next step up adds a horizontal applied force and friction. Friction always opposes relative sliding, or the tendency to slide, so its direction depends on which way the object is being pushed or pulled, not on some fixed rule.

Worked example

A 10 kg block sits on a horizontal floor. A horizontal force of 40 N is applied to the right, but the block doesn't move. Draw the FBD and find the friction force.

Forces: \(W=mg=10(9.8)=98\text{ N}\) down; \(N\) up; applied force \(P=40\text{ N}\) right; friction \(f\) left (opposing the push, since the block is not moving).

\(\Sigma F_x=0\): \(f=P=40\text{ N}\).

\(\Sigma F_y=0\): \(N=W=98\text{ N}\).

Practice 4

A 5 kg box rests on a table, nothing else touching it. List the forces and find \(N\) (use \(g=9.8\text{ m/s}^2\)).

Solution

\(W=5(9.8)=49\text{ N}\) down, \(N\) up. \(\Sigma F_y=0 \Rightarrow N=49\text{ N}\).

Practice 5

A 20 kg crate sits on the floor. A person pushes horizontally with 60 N but the crate doesn't move. Draw the FBD and find the friction force.

Solution

Forces: \(W=196\text{ N}\) down, \(N\) up, \(P=60\text{ N}\) (push direction), \(f\) opposing \(P\). \(\Sigma F_x=0 \Rightarrow f=60\text{ N}\).

Practice 6

An 8 kg block on a horizontal surface has an applied force of 15 N right and a friction force of 15 N left already shown on its FBD. Is the block in equilibrium? Find \(N\).

Solution

Yes, since \(\Sigma F_x = 15-15=0\). \(N=W=8(9.8)=78.4\text{ N}\).

Practice 7

A block is pulled by a horizontal rope with tension \(T\) across a rough floor at constant velocity. Draw the FBD and state the equation that gives \(T\).

Solution

Forces: \(W\) down, \(N\) up, \(T\) forward (applied by rope), kinetic friction \(f_k=\mu_kN\) backward (block is sliding). Constant velocity means equilibrium: \(\Sigma F_x=0 \Rightarrow T=f_k=\mu_kN=\mu_kW\) (since \(N=W\) here).

Practice 8

A block has two horizontal applied forces: 25 N right and 10 N left, plus friction \(f\). The block is on the verge of sliding right. Which way does \(f\) act, and what's its magnitude?

Solution

Friction opposes the impending motion (rightward), so it acts left. At the verge of sliding, \(\Sigma F_x=0\): \(f = 25-10 = 15\text{ N}\).

2 Beginner–intermediate: concurrent forces at angles

When several forces meet at a single point (like ropes tied to a hanging ring), decompose every force into \(x\) and \(y\) components before applying equilibrium. Remember: rope tension always pulls, directed along the rope, away from the body.

Worked example

A 100 N weight hangs from a ring held by two ropes: rope A at 30° above horizontal (left), rope B at 60° above horizontal (right). Find \(T_A\) and \(T_B\).

\(\Sigma F_x=0\): \(T_B\cos60^\circ = T_A\cos30^\circ \Rightarrow T_A = T_B/\sqrt3\).

\(\Sigma F_y=0\): \(T_A\sin30^\circ+T_B\sin60^\circ = 100\).

Substituting: \(T_B\left(\dfrac{0.5}{\sqrt3}+\dfrac{\sqrt3}2\right)=100 \Rightarrow T_B(1.1547)=100 \Rightarrow T_B=86.6\text{ N}\).

\(T_A = 86.6/\sqrt3 = 50.0\text{ N}\).

Practice 9

A 150 N traffic light hangs from two cables, each making 45° with the horizontal. Find the tension in each cable.

Solution

By symmetry each cable carries half the weight vertically: \(T\sin45^\circ = 75 \Rightarrow T = 75/0.7071 = 106.07\text{ N}\) (both cables).

Practice 10

A 200 N weight hangs from two ropes making 37° and 53° with the horizontal (use \(\sin37^\circ\approx0.6,\ \cos37^\circ\approx0.8,\ \sin53^\circ\approx0.8,\ \cos53^\circ\approx0.6\)). Find both tensions.

Solution

\(\Sigma F_x=0\): \(T_1(0.8)=T_2(0.6) \Rightarrow T_1=0.75T_2\).

\(\Sigma F_y=0\): \(0.75T_2(0.6)+T_2(0.8)=200 \Rightarrow T_2(1.25)=200 \Rightarrow T_2=160\text{ N}\), \(\ T_1=120\text{ N}\).

Practice 11

A 120 N sign hangs symmetrically from two ropes, each at 60° above horizontal. Find the tension in each rope.

Solution

\(2T\sin60^\circ = 120 \Rightarrow T = 120/(2\times0.8660) = 69.28\text{ N}\).

Practice 12

A 90 N ball is held by a rope at 20° from vertical while a horizontal force \(P\) pushes it sideways. Find \(T\) and \(P\).

Solution

\(\Sigma F_y=0\): \(T\cos20^\circ=90 \Rightarrow T=90/0.9397=95.78\text{ N}\).

\(\Sigma F_x=0\): \(P=T\sin20^\circ=95.78\times0.3420=32.76\text{ N}\).

Practice 13

A 40 N picture hangs symmetrically from two wires, each making 70° with the horizontal. Find the tension in each wire.

Solution

\(2T\sin70^\circ=40 \Rightarrow T = 40/(2\times0.9397)=21.28\text{ N}\).

3 Intermediate: inclined planes

On an incline, it's almost always easier to rotate your axes to align with the slope: weight then splits into a component into the surface (\(W\cos\theta\), balanced by \(N\)) and a component along the surface (\(W\sin\theta\), balanced by whatever else is holding the block, a rope or friction).

Worked example

A 20 kg block rests on a frictionless 30° incline, held by a rope parallel to the slope. Find the tension and the normal force.

\(W=20(9.8)=196\text{ N}\).

\(N=W\cos30^\circ=196(0.8660)=169.7\text{ N}\).

\(T=W\sin30^\circ=196(0.5)=98\text{ N}\).

Practice 14

A 15 kg crate sits on a frictionless 20° incline, held by a rope parallel to the slope. Find \(T\) and \(N\).

Solution

\(W=147\text{ N}\). \(N=147\cos20^\circ=138.13\text{ N}\). \(T=147\sin20^\circ=50.27\text{ N}\).

Practice 15

A 10 kg block rests on a rough 25° incline with no rope, friction alone holding it. Find the friction force required.

Solution

\(W=98\text{ N}\). Component down the slope: \(W\sin25^\circ=98(0.4226)=41.42\text{ N}\). Friction must balance this up the slope: \(f=41.42\text{ N}\).

Practice 16

A 300 N block sits on a frictionless 37° incline (\(\sin37^\circ=0.6,\cos37^\circ=0.8\)), held by a horizontal rope. Find the tension and the normal force.

Solution

Along the incline: \(T\cos37^\circ = W\sin37^\circ \Rightarrow T = W\tan37^\circ = 300(0.75)=225\text{ N}\).

Perpendicular to incline: \(N = W\cos37^\circ + T\sin37^\circ = 240+135=375\text{ N}\).

Practice 17

A 25 kg block on a frictionless 15° incline is released from rest. Find its acceleration along the slope.

Solution

\(W=245\text{ N}\). Down-slope force \(=W\sin15^\circ=63.4\text{ N}\). \(a=F/m=63.4/25=2.54\text{ m/s}^2\) down the slope.

Practice 18

A 100 N block on a 40° incline (\(\mu_s=0.3\)) is held by a rope parallel to the surface, on the verge of sliding down (friction at maximum, acting up-slope). Find the tension required.

Solution

\(N=100\cos40^\circ=76.6\text{ N}\). \(f_{max}=\mu N=0.3(76.6)=22.98\text{ N}\).

Along the slope: \(T+f_{max}=W\sin40^\circ=64.3\text{ N} \Rightarrow T=64.3-22.98=41.32\text{ N}\).

4 Intermediate: connected bodies & pulleys

When two bodies are connected, draw a separate FBD for each one, even though they move together. An ideal (massless, frictionless) rope carries the same tension throughout, and connected bodies share the same magnitude of acceleration, that shared \(T\) and shared \(a\) are what let you combine the two bodies' equations and solve.

Worked example

Two blocks, \(m_1=4\text{ kg}\) and \(m_2=6\text{ kg}\), hang on either side of a frictionless, massless pulley (an Atwood machine). Find the acceleration and the tension.

Block 1 (rises): \(T-W_1=m_1a\). Block 2 (falls): \(W_2-T=m_2a\).

Add the two equations: \(W_2-W_1=(m_1+m_2)a \Rightarrow a = \dfrac{58.8-39.2}{10}=1.96\text{ m/s}^2\).

\(T=W_1+m_1a=39.2+4(1.96)=47.04\text{ N}\) (check: \(W_2-m_2a=58.8-11.76=47.04\text{ N}\) ✓).

Practice 19

Two blocks, \(m_1=3\text{ kg}\) and \(m_2=5\text{ kg}\), hang on an Atwood machine. Find the acceleration and tension.

Solution

\(a=\dfrac{(5-3)(9.8)}{8}=\dfrac{19.6}{8}=2.45\text{ m/s}^2\). \(T=W_1+m_1a=29.4+3(2.45)=36.75\text{ N}\).

Practice 20

A 5 kg block on a frictionless table is connected over a pulley at the table's edge to a 2 kg block hanging vertically. Find the acceleration and tension.

Solution

Hanging block: \(W_2-T=m_2a\). Table block (only horizontal force is \(T\)): \(T=m_1a\).

Adding: \(W_2=(m_1+m_2)a \Rightarrow a=19.6/7=2.8\text{ m/s}^2\). \(T=5(2.8)=14\text{ N}\).

Practice 21

Block A (5 kg) is pulled right by \(P=30\text{ N}\) and towed, via a connecting string, by block B (3 kg) behind it, both on a frictionless surface. List the forces on each block's separate FBD (no solving needed).

Solution

Block A: weight \(W_A\) down, normal \(N_A\) up, applied \(P\) forward, tension \(T\) backward (string pulls A toward B).

Block B: weight \(W_B\) down, normal \(N_B\) up, tension \(T\) forward (string pulls B toward A).

Practice 22

For the setup in Practice 21, find the acceleration of the system and the tension in the string.

Solution

Total mass \(=8\text{ kg}\), \(a=P/\text{total}=30/8=3.75\text{ m/s}^2\).

For block B: \(T=m_Ba=3(3.75)=11.25\text{ N}\) (check via A: \(P-T=30-11.25=18.75=5(3.75)\) ✓).

Practice 23

An Atwood machine has \(m_1=m_2=2\text{ kg}\). Find the acceleration and tension, and explain physically why the result makes sense.

Solution

\(a=(W_2-W_1)/(m_1+m_2)=0/4=0\). \(T=W_1=2(9.8)=19.6\text{ N}\).

Equal weights on both sides means the system is perfectly balanced, no net force, so it stays at rest (or at whatever constant velocity it started with) — each side's tension simply supports its own weight.

5 Advanced: rigid bodies, supports & moments

Once a body has size (a beam, not a point), balancing forces isn't enough, it also can't be allowed to rotate. That adds a third equation, \(\Sigma M=0\) (sum of moments about any chosen point equals zero). Choosing the pivot point cleverly, usually at an unknown reaction, eliminates that reaction from the moment equation and often lets you solve for the other reaction directly.

Worked example

A simply-supported beam, 6 m long, has a pin at A (left end) and a roller at B (right end), with a 300 N point load 2 m from A. Find the reactions.

\(\Sigma F_x=0\): \(R_{Ax}=0\) (no horizontal loads).

\(\Sigma M_A=0\) (taking moments about A eliminates both pin reactions): \(R_B(6)-300(2)=0 \Rightarrow R_B=100\text{ N}\).

\(\Sigma F_y=0\): \(R_{Ay}+R_B-300=0 \Rightarrow R_{Ay}=200\text{ N}\).

Practice 24

A simply-supported beam (pin at A, roller at B), 8 m long, carries a 400 N point load 3 m from A. Find the reactions.

Solution

\(\Sigma M_A\): \(R_B(8)-400(3)=0 \Rightarrow R_B=150\text{ N}\). \(\Sigma F_y\): \(R_{Ay}=400-150=250\text{ N}\).

Practice 25

A beam 10 m long (pin at A, roller at B) carries a 500 N point load 6 m from A. Find the reactions.

Solution

\(\Sigma M_A\): \(R_B(10)-500(6)=0 \Rightarrow R_B=300\text{ N}\). \(\Sigma F_y\): \(R_{Ay}=500-300=200\text{ N}\).

Practice 26

A cantilever beam is fixed at A (a wall) and is 4 m long, with a 250 N downward load at the free end. Find the reaction force and reaction moment at A.

Solution

\(\Sigma F_y=0\): \(R_{Ay}=250\text{ N}\).

\(\Sigma M_A=0\): \(M_A=250(4)=1000\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}\), reacting to prevent the beam from rotating downward about the wall.

Practice 27

A simply-supported beam, 5 m long (pin at A, roller at B), carries a uniformly distributed load of \(w=100\text{ N/m}\) over its whole length. Find the reactions.

Solution

Total load \(=wL=500\text{ N}\), acting at the midpoint (2.5 m from A).

\(\Sigma M_A\): \(R_B(5)-500(2.5)=0 \Rightarrow R_B=250\text{ N}\). By symmetry, \(R_{Ay}=250\text{ N}\) too.

Practice 28

A beam 6 m long (pin at A, roller at B) carries two point loads: 200 N at 2 m from A and 300 N at 4 m from A. Find the reactions.

Solution

\(\Sigma M_A\): \(R_B(6)=200(2)+300(4)=1600 \Rightarrow R_B=266.67\text{ N}\).

\(\Sigma F_y\): \(R_{Ay}=500-266.67=233.33\text{ N}\).

Practice 29

Explain why a roller support can only provide one reaction force, while a pin support provides two.

Solution

A roller can only resist force perpendicular to the surface it rolls on, any force along the surface and it simply rolls away. A pin, being fixed in place like a hinge, resists motion in any direction in the plane (both horizontal and vertical) since it cannot roll, but it still permits free rotation, so it cannot resist a moment.

6 Advanced: multi-surface systems (ladder problems)

The hardest FBDs involve an object touching two different surfaces at once, like a ladder against a wall and the floor. Each surface gets its own normal force (perpendicular to that specific surface), and possibly its own friction force. With three unknowns typically in play, all three equilibrium equations, \(\Sigma F_x=0\), \(\Sigma F_y=0\), \(\Sigma M=0\), are usually needed together.

Worked example

A uniform 200 N ladder, 5 m long, leans against a frictionless wall at the top and a rough floor at the base, at 60° to the floor. Find \(N_{wall}\), \(N_{floor}\), and \(f_{floor}\).

\(\Sigma F_y=0\): \(N_{floor}=W=200\text{ N}\) (the wall, being frictionless, contributes no vertical force).

\(\Sigma M_{base}=0\) (taking moments about the base eliminates \(N_{floor}\) and \(f_{floor}\), both acting there): the weight's moment arm is its horizontal distance from the base, \((L/2)\cos\theta\); the wall force's moment arm is the ladder's height, \(L\sin\theta\).

\(N_{wall}(5\sin60^\circ) = 200\left(\dfrac52\cos60^\circ\right) \Rightarrow N_{wall}(4.33)=200(1.25)=250 \Rightarrow N_{wall}=57.7\text{ N}\).

\(\Sigma F_x=0\): \(f_{floor}=N_{wall}=57.7\text{ N}\).

Practice 30

A uniform 150 N ladder, 4 m long, leans against a frictionless wall at 65° to a rough floor. Find \(N_{wall}\), \(N_{floor}\), and \(f_{floor}\).

Solution

\(N_{floor}=150\text{ N}\).

\(\Sigma M_{base}\): \(N_{wall}(4\sin65^\circ)=150(2\cos65^\circ) \Rightarrow N_{wall}(3.625)=150(0.8452)=126.79 \Rightarrow N_{wall}=34.98\text{ N}\). \(f_{floor}=34.98\text{ N}\).

Practice 31

A 250 N ladder, 6 m long, at 70° to the floor (frictionless wall, rough floor), with a 500 N person standing 4 m up the ladder (measured along the ladder). Find \(N_{wall}\).

Solution

\(\Sigma M_{base}\): \(N_{wall}(6\sin70^\circ) = 250(3\cos70^\circ) + 500(4\cos70^\circ)\).

\(N_{wall}(5.638) = 250(1.026)+500(1.368) = 256.5+684=940.5 \Rightarrow N_{wall}=166.85\text{ N}\).

Practice 32

If the floor beneath a wall-leaning ladder were also frictionless, could the ladder stay in equilibrium at an angle? Explain using the FBD.

Solution

No. \(\Sigma F_x=0\) requires the wall's horizontal force \(N_{wall}\) to be balanced by some horizontal force at the floor. A frictionless floor can only push perpendicular to itself, purely vertical, and can supply no horizontal force at all. With nothing to balance \(N_{wall}\) horizontally, the base would slide out; a frictionless-floor, frictionless-wall ladder can only balance in the trivial vertical (0°) position.

Practice 33

A horizontal boom, 3 m long, is hinged (pin) to a wall at A, with negligible weight, and supports a 180 N sign at its outer end. A cable from that same outer end to a higher point on the wall makes 40° with the boom. Find the cable tension \(T\).

Solution

Taking moments about the pin A (eliminates the pin reactions): both the sign's weight and the cable's vertical component act at the same point, 3 m out, so their moment arms are equal and cancel in the ratio:

\(T\sin40^\circ(3) = 180(3) \Rightarrow T\sin40^\circ=180 \Rightarrow T=180/0.6428=280.05\text{ N}\).

Practice 34

For the boom in Practice 33, find the horizontal and vertical pin reactions at A.

Solution

\(\Sigma F_x=0\): \(R_x = T\cos40^\circ = 280.05(0.766) = 214.5\text{ N}\), balancing the cable's horizontal pull toward the wall.

\(\Sigma F_y=0\): \(R_y + T\sin40^\circ - 180 = 0 \Rightarrow R_y = 180-180=0\text{ N}\), since the cable's vertical component alone (found above) already exactly supports the sign, a clean result of both forces acting at the very same point.

Where to go from here. Every later DPEN102 topic, internal forces and stress (Week 4), shear force/bending moment diagrams (Week 5), truss method of sections (Week 6), friction systems (Week 7), even centroids and energy methods, starts by drawing a correct FBD. The 5-step method and the equilibrium equations (\(\Sigma F_x=0,\ \Sigma F_y=0,\ \Sigma M=0\)) built here are the tools every one of those weeks reuses directly.
