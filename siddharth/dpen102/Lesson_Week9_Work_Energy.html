Work, Energy & Conservation

When the force varies with position (springs, gravity along a curved path), tracking forces and acceleration directly gets messy fast. Energy methods sidestep that entirely: compare the total energy at a start state and an end state, without ever needing to know what happens in between.

0 The work–energy theorem

Net work done on an object always shows up as a change in its kinetic energy, nothing more, nothing less.

Key idea

$$W_{net} = \Delta K = K_2-K_1, \qquad K=\frac12mv^2.$$

Worked example 1

A 5 kg block starts at rest; a net force does 40 J of work on it. Find its final speed.

\(\Delta K=40\text{ J}=K_2-0\). \(40=\dfrac12(5)v^2 \Rightarrow v^2=16 \Rightarrow v=4\text{ m/s}\).

Worked example 2

A 10 kg cart moving at 3 m/s has +35 J of net work done on it. Find its final speed.

\(K_1=\dfrac12(10)(3)^2=45\text{ J}\). \(K_2=45+35=80\text{ J}\).

\(80=\dfrac12(10)v^2 \Rightarrow v^2=16 \Rightarrow v=4\text{ m/s}\).

Practice 1

An 8 kg object starts at rest, net work \(=64\text{ J}\). Find the final speed.

Solution

\(64=\dfrac12(8)v^2 \Rightarrow v^2=16 \Rightarrow v=4\text{ m/s}\).

Practice 2

A 3 kg ball moving at 6 m/s has \(-20\text{ J}\) of net work done on it. Find the final speed.

Solution

\(K_1=\dfrac12(3)(36)=54\text{ J}\). \(K_2=54-20=34\text{ J}\). \(v^2=2(34)/3=22.67 \Rightarrow v=4.76\text{ m/s}\).

Practice 3

A 12 kg crate accelerates from 2 m/s to 5 m/s. Find the net work done.

Solution

\(K_1=\dfrac12(12)(4)=24\text{ J}\). \(K_2=\dfrac12(12)(25)=150\text{ J}\). \(W_{net}=150-24=126\text{ J}\).

Practice 4

A 6 kg object decelerates from 8 m/s to 3 m/s. Find the net work done.

Solution

\(K_1=\dfrac12(6)(64)=192\text{ J}\). \(K_2=\dfrac12(6)(9)=27\text{ J}\). \(W_{net}=27-192=-165\text{ J}\).

Practice 5

A calculation gives a NEGATIVE final kinetic energy for some scenario. What does this physically mean?

Solution

It's physically impossible, KE can never be negative. It means the object actually reaches zero velocity (stops) before the full amount of work is delivered, the scenario as stated exceeds the object's available kinetic energy, so the true stopping point must be recalculated up to \(K=0\).

Practice 6

A 4 kg object at rest has 50 J of applied work done on it, while friction does \(-18\text{ J}\) simultaneously. Find the final KE and speed.

Solution

Net work \(=50-18=32\text{ J}=K_2\). \(v^2=2(32)/4=16 \Rightarrow v=4\text{ m/s}\).

1 Gravitational potential energy

Height stores energy. As a conservative force, gravity's work depends only on the change in height, never on the path taken.

Key idea

$$U_g=mgh, \qquad K_1+U_1=K_2+U_2 \text{ (no friction or other losses)}.$$

Worked example 1

A 2 kg ball is dropped from rest at height 10 m. Find its speed just before hitting the ground (ignore air resistance).

\(0+mgh=\dfrac12mv^2+0 \Rightarrow v=\sqrt{2gh}=\sqrt{2(9.8)(10)}=\sqrt{196}=14\text{ m/s}\).

Worked example 2

A 5 kg cart starts from rest atop a frictionless 8 m ramp. Find its speed at the bottom.

\(v=\sqrt{2(9.8)(8)}=\sqrt{156.8}=12.52\text{ m/s}\) (the mass cancelled out, as always for this formula).

Practice 7

A 3 kg ball is dropped from 20 m. Find its speed at the ground.

Solution

\(v=\sqrt{2(9.8)(20)}=\sqrt{392}=19.8\text{ m/s}\).

Practice 8

A cart on a frictionless 5 m ramp starts from rest. Find its speed at the bottom.

Solution

\(v=\sqrt{2(9.8)(5)}=\sqrt{98}=9.9\text{ m/s}\).

Practice 9

A 1 kg ball is thrown upward, reaching a max height of 4 m. Find its initial speed.

Solution

All KE converts to PE at the top: \(v=\sqrt{2(9.8)(4)}=\sqrt{78.4}=8.85\text{ m/s}\).

Practice 10

A frictionless roller coaster car starts from rest at the top of a 30 m hill. Find its speed at the bottom.

Solution

\(v=\sqrt{2(9.8)(30)}=\sqrt{588}=24.25\text{ m/s}\).

Practice 11

Explain why the mass cancels out of \(v=\sqrt{2gh}\), why a bowling ball and a tennis ball dropped from the same height hit the ground at the same speed (ignoring air resistance).

Solution

Both \(K=\dfrac12mv^2\) and \(U_g=mgh\) contain mass as a simple linear factor. Setting them equal and solving for \(v\) cancels \(m\) from both sides entirely, final speed depends only on \(g\) and \(h\), never on how heavy the object is.

Practice 12

A 2 kg object slides down a frictionless curved ramp of height 6 m (any shape). Find its speed at the bottom, and explain why the ramp's shape doesn't matter.

Solution

\(v=\sqrt{2(9.8)(6)}=\sqrt{117.6}=10.84\text{ m/s}\). The shape doesn't matter because energy conservation depends only on the start and end heights, not the path between them, as long as there's no friction or other energy loss.

2 Spring potential energy

A stretched or compressed spring stores energy exactly like height does, and the same conservation approach applies.

Key idea

$$U_s=\frac12kx^2, \qquad K_1+U_{s1}=K_2+U_{s2} \text{ (add } U_g \text{ if height also changes).}$$

Worked example 1

A spring, \(k=200\text{ N/m}\), compressed 0.15 m, launches a 0.5 kg block on a frictionless surface. Find its launch speed.

\(U_s=\dfrac12(200)(0.15)^2=2.25\text{ J}\), all converts to KE: \(2.25=\dfrac12(0.5)v^2 \Rightarrow v=3\text{ m/s}\).

Worked example 2

A spring, \(k=500\text{ N/m}\), compressed 0.2 m, launches a 1 kg block up a frictionless 20° incline. How far up the incline does it travel before stopping?

\(U_s=\dfrac12(500)(0.2)^2=10\text{ J}\), all converts to \(U_g=mgh\), \(h=d\sin20^\circ\):

\(10=1(9.8)(d)(0.342)=3.352d \Rightarrow d=2.984\text{ m}\).

Practice 13

A spring \(k=150\text{ N/m}\), compressed 0.1 m, launches a 0.3 kg block (frictionless). Find the launch speed.

Solution

\(U_s=\dfrac12(150)(0.01)=0.75\text{ J}\). \(v^2=2(0.75)/0.3=5 \Rightarrow v=2.236\text{ m/s}\).

Practice 14

A spring \(k=300\text{ N/m}\), compressed 0.25 m. Find the stored PE.

Solution

\(U_s=\dfrac12(300)(0.0625)=9.375\text{ J}\).

Practice 15

A 2 kg block moving at 4 m/s compresses a spring (\(k=800\text{ N/m}\), frictionless surface) until it momentarily stops. Find the maximum compression.

Solution

\(K_1=\dfrac12(2)(16)=16\text{ J}=U_s=\dfrac12(800)x^2 \Rightarrow x^2=0.04 \Rightarrow x=0.2\text{ m}\).

Practice 16

A spring \(k=400\text{ N/m}\), compressed 0.18 m, launches a 0.8 kg block up a frictionless 15° incline. Find the distance travelled before stopping.

Solution

\(U_s=\dfrac12(400)(0.0324)=6.48\text{ J}\). \(6.48=0.8(9.8)(d)(\sin15^\circ)=2.029d \Rightarrow d=3.194\text{ m}\).

Practice 17

Explain why, in a frictionless spring-launch problem, the block's final speed doesn't depend on the spring's natural length or mounting.

Solution

\(U_s=\dfrac12kx^2\) only depends on the spring constant and the compression distance from natural length, and energy conservation only cares about total stored energy, not the spring's absolute length or orientation, those details never enter the balance.

Practice 18

A spring launches a block horizontally, which then travels up a frictionless ramp. Explain why you can find the final height without knowing the intermediate speed.

Solution

Mechanical energy is conserved end-to-end, so spring PE converts directly to gravitational PE at the final point regardless of the path, you can go straight from \(U_{s1}\) to \(U_{g2}\) without ever needing the intermediate velocity as a separate step.

3 Advanced: combined spring & gravity conservation

When both a spring and gravity act, track every energy form present at each state and set the totals equal.

Key idea

$$K_1+U_{s1}+U_{g1}=K_2+U_{s2}+U_{g2}.$$

Worked example

A 2 kg block starts at rest against a spring (\(k=600\text{ N/m}\), compressed 0.3 m) at the bottom of a frictionless 25° incline. Find its speed after travelling 1.5 m up the incline (spring already released).

State 1: \(K_1=0\), \(U_{s1}=\dfrac12(600)(0.3)^2=27\text{ J}\), \(U_{g1}=0\).

State 2: \(U_{s2}=0\) (spring released). Height gained \(h=1.5\sin25^\circ=0.6339\text{ m}\), \(U_{g2}=2(9.8)(0.6339)=12.42\text{ J}\).

\(27=K_2+12.42 \Rightarrow K_2=14.58\text{ J} \Rightarrow v=\sqrt{2(14.58)/2}=3.818\text{ m/s}\).

Practice 19

A 1.5 kg block, spring \(k=500\text{ N/m}\) compressed 0.25 m, frictionless 20° incline. Find its speed after 1 m up the incline.

Solution

\(U_{s1}=\dfrac12(500)(0.0625)=15.625\text{ J}\). \(h=1\sin20^\circ=0.342\text{ m}\), \(U_{g2}=1.5(9.8)(0.342)=5.027\text{ J}\). \(K_2=15.625-5.027=10.598\text{ J}\). \(v=\sqrt{2(10.598)/1.5}=3.759\text{ m/s}\).

Practice 20

A 3 kg block, spring \(k=900\text{ N/m}\) compressed 0.2 m, frictionless 30° incline. Find its speed after 0.8 m.

Solution

\(U_{s1}=\dfrac12(900)(0.04)=18\text{ J}\). \(h=0.8\sin30^\circ=0.4\text{ m}\), \(U_{g2}=3(9.8)(0.4)=11.76\text{ J}\). \(K_2=18-11.76=6.24\text{ J}\). \(v=\sqrt{2(6.24)/3}=2.04\text{ m/s}\).

Practice 21

Using Practice 19's numbers, find the MAXIMUM distance the block travels up the incline before stopping.

Solution

At max distance, \(K_2=0\): \(15.625=1.5(9.8)(d)(\sin20^\circ)=5.027d \Rightarrow d=3.108\text{ m}\).

Practice 22

What does a negative calculated \(K_2\) tell you in a spring-launch-up-an-incline problem?

Solution

The assumed distance/height is physically unreachable with the given spring energy, the block runs out of KE (stops) before that point. Instead, solve for the maximum distance/height where \(K_2=0\).

Practice 23

A 2 kg block on a frictionless horizontal surface is pushed by a spring (\(k=400\text{ N/m}\), compression 0.15 m) and then travels up a frictionless ramp. Find the maximum height it reaches.

Solution

\(U_{s1}=\dfrac12(400)(0.0225)=4.5\text{ J}=mgh \Rightarrow h=4.5/(2\times9.8)=0.2296\text{ m}\).

Practice 24

Explain why a consistent reference point (height = 0) for gravitational PE matters, and how choosing a different reference point would (or wouldn't) change the final speed.

Solution

The reference height is arbitrary, only changes in height matter physically. As long as the same reference is used consistently for both the initial and final states, the calculated speed is identical regardless of which height is called zero, since only the difference in \(U_g\) enters the energy balance, not its absolute value.
