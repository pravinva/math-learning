The Core Idea: Scalars vs Vectors

Imagine pushing a heavy door. The amount you push matters — but so does the direction. A 100 N force pointing toward the hinges does almost nothing. The same 100 N force pointing perpendicular to the door swings it easily. This is the essence of vectors: direction is as important as magnitude.

A scalar has magnitude only: temperature is 22°C, mass is 5 kg, time is 3 seconds. A vector has magnitude AND direction: 500 N at 30° above horizontal, 60 km/h north, displacement of 3 m upward.

In engineering diagrams, vectors are drawn as arrows. The arrow length represents magnitude; the arrow direction is the vector direction. Every force you ever encounter is a vector and must always be shown with both pieces of information.

Newton's Laws — The Foundation

Law 1 (Inertia): A body at rest stays at rest, and a body in motion stays in motion, unless acted on by a net external force. For statics: a structure that isn't moving has zero net force and zero net moment — this is equilibrium.

Law 2 (Force = mass × acceleration): \(\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}\). In statics, acceleration is zero, so net force is zero. In dynamics, this directly gives acceleration.

Law 3 (Action-Reaction): Every force has an equal and opposite reaction. When a beam sits on a wall, the beam pushes the wall down — and the wall pushes the beam up with equal force. This is why reaction forces at supports exist.

Breaking a Force into Components

Any force in 2D can be replaced by two perpendicular components — one horizontal and one vertical — that together have the exact same effect as the original force. This process is called resolution, and it's the key operation in statics.

If a force \(F\) acts at angle \(\theta\) from the horizontal, its components are:

$$F_x = F\cos\theta \qquad F_y = F\sin\theta$$

And the reverse: if you know the components, find the original force magnitude and angle:

$$F = \sqrt{F_x^2 + F_y^2} \qquad \theta = \arctan\!\left(\frac{|F_y|}{|F_x|}\right)$$
Critical: What is θ measured from?

If θ is from the horizontal: use cos for \(F_x\), sin for \(F_y\).
If θ is from the vertical: use sin for \(F_x\), cos for \(F_y\).

Always draw the angle on your sketch before writing any equation. One wrong substitution here cascades through the entire problem.

Finding the Resultant of Multiple Forces

When multiple forces act on a particle, you find the resultant by resolving each force into components, summing all x-components and all y-components separately, then combining:

$$R_x = \sum F_x \qquad R_y = \sum F_y \qquad R = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2} \qquad \theta_R = \arctan\!\left(\frac{|R_y|}{|R_x|}\right)$$
Sign Convention — Set This Up First, Never Change It

Define positive x as right and positive y as up. Forces pointing left or down get negative signs. This convention must stay consistent throughout the entire problem — switching it halfway through is the single most common source of errors.

Particle Equilibrium

A particle is in equilibrium when the resultant of all forces acting on it is zero. This gives two equations that let you find up to two unknowns:

$$\sum F_x = 0 \qquad \sum F_y = 0$$

Classic application: a hanging weight supported by two cables. You know the weight, and you know the cable angles — the two equations give you both tensions.

What a Free Body Diagram Really Does

Imagine a bridge beam. It has its own weight, a truck sitting on it, rain loading it, wind pushing it sideways, and two supports holding it up. You can't solve for the support forces while mentally juggling all of this simultaneously.

A Free Body Diagram (FBD) is the solution to this problem. You draw the beam by itself, completely detached from its surroundings, and replace every contact or connection with the force that contact exerts. Now you have one object with a finite list of forces — and three equations to solve them.

The FBD is not just a formality. It is the problem. Engineers who skip FBDs or draw sloppy ones make errors. Engineers who draw careful FBDs solve problems correctly.

The 5-Step FBD Procedure — Do These In Order, Every Time

Step 1 — Choose your body. What are you isolating? A whole beam? A joint? A specific member? Be explicit.

Step 2 — Draw the outline of the body alone, detached from everything around it. Keep it simple — a line for a beam, a dot for a particle.

Step 3 — Add ALL external forces. Applied loads (given in the problem), weight of the body acting at its centroid, reaction forces at each support, normal forces from surfaces, tensions in cables. Every one. If you miss a force, your equations are wrong.

Step 4 — Add a coordinate system. Pick positive x direction and positive y direction. Mark them. Never deviate from this choice.

Step 5 — Write the equilibrium equations and solve for unknowns.

Simply Supported Beam — The Most Common Case

A simply supported beam has a pin at one end (\(R_{Ax}\), \(R_{Ay}\)) and a roller at the other (\(R_B\) vertical only). This gives 3 unknowns total. The three equilibrium equations (\(\sum F_x = 0\), \(\sum F_y = 0\), \(\sum M = 0\)) solve them exactly. This is called a statically determinate structure.

The Three Equilibrium Equations
$$\sum F_x = 0 \qquad \sum F_y = 0 \qquad \sum M_A = 0 \quad \text{(moments about any point } A\text{)}$$

The moment equation \(\sum M_A = 0\) is your most powerful tool. The moment of a force about a point = force × perpendicular distance from that point to the force's line of action. Taking moments about a support eliminates that support's reaction from the equation, letting you solve for other unknowns directly.

UDL (uniformly distributed load) of intensity \(w\) (kN/m) over length \(L\): replace with a single resultant force \(W = wL\) acting at the midpoint of the loaded region.

Sign Convention for Moments — Must State This Every Problem

Pick one: either CCW positive or CW positive. Write it down. Then: a force creates a positive moment if it tends to rotate the body in your chosen positive direction. A force directly below or above a moment point creates zero moment (the perpendicular distance is zero). Forces whose lines of action pass through the moment point create zero moment.

The Moment of a Force

Push a door near the hinge — it barely moves. Push the same force at the far edge — it swings easily. The force is the same; what changed is the perpendicular distance from the pivot to the force's line of action. This distance is the moment arm, and the product of force and moment arm is the moment:

$$M_O = F \times d_\perp$$

where \(d_\perp\) is the perpendicular distance from point O to the line of action of the force — not the distance to the point of application.

Units: N·m or kN·m. Sign convention: CCW positive, CW negative (state this at the start of every problem).

Varignon's Theorem — Your Best Friend

Instead of finding the perpendicular distance directly (often geometric pain), resolve the force into components and take moments of each component separately. The moment of the resultant equals the sum of moments of its components:

\(M_O = F_x \times y - F_y \times x\)   where \(x, y\) are coordinates of the force's point of application from O.

This works because perpendicular distance from O to a horizontal force = vertical coordinate of application, and vice versa.

Couples — Pure Rotation With No Net Force

A couple is two equal, opposite, parallel forces separated by a distance \(d\). Their net force is zero (they don't translate the body), but they produce a pure moment. Key property: the moment of a couple is the same about every point in the body — it can be moved anywhere without changing its effect.

$$M_{couple} = F \times d$$
Trusses — Why Triangles Are Strong

A truss is a structure of straight members connected by pin joints at their ends, with loads applied only at joints. Because of this, every member carries either pure tension (it's being stretched — the force pulls outward on both ends) or pure compression (it's being squashed — the force pushes inward on both ends). No bending occurs in any member.

This is why trusses are so efficient — the material is working in its strongest mode (axial) throughout. Bridge trusses, roof structures, and transmission towers all exploit this principle.

Method of Joints — Systematic Truss Analysis

Principle: At each pin joint, all forces are concurrent (meeting at a point), so only \(\sum F_x = 0\) and \(\sum F_y = 0\) apply. No moment equation needed.

Procedure: 1) Find support reactions first using the whole-truss FBD. 2) Start at a joint with only 2 unknown member forces (usually a support joint). 3) Solve the two equilibrium equations for those two unknowns. 4) Move to an adjacent joint, repeat. 5) Assume unknown members are in tension (force pulls away from joint). If the answer is negative, the member is actually in compression.

Method of Sections — When You Need Just One Member

Cut through the truss (through the member you want, plus at most 2 others — max 3 unknowns). Take one side as a free body. Apply \(\sum F_x = 0\), \(\sum F_y = 0\), and \(\sum M = 0\). Taking moments about the intersection of two unknown forces isolates the third — very efficient.

What Happens Inside a Beam

A beam carrying loads is not just transferring forces from one end to the other. Inside, at every cross-section, the two halves of the beam are exerting forces and moments on each other to stay in equilibrium. These are internal forces.

Cut any beam at a position \(x\) and consider one side. For that cut portion to be in equilibrium with the external loads on it, the cut face must provide:

Shear force V: the internal force parallel to the cut face — it prevents the two halves from sliding past each other vertically.

Bending moment M: the internal moment at the cut — it prevents the two halves from rotating relative to each other. It is the bending moment that causes a beam to curve, and if it's too large, the beam cracks.

Sign Convention — Must Be Stated, Must Be Consistent

Positive shear (V > 0): The left portion is pushed upward by the right, or equivalently, the left portion has net upward external forces. On a diagram: left side of cut has forces that would rotate CW.

Positive bending moment (M > 0): Sagging — the beam curves concave upward like a smile. The bottom fibres are in tension. On a cross-section: the cut face on the left has a CCW moment acting on it.

How to Find V and M at Any Section

Cut the beam at position \(x\). Consider the left portion only. Sum all external forces to the left of the cut:

\(V(x) = \sum (\text{upward forces to left}) - \sum(\text{downward forces to left})\)

\(M(x) = \sum(\text{CCW moments of forces to left about the cut})\)

Key Rules for Drawing the Diagrams
Concentrated load P↓: causes a sudden jump (drop) of P in the shear force diagram at that point.
UDL of intensity w (kN/m): causes the SFD to slope linearly. Slope = −w (SFD decreases at rate w per metre moving right).
Reaction ↑: causes a sudden upward jump in the SFD equal to the reaction value.
Slope of BMD = shear force: \(dM/dx = V\). So the BMD slopes upward where V > 0 and downward where V < 0.
Maximum bending moment: occurs where V = 0 (shear changes sign). This is where the beam is most likely to fail in bending.
Applied moment M: causes a sudden jump in the BMD equal to M. Direction matters (CW applied → jump upward if moving right).
$$\frac{dV}{dx} = -w(x) \qquad \frac{dM}{dx} = V(x)$$

Understanding Friction

When two surfaces are in contact, microscopic asperities interlock. Push the surfaces sideways and these asperities resist — this is friction. The resistance is proportional to how hard the surfaces are pressed together (the normal force), and it doesn't depend on contact area.

Friction has two regimes depending on whether the surfaces are sliding:

Static (impending motion)
\(F_s \leq \mu_s N\)
Body on verge of slipping when \(F_s = \mu_s N\)
Kinetic (sliding)
\(F_k = \mu_k N\)
Acts while surfaces slide; \(\mu_k < \mu_s\)
Normal force
\(N = mg\cos\theta\)
On inclined plane at angle θ
Angle of friction
\(\phi = \arctan(\mu_s)\)
Block slips when \(\theta > \phi\)
Most Common Friction Mistake

The friction force is not always at its maximum. For a body in equilibrium (not on the verge of slipping), friction adjusts to whatever value maintains equilibrium, up to a maximum of \(\mu_s N\). Only when the body is about to slip does \(F = \mu_s N\). When in doubt, first check if equilibrium is possible; if the required friction force exceeds \(\mu_s N\), the body slips.

Centroids of Areas

The centroid of an area is its geometric centre — the point where the area would balance if cut out of a thin flat plate. For composite areas (built from simple shapes), use the weighted average:

$$\bar{x} = \frac{\sum A_i \bar{x}_i}{\sum A_i} \qquad \bar{y} = \frac{\sum A_i \bar{y}_i}{\sum A_i}$$

To subtract a shape (e.g. a hole), give it a negative area in the sum. The centroid coordinates \(\bar{x}_i, \bar{y}_i\) of each part are measured from a common reference point.

Second Moment of Area \(I\) — Bending Stiffness

The second moment of area (also called moment of inertia of the area) measures how effectively a cross-section resists bending. A large \(I\) means the beam is stiff — it resists bending for the same applied moment. This is why I-beams (flanges far from the neutral axis) are so efficient: moving material away from the centroid dramatically increases \(I\).

Rectangle (about centroidal axis)
\(I_{x} = \dfrac{bh^3}{12}\)
b = width, h = height
Circle (about centroidal axis)
\(I_{x} = \dfrac{\pi r^4}{4} = \dfrac{\pi d^4}{64}\)
Parallel Axis Theorem
\(I = I_{centroid} + A d^2\)
d = distance from centroid to new axis
Why the Parallel Axis Theorem Matters

A T-beam flange sits above the neutral axis. To find its contribution to the total \(I\), you first find \(I\) of the flange about its own centroid, then add \(Ad^2\) where \(d\) is how far the flange centroid is from the beam's overall neutral axis. The \(Ad^2\) term usually dominates — which is precisely why flanges work. Even a small flange area contributes enormously to \(I\) when it's far from the neutral axis.

Stress — Force Per Unit Area

Imagine two rods, both carrying the same 10 kN axial load. One has a 10 mm² cross-section, the other has a 1000 mm² cross-section. The narrow rod is 100 times more likely to break — not because of the force, but because of the intensity of force at each unit of area. This intensity is stress.

$$\sigma = \frac{F}{A} \quad \text{(Pa = N/m}^2\text{, or MPa = N/mm}^2\text{)}$$

Positive = tensile (pulling apart). Negative = compressive (squeezing together). Engineering materials have different strength limits for each.

Shear stress acts parallel to the cross-section — like a bolt in a joint being sliced. Same formula but different symbol:

$$\tau = \frac{V}{A} \qquad \text{where V = shear force, A = shear area}$$
Strain — Fractional Deformation

Under stress, materials deform. A 2 m rod that stretches by 1 mm has deformed by 1/2000 = 0.0005 of its original length. This ratio is strain — dimensionless, usually a very small number.

$$\varepsilon = \frac{\delta}{L} = \frac{\Delta L}{L}$$
Young's Modulus — The Material's Stiffness

For elastic materials (where the deformation is recoverable), stress is proportional to strain. The ratio is Young's Modulus \(E\) — a fundamental material property. A larger \(E\) means a stiffer material: for the same stress, it deforms less.

$$E = \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon} \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad \delta = \frac{FL}{AE}$$
Typical Values of Young's Modulus

Steel: \(E \approx 200\) GPa  |  Aluminium: \(E \approx 70\) GPa  |  Concrete: \(E \approx 25-30\) GPa  |  Timber: \(E \approx 10\) GPa  |  Rubber: \(E \approx 0.01-0.1\) GPa

Factor of Safety

No engineer designs to the exact failure load. Uncertainties in material properties, loading, and geometry require a safety margin. The factor of safety (FOS) reduces the allowable stress below the failure stress:

$$\sigma_{allow} = \frac{\sigma_{failure}}{FOS} \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad A_{min} = \frac{F}{\sigma_{allow}}$$
Work and Energy — A Powerful Alternative to Newton's Laws

For dynamics problems, you often need to find velocity rather than acceleration. The work-energy theorem bypasses acceleration entirely: the net work done on a body equals the change in its kinetic energy.

$$W_{net} = \Delta KE = \frac{1}{2}mv_2^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_1^2$$

Work done by force \(F\) through displacement \(s\) at angle \(\theta\) to the displacement: \(W = Fs\cos\theta\).

For conservative systems (no friction, no dissipation): \(KE_1 + PE_1 = KE_2 + PE_2\), where \(PE = mgh\) for gravity.

A 15 kg block starts at rest at the top of a 4 m frictionless ramp inclined at 40°. Find its speed at the bottom using: (a) work-energy theorem, (b) conservation of energy. Both methods should give the same answer.
▶
1

(a) Work-Energy Theorem: The only force doing work is the component of gravity along the ramp (normal force does no work — perpendicular to motion). Work done = \(W = F \times s = mg\sin40° \times 4 = 15 \times 9.81 \times 0.6428 \times 4 = 378.3\) J.

2

\(W = \Delta KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 - 0\)
\(378.3 = \frac{1}{2}(15)v^2 \Rightarrow v^2 = 50.44 \Rightarrow v = 7.10\) m/s

3

(b) Conservation of Energy: Height dropped \(h = 4\sin40° = 2.571\) m.
\(mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \Rightarrow v = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2 \times 9.81 \times 2.571} = \sqrt{50.44} = 7.10\) m/s ✓

Both methods give identical answers because they're mathematically equivalent. In practice, use energy methods when you know start/end states; use Newton's Law when you need forces or accelerations during the motion.

AnswerSpeed at bottom = 7.10 m/s (both methods agree)

From Equilibrium to Motion

Statics assumes acceleration is zero. Dynamics removes that assumption. When the net force is not zero, bodies accelerate according to Newton's second law:

$$\sum F_x = ma_x \qquad \sum F_y = ma_y$$

For many exam problems, work-energy is faster than solving acceleration as a function of time:

$$W_{net} = \Delta KE = \frac{1}{2}mv_2^2-\frac{1}{2}mv_1^2$$
Method Choice Rule

Use \(\sum F=ma\) when you need acceleration/tension during motion. Use work-energy when you need speed after moving through distance/height and forces are easy to compute over displacement.

Dynamic Friction and Suddenly Applied Loads
Kinetic friction
\(F_k=\mu_kN\), opposite motion direction
Inclined motion
\(ma = mg\sin\theta - \mu_k mg\cos\theta\) (downslope positive)
Sudden loading check
Compare resulting stress to allowable and comment on safety margin
