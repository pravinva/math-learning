1 Reactions first, then sign convention

Every SFD/BMD problem starts the same way every other statics problem does: find the support reactions first, using \(\Sigma F_y=0\) and \(\Sigma M=0\). Only once those are known can you build \(V(x)\) and \(M(x)\).

Reading the shear diagram, left to right

Starting from the left end, \(V(x)\) is the running total of all upward forces encountered so far. An upward reaction makes \(V\) jump up instantly; a downward point load makes \(V\) drop instantly, by exactly that load's magnitude. If reactions were computed correctly, \(V\) always returns to exactly zero at the right end.

Worked example

Find the reactions for a beam, span 6 m, pin at A, roller at B, with a single 120 N load 2 m from A.

\(\Sigma M_A\): \(R_B(6)-120(2)=0 \Rightarrow R_B=40\text{ N}\).

\(\Sigma F_y\): \(R_A=120-40=80\text{ N}\).

Practice 1

Beam span 5 m, pin-roller, single 80 N load at \(x=3\text{ m}\). Find \(R_A,R_B\).

Solution

\(\Sigma M_A\): \(R_B(5)=80(3)=240 \Rightarrow R_B=48\text{ N}\). \(R_A=32\text{ N}\).

Practice 2

Beam span 7 m, single 140 N load at \(x=4\text{ m}\). Find \(R_A,R_B\).

Solution

\(R_B=140(4)/7=80\text{ N}\). \(R_A=60\text{ N}\).

Practice 3

Beam span 6 m with a UDL \(w=40\text{ N/m}\) over the full length, no point loads. Find \(R_A,R_B\).

Solution

Total load \(=40(6)=240\text{ N}\), symmetric: \(R_A=R_B=120\text{ N}\).

2 Point loads: piecewise shear diagrams

With only point loads (no distributed load), the shear diagram is a series of horizontal steps: constant between loads, jumping instantly at each load or reaction.

Worked example

For the beam above (\(L=6\), \(R_A=80\text{ N}\) at \(x=0\), 120 N down at \(x=2\), \(R_B=40\text{ N}\) at \(x=6\)), plot \(V(x)\).

\(0

At \(x=2\): \(V\) drops by 120: \(V=80-120=-40\text{ N}\).

\(2

At \(x=6\): \(V\) jumps by \(R_B=40\): \(V=-40+40=0\) ✓ (closes to zero, confirming the reactions).

Practice 7

A cantilever, fixed at A, length 4 m, single 90 N load at the free end. Find \(V(x)\) along the span.

Solution

Fixed-end reaction \(R_{Ay}=90\text{ N}\) up. \(V=90\text{ N}\) constant for the entire span (only the fixed reaction acts until the tip), dropping to \(0\) right at the free end after the load.

Practice 8

Using Practice 4's beam, explain why the shear diagram must return to exactly zero at the right end if the reactions were computed correctly.

Solution

By the time you reach the far right end, every vertical force on the beam (all loads plus both reactions) has been accounted for in the running total. Since the whole beam is in equilibrium (\(\Sigma F_y=0\)), that running total must sum to exactly zero, a nonzero result flags an arithmetic or reaction error.

3 The V–M relationship & distributed loads

With a distributed load, \(V\) no longer jumps in steps, it slopes smoothly. And moment, in turn, is simply the running area under the shear diagram.

Key idea

$$\frac{dV}{dx}=w(x), \qquad \frac{dM}{dx}=V(x), \qquad M(x)=\int V(x)\,dx.$$

The maximum (or minimum) moment occurs wherever \(V=0\), since that's exactly where \(M\)'s slope is zero.

Worked example

A simply-supported beam, \(L=6\text{ m}\), carries a UDL \(w=50\text{ N/m}\) over its full length. Find \(V(x)\), \(M(x)\), and the maximum moment.

Total load \(=300\text{ N}\), symmetric: \(R_A=R_B=150\text{ N}\).

\(V(x)=R_A-wx=150-50x\). \(V=0\) at \(x=3\text{ m}\) (midspan, as expected by symmetry).

\(M(x)=\int V\,dx = 150x-25x^2\) (constant of integration is zero, since \(M=0\) at the simply-supported end).

\(M_{max}=M(3)=150(3)-25(9)=450-225=225\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}\).

Practice 9

Beam \(L=4\text{ m}\), UDL \(w=80\text{ N/m}\), simply supported. Find \(R_A,R_B\), \(V(x)\), and \(M_{max}\).

Solution

Total \(=320\text{ N}\), \(R_A=R_B=160\text{ N}\). \(V(x)=160-80x\), zero at \(x=2\). \(M(x)=160x-40x^2\). \(M_{max}=160(2)-40(4)=160\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}\).

Practice 10

Beam \(L=10\text{ m}\), UDL \(w=30\text{ N/m}\), simply supported. Find \(M_{max}\).

Solution

\(R_A=R_B=150\text{ N}\). \(V(x)=150-30x\), zero at \(x=5\). \(M(x)=150x-15x^2\). \(M_{max}=150(5)-15(25)=375\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}\).

Practice 11

State the general formula for the maximum moment of a simply-supported beam under a full-span UDL, and verify it against Practice 10's answer.

Solution

\(M_{max}=\dfrac{wL^2}{8}\). For Practice 10: \(\dfrac{30(10)^2}{8}=\dfrac{3000}{8}=375\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}\), matching ✓.

Practice 12

A cantilever, fixed at A, length 3 m, UDL \(w=40\text{ N/m}\) over its full length. Find the reaction force and moment at A.

Solution

Total load \(=120\text{ N}\) at the centroid, 1.5 m from A. \(R_{Ay}=120\text{ N}\). \(M_A=120(1.5)=180\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}\).

Practice 13

For the worked example beam (\(L=6,\ w=50\text{ N/m}\)), find \(M\) at \(x=1\text{ m}\) and \(x=5\text{ m}\), and confirm the diagram is symmetric about midspan.

Solution

\(M(1)=150(1)-25(1)=125\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}\). \(M(5)=150(5)-25(25)=750-625=125\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}\). Equal, confirming symmetry ✓.

Practice 14

Explain why, for a simply-supported beam under only a full-span UDL, the maximum bending moment always occurs exactly at midspan.

Solution

Since \(dV/dx=-w\) is constant, \(V(x)\) is a straight line, and with \(R_A=R_B\) by symmetry, that line crosses zero exactly at the midpoint. Since \(M\) is maximised wherever \(V=0\), the peak moment sits at midspan.

4 Advanced: combined loading, full SFD/BMD

Real beams often mix point loads and distributed loads. Build the SFD region by region (each new load type changes the local slope), then build the BMD as the running area underneath it, always finding reactions first.

Worked example

Beam \(L=6\text{ m}\), pin-roller, with a 100 N point load at \(x=2\) and a UDL \(w=30\text{ N/m}\) over the full span. Find the reactions, then \(V\) and \(M\) at \(x=4\text{ m}\).

UDL total \(=180\text{ N}\) at \(x=3\) (centroid). \(\Sigma M_A\): \(R_B(6)=100(2)+180(3)=740 \Rightarrow R_B=123.33\text{ N}\), \(R_A=156.67\text{ N}\).

\(V(4)\): starting from \(R_A\), subtract the UDL up to \(x=4\) and the point load: \(V(4)=156.67-30(4)-100=156.67-120-100=-63.33\text{ N}\).

\(M(4)\): take moments of everything left of the cut, about the cut. UDL from 0 to 4 totals \(120\text{ N}\) at its centroid \(x=2\) (2 m from the cut):

\(M(4)=R_A(4)-100(4-2)-120(2)=626.68-200-240=186.68\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}\).

Practice 15

For the worked example beam, find \(V\) and \(M\) at \(x=5\text{ m}\).

Solution

\(V(5)=156.67-30(5)-100=-93.33\text{ N}\).

UDL from 0 to 5 totals \(150\text{ N}\) at centroid \(x=2.5\) (2.5 m from the cut): \(M(5)=156.67(5)-100(3)-150(2.5)=783.35-300-375=108.35\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}\).

Practice 16

For the worked example beam, verify that \(V\) returns to zero just after adding \(R_B\) at \(x=6\).

Solution

\(V(6^-)=156.67-30(6)-100=-123.33\text{ N}\). Adding \(R_B=123.33\text{ N}\): \(V(6^+)=-123.33+123.33=0\) ✓.

Practice 17

A beam \(L=5\text{ m}\) has a UDL \(w=60\text{ N/m}\) over its full span plus a point load 150 N at \(x=3\), pin at A, roller at B. Find the reactions.

Solution

UDL total \(=300\text{ N}\) at \(x=2.5\). \(\Sigma M_A\): \(R_B(5)=300(2.5)+150(3)=1200 \Rightarrow R_B=240\text{ N}\). \(R_A=300+150-240=210\text{ N}\).

Practice 18

Explain why the bending moment diagram for a beam with combined point and distributed loads is generally NOT a single straight line or single parabola across the whole span.

Solution

\(M\)'s curvature is governed by \(d^2M/dx^2=dV/dx=w(x)\). Where no distributed load acts, \(V\) is constant and \(M\) is a straight line; where a UDL acts, \(V\) changes linearly and \(M\) becomes a parabola. Since \(w(x)\) itself changes from region to region, the shape of \(M(x)\) necessarily changes piecewise too, a different curve type in each region.

Where to go from here. The reaction-finding and equilibrium skills here carry straight into Week 6's truss analysis, where the same \(\Sigma M=0\) logic, applied to a cut section instead of a whole beam, solves for individual member forces.
