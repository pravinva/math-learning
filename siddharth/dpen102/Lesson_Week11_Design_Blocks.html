1 Checking if a section is safe

The most common real design question: given an actual load and a chosen section, does it pass?

Key idea

Compute \(\sigma_{work}=F/A\), compare against \(\sigma_{allow}=\sigma_y/FoS\). \(\sigma_{work}\le\sigma_{allow}\): PASS. Otherwise: FAIL.

Worked example 1

A tie rod, diameter 20 mm, carries 60 kN. \(\sigma_y=300\text{ MPa}\), \(FoS=2.5\). Is it safe?

\(A=\pi(10)^2=314.16\text{ mm}^2\). \(\sigma_{work}=60{,}000/314.16=190.99\text{ MPa}\).

\(\sigma_{allow}=300/2.5=120\text{ MPa}\). \(190.99>120\): FAILS.

Worked example 2

The same rod's diameter is increased to 28 mm. Recheck.

\(A=\pi(14)^2=615.75\text{ mm}^2\). \(\sigma_{work}=60{,}000/615.75=97.44\text{ MPa}\).

\(97.44<120\): PASSES, margin \(=120-97.44=22.56\text{ MPa}\) (18.8%).

Practice 1

A rod, diameter 15 mm, carries 30 kN. \(\sigma_y=250\text{ MPa}\), \(FoS=2\). Is it safe?

Solution

\(A=\pi(7.5)^2=176.7\text{ mm}^2\). \(\sigma_{work}=30{,}000/176.7=169.8\text{ MPa}\). \(\sigma_{allow}=125\text{ MPa}\). \(169.8>125\): FAILS.

Practice 2

A column, area 2000 mm², carries 180 kN compressive. \(\sigma_y=300\text{ MPa}\), \(FoS=3\). Safe?

Solution

\(\sigma_{work}=180{,}000/2000=90\text{ MPa}\). \(\sigma_{allow}=100\text{ MPa}\). \(90<100\): PASSES.

Practice 3

A bolt, diameter 10 mm, carries 15 kN. \(\sigma_y=400\text{ MPa}\), \(FoS=2.5\). Safe? Find the margin.

Solution

\(A=78.54\text{ mm}^2\). \(\sigma_{work}=15{,}000/78.54=190.99\text{ MPa}\). \(\sigma_{allow}=160\text{ MPa}\). FAILS by 30.99 MPa.

Practice 4

A cable, area 50 mm², carries 4 kN. \(\sigma_y=1000\text{ MPa}\), \(FoS=4\). Safe?

Solution

\(\sigma_{work}=4000/50=80\text{ MPa}\). \(\sigma_{allow}=250\text{ MPa}\). \(80<250\): PASSES, comfortably.

Practice 5

Explain the difference between "the material yields" and "the design fails," in terms of \(\sigma_{work}\), \(\sigma_y\), and \(\sigma_{allow}\).

Solution

Yielding happens only when \(\sigma_{work}\) exceeds \(\sigma_y\), actual physical failure onset. "Design fails" in the engineering sense happens earlier, whenever \(\sigma_{work}\) exceeds \(\sigma_{allow}\) (which is \(\sigma_y\) divided by \(FoS\), a deliberately lower threshold). A design can fail its safety check well before the material would actually yield, that's the whole point of building in a margin.

Practice 6

Design A has \(\sigma_{work}=80\text{ MPa}\), Design B has \(\sigma_{work}=110\text{ MPa}\), same load and material, \(\sigma_{allow}=100\text{ MPa}\). Which passes, and what does this tell you about their areas?

Solution

Design A passes (80<100), Design B fails. Since \(\sigma=F/A\) with the same \(F\), Design B's higher stress means it has a smaller area than Design A, Design B is undersized for this load.

2 Finding the maximum safe load

The reverse question: given a chosen section, how much load can it actually carry before the allowable stress is exceeded?

Key idea

$$F_{max}=\sigma_{allow}\times A.$$

Worked example 1

A column, area 2500 mm², \(\sigma_y=280\text{ MPa}\), \(FoS=2.8\). Find the maximum safe axial load.

\(\sigma_{allow}=280/2.8=100\text{ MPa}\). \(F_{max}=100(2500)=250{,}000\text{ N}=250\text{ kN}\).

Worked example 2

If the column above must actually support 220 kN, does it pass? Find the percentage margin.

\(220<250\): PASSES. Margin \(=250-220=30\text{ kN}\), or \(30/250=12\%\).

Practice 7

A rod, area 400 mm², \(\sigma_y=320\text{ MPa}\), \(FoS=2\). Find the maximum safe load.

Solution

\(\sigma_{allow}=160\text{ MPa}\). \(F_{max}=160(400)=64{,}000\text{ N}=64\text{ kN}\).

Practice 8

For Practice 7, if the actual load is 55 kN, does it pass? Find the margin.

Solution

\(55<64\): PASSES. Margin \(=9\text{ kN}\) (14.1%).

Practice 9

A beam section, area 6000 mm², \(\sigma_y=250\text{ MPa}\), \(FoS=2.5\). Find the maximum safe axial load.

Solution

\(\sigma_{allow}=100\text{ MPa}\). \(F_{max}=100(6000)=600{,}000\text{ N}=600\text{ kN}\).

Practice 10

For Practice 9, the actual demand is 640 kN. Does it pass?

Solution

\(640>600\): FAILS, by 40 kN (6.7% over capacity).

Practice 11

A cable, area 30 mm², \(\sigma_{allow}=300\text{ MPa}\) (given directly). Find the max safe load, in kN.

Solution

\(F_{max}=300(30)=9000\text{ N}=9\text{ kN}\).

Practice 12

Explain why "percentage margin" is often more useful than a raw kN difference when reporting a pass/fail result.

Solution

A raw difference (say, 30 kN) doesn't convey how significant that gap is relative to total capacity, 30 kN out of 250 kN (12%) is comfortable, but 30 kN out of a 40 kN capacity (75%) would be alarmingly tight. Percentage margin puts the safety cushion in proper context regardless of the load's absolute size.

3 Composite section design blocks

Real sections aren't simple rectangles, they're composites. The full design block strings the Week 8 geometry tools directly into the Week 4 stress check.

Worked example

An L-shaped bracket: vertical leg 100×20 mm (area 2000, centroid \(x=10,y=60\)), horizontal leg 80×20 mm (area 1600, centroid \(x=50,y=10\)). Find the composite centroid, then check whether it's safe under a pure axial load of 90 kN, \(\sigma_y=260\text{ MPa}\), \(FoS=2.6\).

Total area \(=3600\text{ mm}^2\). \(\bar x=\dfrac{2000(10)+1600(50)}{3600}=27.78\text{ mm}\). \(\bar y=\dfrac{2000(60)+1600(10)}{3600}=37.78\text{ mm}\).

For a pure axial check, only the total area matters: \(\sigma_{allow}=260/2.6=100\text{ MPa}\). \(\sigma_{work}=90{,}000/3600=25\text{ MPa}\).

\(25<100\): PASSES, with a huge margin (the centroid, while found, wasn't actually needed for this particular check).

Practice 13

An L-shaped bracket: leg 1 area 1500 (centroid \(x=8,y=45\)), leg 2 area 1200 (centroid \(x=40,y=8\)). Find the composite centroid.

Solution

Total \(=2700\). \(\bar x=\dfrac{1500(8)+1200(40)}{2700}=22.22\text{ mm}\). \(\bar y=\dfrac{1500(45)+1200(8)}{2700}=28.56\text{ mm}\).

Practice 14

The bracket in Practice 13 (total area 2700 mm²) carries a pure axial load of 150 kN. \(\sigma_y=240\text{ MPa}\), \(FoS=2.4\). Check if safe.

Solution

\(\sigma_{allow}=100\text{ MPa}\). \(\sigma_{work}=150{,}000/2700=55.56\text{ MPa}\). \(55.56<100\): PASSES.

Practice 15

A T-shaped column section, total area 4400 mm², carries 380 kN axial. \(\sigma_y=275\text{ MPa}\), \(FoS=2.75\). Check if safe, find the margin as a percentage.

Solution

\(\sigma_{allow}=100\text{ MPa}\). \(\sigma_{work}=380{,}000/4400=86.36\text{ MPa}\). \(86.36<100\): PASSES, margin \(=13.64\%\).

Practice 16

Explain why, for a PURE AXIAL load check (not bending), the centroid location and second moment of area \(I\) aren't actually needed, only the total area is.

Solution

Axial stress is simply \(\sigma=F/A\), force divided by TOTAL cross-sectional area, it doesn't care where that area is distributed or how it's shaped, only how much of it there is. Centroid and \(I\) only become relevant once bending enters the picture, since bending stress depends on distance from the centroid via \(\sigma=Mc/I\).

Practice 17

If the same bracket from Practice 14 instead carried a bending moment (not pure axial), which additional section properties would you need, and why?

Solution

The composite centroid (to establish the neutral axis), the composite second moment of area \(I\) about that centroid, and the extreme-fibre distance \(c\) from the centroid to the outermost point, all needed to apply \(\sigma=Mc/I\) for the bending stress check.

4 Advanced: shape & design decisions

For the same amount of material, different shapes give wildly different bending stiffness, even though axial capacity stays identical. Choosing the shape is itself a design decision.

Worked comparison

Two beam sections, both area \(4000\text{ mm}^2\). Section A, a solid square (\(s\approx63.25\text{ mm}\)): \(I=s^4/12\approx1{,}333{,}733\text{ mm}^4\). Section B, an I-shape concentrating material in two flanges: \(I\approx30{,}000{,}000\text{ mm}^4\), roughly 22× stiffer for the exact same amount of material, exactly the parallel-axis lesson from Week 8 in action.

Practice 18

Two sections, same area and material: Section X has \(I=5{,}000{,}000\text{ mm}^4\), Section Y has \(I=18{,}000{,}000\text{ mm}^4\). Which resists bending deflection better, and why?

Solution

Section Y, higher \(I\) directly means greater bending stiffness (less deflection) for the same material and loading.

Practice 19

A designer has a fixed material budget and needs to maximise bending stiffness. Based on Weeks 8 and 11, what general shape strategy should they follow?

Solution

Concentrate material as far from the centroidal axis as possible (like an I-beam's flanges), since the parallel-axis transfer term \(Ad^2\) grows quadratically with distance, maximising \(I\) for a given area.

Practice 20

Does changing a section's shape, keeping the same total area, change its PURE AXIAL load capacity? Explain.

Solution

No. Axial capacity depends only on total area (\(\sigma=F/A\)), so reshaping the same amount of material doesn't change how much pure axial load it can carry, even though it dramatically changes bending performance.

Practice 21

Summarise, in your own words, why "bigger area" and "better section" are not the same design goal.

Solution

Bigger area helps axial capacity roughly proportionally, but bending performance depends on how that area is arranged (via \(I\)), not just how much there is. A well-shaped section can outperform a poorly-shaped one with the same, or even more, material, good design is about the placement of material, not just its quantity.
