Vectors, Components & Equilibrium Setup

Force has a direction, not just a size, so it must be added like a vector, not like a plain number. This week builds the algebra: converting between magnitude-angle and component form, combining several forces into one resultant, and writing the full equilibrium equations for a body with support reactions.

0 Scalars vs vectors

A scalar has magnitude only, mass, time, speed, temperature. A vector has both magnitude and direction, force, velocity, displacement. Two 50 N forces pointing in opposite directions do not add up to 100 N, they cancel to zero. Forces must always be combined as vectors.

1 Magnitude-angle ↔ components

Any force can be described two equivalent ways: as a magnitude and an angle, or as horizontal and vertical components. Converting between them is ordinary right-triangle trigonometry.

Key idea

Given magnitude \(F\) and angle \(\theta\) from the positive \(x\)-axis: $$F_x=F\cos\theta, \qquad F_y=F\sin\theta.$$

Given components \(F_x,F_y\): $$F=\sqrt{F_x^2+F_y^2}, \qquad \theta=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{F_y}{F_x}\right) \text{ (adjust for quadrant).}$$

Worked example 1

A force of 100 N acts at 40° above horizontal. Find its components.

\(F_x=100\cos40^\circ=76.6\text{ N}\). \(F_y=100\sin40^\circ=64.3\text{ N}\).

Worked example 2

A force has components \(F_x=30\text{ N}, F_y=40\text{ N}\). Find its magnitude and angle.

\(F=\sqrt{900+1600}=\sqrt{2500}=50\text{ N}\). \(\theta=\tan^{-1}(40/30)=53.13^\circ\).

Practice 1

A force of 80 N acts at 25° above horizontal. Find its components.

Solution

\(F_x=80\cos25^\circ=72.5\text{ N}\). \(F_y=80\sin25^\circ=33.8\text{ N}\).

Practice 2

A force of 150 N acts at 60° above horizontal. Find its components.

Solution

\(F_x=150\cos60^\circ=75\text{ N}\). \(F_y=150\sin60^\circ=129.9\text{ N}\).

Practice 3

A force has components \(F_x=12\text{ N}, F_y=5\text{ N}\). Find its magnitude and angle.

Solution

\(F=\sqrt{144+25}=\sqrt{169}=13\text{ N}\). \(\theta=\tan^{-1}(5/12)=22.6^\circ\).

Practice 4

A force has components \(F_x=-9\text{ N}, F_y=12\text{ N}\). Find its magnitude and angle from the positive \(x\)-axis.

Solution

\(F=\sqrt{81+144}=\sqrt{225}=15\text{ N}\). Reference angle \(=\tan^{-1}(12/9)=53.13^\circ\); since \(F_x<0,F_y>0\) (Quadrant II): \(\theta=180-53.13=126.87^\circ\).

Practice 5

A force of 200 N acts straight up (90° from horizontal). State its components without calculation, and explain why.

Solution

\(F_x=0, F_y=200\text{ N}\), since \(\cos90^\circ=0\) and \(\sin90^\circ=1\), all the force lies along the \(y\)-direction with none along \(x\).

Practice 6

Explain why \(F_x=F\cos\theta\) and \(F_y=F\sin\theta\) (not the reverse), when \(\theta\) is measured from the positive \(x\)-axis.

Solution

By definition of an angle measured from the \(x\)-axis, the horizontal leg of the right triangle relates to cosine and the vertical leg to sine. \(\cos\theta\) always gives the fraction of \(F\) along the reference axis, \(\sin\theta\) the fraction perpendicular to it.

2 Resultant of multiple forces

To combine several forces acting at a point, sum all the \(x\)-components together, sum all the \(y\)-components together, then recombine into a single resultant.

Key idea

$$R=\sqrt{(\Sigma F_x)^2+(\Sigma F_y)^2}, \qquad \theta=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{\Sigma F_y}{\Sigma F_x}\right).$$

Worked example

Three forces act on a point: \(F_1=50\text{ N}\) at 0°, \(F_2=70\text{ N}\) at 90°, \(F_3=40\text{ N}\) at 180°. Find the resultant.

\(F_1=(50,0)\), \(F_2=(0,70)\), \(F_3=(-40,0)\).

\(\Sigma F_x=50+0-40=10\text{ N}\). \(\Sigma F_y=0+70+0=70\text{ N}\).

\(R=\sqrt{100+4900}=\sqrt{5000}=70.7\text{ N}\). \(\theta=\tan^{-1}(70/10)=81.87^\circ\).

Practice 7

Two forces: \(F_1=60\text{ N}\) at 0°, \(F_2=80\text{ N}\) at 90°. Find the resultant magnitude and angle.

Solution

\(\Sigma F_x=60, \Sigma F_y=80\). \(R=\sqrt{3600+6400}=\sqrt{10000}=100\text{ N}\). \(\theta=\tan^{-1}(80/60)=53.13^\circ\).

Practice 8

Three equal forces, 30 N each, at 0°, 120°, and 240°. Find the resultant.

Solution

\(F_1=(30,0)\), \(F_2=(-15,25.98)\), \(F_3=(-15,-25.98)\). \(\Sigma F_x=30-15-15=0\). \(\Sigma F_y=0+25.98-25.98=0\). \(R=0\), the three equal forces spaced 120° apart perfectly cancel.

Practice 9

Two forces: \(F_1=100\text{ N}\) at 30°, \(F_2=80\text{ N}\) at 150°. Find the resultant.

Solution

\(F_1=(86.6,50)\), \(F_2=(-69.28,40)\). \(\Sigma F_x=17.32\), \(\Sigma F_y=90\). \(R=\sqrt{300+8100}=91.65\text{ N}\). \(\theta=\tan^{-1}(90/17.32)=79.11^\circ\).

Practice 10

Explain why you cannot simply add force magnitudes directly (e.g., \(50\text{ N}+70\text{ N}=120\text{ N}\)) unless they point in exactly the same direction.

Solution

Force is a vector; adding magnitudes directly ignores direction entirely. Two forces at an angle to each other partially cancel or reinforce depending on their components, only when perfectly aligned does simple magnitude addition give the correct resultant.

Practice 11

If \(\Sigma F_x=0\) and \(\Sigma F_y=0\) for a set of forces on a point, what does this tell you about the resultant, and physically?

Solution

The resultant is exactly zero (\(R=0\)), meaning the point is in equilibrium under these forces, no net force acts on it at all.

3 i, j, k notation

\(\vec A=A_x\hat i+A_y\hat j+A_z\hat k\) writes a vector using unit vectors along \(x\), \(y\), \(z\). Adding vectors this way is just adding matching components.

Worked example 1

Write a force with \(F_x=45\text{ N}, F_y=60\text{ N}\) in \(\hat i, \hat j\) notation.

\(\vec F=45\hat i+60\hat j\) (N).

Worked example 2

Add \(\vec A=3\hat i+5\hat j\) and \(\vec B=-2\hat i+4\hat j\).

\(\vec A+\vec B=(3-2)\hat i+(5+4)\hat j=1\hat i+9\hat j\).

Practice 12

Write a force with \(F_x=25\text{ N}, F_y=-10\text{ N}\) in \(\hat i,\hat j\) notation.

Solution

\(\vec F=25\hat i-10\hat j\) (N).

Practice 13

Add \(\vec A=6\hat i-3\hat j\) and \(\vec B=2\hat i+7\hat j\).

Solution

\(\vec A+\vec B=8\hat i+4\hat j\).

Practice 14

Find the magnitude of \(\vec F=8\hat i-15\hat j\).

Solution

\(|\vec F|=\sqrt{64+225}=\sqrt{289}=17\text{ N}\).

Practice 15

For a purely 2D problem written as \(\vec A=4\hat i+3\hat j+0\hat k\), why is the \(\hat k\) component zero?

Solution

The problem is entirely in the \(x\)-\(y\) plane, so there's no force component along the \(z\)-axis, it's explicitly zero (or often simply omitted).

Practice 16

Subtract \(\vec B=5\hat i+2\hat j\) from \(\vec A=9\hat i+8\hat j\).

Solution

\(\vec A-\vec B=4\hat i+6\hat j\).

4 Advanced: full equilibrium with support reactions

Combining components (Sections 1–3) with support reactions (roller = 1 unknown, pin = 2 unknowns, fixed = 2 + a moment) gives the full three-equation system used in every later beam and truss problem.

Worked example

A beam, pin at A, roller at B, length 4 m. A single 100 N force acts at 30° below horizontal (down-right), 2 m from A. Find all three reactions.

Resolve the applied force: \(F_x=100\cos30^\circ=86.6\text{ N}\) (right), \(F_y=-100\sin30^\circ=-50\text{ N}\) (down).

\(\Sigma F_x=0\): \(R_{Ax}+86.6=0 \Rightarrow R_{Ax}=-86.6\text{ N}\) (the pin actually pushes left).

\(\Sigma M_A=0\) (only the vertical component of the applied force creates a moment about A): \(R_B(4)-50(2)=0 \Rightarrow R_B=25\text{ N}\).

\(\Sigma F_y=0\): \(R_{Ay}+25-50=0 \Rightarrow R_{Ay}=25\text{ N}\).

Practice 17

For the worked example beam, verify \(R_{Ay}\) by taking moments about B instead of A.

Solution

\(\Sigma M_B\): the load sits \(4-2=2\text{ m}\) from B: \(R_{Ay}(4)-50(2)=0 \Rightarrow R_{Ay}=100/4=25\text{ N}\), matching ✓.

Practice 18

A beam, pin at A, roller at B, span 6 m, a single 150 N force at 45° below horizontal (down-right), acting 3 m from A. Resolve the force into components.

Solution

\(F_x=150\cos45^\circ=106.07\text{ N}\). \(F_y=-150\sin45^\circ=-106.07\text{ N}\).

Practice 19

For Practice 18's beam, find \(R_B\) using moments about A.

Solution

\(\Sigma M_A\): \(R_B(6)-106.07(3)=0 \Rightarrow R_B=318.21/6=53.03\text{ N}\).

Practice 20

Explain why the horizontal component of an angled applied force does NOT enter the moment equation about a point on the beam's own horizontal axis, only the vertical component does.

Solution

Since the beam lies along the horizontal axis, a horizontal force's line of action passes at the same height as the beam, zero perpendicular offset, so its moment arm about any point on that axis is zero. Only the vertical component, offset perpendicular to the beam's line, creates a rotational effect about a point on that axis.
