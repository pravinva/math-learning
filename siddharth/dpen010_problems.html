Topic 1 — Problem Set
Indices & Surds
Index laws with symbolic exponents, fractional and negative indices, surd simplification, rationalising with conjugates, and solving index equations.
5 Solved · 5 Practice · Medium and Hard
SOLVED
Worked Examples
Work on paper first, then compare.
1
MEDIUMSOLVED
Simplify \(\dfrac{x^{3/2} \cdot x^{-1/2}}{x}\). Express with a positive exponent.
▶
SOLUTION
1
Numerator: \(x^{3/2} \cdot x^{-1/2} = x^{3/2 + (-1/2)} = x^1 = x\)
2
Divide: \(\dfrac{x}{x} = x^{1-1} = x^0 = 1\)
ANSWER 1
Pattern: same base → add exponents when multiplying, subtract when dividing. Work one operation at a time.
2
MEDIUMSOLVED
Simplify \(\dfrac{(a^2 b^3)^{1/2}}{a^{1/2}\, b}\). Leave no negative or fractional exponents.
▶
SOLUTION
1
Expand numerator: \((a^2 b^3)^{1/2} = a^{2 \cdot 1/2}\, b^{3 \cdot 1/2} = a\, b^{3/2}\)
2
Divide: \(\dfrac{a\, b^{3/2}}{a^{1/2}\, b} = a^{1-1/2}\, b^{3/2-1} = a^{1/2}\, b^{1/2}\)
3
Write as a single surd: \((ab)^{1/2} = \sqrt{ab}\)
ANSWER \(\sqrt{ab}\)
3
MEDIUMSOLVED
Rationalise \(\dfrac{3}{\sqrt{a}+\sqrt{b}}\). Assume \(a \neq b\).
▶
SOLUTION
1
Multiply by the conjugate: \(\dfrac{3}{\sqrt{a}+\sqrt{b}} \times \dfrac{\sqrt{a}-\sqrt{b}}{\sqrt{a}-\sqrt{b}}\)
2
Denominator (difference of squares): \((\sqrt{a})^2 - (\sqrt{b})^2 = a - b\)
3
Numerator: \(3(\sqrt{a}-\sqrt{b})\)
ANSWER \(\dfrac{3(\sqrt{a}-\sqrt{b})}{a-b}\)
Key: the conjugate of \((\sqrt{a}+\sqrt{b})\) is \((\sqrt{a}-\sqrt{b})\). Their product always eliminates surds via difference of squares.
4
HARDSOLVED
Simplify \(\dfrac{x^n \cdot x^{2n}}{x^{3n-1}}\). Express the result in the simplest index form.
▶
SOLUTION
1
Numerator: \(x^n \cdot x^{2n} = x^{n+2n} = x^{3n}\)
2
Divide: \(\dfrac{x^{3n}}{x^{3n-1}} = x^{3n-(3n-1)} = x^1 = x\)
ANSWER \(x\) (for all values of \(n\))
The symbolic exponent \(n\) cancels completely — the result is independent of \(n\). This kind of telescoping is common in exam problems.
5
HARDSOLVED
Solve for \(x\): \(4^{x+1} = 8^{x-1}\)
Write both sides with base 2.
▶
SOLUTION
1
Write in base 2: \(4 = 2^2\) and \(8 = 2^3\).
2
LHS: \((2^2)^{x+1} = 2^{2(x+1)} = 2^{2x+2}\)
3
RHS: \((2^3)^{x-1} = 2^{3(x-1)} = 2^{3x-3}\)
4
Equal bases → equal exponents: \(2x+2 = 3x-3 \Rightarrow x = 5\)
ANSWER \(x = 5\)

MEDIUMPRACTICE
Simplify \(2\sqrt{75} - 3\sqrt{48} + \sqrt{12}\).
Find the largest perfect square factor of each radicand.
▶
SOLUTION

\(\sqrt{75}=5\sqrt{3},\quad \sqrt{48}=4\sqrt{3},\quad \sqrt{12}=2\sqrt{3}\)
2
\(2(5\sqrt{3}) - 3(4\sqrt{3}) + 2\sqrt{3} = 10\sqrt{3} - 12\sqrt{3} + 2\sqrt{3} = 0\)
ANSWER \(0\)
8
MEDIUMPRACTICE
Rationalise \(\dfrac{\sqrt{x+h}-\sqrt{x}}{h}\) (assume \(h \neq 0\)). This expression appears in differentiation from first principles.
▶
SOLUTION
1
Multiply by conjugate: \(\dfrac{\sqrt{x+h}-\sqrt{x}}{h} \cdot \dfrac{\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}}{\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}}\)
2
Numerator: \((x+h) - x = h\). Denominator: \(h(\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x})\)
3
Cancel \(h\): \(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}}\)
ANSWER \(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{x+h}+\sqrt{x}}\)
As \(h \to 0\), this approaches \(\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}\) — the derivative of \(\sqrt{x}\). Rationalising is the key step in many first-principles proofs.
9
HARDPRACTICE
If \(a^m = b\) and \(a^n = c\), express \(a^{2m-3n}\) in terms of \(b\) and \(c\) only.
▶
SOLUTION
1
\(a^{2m-3n} = a^{2m} \cdot a^{-3n} = (a^m)^2 \cdot (a^n)^{-3}\)
2
Substitute: \(= b^2 \cdot c^{-3} = \dfrac{b^2}{c^3}\)
ANSWER \(\dfrac{b^2}{c^3}\)
10
HARDPRACTICE
Solve \(9^x = 27^{x-1}\) for \(x\).
Write both sides as powers of 3.
▶
SOLUTION
1
\(9=3^2,\quad 27=3^3\). So: \((3^2)^x = (3^3)^{x-1} \Rightarrow 3^{2x} = 3^{3x-3}\)
2
Equal exponents: \(2x = 3x-3 \Rightarrow x = 3\)
ANSWER \(x = 3\)
Topic 2 — Problem Set
Logarithms
Problems with symbolic parameters — expanding log expressions, solving exponential equations with substitution, proving identities, and working with general bases.
5 Solved · 5 Practice · Medium and Hard
SOLVED
Worked Examples
Work on paper first.
1
MEDIUMSOLVED
Simplify \(\log_a(a^3) + \log_a\!\left(\dfrac{1}{a}\right)\).
▶
SOLUTION
1
\(\log_a(a^3) = 3\) (power law: exponent comes down, log base cancels)
2
\(\log_a\!\left(\tfrac{1}{a}\right) = \log_a(a^{-1}) = -1\)
3
Sum: \(3 + (-1) = 2\)
ANSWER 2
2
MEDIUMSOLVED
If \(\log_2 p = a\) and \(\log_2 q = b\), express \(\log_2\!\left(\dfrac{8p^2}{\sqrt{q}}\right)\) in terms of \(a\) and \(b\).
▶
SOLUTION
1
Split: \(\log_2 8 + \log_2 p^2 - \log_2 q^{1/2}\)
2
\(\log_2 8 = 3\) (since \(2^3=8\)), \(\log_2 p^2 = 2a\), \(\log_2 q^{1/2} = \tfrac{1}{2}b\)
3
Result: \(3 + 2a - \tfrac{b}{2}\)
ANSWER \(3 + 2a - \dfrac{b}{2}\)
3
MEDIUMSOLVED
Solve \(2^{2x} - 5 \cdot 2^x + 4 = 0\).
Let \(u = 2^x\) to create a quadratic.
▶
SOLUTION
1
Let \(u = 2^x\). Note \(2^{2x} = (2^x)^2 = u^2\). Equation becomes: \(u^2 - 5u + 4 = 0\)
2
Factor: \((u-1)(u-4) = 0\), so \(u=1\) or \(u=4\).
3
Back-substitute: \(2^x = 1 \Rightarrow x = 0\); \(2^x = 4 = 2^2 \Rightarrow x = 2\)
ANSWER \(x = 0\) or \(x = 2\)
Substitution \(u = a^x\) converts exponential equations into polynomials. A very common exam technique.
4
HARDSOLVED
Prove that \(\log_a x = \dfrac{1}{\log_x a}\).
▶
SOLUTION
1
Apply the change of base formula with base \(x\): \(\log_a x = \dfrac{\log_x x}{\log_x a}\)
2
Since \(\log_x x = 1\): \(\log_a x = \dfrac{1}{\log_x a}\) ∎
KEY IDEA Change of base to an intermediate base; numerator becomes 1 since \(\log_b b = 1\).
5
HARDSOLVED
Solve \(3^{x+2} = 7^{x-1}\). Give an exact answer and a decimal approximation.
▶
SOLUTION
1
Take \(\ln\) of both sides: \((x+2)\ln 3 = (x-1)\ln 7\)
2
Expand: \(x\ln 3 + 2\ln 3 = x\ln 7 - \ln 7\)
3
Collect \(x\) terms: \(x(\ln 3 - \ln 7) = -\ln 7 - 2\ln 3\)
4
\(x = \dfrac{-\ln 7 - 2\ln 3}{\ln 3 - \ln 7} = \dfrac{\ln 7 + 2\ln 3}{\ln 7 - \ln 3} = \dfrac{\ln(9 \cdot 7)}{\ln(7/3)} = \dfrac{\ln 63}{\ln(7/3)} \approx \dfrac{4.143}{0.847} \approx 4.89\)
ANSWER \(x = \dfrac{\ln 63}{\ln(7/3)} \approx 4.89\)

Write \(\log\!\left(\dfrac{x^2\sqrt{y}}{z^3}\right)\) as a sum/difference of separate logs.

Quotient law: \(\log(x^2\sqrt{y}) - \log(z^3)\)
2
Product law: \(\log x^2 + \log y^{1/2} - \log z^3\)
3
Power law: \(2\log x + \tfrac{1}{2}\log y - 3\log z\)
ANSWER \(2\log x + \tfrac{1}{2}\log y - 3\log z\)
8
MEDIUMPRACTICE
Solve \(e^{2x} - 3e^x - 10 = 0\). Give exact answers.
Same substitution technique as Solved Q3 — let \(u = e^x\).
▶
SOLUTION
1
Let \(u = e^x\): \(u^2 - 3u - 10 = 0 \Rightarrow (u-5)(u+2) = 0\)
2
\(u = 5\) or \(u = -2\). Since \(e^x > 0\) always, \(u = -2\) is rejected.
3
\(e^x = 5 \Rightarrow x = \ln 5\)

9
HARDPRACTICE
Prove the chain rule for logs: \(\log_a x \cdot \log_x b = \log_a b\).
Apply the change of base formula to each log, using the same intermediate base.
▶
SOLUTION
1
Change both logs to base \(a\): \(\log_a x = \dfrac{\ln x}{\ln a}\) and \(\log_x b = \dfrac{\ln b}{\ln x}\)
2
Multiply: \(\dfrac{\ln x}{\ln a} \cdot \dfrac{\ln b}{\ln x} = \dfrac{\ln b}{\ln a} = \log_a b\) ∎
KEY STEP The \(\ln x\) cancels — the intermediate base disappears completely.
10
HARDPRACTICE

Factor 72 into powers of 2 and 3.
▶
SOLUTION

\(72 = 8 \times 9 = 2^3 \times 3^2\)
2
\(\log_b 72 = \log_b(2^3 \cdot 3^2) = 3\log_b 2 + 2\log_b 3 = 3p + 2q\)
ANSWER \(3p + 2q\)
Topic 3 — Problem Set
Polynomials
Remainder and factor theorems with general parameters, quadratic discriminant analysis, factoring cubics, and working with roots symbolically.
5 Solved · 5 Practice · Medium and Hard
SOLVED
Worked Examples
Work on paper first.
1
MEDIUMSOLVED
Find \(k\) such that \((x-2)\) is a factor of \(P(x) = x^3 - kx^2 + 3x - 2\).
▶
SOLUTION
1
By Factor Theorem, \((x-2)\) is a factor \(\Leftrightarrow P(2) = 0\).
2
\(P(2) = 8 - 4k + 6 - 2 = 12 - 4k = 0 \Rightarrow k = 3\)
ANSWER \(k = 3\)
2
MEDIUMSOLVED
For \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\), show that the sum of the roots is \(-b/a\) and the product is \(c/a\).
▶
SOLUTION
1
Let roots be \(\alpha, \beta\). Then \(ax^2+bx+c = a(x-\alpha)(x-\beta)\).
2
Expand: \(a(x-\alpha)(x-\beta) = a[x^2 - (\alpha+\beta)x + \alpha\beta]\)
3
Matching coefficients: \(b = -a(\alpha+\beta) \Rightarrow \alpha+\beta = -\dfrac{b}{a}\) and \(c = a\alpha\beta \Rightarrow \alpha\beta = \dfrac{c}{a}\) ∎
RESULT Sum \(= -b/a\), Product \(= c/a\) — Vieta's formulas.
3
MEDIUMSOLVED
Find values of \(m\) for which \(x^2 + mx + (m+3) = 0\) has two distinct real roots.
▶
SOLUTION
1
Two distinct real roots \(\Leftrightarrow \Delta > 0\). Here \(\Delta = m^2 - 4(m+3) = m^2 - 4m - 12\).
2
Solve \(m^2 - 4m - 12 > 0\). Factor: \((m-6)(m+2) > 0\)
3
Parabola opens up, so: \(m < -2\) or \(m > 6\).
ANSWER \(m \in (-\infty,-2) \cup (6,\infty)\)
4
HARDSOLVED
Factor \(P(x) = 2x^3 - x^2 - 5x - 2\) completely.
▶
SOLUTION
1
Test rational roots: try \(x = -\frac{1}{2}\): \(2(-\frac{1}{8}) - \frac{1}{4} + \frac{5}{2} - 2 = -\frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{4} + \frac{10}{4} - \frac{8}{4} = \frac{0}{4} = 0\) ✓
2
So \((2x+1)\) is a factor. Divide: \(P(x) = (2x+1)(x^2 - x - 2)\)
3
Factor quadratic: \(x^2 - x - 2 = (x-2)(x+1)\)
ANSWER \((2x+1)(x-2)(x+1)\), roots at \(x = -\frac{1}{2}, 2, -1\)
5
HARDSOLVED
The roots of \(x^3 - px^2 + qx - r = 0\) are \(\alpha, \beta, \gamma\). Find \(\alpha+\beta+\gamma\), \(\alpha\beta+\beta\gamma+\gamma\alpha\), and \(\alpha\beta\gamma\) in terms of \(p,q,r\).
▶
SOLUTION
1
Expand \((x-\alpha)(x-\beta)(x-\gamma)\) and match coefficients with \(x^3 - px^2 + qx - r\).
2
\(x^2\) coefficient: \(-(\alpha+\beta+\gamma) = -p \Rightarrow \alpha+\beta+\gamma = p\)
3
\(x\) coefficient: \(\alpha\beta+\beta\gamma+\gamma\alpha = q\)
4
Constant: \(-\alpha\beta\gamma = -r \Rightarrow \alpha\beta\gamma = r\)
ANSWER Sum \(=p\), pair-products \(=q\), triple-product \(=r\) — Vieta's formulas for cubics.

HARDPRACTICE
Given \(\alpha, \beta\) are roots of \(x^2 - 3x + 1 = 0\), find \(\alpha^2 + \beta^2\) and \(\alpha^3 + \beta^3\) without finding \(\alpha\) or \(\beta\) individually.
Use Vieta's formulas: \(\alpha+\beta=3\), \(\alpha\beta=1\). Then use algebraic identities.
▶
SOLUTION

\(\alpha+\beta = 3\), \(\alpha\beta = 1\) (Vieta's).

\(\alpha^2+\beta^2 = (\alpha+\beta)^2 - 2\alpha\beta = 9 - 2 = 7\)

10
HARDPRACTICE
For what values of \(k\) does \(x^2 + kx + 4 = 0\) have: (a) two equal roots, (b) no real roots?
▶
SOLUTION
1
\(\Delta = k^2 - 16\)
2
(a) Equal roots: \(\Delta = 0 \Rightarrow k^2 = 16 \Rightarrow k = \pm 4\)
3
(b) No real roots: \(\Delta < 0 \Rightarrow k^2 < 16 \Rightarrow -4 < k < 4\)

Topic 4 — Problem Set
Sequences & Series
Finding unknown parameters from given terms, general-variable sum formulas, infinite series, and classic two-sequence problems.
5 Solved · 5 Practice · Medium and Hard
SOLVED
Worked Examples
Work on paper first.
1
MEDIUMSOLVED
Show that \(T_n = 3n-1\) is arithmetic, and find \(S_n\) (sum of first \(n\) terms) as a formula in \(n\).
▶
SOLUTION
1
\(T_{n+1} - T_n = [3(n+1)-1] - [3n-1] = 3\) — constant difference, so arithmetic with \(d=3\).
2
\(a = T_1 = 2\). Sum formula: \(S_n = \dfrac{n}{2}[2a + (n-1)d] = \dfrac{n}{2}[4 + 3(n-1)] = \dfrac{n}{2}(3n+1)\)
ANSWER \(S_n = \dfrac{n(3n+1)}{2}\)
2
MEDIUMSOLVED
Find the sum to infinity of \(1 + x + x^2 + x^3 + \cdots\) and state the values of \(x\) for which this is valid.
▶
SOLUTION
1
Geometric series with \(a = 1\), \(r = x\). Converges only when \(|x| < 1\).
2
\(S_\infty = \dfrac{a}{1-r} = \dfrac{1}{1-x}\)
ANSWER \(S_\infty = \dfrac{1}{1-x}\) for \(|x| < 1\)
This is one of the most useful series in mathematics — it appears in calculus, probability, and signal processing.
3
MEDIUMSOLVED
In a geometric sequence, \(T_2 + T_3 = 6\) and \(T_4 + T_5 = 54\). Find \(a\) and \(r\).
▶
SOLUTION
1
\(T_2+T_3 = ar+ar^2 = ar(1+r) = 6\)
2
\(T_4+T_5 = ar^3+ar^4 = ar^3(1+r) = 54\)
3
Divide equation 2 by equation 1: \(\dfrac{ar^3(1+r)}{ar(1+r)} = r^2 = \dfrac{54}{6} = 9 \Rightarrow r = 3\)
4
Back-substitute: \(3a(1+3) = 6 \Rightarrow 12a = 6 \Rightarrow a = \dfrac{1}{2}\)
ANSWER \(a = \dfrac{1}{2},\ r = 3\)
4
HARDSOLVED
The sum of \(n\) terms of a series is given by \(S_n = 3n^2 + 5n\). Find the first term, common difference, and general term \(T_n\).
▶
SOLUTION
1
\(T_n = S_n - S_{n-1} = (3n^2+5n) - [3(n-1)^2+5(n-1)]\)
2
\(= 3n^2+5n - [3n^2-6n+3+5n-5] = 6n+2\)
3
\(T_1 = 8\), \(T_2 = 14\), common difference \(d = 6\). Check: \(S_1 = T_1 = 8 = 3+5\) ✓
ANSWER \(T_n = 6n+2\), first term \(= 8\), \(d = 6\)
5
HARDSOLVED
Express the repeating decimal \(0.\overline{27} = 0.272727\ldots\) as a fraction using the infinite geometric series formula.
▶
SOLUTION
1
\(0.\overline{27} = \dfrac{27}{100} + \dfrac{27}{10000} + \cdots\) — geometric series with \(a = \dfrac{27}{100}\), \(r = \dfrac{1}{100}\)
2
\(S_\infty = \dfrac{27/100}{1 - 1/100} = \dfrac{27/100}{99/100} = \dfrac{27}{99} = \dfrac{3}{11}\)
ANSWER \(\dfrac{3}{11}\)

MEDIUMPRACTICE
Find the value of \(x\) for which \(x,\ x+3,\ x+9\) form a geometric sequence.

▶
SOLUTION
1
\(\dfrac{x+3}{x} = \dfrac{x+9}{x+3} \Rightarrow (x+3)^2 = x(x+9)\)
2
\(x^2 + 6x + 9 = x^2 + 9x \Rightarrow 9 = 3x \Rightarrow x = 3\)
3
Sequence: 3, 6, 12 (ratio 2) ✓
ANSWER \(x = 3\)
8
MEDIUMPRACTICE
Three numbers form an arithmetic sequence with sum 15 and product 80. Find the three numbers.
Let the three terms be \(a-d, a, a+d\). The sum simplifies immediately.
▶
SOLUTION
1
Let terms be \(a-d, a, a+d\). Sum: \(3a = 15 \Rightarrow a = 5\)
2
Product: \((5-d)(5)(5+d) = 80 \Rightarrow 5(25-d^2) = 80 \Rightarrow 25-d^2 = 16 \Rightarrow d^2 = 9 \Rightarrow d = \pm 3\)
3
Numbers: 2, 5, 8 (or 8, 5, 2)
ANSWER 2, 5, 8
9
HARDPRACTICE
An arithmetic series has first term \(a\) and common difference \(d\). Prove that \(S_n = \dfrac{n}{2}(2a+(n-1)d)\) by pairing the first and last terms.
▶
SOLUTION
1
Write \(S_n\) forwards: \(S_n = a + (a+d) + \cdots + (a+(n-1)d)\)
2
Write \(S_n\) backwards: \(S_n = (a+(n-1)d) + \cdots + (a+d) + a\)
3
Add: \(2S_n = n[a + a+(n-1)d] = n[2a+(n-1)d]\), so \(S_n = \dfrac{n}{2}[2a+(n-1)d]\) ∎
METHOD Pairing opposite terms — each pair sums to \(2a+(n-1)d\), and there are \(n\) such pairs (each counted twice).
10
HARDPRACTICE
Find the smallest \(n\) such that the sum of the geometric series \(2 + 6 + 18 + \cdots\) exceeds 1000.
▶
SOLUTION
1
\(S_n = 3^n - 1 > 1000 \Rightarrow 3^n > 1001\)
2
Take logs: \(n > \dfrac{\ln 1001}{\ln 3} = \dfrac{6.909}{1.099} \approx 6.29\)
3
Since \(n\) must be a positive integer: \(n = 7\). Check: \(S_7 = 3^7 - 1 = 2187 - 1 = 2186 > 1000\) ✓
ANSWER \(n = 7\)
Topic 5 — Problem Set
Coordinate Geometry
General-variable problems — perpendicular bisectors, tangent conditions, circle equations from geometric conditions, and area formulas.
5 Solved · 5 Practice · Medium and Hard
SOLVED
Worked Examples
Work on paper first.
1
MEDIUMSOLVED
Find the equation of the perpendicular bisector of the segment joining \(A(1,3)\) and \(B(5,-1)\).
▶
SOLUTION
1
Midpoint: \(M = (3, 1)\).
2
Gradient of AB: \(m = \dfrac{-1-3}{5-1} = -1\). Perpendicular gradient: \(1\).
3
Equation through \(M(3,1)\) with gradient 1: \(y-1 = 1(x-3) \Rightarrow y = x-2\)
ANSWER \(y = x - 2\)
2
MEDIUMSOLVED
Show that for any \(a, b\) (not both zero), the points \(O(0,0)\), \(A(a,b)\), \(B(-b,a)\) form a right angle at \(O\).
▶

HARDSOLVED
Find the area between \(y = x^2\) and \(y = x + 2\).
▶
SOLUTION
1
Intersections: \(x^2 = x+2 \Rightarrow x^2-x-2=0 \Rightarrow (x-2)(x+1)=0 \Rightarrow x = -1, 2\)
2
On \([-1,2]\), the line \(y=x+2\) is above the parabola \(y=x^2\).
3
Area \(= \displaystyle\int_{-1}^{2}[(x+2)-x^2]\,dx = \left[\dfrac{x^2}{2}+2x-\dfrac{x^3}{3}\right]_{-1}^{2} = (2+4-\tfrac{8}{3}) - (\tfrac{1}{2}-2+\tfrac{1}{3}) = \dfrac{10}{3} - (-\tfrac{7}{6}) = \dfrac{10}{3}+\dfrac{7}{6} = \dfrac{27}{6} = \dfrac{9}{2}\)
ANSWER \(\dfrac{9}{2}\) square units

HARDSOLVED
Find the total area between \(y = \sin x\) and the \(x\)-axis for \(x \in [0, 2\pi]\). Explain why you cannot simply evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}\sin x\,dx\).
▶
SOLUTION
1
\(\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}\sin x\,dx = [-\cos x]_0^{2\pi} = -1-(-1) = 0\). This gives 0, not the area — because the positive arch \([0,\pi]\) and negative arch \([\pi,2\pi]\) cancel.
2
Correct approach: \(\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi}\sin x\,dx + \left|\int_{\pi}^{2\pi}\sin x\,dx\right|\)
3
\(\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi}\sin x\,dx = 2\) and \(\displaystyle\int_{\pi}^{2\pi}\sin x\,dx = -2\). Total area \(= 2 + 2 = 4\).
ANSWER Total area = 4 square units (not 0)
