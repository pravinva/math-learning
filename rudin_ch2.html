Chapter 2: Positive Borel Measures

A guided tour of the most notoriously dense chapter in Rudin — with full hand-holding included

Chapter 2 of Papa Rudin is where many first-year graduate students hit a wall. The goal is the Riesz Representation Theorem — a deep, beautiful result — but Rudin proves it in 12 pages of airtight prose with minimal motivation. Students get lost in a forest of σ-algebras and set constructions without a map.

This guide gives you the map first. We build every concept from intuition, then meet the formalism, then see exactly what Rudin is doing and why.

The punchline (spoiler)

Any reasonable "integration machine" on a nice space is secretly just integration against a measure. The theorem finds that measure — and proves it's unique.

Here's the journey: topology → measurable sets → the theorem statement → the proof construction → regularity → Lebesgue measure as the payoff.

Why do we need Borel measures?

The story starts with a problem: the Riemann integral you learned in calculus breaks for functions analysts care about.

Example — the Dirichlet function

Define f: [0,1] \to \mathbb{R} by f(x) = 1 if x \in \mathbb{Q} and f(x) = 0 if x \notin \mathbb{Q}.

Red dots: rationals (f = 1), scattered densely. Baseline: irrationals (f = 0). Every subinterval contains both — upper Riemann sums are always 1, lower sums always 0. The Riemann integral does not exist.

Yet intuitively this function should integrate to 0 — the rationals are "tiny." There are countably many rationals but uncountably many irrationals. The irrationals dominate completely.

Lebesgue's insight: instead of slicing the domain into intervals, slice the range, and ask how large the set is where f \ge t for each threshold t. To do that, we need a way to assign "size" to sets. That's a measure.

The key question Chapter 2 answers

Where do measures come from? Answer: from integration machines (positive linear functionals). The Riesz theorem says every reasonable one comes from a unique measure.

Topology primer: the stage we work on

Rudin's theorem applies to locally compact Hausdorff (LCH) spaces. Understand these and the proof becomes navigable.

Hausdorff spaces

Definition — Hausdorff

A topological space X is Hausdorff if distinct points can be separated by disjoint open sets: for x \ne y, there exist open sets U \ni x and V \ni y with U \cap V = \emptyset.

This is a sanity condition. Every metric space is Hausdorff. The real line, \mathbb{R}^n, any manifold — all Hausdorff. It just means points can be told apart by open sets.

Compact sets

Definition — compact

A set K \subset X is compact if every open cover of K has a finite subcover. In metric spaces, equivalently: every sequence in K has a subsequence converging to a point in K.

Think: "finite in spirit." On \mathbb{R}^n, compact = closed and bounded (Heine–Borel). The interval [0,1] is compact; the real line is not. Compact sets are crucial because continuous functions on them are bounded and integrals over them are finite.

Locally compact spaces

Definition — locally compact

A Hausdorff space X is locally compact if every point has a compact neighborhood: for each x \in X, there exists open U \ni x with \overline{U} compact.

\mathbb{R}^n is locally compact — a small ball around any point has compact closure. But the space of all continuous functions with the sup norm is not locally compact (the unit ball is not compact in infinite dimensions).

Key examples for the theorem

\mathbb{R}, \mathbb{R}^n, any open subset of \mathbb{R}^n, any compact Hausdorff space — all are LCH. These are the spaces Chapter 2 covers.

Why exactly does the proof need LCH?

Urysohn's lemma: In an LCH space, given compact K and open V \supset K, there exists a continuous function f with f = 1 on K, 0 \le f \le 1, and \text{supp}(f) \subset V. This lets us "probe" open sets using functions — which is how we define the measure.

Finite inner approximation: Local compactness means compact subsets fill up any open set from inside, so inner regularity works. Without it, the construction in Step 3 of the proof fails.

σ-Algebras and Borel sets

A measure can't meaningfully assign sizes to every subset (Banach–Tarski shows this leads to paradoxes). We designate a collection of "measurable" sets using a σ-algebra.

Definition — σ-algebra

A collection \mathfrak{M} of subsets of X is a σ-algebra if: (1) X \in \mathfrak{M}, (2) A \in \mathfrak{M} \Rightarrow A^c \in \mathfrak{M}, and (3) if A_1, A_2, \ldots \in \mathfrak{M} then \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty A_n \in \mathfrak{M}.

Think of a σ-algebra as "the universe of sets we've agreed to measure." If we can measure a set, we can measure its complement. If we can measure each piece, we can measure the countable union.

The Borel σ-algebra

Definition — Borel sets

The Borel σ-algebra \mathfrak{B}(X) is the smallest σ-algebra on X containing all open sets. Its members are Borel sets.

Borel sets are everything you can build from open sets using complements and countable unions/intersections. The hierarchy on \mathbb{R}:

Is every subset of ℝ a Borel set?

No — there are subsets of \mathbb{R} that are not Borel. But constructing such sets requires the axiom of choice in an essential way. Every set you can write down explicitly is almost certainly a Borel set. For the purposes of analysis, Borel sets cover everything you'll ever need to measure.

Cc(X) and positive linear functionals

Now the setup. Chapter 2 starts not with a measure, but with a linear functional — an integration-like operation on functions — and derives the measure from it.

Definition — Cc(X)

C_c(X) is the vector space of all continuous functions f: X \to \mathbb{R} whose support \text{supp}(f) = \overline{\{x : f(x) \ne 0\}} is compact. These are continuous functions that vanish outside some bounded region.

On \mathbb{R}: functions in C_c(\mathbb{R}) are continuous and zero outside some interval [-M, M]. Smooth bump functions are the canonical example.

Two key notations

Rudin uses two shorthand symbols that appear constantly in the proof. Learn these — they're the grammar of the argument.

Notation (crucial)

f \prec V ("f is below V"): means f \in C_c(X), 0 \le f \le 1, \text{supp}(f) \subset V. The function lives inside the open set V.

K \prec f ("K is below f"): means f \in C_c(X), 0 \le f \le 1, f \equiv 1 on K. The function is at least 1 on the compact set K.

These are the "probes." We can't ask the functional \Lambda directly about sets, but we can use f \prec V to probe an open set from inside, and K \prec f to probe a compact set from above.

Positive linear functionals

Definition — positive linear functional

\Lambda: C_c(X) \to \mathbb{R} is a positive linear functional if it is linear (\Lambda(af+bg) = a\Lambda f + b\Lambda g) and positive (f \ge 0 \Rightarrow \Lambda f \ge 0).

Three examples

Standard integral: \Lambda f = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\, dx. This gives Lebesgue measure — see Section 8.

Point evaluation: \Lambda f = f(x_0) for a fixed x_0. This gives the Dirac delta measure \delta_{x_0}.

Weighted integral: \Lambda f = \int f(x)\, w(x)\, dx for a non-negative function w. This gives the measure d\mu = w\, dx.

The Riesz Representation Theorem

Here it is. The destination of all of Chapter 2.

Theorem — Riesz Representation (Rudin, Theorem 2.14)

Let X be a locally compact Hausdorff space, and let \Lambda be a positive linear functional on C_c(X).

Then there exists a unique positive Borel measure \mu, defined on a σ-algebra \mathfrak{M} \supset \mathfrak{B}(X), such that:

\Lambda f = \int_X f\, d\mu \quad \text{for all } f \in C_c(X)

Moreover, \mu is outer regular on all E \in \mathfrak{M}, inner regular on all open sets and all sets with finite measure, and compact sets have finite measure.

What this actually says

You are handed just a functional — a rule that eats continuous compactly-supported functions and outputs numbers, satisfying linearity and positivity. No measure, no σ-algebra, nothing else given to you.

The theorem says: buried inside your functional is a unique measure. The theorem excavates it. Every positive linear functional on C_c(X) is, deep down, integration against some measure.

Why the proof is hard

Rudin must simultaneously construct the σ-algebra and the measure from scratch, using only \Lambda. He defines \mu on open sets first, extends to all sets, identifies which sets are "measurable," verifies this is a σ-algebra and \mu is a measure, then verifies \Lambda f = \int f\, d\mu. Each step depends on the previous. This is what takes 12 pages.

The proof — step by step

Five stages. Each builds on the previous. Walk through at your own pace.

Regularity

The measure produced by the Riesz theorem is regular — it respects the topology of the space. This is what makes it unique and well-behaved.

Definition — outer regular

A measure \mu is outer regular on E if:

\mu(E) = \inf\{\mu(V) : E \subset V,\, V \text{ open}\}

You can squeeze down to E from outside with open sets.

Definition — inner regular

A measure \mu is inner regular on E if:

\mu(E) = \sup\{\mu(K) : K \subset E,\, K \text{ compact}\}

You can fill E up from inside with compact sets, and their measures converge to \mu(E).

Concrete intuition on ℝ

Outer regularity: For any measurable E \subset \mathbb{R}, find an open set slightly bigger with measure at most \mu(E) + \varepsilon. Think: cover E with an open set by adding tiny intervals around each boundary point.

Inner regularity: For measurable E with finite measure, there's a compact (closed and bounded) subset with measure at least \mu(E) - \varepsilon.

Why does regularity imply uniqueness?

Suppose \mu_1 and \mu_2 both satisfy \Lambda f = \int f\, d\mu_i and are both regular. For any open V: \mu_i(V) = \sup\{\Lambda f : f \prec V\}, so \mu_1(V) = \mu_2(V) for all open V. By outer regularity, \mu_1(E) = \inf \mu_1(V) = \inf \mu_2(V) = \mu_2(E) for all measurable E. So \mu_1 = \mu_2.

The payoff: Lebesgue measure

The Riesz Representation Theorem is not abstract — it literally constructs Lebesgue measure as a special case.

Lebesgue measure from the theorem

Take X = \mathbb{R}^n. Define the positive linear functional:

\Lambda f = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} f(x_1, \ldots, x_n)\, dx_1 \cdots dx_n

where the right side is the ordinary Riemann integral (well-defined since f \in C_c(\mathbb{R}^n)). The Riesz theorem gives a unique regular Borel measure m on \mathbb{R}^n with \Lambda f = \int f\, dm. This m is Lebesgue measure.

We defined \Lambda using a Riemann integral on nice functions only. The theorem extended it to a full σ-algebra and measure — giving us Lebesgue measure on all Borel sets, including the Cantor set, the rationals, and every other set you'd want to measure.

Properties that fall out automatically

m([a,b]) = b-a — intervals have their expected length

m(\{x\}) = 0 — single points have measure zero

m(\mathbb{Q}) = 0 — the rationals have measure zero (so the Dirichlet function integrates to 0!)

m(E+t) = m(E) — translation invariance

m is the unique translation-invariant regular Borel measure with m([0,1]) = 1

The full picture

Chapter 3 uses this Lebesgue measure to build the L^p spaces. Chapter 4 uses L^p to prove foundational Hilbert space results. The entire rest of Rudin rests on what Chapter 2 built.
