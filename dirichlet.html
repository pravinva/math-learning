The Dirichlet Distribution

A probability distribution over probability distributions. From dice and paint to Bayesian inference, topic models, and infinite-dimensional measure theory.

Bayesian Statistics Machine Learning NLP / LDA Information Theory
Why should you care?

The Core Problem

Imagine you're asked: "What are the chances that the next customer buys Product A, B, or C?" You have some sales data, but not much. You need a probability for each product — and those probabilities must sum to 1.

Now here's the twist: you're not confident in your estimate. Maybe your data is thin. Maybe the market is shifting. You don't want a single fixed answer — you want to express a range of plausible probability vectors, some more likely than others.

The Dirichlet distribution is the answer. It is a distribution whose outcomes are themselves probability vectors. Instead of sampling a number, you sample an entire pie chart — a complete allocation of probability across categories.

This makes it foundational for:

🎲
Bayesian Inference
Express uncertainty about the "true" probability of outcomes before seeing data, then update cleanly after seeing it.
📰
Topic Modelling
Every document is a mixture of topics. Every topic is a mixture of words. Dirichlet governs both mixtures in LDA.
🧬
Population Genetics
Model allele frequency distributions across subpopulations with uncertainty baked in.
🤖
Generative AI
Dirichlet Processes are the backbone of infinite mixture models and nonparametric Bayesian learning.
Context & Lineage

A Brief History

1805–1859
Peter Gustav Lejeune Dirichlet — German mathematician, student of Gauss. Known for Dirichlet series, the pigeonhole principle, and foundational work in analytic number theory. The distribution bears his name by analogy: his mathematical spirit of rigorous decomposition onto simplices influenced those who formalised it later.
1900s–1950s
Beta distribution formalised as the conjugate prior for the Binomial. The Dirichlet is the natural multivariate generalisation of the Beta. Early use in genetics (Sewall Wright) to model allele frequencies.
1973
Ferguson (1973) defined the Dirichlet Process — an infinite-dimensional generalisation — enabling nonparametric Bayesian statistics. This paper is one of the most cited in all of statistics.
1980s–1990s
Widespread adoption in Bayesian statistics. The Dirichlet-Multinomial model becomes the workhorse for categorical data with uncertainty about category probabilities.
2003
Blei, Ng, Jordan publish Latent Dirichlet Allocation — LDA. One of the most influential ML papers of the 21st century. Dirichlet enters mainstream NLP, information retrieval, and later bioinformatics and recsys.
2010s–present
Dirichlet likelihoods appear in neural network training (label smoothing), variational autoencoders, Bayesian deep learning, and federated learning (heterogeneous data partitioning). Still actively researched.
Level 1 — Simple Terms

Ground Up: Thinking with Paint

Start with a simplex

Suppose you have 3 colours — red, blue, green — and you must paint a wall using all three. The constraint is that the fractions must sum to 100%. So (50%, 30%, 20%) is valid. (60%, 60%, 0%) is not.

All valid fractions form a triangle — called the 2-simplex. Every point inside this triangle is a valid colour allocation. The corners represent pure allocations (all red, all blue, all green).

💡
The Dirichlet distribution is just a way to assign a probability — a level of plausibility — to every point in this triangle. Some colour allocations seem more likely than others. Dirichlet tells you which.

The role of α (alpha)

The Dirichlet has one key parameter: a vector α = (α₁, α₂, α₃). Think of each αᵢ as a "pseudo-count" — as if you'd already seen that colour appear αᵢ times before any real data.

The concentration story

The sum α₀ = α₁ + α₂ + α₃ controls how concentrated (certain) your belief is:

α valuesEffectMetaphor
All αᵢ = 0.1Samples are extreme — almost all weight on one categorySparse, spiky
All αᵢ = 1Uniform — every allocation equally likelyIgnorance prior
All αᵢ = 10Samples cluster near the centre (equal fractions)Confident, smooth
α = (5, 1, 1)Red gets most weight most of the timeInformed prior
🔑
Key insight: When all αᵢ = 1, you have a completely flat (uniform) prior — you have no preference. When αᵢ > 1, you expect category i to appear more. When αᵢ < 1, you expect sparsity — only a few categories dominate at a time.
Level 2 — Medium Terms

The Mathematics, Made Accessible

The probability density function

The Dirichlet distribution over a K-category probability vector p = (p₁, …, pK) has this density:

Dir(p | α) = 1/B(α) · ∏ᵢ pᵢ^(αᵢ − 1)

where the normalising constant B(α) is the multivariate Beta function:
B(α) = ∏ᵢ Γ(αᵢ) / Γ(α₀) and α₀ = Σᵢ αᵢ

Subject to: p₁ + p₂ + … + pK = 1, all pᵢ ≥ 0
∏ᵢ pᵢ^(αᵢ − 1)The kernel. Each category gets a "reward" based on how much probability it receives. αᵢ − 1 controls whether the distribution favours or penalises that category getting mass.
Γ(αᵢ)The Gamma function — the continuous generalisation of factorial: Γ(n) = (n−1)! for integers. Makes the formula work for any positive real α.
1/B(α)The normalising constant — ensures the density integrates to 1 over the simplex. We rarely compute it explicitly; it cancels in Bayesian updates.
α₀ = ΣαᵢTotal concentration. Larger α₀ = sharper, more peaked distribution. Smaller = more diffuse. Controls effective "sample size" of the prior.

Connection to the Beta distribution

The Beta distribution is the special case with K = 2 categories. If p ~ Beta(α₁, α₂), that's the same as (p, 1−p) ~ Dir(α₁, α₂). The Beta is the Dirichlet on a line segment; the Dirichlet lives on higher-dimensional triangles (simplices).

Conjugate prior for the Multinomial

This is where the elegance lives. If you observe data from a Multinomial (K-sided die rolled N times, counts = c₁, …, cK), and your prior belief about the die's probabilities is Dirichlet(α), then after seeing the data your updated belief is:

Prior: p ~ Dir(α₁, …, αK)
Data: x ~ Multinomial(p), observed counts c = (c₁, …, cK)
Posterior: p | x ~ Dir(α₁+c₁, …, αK+cK)

→ Just add counts to α. That's it. No integrals needed.
✨
Conjugate prior means the prior and posterior have the same distributional form. The Dirichlet-Multinomial pair is the canonical example — updating your belief is as simple as adding observation counts to your pseudo-counts. This closure is why Dirichlet is so computationally convenient.

How to sample from a Dirichlet

Algorithm: Gamma trick (used in practice)
1. For each i = 1…K, sample: yᵢ ~ Gamma(αᵢ, 1)
2. Normalise: pᵢ = yᵢ / Σⱼ yⱼ
3. Return p = (p₁, …, pK) ← valid Dirichlet sample
Level 3 — Advanced

The Deep Structure

Geometric view: measure on the simplex

The (K−1)-simplex Δ^(K−1) = {p ∈ ℝᴷ : pᵢ ≥ 0, Σpᵢ = 1} is a (K−1)-dimensional manifold embedded in ℝᴷ. The Dirichlet is an absolutely continuous probability measure on this manifold with respect to the Lebesgue measure restricted to the simplex.

The parameter vector α encodes a Riemannian geometry on the simplex. The Fisher information metric of the Dirichlet family is:

g_{ij}(α) = ψ₁(αᵢ)δᵢⱼ − ψ₁(α₀)

where ψ₁ is the trigamma function (second derivative of log Γ).
This makes the Dirichlet family an exponential family with natural parameters (α₁−1, …, αK−1).

Exponential family form

Written in exponential family form with natural parameters η = α − 1:

p(x | η) = h(x) · exp( ηᵀT(x) − A(η) )

h(x) = 1 (base measure)
T(x) = (log x₁, …, log xK) (sufficient statistic)
A(η) = log B(η+1) (log-partition function)

The sufficient statistic being log xᵢ (not xᵢ itself) means the Dirichlet lives in log-space — it measures geometric averages, not arithmetic ones. This connects it to the theory of compositions and the Aitchison geometry of the simplex.

Aitchison geometry

The simplex with the Aitchison inner product forms a Hilbert space under centred log-ratio (clr) coordinates. The Dirichlet in this space is analogous to the Gaussian in Euclidean space — it's the maximum entropy distribution on the simplex subject to constraints on the expected log-ratios.

clr(p)ᵢ = log pᵢ − (1/K)Σⱼ log pⱼ

The Dirichlet is characterised by:
E[clr(p)ᵢ] = ψ(αᵢ) − ψ(α₀) (digamma difference)

KL divergence between Dirichlets

Closed-form KL divergence — critical for variational inference:

KL[ Dir(α) ‖ Dir(β) ] = log B(β)/B(α) + Σᵢ (αᵢ − βᵢ) · ψ(αᵢ) − ψ(α₀)

Latent Dirichlet Allocation — the generative model

For each document d in corpus:
θ_d ~ Dir(α) ← topic mixture for doc d

For each word position n in doc d:
z_dn ~ Categorical(θ_d) ← pick a topic
w_dn ~ Categorical(φ_{z_dn}) ← pick a word from that topic

For each topic k:
φ_k ~ Dir(β) ← word distribution for topic k

Inference is intractable exactly, so LDA uses variational EM (Blei et al.) or collapsed Gibbs sampling (Griffiths & Steyvers). Both exploit the Dirichlet-Multinomial conjugacy to marginalise out the discrete assignments analytically.

The logistic-normal alternative

The Dirichlet has no covariance structure between categories (correlation between pᵢ and pⱼ is always negative — increasing one must decrease others). The logistic-normal distribution, obtained by pushing a multivariate Gaussian through a softmax, allows arbitrary covariance. It is less analytically tractable but more expressive. This trade-off drives much modern research.

Abstraction & Higher Structures

The Generalisation Ladder

The Dirichlet is a node in a much larger web of mathematical structures. Here is the ascent:

1

Beta Distribution — K=2 case

Dir(α₁, α₂) on [0,1]. The simplest nontrivial case — models a single probability. Prior for a biased coin. All Beta intuitions generalise to Dirichlet.

2

Dirichlet Distribution — finite K categories

The K-category generalisation. Lives on the (K−1)-simplex. Conjugate prior for the Multinomial. The subject of this entire document.

3

Dirichlet-Multinomial (Polya Distribution)

The marginal distribution over counts after integrating out the Dirichlet-distributed probabilities. Exhibits overdispersion relative to Multinomial — captures clustering (Polya urn scheme: balls beget balls of the same colour).

4

Hierarchical Dirichlet — mixture of Dirichlets

Each component of an LDA topic model is itself a Dirichlet. α is no longer fixed but drawn from a higher-level prior. This nesting allows shared structure across groups (e.g., topics shared across document collections).

5

Dirichlet Process (DP) — K → ∞

Ferguson (1973): a distribution over infinite discrete probability measures. Written G ~ DP(α, G₀). G₀ is the base measure (where atoms are drawn from); α controls concentration. A DP sample is an almost-surely discrete measure — a countably infinite set of atoms with probabilities summing to 1. The "infinite dimensional Dirichlet."

6

Chinese Restaurant Process & Stick-Breaking

Two equivalent representations of the DP. Stick-breaking: pₖ = vₖ ∏ⱼ<ₖ (1−vⱼ), vₖ ~ Beta(1,α). The Chinese Restaurant Process gives the partition structure: customer n joins table k with prob proportional to table size, or opens a new table with prob ∝ α. Rich-get-richer dynamics (Polya urn at infinite scale).

7

Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP)

Teh et al. (2006). Shares atoms (topics) across groups. Each group's distribution is a DP drawn from a global DP. Used in topic modelling when the number of topics is unknown. The global DP defines the shared "menu"; each document draws its own distribution over that menu.

8

Pitman-Yor Process — power-law generalisation

PY(d, α, G₀) with discount parameter d ∈ [0,1). When d = 0, reduces to DP. When d > 0, produces power-law distributed cluster sizes (Zipf's law). Critical for natural language (word frequencies follow Zipf), used in language model smoothing (Kneser-Ney is an approximate PY process).

9

Completely Random Measures & Lévy Processes

The most general framework. A completely random measure (CRM) assigns independent random masses to disjoint sets. The DP is a CRM normalised to be a probability measure. Unnormalised CRMs (Gamma process, Beta process) underlie Indian Buffet Processes and feature allocation models. Connects Bayesian nonparametrics to Lévy process theory and infinite divisibility.

The Unifying Thread

At every level, the core idea is the same: represent uncertainty about a probability distribution itself, not just about outcomes. The Dirichlet is the simplest rigorous answer to "what distribution should I put over the simplex?" Every generalisation relaxes a constraint: infinite categories (DP), shared atoms across groups (HDP), power-law tails (PY), or full independence structure (CRMs). The mathematics always traces back to the Gamma function and the simplex.

Relation to Information Geometry

The exponential family of Dirichlet distributions, parameterised by α, forms a statistical manifold. The Fisher-Rao metric on this manifold is curved — the geometry is not flat Euclidean. Natural gradient descent (used in modern deep learning) corresponds to gradient descent in this curved geometry, underpinning why methods like Adam converge faster than vanilla SGD.

Maximum Entropy Characterisation

The Dirichlet Dir(α) is the maximum entropy distribution on the simplex subject to fixing:

E[log pᵢ] = ψ(αᵢ) − ψ(α₀) for all i

This connects the Dirichlet to the principle of maximum entropy (Jaynes) — it is the "most ignorant" distribution consistent with given constraints on expected log-probabilities. This is the precise sense in which Dir(1,…,1) expresses "total ignorance."
