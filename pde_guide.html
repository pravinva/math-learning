Why the Universe Speaks in Partial Derivatives

A journey through the most powerful language mathematics has ever discovered

There is a peculiar and profound fact about physical reality: the laws that govern it are almost universally expressed not as relationships between quantities themselves, but between rates of change in multiple directions simultaneously. Heat flows according to how temperature varies through space and time. Sound propagates according to how pressure varies through air. Light obeys equations about how electric and magnetic fields evolve. Even the fabric of spacetime — the geometric stage on which all physics plays out — is governed by a system of equations relating curvature to matter content. Every one of these is a partial differential equation.

This is not a coincidence. It reflects something deep about how nature operates: locally. A point of material does not "know" the global state of the universe — it only responds to what its immediate neighbourhood is doing. The mathematical encoding of this local causality is the derivative, and when the quantity in question lives in a world with more than one dimension (which everything does), we get partial derivatives.

The unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics in the natural sciences is something bordering on the mysterious. — Eugene Wigner, 1960

The Scale of the Subject

PDEs are not a niche curiosity. They are the backbone of nearly every quantitative field of human inquiry. Consider:

Physics

Maxwell's equations govern all of electromagnetism. Schrödinger's equation governs quantum mechanics. Einstein's field equations govern gravity. All PDEs.

Engineering

Fluid dynamics (Navier-Stokes), structural analysis (elasticity equations), heat transfer, aerodynamics — all built on PDEs.

Finance

The Black-Scholes equation — which revolutionised derivatives pricing — is a PDE. Stochastic control theory runs on Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equations.

Biology

Turing's reaction-diffusion equations explain how animal coat patterns form. Neural field equations model brain dynamics. Epidemic models use PDE systems.

Geometry

The Ricci flow — a PDE on the metric of a manifold — was used by Perelman to prove the Poincaré Conjecture, one of mathematics' greatest achievements.

AI & Imaging

Image denoising, segmentation, and the theoretical underpinning of many machine learning methods are rooted in PDE theory and variational calculus.

What You Will Learn

This course takes you from first principles all the way to the frontier of modern research. We start with intuition and build steadily toward rigour. You will understand not just how to solve PDEs, but why the theory is structured the way it is — and why mathematicians had to invent entirely new concepts (Sobolev spaces, distributions, weak solutions) just to make sense of them.

How to use this guide
Each page builds on the previous. The early pages are accessible to anyone with calculus. As we progress, the pages grow denser — more content, more depth, more technical precision. Interactive elements let you experiment with concepts. Take your time.

The journey ahead is challenging but beautiful. PDEs sit at the intersection of analysis, geometry, physics, and computation. Learning their theory means learning how mathematicians think about infinity, continuity, and the structure of space itself. Few areas of mathematics are as alive, as useful, or as deep.

A History in People

Three centuries of genius, rivalry, and discovery that built the theory

The history of PDEs is inseparable from the history of physics. Every major equation was born from a physical problem, and every major mathematical breakthrough was forced by a physical reality that refused to fit into existing frameworks. This is a story of extraordinary individuals, bitter controversies, and moments of sudden illumination that changed how humanity understands the world.

1687
Newton publishes the Principia Mathematica. His laws of motion are ordinary differential equations — but the conceptual foundation for PDEs is implicitly there. The notion that nature obeys local, instantaneous rules is born.
1746
Jean le Rond d'Alembert writes down the first PDE — the one-dimensional wave equation — while studying the vibrating string problem. He obtains the general solution $u = f(x+ct) + g(x-ct)$. A new era begins.
1750s
Euler and Daniel Bernoulli enter a famous dispute over what "functions" are allowed as solutions to the wave equation. Bernoulli insists trigonometric series suffice; Euler disagrees. They are both right, in ways neither fully understood. The controversy would take 70 years to resolve.
1782
Laplace writes down $\nabla^2 u = 0$ while studying gravitational potential theory. The equation bearing his name will prove to be one of the most fundamental objects in all of mathematics.
1807–22
Joseph Fourier submits his theory of heat conduction to the Paris Academy. His idea — that any function can be written as a (possibly infinite) sum of sines and cosines — is so radical that Lagrange and Laplace initially reject it. His eventual masterwork Théorie Analytique de la Chaleur (1822) transforms mathematics forever.
1828
George Green, a self-taught miller's son with only one year of university, publishes his theorem relating volume integrals to surface integrals. Green's functions — his key tool — will become the foundation of a vast solution theory.
1860s
Riemann classifies second-order PDEs and introduces the concept of characteristics. Maxwell unifies electricity and magnetism in four PDEs. The wave-like nature of light falls out as a prediction. Science is never the same.
1900
Hadamard formulates the concept of a well-posed problem — perhaps the most important conceptual contribution to PDE theory. He shows that some physically meaningful problems have solutions that are catastrophically unstable, raising deep questions about what "solving" a PDE even means.
1926
Schrödinger writes down the equation governing quantum wavefunctions — a PDE that underpins all of modern physics and chemistry.
1945–50
Laurent Schwartz develops the theory of distributions, giving rigorous meaning to objects like the Dirac delta function (which physicists had been using informally for decades). He wins the Fields Medal in 1950. PDEs finally have a language adequate to their needs.
1950s–70s
Sobolev, Gårding, Lax, Nirenberg and others build the modern functional analytic framework. Sobolev spaces become the natural habitat for solutions. The abstract theory reaches full maturity.
2003
Grigori Perelman uses the Ricci flow PDE — introduced by Richard Hamilton in 1982 — to prove the Poincaré Conjecture, one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems. The power of geometric PDEs reaches its apex.
The Living Subject
PDEs remain one of the most active areas of mathematical research. The Navier-Stokes existence and smoothness problem — whether solutions to the fluid dynamics equations always exist and remain smooth — is still unsolved and carries a $1,000,000 prize from the Clay Mathematics Institute.

What Is a PDE?

Building the language from the ground up — notation, order, and linearity

Before we can study partial differential equations, we need to understand precisely what kind of object they are. An ordinary differential equation (ODE) involves a function of one variable and its derivatives. A partial differential equation involves a function of two or more variables, and its partial derivatives with respect to those variables. The jump from one variable to many is, it turns out, a qualitative leap — not merely a quantitative one.

The Basic Concept

Suppose $u = u(x, y)$ is a function of two real variables. The partial derivative $\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}$ measures how $u$ changes as $x$ varies while $y$ is held fixed. Similarly $\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}$ measures change with respect to $y$ alone. A partial differential equation is any equation relating $u$ to some combination of its partial derivatives.

General form of a PDE
$$F\!\left(x,\, y,\, u,\, \frac{\partial u}{\partial x},\, \frac{\partial u}{\partial y},\, \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2},\, \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x \partial y},\, \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2},\, \ldots\right) = 0$$

Notation

Several notations are in common use, and fluency with all of them is essential. We illustrate with a function $u(x,t)$ of space $x$ and time $t$:

NotationMeaningCommon in
$\dfrac{\partial u}{\partial t}$Partial derivative of $u$ with respect to $t$Analysis, physics texts
$u_t$Same — subscript notationMost PDE textbooks
$\partial_t u$Same — operator notationModern analysis
$u_{xx}$Second partial in $x$: $\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}$Standard
$\nabla u$Gradient: $(u_{x_1}, \ldots, u_{x_n})$Vector calculus, geometry
$\Delta u$ or $\nabla^2 u$Laplacian: $u_{x_1 x_1} + \cdots + u_{x_n x_n}$Universal
$D^\alpha u$Multi-index notation for any partial derivativeSobolev spaces, distributions

Order

The order of a PDE is the order of the highest derivative that appears. Most equations arising in applications are second-order or lower. First-order equations arise in conservation laws and transport phenomena. Second-order equations arise from every kind of wave, diffusion, or equilibrium problem.

First Order Examples

The transport (advection) equation: $u_t + c\, u_x = 0$

The eikonal equation: $|\nabla u| = \frac{1}{c(x)}$

Second Order Examples

The heat equation: $u_t = \kappa \Delta u$

The wave equation: $u_{tt} = c^2 \Delta u$

Laplace's equation: $\Delta u = 0$

Linearity — The Most Important Distinction

A PDE is linear if it can be written in the form $Lu = f$, where $L$ is a linear operator (meaning $L(au + bv) = aLu + bLv$ for constants $a,b$), and $f$ is a given function. This seemingly technical condition has enormous consequences:

The Superposition Principle
If $u_1$ and $u_2$ are both solutions of the homogeneous equation $Lu = 0$, then so is any linear combination $c_1 u_1 + c_2 u_2$. More powerfully, so is any (possibly infinite) superposition $\sum_{n} c_n u_n$, provided convergence holds. This is the foundation of Fourier methods.

Nonlinear PDEs lack superposition. They are vastly harder — but also richer, capable of describing shocks, turbulence, solitons, and phase transitions that linear equations cannot capture.

A Taxonomy of Linearity

Linear PDEs +
All coefficients depend only on the independent variables (not on $u$ or its derivatives). E.g., $u_t = \kappa(x) u_{xx}$ where $\kappa$ is a given function of $x$ only. Superposition holds fully.
Semilinear PDEs +
Linear in the highest-order derivatives only; lower-order terms may be nonlinear. E.g., $\Delta u = f(u)$ where $f$ is some nonlinear function of $u$ itself (like $u^3$). Much of nonlinear elliptic theory deals with these.
Quasilinear PDEs +
Linear in the highest-order derivatives, but coefficients may depend on lower-order derivatives of $u$. The minimal surface equation and many equations of nonlinear elasticity fall here. The method of characteristics extends to these.
Fully Nonlinear PDEs +
Nonlinear even in the highest-order derivatives. Examples: the Monge-Ampère equation $\det(D^2 u) = f$ and the Hamilton-Jacobi equation $u_t + H(Du) = 0$. Require entirely different solution theories (viscosity solutions).

The Grand Classification

Elliptic, parabolic, and hyperbolic — the three species of second-order PDE

The classification of second-order linear PDEs is one of the most beautiful and consequential results in the entire theory. It tells us not just how to label equations, but that fundamentally different types of phenomena — equilibrium, diffusion, and wave propagation — obey qualitatively different mathematical laws. The classification determines what kind of data you need to prescribe, how information travels through the domain, and what kind of regularity to expect from solutions.

The General Second-Order Linear PDE

In two independent variables $x$ and $y$, the most general second-order linear PDE is:

Principal Part
$$A\, u_{xx} + 2B\, u_{xy} + C\, u_{yy} + D\, u_x + E\, u_y + F\, u = G$$

where $A, B, C, D, E, F, G$ may be functions of $x$ and $y$.

The key is the discriminant $\Delta = B^2 - AC$ — exactly analogous to the discriminant of a conic section in algebra. This analogy is not superficial: the equation's behaviour near each point $(x_0, y_0)$ is governed by the sign of $\Delta(x_0, y_0)$.

Elliptic
$$B^2 - AC < 0$$

Prototype: Laplace's equation $\Delta u = 0$

Physics: Equilibrium, steady-state

Data: Boundary values everywhere

Character: Infinitely smooth, information from all directions

Parabolic
$$B^2 - AC = 0$$

Prototype: Heat equation $u_t = \Delta u$

Physics: Diffusion, irreversible

Data: Initial values + boundary values

Character: Instantly smooths, forward in time only

Hyperbolic
$$B^2 - AC > 0$$

Prototype: Wave equation $u_{tt} = c^2 \Delta u$

Physics: Propagation, reversible

Data: Initial position + velocity

Character: Preserves singularities, finite speed

Why This Classification Matters So Deeply

The type of a PDE is not just a label — it determines the entire solution theory:

Elliptic: The Language of Equilibrium

Elliptic equations describe systems that have reached a steady state. There is no time variable — the solution represents a balance of forces throughout a region. Crucially, the solution at any interior point is influenced by the boundary conditions everywhere. Change the temperature at any part of the boundary of a room, and the equilibrium temperature throughout the entire room shifts. Solutions are infinitely smooth in the interior — even if the data is rough, the equation irons out singularities.

Parabolic: The Language of Diffusion

Parabolic equations model processes that evolve irreversibly in time and tend toward equilibrium. The heat equation is the archetype: heat spreads from hot to cold, smoothing temperature gradients. The remarkable fact is that an initial temperature profile that is discontinuous (say, a step function) instantly becomes a smooth function for any $t > 0$. This infinite propagation speed is unphysical but mathematically elegant and approximately correct in diffusion contexts.

Hyperbolic: The Language of Waves

Hyperbolic equations model wave propagation. They have finite propagation speed — disturbances travel at speed $c$ and no faster. This is deeply physical (nothing travels faster than light). Crucially, singularities in the initial data are preserved and propagated along characteristic curves. If you start a wave with a sharp front, the front remains sharp forever (in the absence of dissipation).

Variable Type — An Important Subtlety
A PDE can change type from point to point if its coefficients depend on position. The Tricomi equation $y\, u_{xx} + u_{yy} = 0$ is elliptic for $y > 0$, parabolic on $y = 0$, and hyperbolic for $y < 0$. Such mixed-type equations arise in transonic aerodynamics, where airflow transitions from subsonic (elliptic) to supersonic (hyperbolic) through the sound barrier.

Boundary & Initial Conditions

A PDE alone is not a problem — you need to tell it where to start and what the walls look like

One of the subtlest and most important lessons in PDE theory is that an equation by itself typically has infinitely many solutions — or perhaps none at all if you choose your supplementary conditions badly. The art is in pairing each equation with exactly the right kind of data. Too little data and the solution is not unique; too much and the problem is overdetermined and has no solution. Getting this right is not arbitrary — the classification of the PDE tells you precisely what kind of data is geometrically and analytically appropriate.

The Domain and Its Boundary

We are always solving a PDE on some domain $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^n$. The boundary $\partial\Omega$ is the edge of this domain, and it is where we impose boundary conditions. For time-dependent problems, we also have an initial time $t=0$ where we impose initial conditions.

Types of Boundary Conditions

For a second-order PDE on a domain $\Omega$ with boundary $\partial\Omega$, there are three classical types:

Dirichlet Conditions: prescribe $u$ on $\partial\Omega$ +

$$u\big|_{\partial\Omega} = g(x)$$

Named after Lejeune Dirichlet. You prescribe the value of the function on the boundary. Physical interpretation: fixed temperature on the walls of a room, or fixed displacement at the endpoints of a string.

Example: Find the steady-state temperature $u$ in a metal plate given fixed temperatures on all edges.

Neumann Conditions: prescribe the normal derivative on $\partial\Omega$ +

$$\frac{\partial u}{\partial \mathbf{n}}\bigg|_{\partial\Omega} = h(x)$$

You prescribe the outward normal derivative of $u$ at the boundary. Physical interpretation: prescribing the heat flux through the walls (rather than their temperature). If $h = 0$, the walls are perfectly insulated.

Crucially, for the Laplace equation with pure Neumann conditions, a compatibility condition $\int_{\partial\Omega} h\, dS = 0$ is necessary for a solution to exist (net flux in must equal net flux out). And the solution is unique only up to a constant.

Robin (Mixed) Conditions: combine $u$ and its normal derivative +

$$\alpha\, u + \beta\, \frac{\partial u}{\partial \mathbf{n}} = g \quad \text{on } \partial\Omega$$

Named after Victor Gustave Robin (though he was not the first to study them — a recurring theme in mathematical naming). Models a boundary that partially conducts heat: the flux through the wall is proportional to the temperature difference between the wall and the outside. Newton's law of cooling is a Robin condition.

Initial Conditions

For time-dependent problems, we need to specify the state of the system at $t=0$. How much data is needed depends on the order in time:

First-order in time (Parabolic)

For the heat equation $u_t = \kappa u_{xx}$, we need one initial condition:

$$u(x,0) = f(x)$$

Specifying just the initial temperature profile is enough to determine all future evolution.

Second-order in time (Hyperbolic)

For the wave equation $u_{tt} = c^2 u_{xx}$, we need two initial conditions:

$$u(x,0) = f(x), \quad u_t(x,0) = g(x)$$

Both position and velocity at $t=0$ are needed. (Just as for second-order ODEs.)

The Principle of Correct Prescription

Each type of equation requires a geometrically natural type of supplementary data, determined by the direction of information flow:

TypeWhere to prescribe dataReason
EllipticOn the entire closed boundary $\partial\Omega$No "direction of propagation"; information fills the domain from all sides
ParabolicAt $t=0$ (initial) + on spatial boundary for all $t>0$Information flows forward in time from initial data
HyperbolicOn a spacelike surface (+ initial velocity)Characteristics determine which data is "seen" by each point
Cauchy-Kowalevski: A Warning
The classical Cauchy-Kowalevski theorem says: for an analytic PDE with analytic data, a unique analytic solution exists locally. It is tempting to conclude that you can always prescribe data on any surface. Hadamard's example demolished this hope: the Laplace equation with Cauchy data (both $u$ and $u_n$ on a curve) can have a unique formal solution that blows up arbitrarily fast — the problem is ill-posed. Nature does not allow arbitrary prescription of data for elliptic equations; only boundary values are appropriate.

Well-Posedness

The most important conceptual framework in all of PDE theory — what it means for a problem to be mathematically sound

In 1902, the French mathematician Jacques Hadamard posed a question so fundamental that it reoriented the entire field of PDEs: what does it mean for a mathematical problem to be correctly formulated? His answer, crystallised in what we now call Hadamard's conditions, is still the gold standard for evaluating any PDE problem today. A problem is well-posed if it satisfies all three conditions: existence, uniqueness, and stability. If any one fails, the problem is ill-posed — and however elegant its mathematics, it cannot be trusted as a model of physical reality.

Jacques Salomon Hadamard
1865–1963 · French mathematician

Hadamard's contributions span complex analysis (prime number theorem), differential geometry, and PDEs. His 1902 formulation of well-posedness was initially controversial — mathematicians were reluctant to accept that some formally sensible problems were fundamentally pathological. His concrete example of an ill-posed elliptic problem (described below) was the decisive argument. He lived to 97, remaining intellectually active until near the end.

The Three Conditions

I. Existence

There must exist at least one solution to the problem. This seems obvious — if you've written down an equation, surely it has a solution? But consider:

  • The Neumann problem for Laplace's equation on a bounded domain has no solution if the boundary data fails the compatibility condition.
  • Nonlinear PDEs can fail to have even local solutions if the initial data is too rough.
  • The most famous open problem in mathematics — whether solutions to Navier-Stokes always exist globally — is an existence question.

Proving existence is often the deepest part of PDE analysis. Modern approaches use functional analysis: reformulate the PDE as a fixed-point problem in an appropriate function space, then invoke a fixed-point theorem (Schauder, Brouwer, Banach) to guarantee a solution exists.

II. Uniqueness

There must be at most one solution. If a problem has multiple solutions, the equation does not determine the physical state from the data — which data should you choose? Uniqueness failures often signal that the problem is underdetermined (not enough conditions imposed).

Energy Method for Uniqueness (Heat Equation)
$$\text{If } u_1, u_2 \text{ both solve } u_t = \kappa u_{xx} \text{ with the same data, let } w = u_1 - u_2.$$ $$\text{Then } w_t = \kappa w_{xx},\; w(x,0) = 0,\; w|_{\partial\Omega} = 0.$$ $$\frac{d}{dt}\int_\Omega w^2\, dx = 2\int_\Omega w w_t\, dx = 2\kappa \int_\Omega w w_{xx}\, dx \stackrel{\text{IBP}}{=} -2\kappa\int_\Omega w_x^2\, dx \leq 0$$ $$\Rightarrow \int_\Omega w^2 dx \leq 0 \Rightarrow w \equiv 0 \Rightarrow u_1 \equiv u_2. \quad \blacksquare$$

III. Stability (Continuous Dependence on Data)

Small changes in the data (initial conditions, boundary values, source terms) must produce small changes in the solution. This is the most physically and computationally critical condition. Without it, any measurement error or numerical rounding in the data leads to completely untrustworthy solutions.

Hadamard's Ill-Posed Example
Consider Laplace's equation on the upper half-plane with Cauchy data: $$\Delta u = 0 \quad (y > 0), \qquad u(x,0) = 0, \quad u_y(x,0) = \frac{\sin(nx)}{n}$$ The unique solution is $u_n(x,y) = \frac{\sinh(ny)\sin(nx)}{n^2}$. As $n\to\infty$, the initial data $\frac{\sin(nx)}{n} \to 0$ uniformly — the data becomes arbitrarily small. But the solution grows like $\frac{e^{ny}}{n^2}$, which for any fixed $y > 0$ becomes arbitrarily large. The problem is catastrophically unstable. This is why Cauchy data for Laplace's equation is not physically meaningful.

Ill-Posedness and Regularisation

Ill-posed problems are not merely academic curiosities — they arise constantly in applications, particularly in inverse problems: recovering the source of a signal from measured data, reconstructing an image from blurred measurements, or determining subsurface geological structure from surface observations. These are inherently ill-posed.

The standard remedy is Tikhonov regularisation: instead of solving the original ill-posed problem exactly, solve a nearby well-posed problem that incorporates a priori information (e.g., that the solution should be smooth). The regularisation parameter balances fidelity to data against smoothness of solution.

Moral of Well-Posedness
Well-posedness is not a technical nicety — it is the minimum requirement for a PDE problem to be trustworthy as a physical model. Whenever you set up a PDE problem, you should ask: does it have a solution? Is it unique? And critically: if I perturb my data by a tiny amount, does my solution change by a tiny amount? If the answer to the last question is "no", you have a problem that will fail in simulation and fail as a model.

The Heat Equation

Diffusion, smoothing, and Fourier's revolutionary insight

The heat equation is one of mathematics' most studied objects — and for good reason. It is the simplest parabolic PDE, yet it contains within it almost every idea that makes the subject deep: Fourier series, eigenfunction expansions, fundamental solutions, the maximum principle, and the profound distinction between reversible and irreversible processes. To fully understand the heat equation is to understand most of classical PDE analysis.

Derivation from First Principles

Consider a homogeneous rod of length $L$ along which heat flows. Let $u(x,t)$ be the temperature at position $x$ and time $t$. The physical laws governing this are:

Fourier's Law of Heat Conduction
Heat flux is proportional to (and in the direction opposite to) the temperature gradient: $\mathbf{q} = -\kappa \nabla u$, where $\kappa > 0$ is the thermal conductivity.

Combine this with conservation of energy (heat stored in a small volume = heat flowing in minus heat flowing out), and you obtain:

The Heat Equation
$$u_t = \kappa\, \Delta u$$

In 1D: $u_t = \kappa\, u_{xx}$. In $\mathbb{R}^n$: $u_t = \kappa\, (u_{x_1x_1} + \cdots + u_{x_nx_n})$. The constant $\kappa > 0$ is the thermal diffusivity.

The Laplacian $\Delta u$ measures the average of $u$ in a small sphere minus the value at the centre. When $\Delta u > 0$, the neighbourhood is on average hotter than the point — so heat flows in, and $u_t > 0$. The equation says: heat flows from hotter to cooler, always. This is the content of the Second Law of Thermodynamics.

The Fundamental Solution

The most important single solution of the heat equation is the heat kernel — the solution to the problem with initial condition $u(x,0) = \delta(x)$ (a unit concentration of heat at the origin at time zero):

Heat Kernel on $\mathbb{R}$
$$\Phi(x,t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi\kappa t}}\, e^{-x^2/(4\kappa t)}, \qquad t > 0$$

This is a Gaussian distribution whose width grows like $\sqrt{t}$ — the famous square-root scaling of diffusion. At $t=0^+$, it is sharply concentrated. For any $t>0$, it has spread to a bell curve with standard deviation $\sqrt{2\kappa t}$. The total area under the curve is always exactly 1 (heat is conserved). By convolution, the general initial value problem has solution:

$$u(x,t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \Phi(x-y,\, t)\, u_0(y)\, dy$$

This formula is exact for any integrable initial data $u_0$ and produces a smooth solution for all $t > 0$ — even if $u_0$ was discontinuous. This instant smoothing is the hallmark of parabolic equations.

The Maximum Principle

One of the most powerful qualitative tools in PDE theory is the maximum principle for the heat equation: the maximum (and minimum) of the temperature over the entire domain-plus-boundary is achieved either at the initial time or on the spatial boundary — never in the interior for $t > 0$. This is physically obvious (heat spontaneously concentrating to a hot spot contradicts the Second Law) but mathematically rich, with far-reaching generalisations to nonlinear equations.

Irreversibility and the Arrow of Time

The heat equation is not time-reversible. If you run it backwards (replace $t \to -t$, giving $u_t = -\kappa u_{xx}$), the equation becomes backward parabolic — and Hadamard-type instabilities arise. Starting from a slightly noisy state at time $T$ and trying to recover the initial condition at $t=0$ is extremely ill-posed. This is not a mathematical limitation; it reflects the genuine physical irreversibility of diffusion.

Fourier himself was aware of this asymmetry, but it was Boltzmann and later mathematicians who made the connection to entropy rigorous. The heat equation is one of the simplest examples where a time-symmetric physical law (conservation of energy) gives rise to time-asymmetric macroscopic behaviour.

The Wave Equation

Vibration, causality, and the geometry of signal propagation

The wave equation was the first PDE ever written down, born from d'Alembert's 1746 study of the vibrating string. It is the prototype of all hyperbolic equations — the mathematical embodiment of oscillation and propagation. Where the heat equation smooths and forgets, the wave equation preserves and transmits. It governs sound, light, water waves, seismic activity, and gravitational radiation. Understanding it means understanding how information travels through space.

The Equation and Its Derivation

Consider a perfectly elastic string under tension $T$ with mass density $\rho$. If $u(x,t)$ is the transverse displacement at position $x$ and time $t$, Newton's second law applied to a small segment gives:

Wave Equation
$$u_{tt} = c^2\, \Delta u, \qquad c = \sqrt{T/\rho}$$

$c$ is the wave speed. In 1D: $u_{tt} = c^2 u_{xx}$. For light in vacuum: $c = 3\times 10^8$ m/s.

d'Alembert's Formula

The 1D wave equation can be factored: $\left(\partial_t - c\partial_x\right)\left(\partial_t + c\partial_x\right) u = 0$. This suggests introducing $\xi = x + ct$, $\eta = x - ct$, which transforms the equation to $u_{\xi\eta} = 0$, giving immediately $u = f(\xi) + g(\eta)$. Imposing initial conditions $u(x,0)=\phi(x)$, $u_t(x,0) = \psi(x)$ yields:

d'Alembert's Formula (1746)
$$u(x,t) = \frac{\phi(x+ct) + \phi(x-ct)}{2} + \frac{1}{2c}\int_{x-ct}^{x+ct} \psi(s)\, ds$$

This formula is exact, explicit, and beautiful. It says the solution is the average of two copies of the initial data — one travelling right at speed $c$, one travelling left. The second term adds the effect of the initial velocity.

The Domain of Dependence

The value of $u(x_0, t_0)$ depends only on the initial data in the interval $[x_0 - ct_0,\; x_0 + ct_0]$. This interval is the domain of dependence of the point $(x_0, t_0)$. It embodies the principle of finite propagation speed: no signal can travel faster than $c$. This is the mathematical expression of special relativity (for light), and of the physically observed fact that sound, water waves, and seismic waves all travel at specific finite speeds.

↑ two waves travelling in opposite directions (d'Alembert decomposition)

Energy Conservation

The wave equation, unlike the heat equation, conserves energy. The total energy (kinetic + potential) is:

$$E(t) = \frac{1}{2}\int_\Omega \left(u_t^2 + c^2|\nabla u|^2\right) dx = \text{const}$$

This follows from multiplying the wave equation by $u_t$ and integrating. The fact that energy is conserved reflects the time-reversibility of the wave equation: unlike the heat equation, you can run waves backwards and recover the original state exactly.

Huygens' Principle

In odd spatial dimensions $n \geq 3$, the wave equation satisfies a remarkable property: disturbances propagate on the wavefront only — not inside it. If you drop a stone into still water (2D), you see concentric rings, but the water inside the rings also disturbs. However, in 3D, a brief sound source produces a sharp pulse — silence before, then the pulse, then silence again. This is Huygens' principle, and it is the reason we can hear speech clearly rather than as a blurry echo of all past sounds.

Why odd dimensions are special
Huygens' principle holds in 3D, 5D, 7D,… but not in 2D, 4D, 6D,… This is related to the structure of the fundamental solution: in odd dimensions it is a delta function supported on the sphere of radius $ct$; in even dimensions it has support inside the sphere too. The reason is connected to properties of Bessel functions and the Radon transform. It remains a deep and beautiful fact.

The Wave Equation and Electromagnetism

In 1865, Maxwell wrote down his four equations unifying electricity and magnetism. From them, one can derive that both the electric field $\mathbf{E}$ and magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ satisfy the wave equation with $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}$. Plugging in the measured values of $\mu_0$ and $\varepsilon_0$, Maxwell obtained $c \approx 3 \times 10^8$ m/s — the known speed of light. His conclusion: light is an electromagnetic wave. This was one of the greatest unifications in the history of science, and it was achieved by solving a PDE.

Laplace & Poisson

Harmony, equilibrium, and the geometry of potential fields

Laplace's equation $\Delta u = 0$ is perhaps the most studied equation in all of mathematics. Its solutions — called harmonic functions — arise in gravitation, electrostatics, fluid flow, complex analysis, and probability. They are extraordinary objects: infinitely smooth, determined entirely by their boundary values, satisfying a remarkable mean value property. The Poisson equation $\Delta u = f$ adds a source term and extends the theory to problems with distributed charges or masses.

Physical Origins

Gravitational Potential

Newton's gravity: the potential $\phi$ of a mass distribution satisfies $\Delta\phi = 4\pi G\rho$ (Poisson) where $\rho$ is mass density. In free space where $\rho=0$: Laplace's equation.

Electrostatics

The electric potential $V$ satisfies $\Delta V = -\rho/\varepsilon_0$ (Poisson). The electric field is $\mathbf{E} = -\nabla V$. Finding the potential from charge distributions is the central problem of electrostatics.

Steady-State Heat

As $t\to\infty$, the heat equation $u_t = \kappa\Delta u$ approaches steady state: $u_t = 0$, giving $\Delta u = 0$. The equilibrium temperature distribution is harmonic.

Ideal Fluid Flow

An incompressible, irrotational fluid flow has a velocity potential $\phi$ satisfying $\Delta\phi = 0$. Complex analysis (conformal mapping) can then be used to find exact flow patterns.

The Mean Value Property

The most characteristic property of harmonic functions is:

Mean Value Theorem for Harmonic Functions
If $u$ is harmonic in a domain $\Omega$ and $B_r(x_0) \subset\subset \Omega$ is any ball compactly contained in $\Omega$, then: $$u(x_0) = \frac{1}{|\partial B_r|}\int_{\partial B_r(x_0)} u\, dS = \frac{1}{|B_r|}\int_{B_r(x_0)} u\, dV$$ The value at any point is exactly the average over any sphere (or ball) centred at that point.

This is a purely local condition that completely characterises harmonic functions. It has a probabilistic interpretation: the value at a point is the expected value of a Brownian motion started there when it first exits the ball. This connects PDE theory to probability in a deep way.

The Maximum Principle

Strong Maximum Principle
A non-constant harmonic function on a connected domain $\Omega$ cannot attain its maximum (or minimum) in the interior of $\Omega$. Therefore the maximum and minimum are attained on $\partial\Omega$.

Consequence: the Dirichlet problem for Laplace's equation has at most one solution (uniqueness). If two solutions agreed on the boundary, their difference would be harmonic, zero on the boundary, hence zero everywhere. Also: the solution is stable — small perturbations of boundary data produce small perturbations of the solution (since the sup norm of the solution is bounded by the sup norm of the boundary data).

Harmonic Functions in Complex Analysis

In two dimensions, harmonic functions and complex analysis are intimately related. If $f(z) = u(x,y) + iv(x,y)$ is a holomorphic function of $z = x+iy$, then both its real part $u$ and imaginary part $v$ are harmonic. Conversely, every harmonic function on a simply connected domain is the real part of some holomorphic function. This means the full machinery of complex analysis — Laurent series, residues, conformal mapping — is available for studying 2D harmonic functions. The resulting theory is extraordinarily rich.

Fundamental Solution of Laplace's Equation

The fundamental solution is the potential of a point source:

$$\Phi(x) = \begin{cases} -\dfrac{1}{2\pi}\ln|x| & n = 2 \\[10pt] \dfrac{1}{n(n-2)\omega_n}\, \dfrac{1}{|x|^{n-2}} & n \geq 3 \end{cases}$$

where $\omega_n$ is the volume of the unit ball in $\mathbb{R}^n$. In 3D: $\Phi(x) = \frac{1}{4\pi|x|}$ — the Coulomb potential.

This satisfies $-\Delta\Phi = \delta_0$ (the Dirac delta at the origin) — it is the potential of a unit point charge. By superposition, the potential of any charge distribution $f$ is $u(x) = \int \Phi(x-y)\, f(y)\, dy$, which solves $-\Delta u = f$.

Separation of Variables

The oldest and most productive technique for generating explicit solutions

Separation of variables is the great workhorse of classical PDE analysis. It is beautifully simple in principle: assume the solution can be written as a product of functions, each depending on only one variable. This reduces the PDE to a system of ODEs, which are far easier to solve. When combined with superposition (linearity!) and Fourier analysis, it yields complete solution formulae for a remarkable range of problems. Every physics student encounters it; its depth, however, runs much further than the textbook treatment suggests.

The Method: Heat Equation on $[0,L]$

Solve $u_t = \kappa u_{xx}$ on $0 < x < L$, $t > 0$, with $u(0,t) = u(L,t) = 0$ (zero temperature at endpoints) and $u(x,0) = f(x)$.

Step 1: Assume $u(x,t) = X(x) T(t)$ and substitute:

$$X(x)\, T'(t) = \kappa\, X''(x)\, T(t) \implies \frac{T'(t)}{\kappa T(t)} = \frac{X''(x)}{X(x)} = -\lambda$$

Since the left side depends only on $t$ and the right only on $x$, both must equal the same constant $-\lambda$. This gives two ODEs:

$$X'' + \lambda X = 0, \qquad T' + \kappa\lambda T = 0$$

Step 2: Solve the $X$ problem with boundary conditions $X(0) = X(L) = 0$. The non-trivial solutions are:

$$X_n(x) = \sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right), \quad \lambda_n = \left(\frac{n\pi}{L}\right)^2, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$

Step 3: Solve the $T$ equation: $T_n(t) = e^{-\kappa\lambda_n t} = e^{-\kappa(n\pi/L)^2 t}$

Step 4: Superpose and match initial condition:

Complete Solution
$$u(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} b_n\, \sin\!\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) e^{-\kappa(n\pi/L)^2 t}$$ $$b_n = \frac{2}{L}\int_0^L f(x)\, \sin\!\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) dx$$

This is complete: the terms decay exponentially in time (higher modes faster), smoothing the solution. As $t\to\infty$, all terms vanish — the rod cools to zero.

Why It Works: Eigenfunctions

The separation procedure reduces to finding the eigenfunctions of the spatial operator $-d^2/dx^2$ with Dirichlet boundary conditions. The functions $\sin(n\pi x/L)$ are those eigenfunctions; the numbers $\lambda_n = (n\pi/L)^2$ are the corresponding eigenvalues. The superposition step works because the eigenfunctions are orthogonal:

$$\int_0^L \sin\!\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)\sin\!\!\left(\frac{m\pi x}{L}\right) dx = \frac{L}{2}\delta_{nm}$$

This orthogonality is the Fourier sine series. It allows us to extract each coefficient $b_n$ independently.

Generalisation: Sturm-Liouville Problems

More generally, separation of variables on non-constant-coefficient operators leads to Sturm-Liouville eigenvalue problems:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[p(x)\frac{dX}{dx}\right] + \left[q(x) + \lambda r(x)\right] X = 0$$

The Sturm-Liouville theory guarantees a countable infinite set of real eigenvalues $\lambda_1 < \lambda_2 < \ldots \to \infty$ and orthogonal eigenfunctions that form a complete basis. This is the foundation of spectral theory for PDEs (page 17).

Limitations
Separation of variables requires: (1) a linear equation, (2) a separable domain (rectangle, ball, cylinder, etc.), and (3) boundary conditions aligned with the coordinate system. For irregularly shaped domains, other techniques (Green's functions, numerical methods) are needed. Despite these limitations, it generates the richest explicit solution theory in the subject.

Fourier's Revolution

The idea that shattered mathematical orthodoxy and changed science forever

In 1807, Joseph Fourier submitted a paper to the Paris Academy claiming that an arbitrary function could be expressed as an infinite sum of sines and cosines. The referees — who included Lagrange and Laplace, the greatest mathematicians of the age — rejected it. They could not accept that a "discontinuous" function (like a step function with a jump) could equal a sum of perfectly smooth sinusoids. Fourier was right, and the controversy he ignited transformed analysis, laid the foundations for signal processing, quantum mechanics, and communication theory, and led to the modern rigorous treatment of the concept of "function" itself.

Jean-Baptiste Joseph Fourier
1768–1830 · French mathematician and Egyptologist

Orphaned at eight, educated by Benedictine monks, imprisoned twice during the Revolution, accompanied Napoleon to Egypt, eventually became prefect of Isère. He studied heat conduction not for abstract reasons but because he wanted to understand the temperature of the Earth's interior and the effect of the atmosphere. His thesis, refined over fifteen years, became the Théorie Analytique de la Chaleur (1822) — a masterpiece that Kelvin called "a great mathematical poem."

Fourier Series

A function $f$ on $[-L, L]$ (under mild conditions) can be written:

Fourier Series
$$f(x) = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\!\left[a_n \cos\!\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) + b_n \sin\!\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)\right]$$ $$a_n = \frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^{L} f(x)\cos\!\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) dx, \qquad b_n = \frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^{L} f(x)\sin\!\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) dx$$

The key insight is orthogonality: sines and cosines of different frequencies are orthogonal in $L^2[-L,L]$. This makes the Fourier coefficients extractable independently, and makes the whole theory work.

The Fourier Transform

Extending Fourier series from a finite interval to all of $\mathbb{R}$ (by taking $L\to\infty$) gives the Fourier transform:

Fourier Transform Pair
$$\hat{f}(\xi) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\, e^{-2\pi i\xi x}\, dx, \qquad f(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}(\xi)\, e^{2\pi i\xi x}\, d\xi$$

The Fourier transform is the most powerful tool for solving constant-coefficient PDEs. Its key property: it converts differentiation to multiplication:

$$\widehat{\frac{\partial^k u}{\partial x^k}}(\xi) = (2\pi i\xi)^k\, \hat{u}(\xi)$$

This turns a PDE (hard: differential equation in $x$, $t$) into an algebraic or ODE problem in the frequency variable $\xi$ (easy). Solve, then invert the transform.

Applying the Fourier Transform to the Heat Equation

Take the Fourier transform in $x$ of $u_t = \kappa u_{xx}$:

$$\partial_t \hat{u}(\xi, t) = -4\pi^2\kappa\xi^2\, \hat{u}(\xi, t) \implies \hat{u}(\xi,t) = \hat{u}_0(\xi)\, e^{-4\pi^2\kappa\xi^2 t}$$ $$\implies u(x,t) = \int \hat{u}_0(\xi)\, e^{-4\pi^2\kappa\xi^2 t}\, e^{2\pi i\xi x}\, d\xi$$

The factor $e^{-4\pi^2\kappa\xi^2 t}$ decays rapidly for large $|\xi|$ (high frequencies). This confirms that the heat equation damps high-frequency oscillations — the smoothing property manifested in frequency space.

Parseval's Theorem and Energy

Parseval / Plancherel Theorem
$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |f(x)|^2\, dx = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |\hat{f}(\xi)|^2\, d\xi$$ The Fourier transform is an isometry of $L^2(\mathbb{R})$ — it preserves the total energy (the $L^2$ norm). This allows one to extend the Fourier transform to all square-integrable functions, not just smooth ones.

The Uncertainty Principle

Perhaps the most famous consequence of Fourier analysis is Heisenberg's uncertainty principle in quantum mechanics. The position wavefunction $\psi(x)$ and momentum wavefunction $\hat{\psi}(\xi)$ are related by the Fourier transform. The mathematical uncertainty principle states:

$$\Delta x \cdot \Delta\xi \geq \frac{1}{4\pi}$$

A function cannot be simultaneously concentrated in both space and frequency. The more localised $f$ is in $x$, the more spread out $\hat{f}$ must be in $\xi$ — and vice versa.

Method of Characteristics

Following information along curves — the geometric heart of first-order theory

The method of characteristics is based on a beautiful geometric insight: for first-order PDEs, there exist special curves in the $(x,t)$-plane along which the PDE reduces to an ordinary differential equation. These curves, called characteristics, are the paths along which information propagates. Following them, you can trace every value of the solution back to the initial data. The method is exact, geometrically transparent, and extends to quasilinear equations — where it reveals the mechanism of shock formation.

The Linear Transport Equation

Start with the simplest case: $u_t + c\, u_x = 0$ (constant advection speed $c$). Along any curve $x = x_0 + ct$ (a straight line), the directional derivative of $u$ is zero: $$\frac{d}{dt} u(x_0+ct,\, t) = u_x \cdot c + u_t = 0$$ So $u$ is constant along each such line — the characteristic. Solution: $u(x,t) = u_0(x - ct)$. The initial profile translates to the right at speed $c$ without changing shape.

General First-Order Quasilinear PDEs

For $a(x,t,u)\, u_x + b(x,t,u)\, u_t = c(x,t,u)$, the characteristic ODEs are:

Characteristic System
$$\frac{dx}{ds} = a(x,t,u), \quad \frac{dt}{ds} = b(x,t,u), \quad \frac{du}{ds} = c(x,t,u)$$

Solve this ODE system with initial data along the initial curve. The solution surface is swept out by the characteristics.

Shock Formation: When Characteristics Cross

The real drama of the method of characteristics comes from quasilinear equations where the wave speed depends on the solution itself. Consider Burgers' equation (inviscid): $u_t + u\, u_x = 0$. Here the characteristic speed is $c = u$ — waves travel at their own height. The characteristics are lines $x = x_0 + u_0(x_0)\, t$. If the initial data has a decreasing part ($u_0' < 0$), faster waves from behind will eventually catch up to slower waves ahead.

Shock Formation Time
$$t_{\text{shock}} = \frac{-1}{\min_{x} u_0'(x)}$$ At this time, characteristics cross — the method breaks down and the classical solution ceases to exist. A discontinuity (shock) forms. Nature resolves this through viscosity; mathematics resolves it through the theory of weak solutions and entropy conditions.

Eikonal Equation and Geometric Optics

The eikonal equation $|\nabla u| = \frac{1}{c(\mathbf{x})}$ governs the wavefronts of high-frequency waves in an inhomogeneous medium. Its characteristics are the rays of geometric optics — the paths along which light travels. The method of characteristics applied to the eikonal equation gives Snell's law of refraction and explains the bending of light rays. It is the rigorous foundation of geometric optics and the mathematical basis for lens design, fibre optics, and gravitational lensing.

Green's Functions

The impulse response — the most powerful tool for linear PDE theory

George Green was a miller's son from Nottingham who taught himself higher mathematics by borrowing books from a subscription library. In 1828, working largely in isolation, he published "An Essay on the Application of Mathematical Analysis to the Theories of Electricity and Magnetism" — one of the most original pieces of mathematical physics of the 19th century. In it, he introduced what we now call Green's functions: solutions due to a point source, from which solutions to arbitrary source distributions can be built by superposition. The work was almost entirely ignored until William Thomson (Lord Kelvin) rediscovered it in 1845 and recognised its importance.

The Core Idea

Consider a linear PDE $Lu = f$ on domain $\Omega$. The Green's function $G(x, y)$ is defined by:

$$L_x\, G(x,y) = \delta(x-y) \quad \text{in } \Omega, \qquad G(x,y) = 0 \quad \text{on } \partial\Omega$$

It is the response of the system to a unit point source at $y$. Once $G$ is known, the solution to $Lu = f$ with homogeneous boundary conditions is:

$$u(x) = \int_\Omega G(x,y)\, f(y)\, dy$$

This is the PDE version of "knowing the response to a delta function lets you compute the response to any input by superposition" — the fundamental principle of linear systems theory.

Green's Function for the Laplacian on a Half-Space

On the upper half-plane $\{y > 0\}$ with $u = 0$ on $\{y = 0\}$, the Green's function is obtained by the method of images: place a "mirror charge" at the reflection of the source point:

$$G(x, x_0) = \Phi(x - x_0) - \Phi(x - x_0^*)$$

where $x_0^*$ is the reflection of $x_0$ in the boundary, and $\Phi$ is the fundamental solution of Laplace's equation. This elegant trick — which has a beautiful physical interpretation as an image charge in electrostatics — gives exact formulae in many geometries.

Poisson's Formula via Green's Functions

For the disk $|x| < R$, the Green's function leads to the Poisson integral formula: the solution to $\Delta u = 0$ inside the disk with boundary data $u = g$ on $|x| = R$ is:

Poisson Integral Formula
$$u(r, \theta) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} \frac{R^2 - r^2}{R^2 - 2Rr\cos(\theta-\phi) + r^2}\, g(\phi)\, d\phi$$

This is a weighted average of the boundary data, with the weight (Poisson kernel) largest near the point on the boundary directly "facing" the interior point. It gives an explicit, closed-form solution for Laplace's equation on the disk — valid for any continuous boundary data.

Green's Theorem and the Representation Formula

Green's functions are intimately tied to Green's second identity:

$$\int_\Omega (u\, \Delta v - v\, \Delta u)\, dx = \int_{\partial\Omega}\!\!\left(u\, \frac{\partial v}{\partial n} - v\, \frac{\partial u}{\partial n}\right) dS$$

Setting $v = G(\cdot, x_0)$ and using $\Delta G = -\delta_{x_0}$ gives an integral representation of any harmonic function in terms of its boundary values — the foundation of boundary element methods in numerical PDE.

Distributions

Generalising functions so that everything is differentiable

By the 1930s, physicists had become accustomed to using the "Dirac delta function" $\delta(x)$ — an object that is "zero everywhere except at the origin, but integrates to 1." It appeared in quantum mechanics, electromagnetism, and signal processing, and it was enormously useful. The problem was that no such function actually existed in classical analysis. No function that is zero almost everywhere can have a nonzero integral. Physicists worked around this by treating $\delta$ as a limit of ordinary functions, but this was unsatisfying. The rigorous resolution — the theory of distributions, or generalised functions — was developed by Laurent Schwartz in the late 1940s, earning him a Fields Medal.

Laurent Schwartz
1915–2002 · French mathematician

Schwartz described the moment of his discovery of distribution theory in 1944 as a sudden revelation during a night walk: he realised that instead of trying to define the "function" $\delta$, one should define it by its action on test functions. The Fields Medal committee in 1950 praised the work as "one of the most important contributions to mathematics in recent times." Schwartz was also notable for his political activism, spending years on Bertrand Russell's anti-war committees.

Test Functions and Duality

The key insight is to reverse the roles: instead of defining $\delta$ as a function, define it as a functional — something that acts on test functions and returns numbers. A test function is a smooth function of compact support: $\phi \in \mathcal{D}(\Omega) = C_c^\infty(\Omega)$.

A distribution is a continuous linear functional on $\mathcal{D}(\Omega)$. That is, a map $T: \mathcal{D}(\Omega) \to \mathbb{R}$ with:

  • $T(\alpha\phi + \beta\psi) = \alpha T(\phi) + \beta T(\psi)$ (linearity)
  • $T(\phi_n) \to T(\phi)$ whenever $\phi_n \to \phi$ in $\mathcal{D}$ (continuity)

The notation is $T(\phi) = \langle T, \phi \rangle$. Every ordinary function $f$ gives a distribution $T_f(\phi) = \int f\phi\, dx$, but there are distributions that are not functions.

The Dirac Delta
$$\langle \delta, \phi \rangle = \phi(0) \quad \text{for all } \phi \in \mathcal{D}$$ This is a perfectly rigorous definition. $\delta$ is a distribution, not a function. But it behaves exactly as physicists intended.

Differentiation of Distributions

The revolutionary step: every distribution has a well-defined derivative — defined so as to extend the classical integration by parts formula:

Derivative of a Distribution
$$\langle T', \phi \rangle = -\langle T, \phi' \rangle$$

For ordinary functions, this is just integration by parts: $\int f' \phi = -\int f\phi'$. For distributions, it's a definition. The consequence: distributions can be differentiated infinitely many times, even if they are discontinuous (like the Heaviside step function), or are not functions at all (like $\delta$).

Heaviside and Dirac are Related
Let $H(x)$ be the Heaviside step function ($H=0$ for $x<0$, $H=1$ for $x>0$). Then in the distributional sense: $$H'(x) = \delta(x)$$ $\int H'\phi = -\int H\phi' = -\int_0^\infty \phi' = \phi(0) = \langle\delta,\phi\rangle$. The derivative of the step function is the delta function — the formalisation of the physicists' intuition.

Why This Matters for PDEs

Distributions allow us to:

  1. Give rigorous meaning to fundamental solutions $-\Delta\Phi = \delta$
  2. Define derivatives of discontinuous functions (shocks in hyperbolic PDEs)
  3. Formulate weak (distributional) solutions to PDEs
  4. Use the Fourier transform on extremely general classes of functions
  5. Connect PDEs to probability through the theory of generalized stochastic processes

The Schwartz space $\mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R}^n)$ of rapidly decreasing functions and its dual $\mathcal{S}'$ (tempered distributions) provide the ideal setting for the Fourier transform — which extends to a continuous bijection on $\mathcal{S}'$. This is the foundation of the modern theory of linear PDEs with constant coefficients.

Sobolev Spaces

The natural habitat of PDE solutions — where analysis meets geometry

Classical analysis studies functions that are differentiable in the pointwise sense. But PDE theory forced mathematicians to realise that this is too restrictive: many physically meaningful problems have solutions with limited regularity — functions that are "almost differentiable" in some averaged sense, even if they have singularities at isolated points or along curves. Sobolev spaces, introduced by Sergei Sobolev in the 1930s, provide the right framework: they encode both function values and derivative values in an integrated, $L^2$ sense. They are simultaneously the most natural setting for the theory of weak solutions and the essential tool for all modern regularity theory.

Sergei Lvovich Sobolev
1908–1989 · Soviet mathematician

Sobolev introduced his function spaces in the 1930s while working on hyperbolic PDEs and the problem of wave propagation in inhomogeneous media. He did much of his most important work in Novosibirsk, where he helped establish the Siberian Branch of the Soviet Academy of Sciences. The Sobolev spaces that bear his name became indispensable only gradually — it took the post-war generation of analysts (Nirenberg, Agmon, Douglis) to fully exploit them.

Definition

For $k \geq 0$ a non-negative integer and $1 \leq p \leq \infty$, the Sobolev space $W^{k,p}(\Omega)$ consists of all functions $u \in L^p(\Omega)$ whose weak (distributional) partial derivatives up to order $k$ also lie in $L^p(\Omega)$:

Sobolev Space
$$W^{k,p}(\Omega) = \bigl\{u \in L^p(\Omega) : D^\alpha u \in L^p(\Omega) \text{ for all } |\alpha| \leq k\bigr\}$$ $$\|u\|_{W^{k,p}} = \left(\sum_{|\alpha|\leq k}\int_\Omega |D^\alpha u|^p\, dx\right)^{1/p}$$

The most important case is $p=2$, written $H^k(\Omega) = W^{k,2}(\Omega)$ — these are Hilbert spaces, and Hilbert space methods (inner products, projections, spectral theory) apply. The norm is:

$$\|u\|_{H^k}^2 = \sum_{|\alpha|\leq k}\int_\Omega |D^\alpha u|^2\, dx$$

Why Weak Derivatives?

A function $v = D^\alpha u$ is a weak derivative of $u$ if for all test functions $\phi \in C_c^\infty(\Omega)$:

$$\int_\Omega u\, D^\alpha \phi\, dx = (-1)^{|\alpha|}\int_\Omega v\, \phi\, dx$$

This is integration by parts without any boundary terms (since $\phi$ has compact support). For smooth $u$, the classical and weak derivatives agree. But weak derivatives exist for much rougher functions — for instance, the function $|x|$ on $(-1,1)$ has weak derivative $\text{sgn}(x)$, even though it is not classically differentiable at $0$.

The Sobolev Embedding Theorems

These are among the most important results in all of analysis, relating membership in a Sobolev space to regularity in classical function spaces:

Sobolev Embedding (key cases)
Let $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ be a bounded domain with smooth boundary.
  • If $k > n/p$, then $W^{k,p}(\Omega) \hookrightarrow C^{k-\lfloor n/p\rfloor - 1}(\bar\Omega)$ — enough derivatives guarantee classical differentiability
  • If $kp < n$, then $W^{k,p}(\Omega) \hookrightarrow L^{p^*}(\Omega)$ where $\frac{1}{p^*} = \frac{1}{p} - \frac{k}{n}$ (Sobolev conjugate exponent)
  • The case $n = 3$, $k=1$, $p=2$: $H^1(\Omega) \hookrightarrow L^6(\Omega)$ — a key estimate in nonlinear analysis

The embedding theorems are the key to bootstrapping regularity: if you can show a solution is in $W^{1,2}$, and the embedding theorem promotes that to $L^6$, and you can then use this to show you're actually in $W^{2,2}$, and iterate… Eventually, for elliptic equations with smooth data, you conclude $u$ is actually $C^\infty$. This is the regularity theory program.

Trace Theory

A subtle but critical issue: elements of $H^1(\Omega)$ are defined only almost everywhere — so what does it mean to prescribe boundary values? The trace theorem guarantees that there is a continuous map $\gamma_0: H^1(\Omega) \to H^{1/2}(\partial\Omega)$ — the boundary trace. Fractional-order Sobolev spaces $H^{1/2}(\partial\Omega)$ naturally appear as the spaces of "admissible boundary data" for elliptic equations. This is one of several places where non-integer orders of differentiability arise naturally.

Weak Solutions

Solving PDEs without solving them — the distributional formulation and the Lax-Milgram theorem

The theory of weak solutions represents the crowning achievement of functional analysis applied to PDEs. The basic philosophy is a subtle reversal: instead of requiring the PDE to hold pointwise (which demands classical differentiability), we multiply by a test function, integrate, and use integration by parts to move derivatives onto the test function. The resulting weak formulation makes sense for functions with far less regularity than classical solutions require — and in the Hilbert space setting, the Lax-Milgram theorem guarantees existence and uniqueness almost effortlessly. This is not a computational trick; it is a genuine change in what we mean by "solution."

From Strong to Weak: The Dirichlet Problem

Consider $-\Delta u = f$ in $\Omega$, $u = 0$ on $\partial\Omega$. A classical (strong) solution requires $u \in C^2(\Omega) \cap C(\bar\Omega)$. To derive the weak form: multiply by $v \in H^1_0(\Omega)$ (test function, zero on boundary) and integrate:

$$\int_\Omega (-\Delta u)\, v\, dx = \int_\Omega f\, v\, dx$$ $$\stackrel{\text{IBP}}{=\!=\!=} \int_\Omega \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\, dx - \underbrace{\int_{\partial\Omega} v\, \frac{\partial u}{\partial n}\, dS}_{= 0 \text{ since } v|_{\partial\Omega}=0} = \int_\Omega f\, v\, dx$$
Weak Formulation of the Dirichlet Problem
Find $u \in H^1_0(\Omega)$ such that: $$a(u,v) := \int_\Omega \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\, dx = \int_\Omega f\, v\, dx =: \ell(v) \quad \forall v \in H^1_0(\Omega)$$

Notice: we have moved one derivative off $u$ and onto $v$. A weak solution only needs to be in $H^1_0(\Omega)$ (one weak derivative in $L^2$) — not $C^2$. The Dirichlet boundary condition $u=0$ on $\partial\Omega$ is now built into the function space $H^1_0$ (functions vanishing in the trace sense).

Lax-Milgram Theorem

Lax-Milgram Theorem
Let $H$ be a Hilbert space, $a: H \times H \to \mathbb{R}$ a bilinear form that is:
  • Bounded: $|a(u,v)| \leq C\|u\|\|v\|$ for all $u,v \in H$
  • Coercive: $a(u,u) \geq \alpha\|u\|^2$ for all $u \in H$, for some $\alpha > 0$
Then for every bounded linear functional $\ell \in H^*$, there exists a unique $u \in H$ such that $a(u,v) = \ell(v)$ for all $v \in H$.

For our Dirichlet problem: $a(u,v) = \int \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\, dx$. Boundedness follows from Cauchy-Schwarz. Coercivity follows from the Poincaré inequality: $\|u\|_{L^2} \leq C\|\nabla u\|_{L^2}$ for $u \in H^1_0(\Omega)$ (the boundary condition is essential here). Applying Lax-Milgram: the Dirichlet problem always has a unique weak solution in $H^1_0(\Omega)$. Existence and uniqueness in two lines of argument.

Regularity: Getting from Weak to Classical

Showing a weak solution exists is only the beginning. The elliptic regularity theorem says: if the data $f$ is smooth and $\partial\Omega$ is smooth, the weak solution is actually a classical solution.

Elliptic Regularity (Schauder Estimates)
If $-\Delta u = f \in H^{k}(\Omega)$ in the weak sense, and $\partial\Omega \in C^{k+2}$, then $u \in H^{k+2}(\Omega)$ and: $$\|u\|_{H^{k+2}} \leq C\!\left(\|f\|_{H^k} + \|u\|_{L^2}\right)$$ In particular: $f \in C^\infty \Rightarrow u \in C^\infty$.

The program of weak solutions and regularity theory is one of the great triumphs of 20th century analysis. It gives a clean, complete existence-uniqueness-regularity theory for elliptic equations with smooth data, and extends — with more effort — to parabolic equations (Lions-Magenes) and linear hyperbolic equations (Kreiss symmetriser theory).

Weak Solutions and Numerical Methods

The weak formulation is also the foundation of the finite element method — the dominant numerical method for PDEs in engineering. One replaces $H^1_0(\Omega)$ with a finite-dimensional subspace of piecewise polynomial functions (the "finite element space") and solves the discrete weak problem. The Lax-Milgram theorem guarantees the discrete problem is well-posed, and Céa's lemma gives a best-approximation property: the finite element solution is the best approximation to the true solution in the energy norm.

Variational Methods

Solutions as minimisers — calculus of variations and the Euler-Lagrange equation

There is another way to think about PDEs — one that connects them to the most ancient and philosophical questions in physics: why does nature choose the paths and shapes it does? The calculus of variations answers: because nature minimises (or makes stationary) some energy or action functional. Every physical law derived from a variational principle automatically comes with a PDE as its Euler-Lagrange equation. This is not a coincidence — it is the deep structure of mechanics, electrodynamics, general relativity, and quantum field theory. Variational methods are simultaneously a solution technique and a worldview.

The Dirichlet Principle

Consider the functional:

Dirichlet Energy
$$E[u] = \int_\Omega |\nabla u|^2\, dx$$

Claim: Among all functions $u \in H^1(\Omega)$ with $u = g$ on $\partial\Omega$, the minimiser of $E[u]$ satisfies $\Delta u = 0$. The Laplacian is the Euler-Lagrange equation of the Dirichlet energy. Harmonic functions are energy minimisers.

Riemann stated the Dirichlet principle as "obviously true" in his 1851 dissertation and used it to prove fundamental theorems of complex function theory. Weierstrass then showed that Riemann's argument was circular: he had assumed a minimiser existed without proof. The scandal shook the mathematical world. Hilbert's 1900 resolution — he proved the existence of a minimiser using the direct method in the calculus of variations — launched modern functional analysis.

The Direct Method

The modern proof of existence for variational problems has a clean structure:

Direct Method in the Calculus of Variations
  1. Take a minimising sequence: $u_n \in \mathcal{A}$ with $E[u_n] \to \inf E$
  2. Show the sequence is bounded in an appropriate Sobolev space
  3. Extract a weakly convergent subsequence $u_{n_k} \rightharpoonup u$ (using Hilbert/reflexive Banach space structure)
  4. Show $E$ is weakly lower semicontinuous: $E[u] \leq \liminf E[u_{n_k}]$
  5. Conclude $u$ is a minimiser

Step 3 is the compactness step — this is where Sobolev embeddings are critical. Step 4 follows from convexity of $E$. The method is robust and applies to a vast range of problems.

Noether's Theorem: Symmetry and Conservation Laws

One of the most beautiful theorems in all of mathematics connects the variational perspective to conserved quantities. Emmy Noether's 1915 theorem states: every continuous symmetry of a variational problem gives rise to a conservation law.

  • Time translation symmetry $\to$ conservation of energy
  • Spatial translation symmetry $\to$ conservation of momentum
  • Rotational symmetry $\to$ conservation of angular momentum
  • Gauge symmetry $\to$ conservation of electric charge

Every conservation law in physics is (in this sense) a consequence of a symmetry, mediated through a variational formulation. The PDE version of Noether's theorem — relating divergence-free quantities to symmetries of Lagrangians — is the foundation of modern mathematical physics.

Spectral Theory

Eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and the harmonic analysis of differential operators

Spectral theory asks: what are the "natural frequencies" of a differential operator? The question is motivated directly by physics — the eigenvalues of the Laplacian on a domain are the resonant frequencies of a drum, the energy levels of a quantum particle in a box, and the decay rates of heat on a surface. The mathematical theory — built on the abstract spectral theory of operators in Hilbert spaces, developed by Hilbert himself and vastly generalised by von Neumann — is one of the deepest and most beautiful in all of mathematics. Mark Kac's famous 1966 question, "Can you hear the shape of a drum?" is a spectral theory question. (The answer, it turned out, is generally no.)

The Laplacian Eigenvalue Problem

Dirichlet Eigenvalue Problem
$$-\Delta u = \lambda u \text{ in } \Omega, \qquad u = 0 \text{ on } \partial\Omega$$

The set of values $\lambda$ for which this has non-trivial solutions is the spectrum of $-\Delta$ on $\Omega$. The key results:

Spectral Theorem for the Laplacian
On a bounded domain $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ with smooth boundary:
  • There exist countably many eigenvalues $0 < \lambda_1 < \lambda_2 \leq \lambda_3 \leq \ldots \to \infty$
  • The corresponding eigenfunctions $\{u_n\}$ form an orthonormal basis for $L^2(\Omega)$
  • Every $f \in L^2(\Omega)$ expands as $f = \sum_n (f, u_n) u_n$
  • The smallest eigenvalue $\lambda_1 > 0$ characterises the Poincaré constant

Weyl's Law: Counting Eigenvalues

How do eigenvalues grow? Hermann Weyl proved in 1911 the beautiful asymptotic:

Weyl's Asymptotic Law
$$\lambda_n \sim \frac{4\pi^2 n^{2/n}}{(\omega_n |\Omega|)^{2/n}} \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

In particular: $N(\lambda) = \#\{\lambda_n \leq \lambda\} \sim \frac{\omega_n |\Omega|}{(2\pi)^n} \lambda^{n/2}$. The count of eigenvalues up to $\lambda$ grows like $\lambda^{n/2}$, and the proportionality constant depends only on the volume $|\Omega|$ — not on the shape.

Weyl's law means you can "hear" the dimension and volume of a domain from its eigenvalue spectrum. More refined terms in the asymptotic detect the boundary length (in 2D) and higher geometric invariants. This launched an entire field connecting spectral theory to geometry — spectral geometry.

"Can You Hear the Shape of a Drum?"

Kac's 1966 question: are there two non-isometric domains with the same eigenvalue spectrum? For 20 years it was open. In 1992, Gordon, Webb, and Wolpert constructed explicit examples of non-congruent planar domains (built from triangles) with identical spectra. The answer is no — you cannot always hear the shape. But the problem remains rich: for smooth convex domains, the question is still open.

Spectral Theory and Quantum Mechanics

In quantum mechanics, observables are self-adjoint operators on a Hilbert space. The Hamiltonian $H = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\Delta + V(x)$ (Schrödinger operator) governs energy. Its eigenvalues $E_n$ are the quantised energy levels, and its eigenfunctions $\psi_n$ are the quantum states. The spectral theorem for self-adjoint operators — which extends the finite-dimensional spectral theorem to infinite dimensions — guarantees these operators can be "diagonalised" and that their eigenfunction expansions converge.

For the hydrogen atom, $V(x) = -e^2/|x|$, the Schrödinger equation can be solved exactly by separation of variables in spherical coordinates. The eigenvalues are $E_n = -\frac{me^4}{2\hbar^2 n^2}$ — the Bohr energy levels, recovering the experimentally observed spectral lines of hydrogen. This was one of the greatest successes of the marriage of PDE theory and quantum mechanics.

Nonlinear PDEs & Shocks

Where linearity breaks down and the real world begins — conservation laws, shocks, and entropy

Linear PDEs are beautiful, tractable, and extensively understood. But the real physical world is profoundly nonlinear: the flow of air around an aircraft cannot be fully captured by linear equations; breaking ocean waves, traffic jams that form and dissolve, the crack propagation in a stressed material — all require nonlinear theory. Nonlinear PDEs resist systematic treatment. There is no general superposition principle, no Fourier decomposition, no Green's function. Instead, one must exploit the specific structure of each equation. The reward is a rich phenomenology: shocks, solitons, turbulence, and spontaneous pattern formation — none of which can occur in linear systems.

Conservation Laws

The prototype nonlinear PDE is the scalar conservation law:

Scalar Conservation Law
$$u_t + \left[f(u)\right]_x = 0, \qquad u(x,0) = u_0(x)$$

$f(u)$ is the flux function. Total "mass" $\int u\, dx$ is conserved. The characteristics of this equation are $\dot x = f'(u)$ — they travel at speed $f'(u(x,t))$, which varies with the solution.

The inviscid Burgers equation $u_t + uu_x = 0$ (flux $f(u) = u^2/2$) is the simplest example. Characteristics travel at speed $u$, so regions where $u$ is large move faster than regions where $u$ is small. If $u_0$ is decreasing in some region, the faster-moving left part of the wave overtakes the slower-moving right part — and characteristics cross in finite time.

Shocks and the Rankine-Hugoniot Condition

When characteristics cross, the classical solution breaks down — $u$ would become multi-valued. Physically, a discontinuity (shock wave) forms. To allow discontinuous solutions, we must reinterpret the conservation law in the weak (distributional) sense. A function $u$ is a weak solution if:

$$\iint \left(u\,\phi_t + f(u)\,\phi_x\right) dx\, dt + \int u_0\, \phi(\cdot,0)\, dx = 0 \quad \forall \phi \in C_c^\infty$$

At a shock moving with speed $s$ along a curve of discontinuity, the weak formulation reduces to the Rankine-Hugoniot condition:

Rankine-Hugoniot Jump Condition
$$s = \frac{f(u_R) - f(u_L)}{u_R - u_L}$$

$u_L$, $u_R$ are the solution values immediately left and right of the shock. The shock speed is determined by the jump in flux divided by the jump in $u$.

Entropy Conditions: Selecting Physical Solutions

The weak formulation allows too many solutions — discontinuities can go in any direction. The physical shock is selected by an additional condition. Lax's entropy condition for a convex flux: $f'(u_L) > s > f'(u_R)$ (characteristics run into the shock from both sides). The equivalent Oleinik entropy condition and Kruzkov's entropy solution framework provide the complete, well-posed theory for scalar conservation laws:

Kruzkov's Theorem (1970)
For $u_0 \in L^\infty(\mathbb{R})$, the Cauchy problem for $u_t + [f(u)]_x = 0$ has a unique entropy solution $u \in L^\infty(\mathbb{R}\times[0,\infty))$. The solution satisfies an $L^1$ contraction estimate: if $u, v$ are entropy solutions with data $u_0, v_0$, then for all $t > 0$: $$\int_{\mathbb{R}} |u(x,t) - v(x,t)|\, dx \leq \int_{\mathbb{R}} |u_0(x) - v_0(x)|\, dx$$

Systems: The Euler Equations

The full Euler equations for inviscid compressible flow form a hyperbolic system of conservation laws:

$$\partial_t \begin{pmatrix}\rho\\\rho u\\E\end{pmatrix} + \partial_x\begin{pmatrix}\rho u\\\rho u^2 + p\\(E+p)u\end{pmatrix} = 0$$

$\rho$ = density, $u$ = velocity, $E$ = total energy, $p$ = pressure. Closing the system requires an equation of state $p = p(\rho, E)$.

This system supports both shock waves and rarefaction waves. Its theory — systems of hyperbolic conservation laws — is vastly more complex than the scalar case. Glimm's existence theorem (1965) is a major result; the general theory remains an active research area.

Viscosity Solutions: Beyond Classical PDE

For first-order nonlinear equations like the Hamilton-Jacobi equation $u_t + H(Du) = 0$, the notion of a classical solution is too restrictive. The theory of viscosity solutions (Crandall-Lions, 1983) provides a notion of weak solution using comparison with smooth sub- and supersolutions. It gives a unique, stable solution theory — and connects to optimal control, differential games, front propagation, and Riemannian geometry. This was another Fields Medal-level breakthrough in PDE theory.

Geometric PDEs

When geometry flows — minimal surfaces, Ricci flow, and the proof of the Poincaré Conjecture

The 20th century witnessed a deep and fruitful unification of PDEs with differential geometry. Geometric PDEs are equations that relate the geometry of a manifold or surface — its curvature, length, area, metric — to differential conditions derived from natural variational principles. They include some of the most celebrated equations in mathematics: the minimal surface equation (arising from soap films), the harmonic map equations (from generalised elasticity and quantum field theory), and the Ricci flow — a PDE introduced by Richard Hamilton in 1982 that became, in the hands of Grigori Perelman, the instrument for resolving the Poincaré Conjecture in 2003.

Minimal Surfaces

A surface $S$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$ is minimal if it is a critical point of the area functional — equivalently, if its mean curvature $H$ vanishes everywhere. The Euler-Lagrange equation for area gives:

$$\text{div}\left(\frac{\nabla u}{\sqrt{1+|\nabla u|^2}}\right) = 0$$

This is a quasilinear elliptic PDE for the height function $u(x,y)$ of the surface. The simplest solution is $u = \text{const}$ (a flat plane). The helicoid and catenoid are other classical solutions. The question of when the Plateau problem (finding a minimal surface spanning a given boundary curve) has a solution, and how many, is a rich and still-active area of research.

Mean Curvature Flow

If a surface moves so that each point moves at speed equal to its mean curvature (in the inward normal direction), we get mean curvature flow:

$$\frac{\partial X}{\partial t} = H\, \mathbf{n}$$

This is a parabolic PDE for the position vector $X$ of the evolving surface. It is the gradient flow for area — the steepest descent for the area functional. Convex surfaces shrink to points in finite time (Huisken, 1984), becoming increasingly spherical. The flow develops singularities (the surface becomes "pinched") which must be handled by surgery — cutting out the singular region and replacing it with a smooth cap. Mean curvature flow with surgery is the foundation of Perelman's work.

Ricci Flow

Introduced by Richard Hamilton in 1982, the Ricci flow is an evolution equation for the metric $g$ of a Riemannian manifold:

Ricci Flow
$$\frac{\partial g_{ij}}{\partial t} = -2\, R_{ij}$$

$R_{ij}$ is the Ricci curvature tensor — a trace of the Riemann curvature tensor. The equation is a nonlinear parabolic system in the metric coefficients $g_{ij}$.

Ricci flow deforms the metric in the direction of decreasing curvature — it tends to "round out" positive curvature regions and spread curvature more uniformly. Hamilton proved in 1982 that any compact 3-manifold with positive Ricci curvature (initial condition) must be diffeomorphic to a sphere. But for general 3-manifolds, the flow can develop singularities.

Grigori Perelman
1966 – · Russian mathematician

Perelman posted three preprints to arXiv in 2002–2003 proving the Poincaré Conjecture (and the more general Geometrisation Conjecture) using Ricci flow with surgery. He developed profound new estimates for the geometry of the flow (monotonicity formulas, non-collapsing theorems) that allowed singularities to be classified and surgically removed. He declined the Fields Medal (2006) and the $1,000,000 Clay Millennium Prize. He remains one of the most extraordinary mathematical minds of the modern era.

Perelman's Proof: A Sketch

The Poincaré Conjecture (1904) states: any simply connected, closed 3-manifold is homeomorphic to the 3-sphere $S^3$. Perelman's strategy:

  1. Start with an arbitrary simply connected 3-manifold $M$
  2. Run the Ricci flow: $\partial_t g = -2\text{Ric}$
  3. The flow develops singularities ("necks" that pinch) at finite time
  4. Perform Ricci flow with surgery: excise the singular regions and cap them off
  5. Using monotonicity of his "entropy functional" and non-collapsing estimates, show the topology is preserved through surgery
  6. The flow eventually terminates in a round metric on $S^3$ — proving $M \cong S^3$

Every step involves deep PDE analysis: parabolic regularity, Shi's estimates for curvature bounds, Hamilton's compactness theorem for Ricci flows, and entirely new analytical tools invented by Perelman. The proof is a monument to the power of PDE methods in pure mathematics.

The Frontier & Open Problems

The deepest unsolved questions at the edge of human knowledge

PDE theory in the 21st century sits at a remarkable intersection: classical problems remain unsolved (Navier-Stokes, Yang-Mills mass gap), new connections to other fields are discovered regularly (random matrix theory, machine learning, quantum information), and entire new sub-fields continue to emerge. What follows is a survey of the most important open problems and active frontiers — the cutting edge of one of mathematics' most dynamic subjects.

The Navier-Stokes Millennium Problem

The Navier-Stokes equations govern viscous, incompressible fluid flow:

Navier-Stokes Equations
$$\mathbf{u}_t + (\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u} - \nu\Delta\mathbf{u} + \nabla p = \mathbf{f}$$ $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0$$

$\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x},t)$ is the velocity field, $p$ is pressure, $\nu$ is kinematic viscosity, $\mathbf{f}$ is body force.

In 2D, global existence and uniqueness of smooth solutions is known (Ladyzhenskaya, 1960s). In 3D, it is an open problem — one of the seven Clay Millennium Prize Problems, carrying a $1,000,000 reward.

The Open Question
Given smooth initial data $\mathbf{u}_0 \in C^\infty(\mathbb{R}^3)$ with $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u}_0 = 0$, do smooth solutions to the 3D Navier-Stokes equations exist for all time $t > 0$? If so, must they remain bounded? Or can the velocity field blow up in finite time (develop a singularity)? The physical significance: does turbulence arise from finite-time blow-up of smooth solutions?

The best current results: Leray (1934) proved global existence of weak solutions but not uniqueness. Partial results rule out blow-up at isolated spacetime points. The gap between the critical regularity at which blow-up is possible and the known a priori estimates remains frustratingly narrow.

Yang-Mills Existence and Mass Gap

The Yang-Mills equations generalise Maxwell's equations to non-Abelian gauge theories — the mathematical foundation of the Standard Model of particle physics. The Millennium Problem asks: in 4D Euclidean space, does a quantum Yang-Mills theory exist rigorously (as a probability measure on the space of connections), and does it have a mass gap (a positive lower bound on the energies of excitations above the vacuum)? This would give a mathematical foundation for the observed masses of subatomic particles. It remains completely open.

Turbulence and the Kolmogorov Theory

Turbulence is one of the last great mysteries of classical physics. The phenomenological theory of Kolmogorov (1941) predicts energy cascades from large to small scales with a $k^{-5/3}$ power spectrum. This is observed experimentally with extraordinary precision, but has never been derived from the Navier-Stokes equations. Making Kolmogorov's predictions rigorous — or showing they follow from first principles — would be a revolution in both mathematics and physics.

Singularities in Geometric Flows

Following Perelman's success, Ricci flow and mean curvature flow in higher dimensions remain incompletely understood. In dimensions $\geq 4$, singularities can be more complex than the cylindrical blow-ups that arise in 3D. Classifying all possible singularities of Ricci flow in dimension 4 (relevant for understanding 4-manifold topology) is a major open problem.

PDEs and Machine Learning

A striking development of the last decade: PDEs are appearing at the foundation of machine learning theory. Neural networks can be understood as discretisations of ODE/PDE dynamical systems (ResNets correspond to explicit Euler schemes for an ODE on the space of features). Implicit regularisation in stochastic gradient descent has PDE descriptions. Mean-field theory for neural network training gives McKean-Vlasov PDEs. Score-based diffusion models (underlying tools like DALL-E and Stable Diffusion) are directly built on the theory of the Fokker-Planck equation and the heat equation.

The Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) Frontier
A new paradigm for solving PDEs numerically uses deep neural networks trained to satisfy PDE constraints. The network is trained by minimising a loss that includes the PDE residual, boundary conditions, and (if available) observed data. PINNs can handle high-dimensional PDEs that are intractable for classical methods, at the cost of reduced rigorousness and uncertain convergence guarantees. Establishing rigorous convergence theory for PINNs is an active research area bridging PDEs and machine learning.

Dispersive PDEs and Solitons

Nonlinear dispersive PDEs — like the nonlinear Schrödinger equation (NLS) $iu_t + \Delta u + |u|^{2\sigma} u = 0$ and the Korteweg-de Vries equation (KdV) $u_t + 6uu_x + u_{xxx} = 0$ — exhibit striking phenomena: solitons (stable localised waves that maintain their shape indefinitely and pass through each other elastically), blow-up, scattering. The complete classification of long-time dynamics (does every solution eventually scatter to free waves? Or form solitons? Can blow-up occur?) is deeply studied but largely open for the critical and supercritical nonlinear Schrödinger equation in high dimensions.

Further Study

A carefully curated reading path from first principles to the research frontier

The journey through PDE theory has many paths, and the right one depends on your background and goals. What follows is an honest, opinionated guide to the best resources at each level — the books that are actually read and taught from, the online resources that are genuinely useful, and the connections to other fields that make the subject inexhaustible.

Prerequisites

Essential Background

Multivariable calculus (gradients, divergence, Stokes theorem), ordinary differential equations (existence-uniqueness, phase portraits), and real analysis (limits, continuity, uniform convergence) are non-negotiable. Linear algebra (eigenvalues, inner products) is heavily used.

Helpful Background

Complex analysis (conformal mapping, residues) is deeply useful for 2D problems. Functional analysis (Banach/Hilbert spaces, operators) becomes essential at the graduate level. Riemannian geometry is needed for geometric PDEs.

Introductory Level

Strauss — Partial Differential Equations: An Introduction +
The best first textbook. Focuses on the Big Three (heat, wave, Laplace) and solution techniques (separation, Fourier transforms, characteristics). Approachable with only calculus background. Beautifully written with good physical motivation. The standard undergraduate text at top universities.
Evans — Partial Differential Equations (AMS Graduate Studies) +
The definitive graduate textbook. Comprehensive, rigorous, and brilliantly organised. Covers representation formulas, Sobolev spaces, second-order elliptic/parabolic/hyperbolic theory, calculus of variations, nonlinear first-order PDEs, and more. Dense but rewarding. The standard first graduate course text.
Folland — Introduction to Partial Differential Equations +
Emphasises harmonic analysis, Fourier transform, and distributions. Beautiful treatment of fundamental solutions and the Laplacian. Best read alongside Evans for a more analysis-flavoured treatment. Folland's writing is exceptionally clear.

Advanced and Specialised

Gilbarg & Trudinger — Elliptic PDEs of Second Order +
The encyclopaedic reference for elliptic theory. Contains essentially everything known about second-order elliptic equations: maximum principles, Schauder estimates, Calderón-Zygmund theory, quasilinear equations, the Monge-Ampère equation. Demanding but indispensable for serious research.
Taylor — Partial Differential Equations (3 volumes) +
Comprehensive treatment using pseudodifferential operators and microlocal analysis. Volume I covers basic theory, Volume II nonlinear equations, Volume III advanced topics. The modern approach for researchers who want to understand the full machinery of the subject.
Smoller — Shock Waves and Reaction-Diffusion Equations +
The standard reference for hyperbolic conservation laws and nonlinear wave theory. Covers Riemann problems, Glimm's scheme, viscosity solutions, and Turing patterns. Well-written and self-contained.
Chow & Knopf / Topping — Lectures on the Ricci Flow +
For geometric PDEs. Chow & Knopf provide a thorough treatment of the technical foundations. Topping's notes (freely available online) give a more accessible introduction. Essential for understanding Perelman's work.

Online Resources

MIT OpenCourseWare

18.152 Introduction to PDEs and 18.156 Differential Analysis both have complete lecture notes, problem sets, and in some cases video lectures. Free and rigorous.

Terry Tao's Blog

terrytao.wordpress.com. Fields Medallist Terence Tao posts expository articles on PDEs, harmonic analysis, and related topics at research level. The notes on wave equations, Navier-Stokes, and dispersive PDEs are exceptional.

ICM Proceedings

The International Congress of Mathematicians proceedings contain survey articles by the world's best mathematicians on active frontiers. Free online. An excellent way to understand where the subject is heading.

arXiv math.AP

The preprint server for new research in Analysis of PDEs. Follow it regularly once you're at the graduate level. It gives you a real-time view of what the community is working on.
